## UNION OF THE COMOROS

REQUEST FOR A STAFF-MONITORED PROGRAM -DEBT SUSTAINABILIT ANALYSIS

Approved By Vivek Arora and Andrea Schaechter (IMF)

Prepared by the staffs of the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the International Development Association (IDA).


Comoros' risk of external debt distress and overall risk of debt distress are high, a deterioration  from the finding of moderate risk in the DSA prepared for the 2019 Article IV consultation. 1 The change mainly reflects higher debt service obligations.  Three out of four external debt burden indicators breach their respective thresholds under the macroeconomic assumptions  underlying  the Staff-Monitored Program (SMP) request. The recent taking up of large non-concessional  loan with a short maturity and the integration  of expected postal bank recapitalization  costs into  underlying  macroeconomic projections were key drivers of the deterioration, while  a pandemic-related  weakening  in economic performance also pushed the debt burden higher. Mitigating  risks to debt sustainability  requires (i) making faster progress on domestic resource mobilization  and accelerating  GDP and exports growth; (ii) ensuring  gradual but sustained fiscal consolidation  over the medium  term; and (iii)  avoiding the contracting of further non-concessional  borrowing.

1 Comoros' debt carrying capacity is assessed as medium ,  given a Composite Indicator of 2.89 based on April 2021 WEO projections and the 2019 Country Policy and Institutional Assessment (CPIA)  rating.

## PUBLIC DEBT COVERAGE

1. The coverage of external debt effectively comprises the entire public sector (Text Table 1). It includes external debt of the central government, the central bank borrowing on behalf of the government (believed to be zero), and government-guaranteed debt of state-owned enterprises (SOEs, also believed to be zero). 2 Subnational government entities cannot take up external debt on their own, and SOEs cannot access the external debt market without a government guarantee. Information on domestic debt is limited to central government debt to domestic commercial banks (the government does not issue tradable debt securities). No information is available on domestic debt incurred by SOEs. The government intends to improve debt coverage through enhanced SOE oversight and improved financial reporting, which is supported under plans for SNPSF reforms.


2. Comoros faces substantial contingent liabilities, estimated at 8.8 percent of GDP. As in the previous DSA, contingent liabilities not captured in government debt are set to 1.8 percent of GDP, the estimated level of domestic arrears that would remain after validation (the authorities intend to commission an audit of these arrears by end-September 2021, and staff will include the results in the next DSA). 3 Contingent liabilities created by SOEs are set to the default level of 2 percent of GDP, and contingent liabilities from the financial system to the default of 5 percent of GDP (lower than in the previous DSA as the cost of recapitalizing postal bank SNPSF has been brought into the macroeconomic projections). 4

## BACKGROUND

## A.   Recent Debt Developments

3. Comoros' public external debt burden has grown quickly in recent years mainly due to the taking up of loans related to large investment projects, some on non-concessional terms. External public debt reached 27.0 percent of GDP at end-2020 (from 18.6 percent of GDP at end-2018) and is expected to rise further to 28.9 percent of GDP at end-2021:
- In 2018, the authorities took up a loan of USD 29.5 million (2.5 percent of 2018 GDP) from a commercial bank (Eastern and Southern African Trade and Development Bank) to help finance the construction of an ambitious hospital project. The loan is repayable over 10 years and has no grant element.
- In 2019, the government took up a loan of USD 82 million (6.9 percent of 2019 GDP) from the Export-Import Bank of China to finance a modernization of equipment at Comoros Telecom. The loan is repayable over 25 years and has a grant element of 35.8 percent.
- In 2020, the authorities took up a loan of USD 54.9 million (4.5 percent of 2020 GDP) through a contract with a foreign construction company to finance the building of a hotel. The loan is repayable over 6½ years and has a grant element of 7 percent.5

Cyclone Kenneth in 2019 and the COVID-19 shock played a more limited role in pushing up the debt burden by weakening macroeconomic performance and giving rise to emergency borrowing from the IMF.

## B.   Macroeconomic Forecasts

8. The macroeconomic forecasts underpinning the present DSA have changed from those used in the previous DSA (Text Table 3). The previous DSA was prepared in the context of the 2019 Article IV consultation and built on baseline projections and their underlying assu mption of 'established policies', as is required for Article IV consultations. In contrast, the present DSA accompanies a request for an IMF StaffMonitored Program (SMP) and therefore builds on a scenario of improved policy implementation. Key improvements include quicker progress in fiscal revenue mobilization, 8 higher rates of investment, and moderately higher long-term growth. At the same time, the current DSA also reflects events that were not anticipated during the preparation of the previous DSA, including the COVID pandemic and recent substantial non-concessional borrowing. Key assumptions are as follows:

· Real sector: Assuming Comoros will be able to vaccinate 35 percent of the population in 2021 and make further progress on vaccination in 2022, and incorporating the short-term fiscal expansion, growth is expected to reach 1.6 percent in 2021 and 3.8 percent in 2022. Thereafter, growth is expected to rise towards its long-term potential of 4.2 percent, with some variation around this potential in the next few years due to the hotel construction project. Staff have revised potential growth down by 0.3 percentage points (from 4.5 percent in an earlier iteration of the policy scenario) to reflect the likely impact of natural disasters, which are becoming more frequent due to climate change.

· Fiscal sector: For 2021-22, projections anticipate a fiscal expansion due to efforts to support the recovery and ongoing investment projects. This will be followed by fiscal consolidation during 2023-26 and a stable fiscal deficit (of 1.3 percent of GDP) thereafter. Consolidation will be achieved mainly through progress in revenue mobilization and by phasing out pandemic-related fiscal support. As a result, all debt burden indicators are projected to remain below, or fall below, their respective thresholds. Projections include a fiscal financing gap starting in 2022 that mainly reflects

## 9. Realism tools do not flag any lack of realism of macroeconomic projections (Figures 3 and

4). The 3-year fiscal adjustment is smaller than in most historical Fund-supported programs. The difference between projected GDP growth in 2022 and growth that would be projected by standard fiscal-growth multipliers can be explained by the fact that the year 2022 should see an at least partial recovery from the pandemic. ## C.   Country Classification and Determination of Stress Test Scenarios

## 10. Comoros is assessed to have medium debt carrying capacity, unchanged from before (Text

Table 4), with a Composite Indicator (CI) of 2.9 based on April 2021 WEO projections and the 2019 Country Policy and Institutional Assessment (CPIA) rating. The relevant thresholds indicating high risk are 40 percent for the PV of debt-to-GDP ratio, 180 percent for the PV of debt-to-exports ratio, 15 percent for the debt service-to-exports ratio, and 18 percent for the debt service-to revenue ratio. These thresholds are applicable to public and publicly guaranteed external debt.

11. Standardized stress tests use default settings. Further, Comoros qualifies for the natural disaster stress test given that it is exposed and vulnerable to natural disaster shocks, such as the eruption of the Karthala volcano or hurricane landfall. Given that the average annual impact of natural disasters is already factored into GDP growth projections, the size of the shock is reduced to 9.7 percent of GDP (from the default value of 10 percent of GDP).

## DEBT SUSTAINABILITY

## A.   External Debt Sustainability

12.

Three out of four external debt burden indicators breach their respective thresholds under the macroeconomic assumptions underlying the SMP request (Figure 1). The debt-to-exports indicator crosses the threshold in 2021 and again during 2023-25; the debt service-to-exports indicator breaches during 2024-27; and the debt service-to-revenue indictor over 2024-25. The recent taking up of large non-concessional loan with a short maturity and the integration of expected postal bank recapitalization costs into underlying macroeconomic projections were key drivers of the deterioration, while a pandemic-related weakening in economic performance also pushed the debt burden higher. 9  From a more structural perspective, the low level of fiscal revenue and the narrow base of exports are key risks for debt sustainability.

13. With this, staff assesses Comoros' risk of external debt distress as high, a deterioration from the finding of moderate risk in the DSA prepared for the 2019 Article IV consultation. Staff does not see country-specific elements that would suggest using judgment to change the mechan ical 'high risk' assessment resulting from threshold breaches in one direction or the other.

9 The authorities estimate the postal bank (SNPSF) recapitalization cost at US$ 26.9 million. This is included in the fiscal framework underlying the SMP as a change in the net acquisition of assets.

## 14. Stress tests and alternative scenarios demonstrate Comoros' vulnerability to shocks . An

exports shock constitutes the most extreme shock for the PV of debt-to-GDP, the PV of debt-to-exports, and the debt service-to-exports ratios, while a one-time depreciation is identified as the most extreme shock for the debt service-to-revenue ratio.

## B.   Total Public Debt Sustainability

15. Overall public debt is projected to remain well below its indicative benchmark under both the macroeconomic assumptions underlying the SMP request and stress tests (Figure 2), reflecting the only very limited domestic borrowing. The PV of total public debt-to-GDP ratio increases gradually but remains well below the benchmark. While an exports shock constitutes the most extreme shock for the PV of debt-to-GDP and PV of debt-to-revenue ratios, a depreciation is identified as the most extreme shock for the debt service-to-revenue ratio.

## C.   Risk Assessment and Vulnerabilities

## 16. Comoros' risk of external debt distress and overall risk of debt distress are assessed as high.

Staff assesses that debt remains sustainable given the only limited size and duration of the breaches. In particular, all debt burden indicators are projected to remain or fall below their respective thresholds during the ten-year projection horizon. Vulnerabilities to the DSA projections remain significant given the possibility of a more prolonged/intensified pandemic, social and political unrest, or further natural disasters.

17. Mitigating risks to public debt requires several policy actions: (i) making faster progress on domestic resource mobilization and accelerating GDP and exports growth; (ii) ensuring gradual but sustained fiscal consolidation over the medium term; and (iii) avoiding the contracting of any further nonconcessional borrowing.
