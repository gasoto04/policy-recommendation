# I. INTRODUCTION  

1． A mission visited Accra during September 10-24, 1998 to conduct the midterm review for the second annual Enhanced Structural Adjustment Facility (ESAF) arrangement and 1998 Article IV consultation discussions.1 In approving the second annual arrangement on March 23, 1998, the Executive Board commended the authorities for adopting appropriate financial policies in 1997, noting in particular the domestic primary balance of 3.2 percent of GDP. Several Executive Directors emphasized the importance of a successful second-year arrangement for restoring confidence in the government's commitment to stable macroeconomic policies and structural reform. Directors strongly supported liberalization of the cocoa market in line with neighboring countries.  

2. The staff reported to the Board on June 10, 1998 on a staff visit in the second half of May. Ghana's performance was found to be broadly in line with the program, but the staff also noted that Ghana was facing a major drought-related energy crisis and that this unanticipated development could weaken growth prospects. Contributing to this uncertainty were shortcomings in statistical reporting and delays in some structural reforms.  

3. Since then, Ghana has shown renewed commitment to the program objectives. All quantitative performance criteria for June 1998 were observed. The overall macroeconomic results are better than were expected at the beginning of the energy crisis. Progress was made on structural reforms, with the government redoubling its effort to catch up in areas where delays had occurred. Nevertheless, structural reforms continue to face obstacles, and Ghana is seeking waivers on the nonobservance of two structural performance criteria. In the attached letter of intent and accompanying memorandum (Appendix I), the authorities reaffirm their commitment to price stability through appropriate fiscal and monetary policies, and to the pursuit of structural reforms. The staff recommends that the waivers be granted and the midterm review completed.  

4. Ghana is on the standard l2-month Article IV consultation cycle; the last consultation was concluded on October 31, 1997. Relations with the Fund and World Bank are summarized in Appendixes III and IV, respectively.  

5. The economic program for 1998 envisaged a rebound in real GDP growth based on a strategy of stabilizing the economy and improving the environment for private investment by increasing the domestic primary fiscal surplus and maintaining monetary growth consistent with declining inflation. In addition, the program encompassed significant structural reforms to improve essential government operations and disengage the government from activities that can be more appropriately pursued by the private sector.  

# A. Macroeconomic Developments  

6. Real GDP was projected to grow by 5.6 percent in 1998, based on the expectation of good cocoa and other agricultural crops, a pickup in private investment in response to a more stable macroeconomic outlook, and a recovery in government investment, mainly in construction. However, in the first half of 1998, drought-related power shortages adversely affected production, notably in cement, aluminum, and mining. Following the initial shock, the supply of power was rotated among firms to minimize the impact on production; agriculture, which constitutes a large share of GDP but is not energy intensive, remained largely unaffected. Real GDP growth is now projected to be 4.5 percent in 1998, higher than anticipated at the onset of the crisis (Table 1).  

7. Inflation declined steadily from more than 70 percent at end-1995 to 20 percent in February 1998, although it ticked up in March and reached 23 percent in April, reflecting in part the effect of the energy crisis on some sectors, such as cement (Figure l). Most of the April price increase was due to a drought-related contraction in food supply. Since then, however, the inflation rate has been gradually declining, falling to 17 percent in September. By the end of the year, inflation is expected to be 12 percent, only slightly above the program forecast of ll percent.  

8. Interest rates are down significantly but still high in real terms. The 91-day treasury bill rate, a key money market reference rate, remained above 40 percent from the end of 1995 to the end of 1997, while the real rate moved from approximately minus 20 percent to plus 20 percent over the same period as inflation trended steadily downward.2 The 9l-day treasury bill rate declined in January 1998 by about 3 percentage points to 39.5 percent, and then declined in steps to 27 percent in early October. However, in September 1998, the real interest rate on 9l-day treasury bills was still about 10 percent. The interest rate on savings deposits in domestic currency was reduced in June by about 3 percentage points to 17 percent; however, lending rates on domestic currency-denominated loans remained in the 35-49 percent range. In response to declining prices and interest rates, the Bank of Ghana  

announced at the end of September a reduction of the rediscount rate from 45 percent to 42 percent.  

9. The external current account is improving but remains vulnerable. Owing mainly to a projected 17 percent increase in export receipts this year, the current account deficit (including official transfers) is expected to decline to about 6 percent of GDP. Imports are projected to increase by 6.3 percent in 1998. The net foreign assets of the Bank of Ghana were forecast to drop in the first half of the year by US\$116 million, but they increased by US\$22 million and are expected to exceed the program target for the year as a whole.  

10. The cedi depreciated by 3 percent in the first three quarters of 1998, considerably less than assumed under the program, reflecting mainly higher-than-projected cocoa exports. In real effective terms, the cedi appreciated by 45 percent from end-1994 to mid-1998—and by 12 percent in the first half of 1998 alone, when some prices increased because of the energy crisis.  

# B. Fiscal Policy and the Government Budget  

11. Ghana has strengthened its fiscal policy since 1997. The presentation of the 1997 budget signaled the government's intention to tighten financial policies following excessive levels of spending and financing in 1996. By end-1997, the government budget showed a domestic primary surplus of 3.2 percent of GDP (Figure 2 and Tables 2-4). This strong performance continued through the first half of 1998. Total revenue was slightly below its nominal benchmark but exceeded its target relative to GDP. The nominal target for tax revenue in the first half was met, with improvements in collection procedures for selfemployed taxpayers and higher imports of petroleum and generators contributing to this result; however, company tax collections suffered as profits declined owing to the energy crisis. For l998 as a whole, tax revenue is projected to be slightly higher than its nominal target under the program as higher petroleum and cocoa export taxes make up for shortfal i direct taxes.  

12. The domestic primary surplus in the first half of 1998 exceeded its target by 0.5 percent of GDP, partly because prolonged wage negotiations delayed the salary structure adjustment that usualy takes place in the second quarter. Instead, the government granted a relief allowance equivalent to a 20 percent salary increase to compensate for drought-related price increases; payment of this allowance began in July. Implementation of a new salary structure in the context of public service reform is under discussion, but the overallincrease in the wage bill for the year is expected to be in line with the original program.  

13.By mid-1998, interest payments on domestic debt were 0.6 percentage point of GDP higher than projected as interest rates did not decline as much as expected (Table 3). About half of this increase derived from recording the interest cost of treasury bill sold at discount at the time of issue, and not at maturity. Higher domestic interest payments were partly  

balanced by lower external interest payments as state enterprises were able to service their government-guaranteed external debt, whose obligations had been included in the budget as a contingency. Lower expenditure on subventions and on goods and services are also expected to help counter higher domestic interest costs for the year.  

14. After allowance is made for the adjustment in accounting for treasury bill sales at discount, the June 1998 performance criterion for net domestic financing of the government was observed. However, net domestic financing for the year is forecast to exceed the original program projection by about 0.7 percentage point of GDP because of higher domestic interest payments.  

15. Although road-related arrears reported at the end of 1997 were paid in the first quarter as projected under program, the stock of arrears was revised upward by 0.8 percent of projected GDP in the second quarter. The government reduced these arrears to 0.5 percent of projected GDP by end-July and plans to take measures to eliminate all road arrears by end2000. To ensure that arrears do not arise again, the government, with World Bank assistance, is auditing road contracts, and future road expenditures willbe planned in the context of the medium-term expenditure framework (MTEF).that was introduced in the third quarter of 1998 for preparation of the 1999 budget (see also paragraph 20 of the Memorandum of Economic and Financial Policies).  

16.Progress is continuing on the implementation of the value-added tax (VAT). The VAT law was adopted by parliament in February 1998, with a single positive rate of 10 percent applied to a broad base, and an effective refund mechanism.3 Regulations were published in July, and the tax willbecome effective in December this year. Staff members have been transferred to the VAT secretariat from other tax agencies, and procedures are being developed for collection of VAT on imports by the customs administration. The assignment of taxpayer identification numbers (TINs) is on schedule, albeit a tight one, for introduction of the tax in December.  

17.The creation of a central revenue authority, a performance criterion for June 1998, ran into difficulties because of constitutional provisions that preserve existing tax agencies as separate institutions. Rather than attempt to amend the constitution, which would take substantial time, the government will establish a central revenue board to achieve the same administrative improvements expected from a central revenue authority. Specifically, the government will have legislation in place by mid-November to appoint a central revenue board to take over the functions of the current governing boards for tax agencies, and a board secretariat to coordinate the activities of agencies.  

# C. Monetary Policy and Financial Sector Issues  

18. Broad money grew by 5.4 percent during the first half of 1998, considerably more than envisaged under the program (1 percent) but much less than the pace in the preceding year (16 percent) (Table 5). Reserve money growth remained below program levels throughout the period, implying an increase in the money multiplier relative to program projections. This outcome appears to have been the result of deposit mobilization efforts by commercial banks. The closure of foreign exchange bureaus that were conducting financial operations other than currency exchange might also account for a drop in currency outside banks.  

19. Bank credit to the private sector, which includes public enterprises, increased 17 percent faster than projected during the first half of 1998, on account of a strong demand for credit to finance imports of power generators and petroleum. In July, however, claims on public enterprises nearly halved. During 1998, private sector credit is expected to exceed the original projection by only a small margin. Budget financing by the banking system was in line with its June target. For the year, however, it is projected to exceed its target slightly, mostly because of a change in the accounting of interest on treasury bills sold at discount.4  

20. The net foreign assets position of the central bank has remained strong since the beginning of the year; it was above the level projected for June by US\$140 million, mainly because cocoa export receipts and export of invisibles were better than expected. Also, some external payments were temporarily delayed, but these are expected to be made in the third quarter. Overall, the net foreign assets position of the central bank is expected to be US\$20 million higher than originally projected by year's end (Table 5).  

21. Faced with a sustained increase in the money multiplier, the Bank of Ghana tightened its reserve money growth target for 1998 from 17 percent to 15.6 percent, in order to achieve the original 18 percent year-end target for broad money growth.  

# D. External Policy and Financing Requirements  

22. Ghana's external situation is gradually improving (Table 6). However, it remains vulnerable to exogenous factors, such as terms of trade changes and weather patterns. The current account deficit (including official transfers) is estimated to decline by about 3 percentage points of GDP in 1998 to about $5\%$ percent of projected GDP owing to an improvement in Ghana's terms of trade and an increase in official transfers (Figure 3 and Table 6). The current account balance is projected to fall over the medium term to just over 4 percent of GDP in 2002 as steady growth in exports more than compensates for the gradual decline in official transfers.  

23. Ghana's trade deficit is projected to narrow in 1998. A strong recovery in exports is expected to outweigh the increase in imports caused by the power shortage, resulting in a US\$120 million improvement in the trade account. Export receipts are projected to increase by about 17 percent in 1998 owing mainly to a bumper cocoa crop and higher world prices. A rise in the volume of gold exports is forecast to offset iower gold prices. Non-oil imports grew by about 8 percent in the first half of the year because of the demand for generators to meet the power shortage. Total import value is expected to increase by $6\%$ percent in 1998.  

24.The capital account is forecast to weaken in 1998. The main factor behind this trend is the amortization for the large nonconcessional borrowing in 1996 and 1997, which is projected to peak in 1999. However, because of the improvement in the current account, the overall balance for 1998 is expected to show a surplus of $\mathbf{US}\$60$ million, and net foreign assets of the Bank of Ghana should exceed the program target. Gross international reserves, which decreased by US\$70 millon in the first half of the year, are projected to turn around in the second half, increasing by $\mathbf{US}\$31$ million for the year. They should close 1998 at the equivalent of 2.6 months of imports.  

25. The balance of payments for 1998 is fully financed. External financing requirements of US\$829 million are expected to be covered by the concessional assistance pledged by donors at the Consultative Group meeting held in November 1997, and by disbursements associated with the second annual ESAF arrangement. However, there was a shortfall in foreign assistance in the first half of 1998, and donors will need to be contacted to identify its causes. Remedial measures may be needed to avoid a similar occurrence in the second half. Ghana still has to complete discussions on a bilateral agreement with a Paris Club creditor. For 1999, external financing requirements are projected to reach US\$785 million, of which $\mathbf{US}\$220$ million in grants and $\mathbf{US5565}$ million in concessional loans, including ESAF disbursements (Table 7).  

# E. Structural Reforms  

26. The main ongoing structural reforms are the liberalization of the energy and cocoa sectors, the divestiture of government ownership of enterprises, and public service reform. Progress has been slower than expected in some areas because of the need to address concerns about the impact of reforms on employment and wages, and to structure divestiture so as to increase competition.  

27. Progress has been made on energy sector reforms. A key objective of the Public Utilities Regulatory Commission (PURC), established in November 1997, was to restore electricity tariffs to economic levels. In February 1998, PURC increased the weighted-average tariff by about 90 percent, and, in September, it approved a further 10O percent increase. The Worid Bank estimates that, in the absence of significant variations from the baseline scenario of key variables such as inflation, the exchange rate, and fuel costs, the power utilities will be able to meet basic revenue requirements without additional tariff increases in 1999. Further, the government adopted a financial recovery plan for the power utilities based on converting debt to equity and deferring debt-service payments on government-guaranteed debt. This plan eliminates pre-1998 arrears in debt service through sales of government shares in the power utilities to the private sector.  

28. The offer for sale of Tema Oil Refinery, a performance criterion for June 1998, was postponed to end-1999, owing to negotiations with creditors to convert their debts into equity in the company. The Ghana National Petroleum Company (GNPC) divested its non-oil-related assets in March and completed the repayment of its long-standing debt to the Bank of Ghana in April. To increase competition in the retail petroleum market, the government continues to implement its deregulation program, which began with the adoption in 1997 of an automatic pricing mechanism for setting ex refinery petroleum prices in line with import prices. Based on this formula, uniform ex depot wholesale prices were introduced in February l998. Retail prices were to be deregulated earlier this year, but this step now awaits action by the newly established Energy Commission. Further to the objective of increasing competition, the Ghana Oil Company Ltd. (GOIL) has been offered for sale on October 29, 1998, with completion of the sale expected in early 1999. The three-month delay in offering GOIL for sale was due to heightened interest by investors in this divestiture, which prompted the government to make a public offer, rather than a private placement as originally planned.  

29. Reform of the cocoa sector is proceeding more slowly than expected. The share of the cocoa price going to farmers was increased from 54 percent to 56 percent in the crop year that started in October 1998. Privatization of the Produce Buying Company (PBC), a subsidiary of the Cocoa Board, would complete the liberalization of the domestic marketing that began in 1993 when private buyers were alowed to operate. Because the PBC controls about $3/4$ of crop purchases, there has been significant discussion within the government, and between the government and the World Bank, regarding whether PBC should be split into two companies before privatization, so as to promote competition. The lack of decision on this issue has delayed the timetable for divestiture of the PBC. Present plans are for completing the valuation of assets by mid-November 1998, together with the draft terms of reference for share flotation, including a shortlist of consultants. The offer for sale of the PBC has been rescheduled to March 1999. Unification of the extension services of the Cocoa Board and the Ministry of Agriculture started in August in three regions, later than envisaged under the program, owing to problems posed by different salary scales in the agencies. Full responsibility for these services will be given to the Cocoa Board in cocoa-producing areas, and to the Ministry of Agriculture in other areas.  

30. The divestiture program is progressing broadly on schedule. At present, the Divestiture Implementation Committee (DIC) has 28 enterprises in its portfolio. DIC completed the sale of half of the government's 80 percent share holding in Twifo Oil Plantation and three-fourths of its 40 percent share holding in Barclays Bank of Ghana. The sale of government shares in the Ghana Bauxite Company was also completed. Negotiations are in process for the sale of 8 enterprises; divestiture has been outsourced in 3 cases; and the State Insurance Corporation and the Ghana Sugar Estates were offered for sale. Mim Timber is also likely to be outsourced after negotiations for sale to the original owner collapsed. Offers for sale are expected shortly in the case of 5 other enterprises. The divestiture of the State Housing Corporation was held up by difficulties stemming from legal claims on the companies. The divestiture of the remaining 5 enterprises is in different stages of progress. The authorities also reiterated their commitment to divest 40 percent of its shares in the Ghana Commercial Bank, the largest state-owned commercial bank. The Financial Sector Adjustment Program Secretariat has just appointed four local investment companies to lead a search for strategic investors for the National Investment Bank, which has been unsuccessfully offered for sale. The World Bank willbe carrying out a review at the end of 1998 to clarify the status of the divestiture program and identify obstacles to the completion of the program.  

31. Ghana's public sector reforms include a number of initiatives to restructure the public sector and to improve the provision of public services. The cabinet approved in December 1997 a strategy for public service reform over the next ten years.6 The first step in this strategy is a pilot project for reform of subvented agencies, which currently employ about 70 percent of the civilian public service and include the Education Service, Health Service, and Highways Authority. Of the current 174 subvented agencies, this project willtarget at least 7 for restructuring, 5 for commercialization, and 5 for shutdown. By the end of the year, approval by the cabinet of specific subvented agencies for the pilot project is expected, and the draft legal framework and procedures for closing down or commercializing subvented agencies will be prepared. Finally, a new remuneration system based on job evaluations of a broad cross-section of civil service positions has been developed, with assistance from Price Waterhouse. The next step is to obtain the support of the social partners to this new approach, which would also consolidate salary negotiations that are carried out with various categories of personnel from subvented agencies. Efforts are also being made to remedy the operational deficiencies of the Integrated Personnel and Payroll Database with the assistance of the United Kingdom.

# II. MEDIUM-TERM OBJECTIVES AND POLICIES  

33. Ghana's medium-term goal is to create conditions for sustainable economic growth. The medium-term strategy is to stabilize the macroeconomic environment by curtailing monetary growth rates to levels consistent with single-digit inflation and maintaining domestic primary surpluses. In addition to fostering a stable macroeconomic environment, the authorities will make the environment more suitable for business, thereby encouraging private investment and saving. Structural reforms remain as described in the policy framework paper (EBD/98/21, 3/9/98), which include further deregulation of the petroleum and cocoa sectors, divestiture and restructuring of state-owned enterprises, and reforming and streamlining of the public service.  

34. During the decade ending in 2007, gross private investment is expected to rise by almost 4 percentage points of GDP (Table 10). This trend reflects the government's efforts to disengage from activities more appropriately pursued by the private sector by continuing and expanding the divestiture program. The government willalso continue to enlarge the scope for private sector activity by improving the regulatory environment for both domestic and foreign private investment. It will seek to increase competitiveness over the long run by vigorously pursuing price stability and raising the efficiency and spending level in education and health.  

35. Over the next decade, gross national saving is expected to increase by almost 5 percentage points of GDP above its 1997 level. The strategy for achieving this increase is to raise public saving by pursuing policies that establish and maintain a stable macroeconomic environment with low inflation and interest rates, thus contributing to a reduction of the public debt-service burden. The government willalso continue its efforts to raise public saving by cutting low-priority expenditure, strengthening tax administration, and broadening the tax base through structural reforms, such as the introduction of the VAT. Also, private national saving is expected to benefit from a gradual increase of income per capita and from the development of the financial sector.  

36. Under sound policies, Ghana's external position is forecast to improve in the medium term. The authorities intend to generate public savings and reduce government investment, with the objective of encouraging private investment and improving the external current account. Structural reforms, such as deregulation of the cocoa and energy sectors, would strengthen the performance of traditional exports, which are expected to grow at an average of about 9 percent in the next five years. The value of imports is assumed to increase slightly faster than GDP, or at an-average of about 6 percent in the next five years. Gross international reserves are expected to remain on average slightly below the equivalent of three months of exports through 2002, and then increase to a level that provides a more comfortable cushion against external shocks. Amortization of external debt and investment inflows will dominate developments in the capital account. The balance will deteriorate in 1998 and 1999 as previous nonconcessional borrowing is amortized, but it will improve in 20o0-01 as investment flows rise and amortization declines. Amortization of medium-term loans increases in 2002, causing the capital account balance to decline. Afterward, the balance improves again as direct investment flows increase.  

37.In summary, the medium-term framework shows that, under sound economic policies, Ghana's GDP can be expected to grow at real rates of 6-7 percent per year during the next decade, provided that fiscal imbalances are reduced. That conclusion can also be reached from the financing perspective: Ghana's ability to accumulate external debt is strained (see paragraph 49), grant assistance is gradually declining, and the domestic debt is growing fast, resulting in an interest burden that is large in terms of fiscal revenue and expenditure (see paragraph 40 and Appendix II). Therefore, the fiscal imbalances need to decline to ensure fiscal sustainability in the medium term.  

38. The medium-term framework helps identify some areas of potential risk for the economic strategy being pursued by the Ghanaian authorities. First, Ghana is expected to benefit from strong export growth during the next decade. This trend, however, assumes that cocoa reforms will move ahead expeditiously, a challenge that has been difficult for the government to meet in the past. Moreover, cocoa production can be affected by weather patterns and disease outbreaks, which could make it diffcult to achieve the expected export levels. Therefore, efforts to modernize the cocoa sector and diversify exports willbe crucial to the success of the strategy. Second, the power shortages that affected the Ghanaian economy in late l997 have subsided but are not completely resolved. While Ghana's capacity to generate thermal power has increased, its cost is higher, and low rainfall could again create a crisis. The implementation of the authorities' plans to deregulate the energy sector should allow the private sector to find alternatives ways for meeting Ghana's medium-term energy needs; however, time is of the essence as energy projects have long lead times. Finally, the medium-term framework makes it evident that Ghana's path for sustained growth is a narrow one: deviations from it could easily lead the country off the track of sustained growth. In the past, elections have often eroded fiscal discipline. With elections in 20oo, the government has an important responsibility to uphold in staying the course of the adjustment effort.  

# IV. DISCUSSION  

39. The medium-term projections based on current policies show a decline in the ratio of total revenue to GDP of $2\%$ percentage points over 1998-2007. The staff pointed to this trend when emphasizing the need to set an agenda for tax policy for the medium term. This downward trend reflects a declining government share of export tax revenue related to the reform of the cocoa sector, a fall in the real value of specific excises on petroleum products, and expected exchange rate and commodity price developments. Also, the tariff reform envisaged by the government may affect revenue from imports relative to GDP. The authorities recognize the need to address this projected decline in revenue; they share the staff s view that erosion of real petroleum taxation because of specific excises or exemption of petroleum from the consumption base of the VAT is not desirable. Beyond that, the base of the VAT may need to be broadened through the elimination of exemptions, and improvements in tax administration may need to be considered.  

40. The ratio of domestic debt to GDP was 17 percent in l997. Domestic interest costs are high, representing l6 percent of total expenditures and 31 percent of tax revenue in 1997. They reflect not only high interest rates on domestic debt, but also a marked increase in the debt stock in recent years to finance budget deficits. The staff conducted an analysis of the sustainability of domestic debt as part of the medium-term framework under the program. This analysis demonstrated the importance of stabilizing domestic financing as a percentage of GDP, in order to help bring down real interest rates. Based on the projected fiscal outcome for 1998, the analysis showed that the medium-term outlook under the program would imply a leveling off of the ratio of domestic debt to GDP at about $20\%$ percent in 1999-2000, followed by a modest downward trend over the medium term (Table 10 and Appendix II). However, these results are sensitive to growth and interest rate assumptions; therefore, the authorities should consider a further tightening of the fiscal stance in the medium term.  

41. The tariff changes described in the Memorandum of Economic and Financial Policies (paragraph 21) and implemented in March were only part of a more ambitious agenda for improving the efficiency of resource allocation. Aspects still under consideration include reductions in zero rating and exemptions.The authorities are also considering changing the tariff rate on some items to adjust for the introduction of the VAT in December. Specifically, tariff rates may be lowered on imports that are currently exempt from sales tax but will be subject to the VAT. The Fund and the World Bank staff are working together with the authorities on a study of the impact of tariffs on revenue and economic efficiency.?  

42. The monetary authorities welcomed in-depth consultations with the Fund staff in the context of financial sector surveillance.10 They explained their interest in deepening the existing financial markets to improve the monetary transmission mechanism and financial savings. They mentioned that, in August 1998, they had taken the initiative to propose a master repurchase agreement and expected that this step would help foster interbank transactions and facilitate liquidity management between treasury bill auctions. They reported difficulties in forecasting liquidity and requested MAE assistance in this area. The authorities also indicated that, in late 1997, the low level of trading in the foreign exchange interbank market induced the central bank to intermediate some operations to reduce temporary foreign exchange imbalances among banks. This was done through back-to-back swap operations, by which the central bank swapped foreign exchange for treasury bills with one bank, while executing the reverse operation with another. The staff was concerned about foreign exchange risk, but the authorities felt that the foreign currency exposure was almost fully hedged, making risks small In any case, the authorities explained that, now that the market had become familiar with the operation, the Bank of Ghana was encouraging banks to conduct them directly.  

43. The staff raised the issue of the high level of dollarization in the Ghanaian financial sector. In June 1998, foreign currency deposits amounted to 2l percent of total deposits, while foreign currency loans represented 3l percent of total loans. Moreover, in the first half of 1998, the expansion in foreign currency loans had accounted for 51 percent of the total expansion in claims on the private sector. The authorities were of the view that the only way to keep dollarization in check was by changing people's perceptions of the adequacy of the country's economic policies. Such a process would take time. They also explained that the avoidance of dollarization was a key consideration behind their policy of requiring bank reserves to be constituted in domestic currency, as well as their decision to close recently a number of foreign exchange bureaus that were not operating in accordance with their licenses. The staff suggested that the banking supervision should also ensure that, in the case of foreign currency loans, credit approval guidelines of commercial banks take into account the borrower's ability to earn foreign currency. The authorities indicated that banks generally followed that approach.  

44. The authorities and staff discussed the sluggish decline of interest rates in response to the fallin inflation and the role that shortcomings in the financial system, including the lack of competition among commercial banks, the banks' dependence on earnings from treasury bills to cover high operational costs, and the large borrowing requirements of the public sector, may have in explaining the outcome. The authorities and the staff shared the view that the improved macroeconomic outlook since the beginning of the year was expected to drive real interest rates down, and noted that treasury billinterest rates had fallen by 8 percentage points from end-June to early October. A problem, however, was that the decline in treasury bill  

rates was reducing banks' profit margins. The staff felt that for sound banks the reduction in profit margins was not a source of concern, and that the problems of weak banks had to be addressed urgently through privatization, mergers, and liquidation. Nevertheless, it cautioned the authorities against allowing interest rates to fall too fast--not primarily because of concerns about weaknesses in the banking system, but mainly because there was a need to monitor the effect of the drop in interest rates on monetary conditions.  

45. The Bank of Ghana (BoG) emphasized its intention to reinforce banking supervision. It will seek to monitor closely the foreign exchange exposure, which the staf had identified as being excessive, particularly in state-owned banks, and it will introduce prudential regulations on open positions before end-1998. In the wake of a massive check fraud uncovered in early 1997, the Bank of Ghana was also modernizing the payment system to reduce risks and improve service. In addition, the authorities are reviewing the 1989 banking law, with a view to updating the minimum capitalization requirements for banks and the penalty rates for noncompliance, to amend and reissue prudential regulations for foreign currency exposure, and to improve the regulations for administering banks in distress. The new regulations are expected to be issued before end-December 1998. While recognizing that Ghana's capital adequacy rules were prudent, the staff recommended that the authorities adopt regulations in line with the Basle Committee on Banking Supervision's guidelines.1 It also recommended that the authorities consider a new central bank law to strengthen the BoG's autonomy and accountability, as well as supervisory powers. Finally, the staff urged the BoG to divest its shares in commercial banks to avoid conflicts of interest; the authorities agreed to divest as soon as practicable.  

46. The staff raised the issue of the significant number of rural banks in difficulties. The authorities indicated that they had been taking measures to deal with this problem. Consideration is being given to the creation of an umbrella organization that will help supervise the rural banks, as well as provide services to them, such as staff training. The authorities noted that the Bank of Ghana had recently closed a number of offices of rural banks that had been operating at variance with their licenses.  

47. The Bank of Ghana announced at the beginning of August a freeze of the licensing of new banks, explaining that most prospective banks did not meet the requirements and that the study of their applications required scarce resources that were best directed toward bank supervision and restructuring. The staff indicated its strong preference for a transparent policy for entry and exit of financial institutions from the market on the basis of well-defined  

minimum requirements, and urged the authorities to lift the licensing freeze. The authorities assured the staff that the freeze was temporary and that it will be kept under review.  

# Box 1. Ghana: Summary of Exchange Rate System and Restrictions  

Ghana has had a floating exchange rate system based on an interbank market since March 1992. It has accepted the obligations of Article VIII, Sections 2, 3, and 4, effective February 21, 1994. It maintains an exchange system that is free of restrictions on payments and transfers for current international transactions. Residents are allowed to maintain foreign exchange accounts. Exporters are subject to surrender requirements, but are generally allowed to retain up to 35 percent of their export proceeds. The Bank of Ghana (BoG) records and confirms foreign capital inflows. Outward direct investment must be approved by the BoG. Inward direct investment requires approval of the Ghana Investment Center, if it is to benefit from the concessions of the Investment Code (1981). The share of nonresidents holdings, either individually or in total, in any one security listed on the Ghana Stock Exchange is limited to 10 percent and 74 percent respectively. Nonresidents are not allowed to bring foreign exchange for the purpose of investing in local money market instruments (BoG and government securities).  

48. The staf noted the appreciation of the real effective exchange rate of the cedi since end-1994 and called the authorities' attention to a staff study on this issue.12 It cautioned the authorities against intervening in the foreign exchange market to prevent the depreciation of the cedi with the objective of moderating price increases. The Bank of Ghana responded that it was committed to maintaining a floating exchange rate system and would continue to aim its foreign exchange operations at attaining the net foreign assets target of its economic program (Box l). It pointed to the fact that net foreign assets of the Bank of Ghana were in excess of their target for June and are expected to remain above target by year-end. The authorities reiterated that the Bank of Ghana should continue to focus its efforts on vigorously pursuing price stability, a strategy that they believe willeventually result in more stable real effective exchange rates.  

49. The external debt-service ratio increased from 27 percent of exports of goods and nonfactor services in 1996 to 32 percent in 1997, reflecting the impact of large nonconcessional borrowing in 1996 (Table 9). Under present assumptions, it is expected that Ghana's external debt service will fall below 20 percent of exports only in 200l. Debt-service payments, excluding to the IMF, amount to 12 percent of domestic revenue, and the net  

present value of the external public and publicly guaranteed debt willbe about 220 percent of exports at end-1998. Given the heavy burden of the external debt, the authorities reiterated their commitment to prudent external debt management. Both the authorities and the staff agree that a moratorium on government-guaranteed nonconcessional external borrowing is needed and should be maintained. In this context, the authorities reiterated their previous commitment to withdraw its guarantees on any nonconcessional loans for the Tano Field Development and Western Power projects. These projects will proceed only if significant private equity participation is secured, so as to eliminate the need for government guarantees. The government also indicated its plans to strengthen the external debt-monitoring capability of the debt-management unit of the Ministry of Finance.  

50.The staff was of the view that structural reforms had been moving too slowly. The authorities responded that wide-ranging structural reforms were time-consuming in a democratic system. Implementation of structural reforms in some areas had been delayed because of the need to debate and broaden support for measures in an open, democratic environment. The government believed, however, that such consensus building yielded more sustainable, lasting reforms.  

51. Cocoa sector reforms have been paced so as to build strong stakeholder support. This approach has caused some delays. For example, the unification of extension services provided by the Ministry of Agriculture and the Cocoa Board was slowed by the need to consider their different salary scales. The government indicated that it remained committed to reviewing cocoa sector operations, with a view to reducing costs at the Cocoa Board and thereby raising the farmers' share of revenue to at least 60 percent by 20o0. The authorities explained their decision to delay briefly the privatization of the PBC as being the result of an ongoing dialogue regarding the pros and cons of splitting the PBC into two companies before privatization in order to enhance competition. The staff urged the authorities not only to privatize PBC as soon as practicable, but also to allow cocoa purchasing agents to export directly without the intermediation of the Cocoa Board. In response, the government indicated that it was planning, with World Bank assistance, a major workshop on cocoa sector reforms in January 1999 aimed at developing a strategy to deepen the sector reforms. The matters raised by the staff will be addressed in that forum.  

52. Now that the Energy Commission has been created to promote competition while safeguarding safety and environmental interests, its members need to be quickly appointed, and its policies need to be clearly articulated to the public. The staff urged the government to acceleate the liberalization of the administrative controls on oil-marketing companies, so as to promote competition; the authorities responded that they expected that their efforts in this area would begin to bear fruit soon. The authorities explained that the sale of the Ghana Oil Company was more advanced than that of the Tema Oil Refinery because of the complications posed by the government's decision to rehabilitate the latter before its sale. While this rehabilitation was intended to improve prospects for sale, it had the effect of postponing the offer for sale to end-1999. The authorities provided the staff with a timetable of milestones for the Tema Oil Refinery project that could be used to monitor progress toward an eventual  

divestiture. The staff encouraged the authorities to give high priority to the study of the West African gas pipeline as a possible private sector-driven long-term solution to Ghana's energy problems.  

# V. STATISTICAL ISSUES 13  

53. Recent missions have noted deficiencies in the national accounts and, more generally, the timeliness of data availability. In response, the Ghana Statistical Service (GSS) is reviewing the methodology for constructing expenditure data for the national accounts based on the 1992 Ghana Living Standards Survey. In addition, the GSS is revising the methodology for estimating deflators of expenditure categories, as well as private savings and fixed capital formation. These ongoing revisions will continue to raise difficuities for the analysis of macroeconomic trends and economic policies in Ghana. The fact that the monetary accounts fail to classify foreign currency deposits by residence of the holder remains a weakness to be addressed. The authorities have been collaborating with the Fund and World Bank staffs in reviewing the methodology for compiling import statistics, with a view to eliminating discrepancies between statistics reported by the customs administration and the GSS, and to reducing the current lag in data availability to no more than six weeks. A public expenditure tracking system will be introduced to improve the quality of expenditure data for monthly monitoring. Finally, the authorities are committed to reducing the current lag in the publication of the consumer price index to a maximum of one month. The staff encouraged the authorities to begin preparations to meet the requirements of the General Data Dissemination System (GDDS) and to make economic and social data more widely available to the public.  

# VI. STAFF APPRAISAL  

54. The progress achieved in macroeconomic stabilization and structural adjustment in 1997-98, although commendable, needs to be consolidated to place Ghana on a sustainable, poverty-reducing growth path. In particular, Ghana will need to move speedily ahead with wide-ranging structural reforms that are crucial to the success of the government's development strategy: tax reforms, cocoa and energy reforms, public sector reforms, and the divestiture program.  

55. At about 20 percent of GDP, the domestic debt does not seem, at first sight, excessive. Nevertheless, the rollover of domestic debt has resulted in significant positive real interest rates that could dampen economic growth. Moreover, domestic interest payments have exceeded expected program levels. In response to these trends, the authorities have taken the initiative to raise the domestic primary surplus beyond its program target, so as to limit the capitalization of domestic interest payments and stabilize the domestic debt. The staff believes this response is a step in the right direction and should help to reduce real interest rates.  

56. In the medium term, the staffhas identified a tendency for tax revenue to decline as a share of GDP. While other tax measures should be considered to address this decline, the government's ability to sustain its fiscal effort depends on the successful implementation of the VAT. Full cooperation by all tax agencies will be required. The staff was disappointed that the proposed central revenue authority could not be set up as planned, but it supports the pragmatic course taken by the government to achieve the same goals through the establishment of a central revenue board.  

57. The accumulation of arrears in the road sector is a threat to the program. The government will need to ensure that fiscal discipline is imposed in this sector and that a further accumulation of arrears is avoided. The staff calls on the government to build on the progress made in incorporating future spending on roads into the MTEF by adopting effective measures to ensure that road expenditures are kept strictly within the limits of the 1999 budget.  

58. Since end-1994, the real effective exchange rate of the cedi has appreciated, with possible negative effects on the external competitiveness of the Ghanaian economy. However, the evidence of loss of competitiveness is not clear-cut, with some indicators pointing in the opposite direction. Although the staff agrees with the Bank of Ghana that reducing the inflation rate, while meeting its net foreign asset target, should be the BoG's top priority, it believes that the authorities should not intervene in the market to slow down the depreciation of the cedi. On the contrary, Ghana's international reserves, at $2\%$ months of imports, could be gradually raised, an action that would help slow down any trend toward appreciation. Consideration should also be given to tackling external competitiveness problems through efforts to increase productivity by investing in education, health, and basic infrastructure, as well as by speeding up reforms in key sectors such as cocoa, in which the government still has a major influence.  

59. Reforms in the cocoa sector have been slow, but the staff is encouraged by the government's decision to conduct a cocoa sector workshop in January l999 aimed at developing a strategy to deepen the sector reforms. Countries with liberal cocoa-marketing arrangements are gaining market share in international markets, and Ghana needs to modernize its cocoa sector to keep pace. The staff regrets the delay in offering the PBC for sale; it urges the authorities to make a final decision on the breakup of PBC as soon as possible to prevent further delays. The staff believes that the authorities should allow the cocoa purchasing agents to export directly without the intermediation of the Cocoa Board.  

60. Regarding the petroleum sector, progress has been achieved in divesting non-oilrelated assets of the GNPC and eliminating its debt to the Bank of Ghana. However, the liberalization of retail prices has been delayed, owing in part to resistance from the oilmarketing companies, which argue, inter alia, that minimum environmental standards are not enforced evenly across the industry. The staff urges the authorities to move ahead with their plans to appoint the Energy Commission, publish ex depot regional prices monthly, and liberalize retail margins while ensuring that al companies meet strict environmental standards. The offer for sale of the Ghana Oil Company should facilitate establishment of a level playing field in this area. The government's divestiture of the Tema Oil Refinery has been complicated by steps to revamp its instalations; it is important to avoid further slippages in the divestiture timetable.  

61. The staff commends the authorities for taking courageous measures to restore electricity tariffs to economic levels and adopting a financial recovery plan for power utilities. The World Bank has indicated that the combined effect of the February and September tariff increases fulfills the objective of allowing the power utilities to meet both their cash operating costs and their debt-service under the financial recovery plan. However, the implementation of the financial recovery plan will need to be monitored closely to ensure the improvement in the economic viability of the power utilities is sustained.  

62. Ghana has made considerable progress in liberalizing the financial sector, building a supervisory framework, and adopting indirect monetary policy instruments. Interest rates are fuly liberalized, and open market operations are used to manage domestic liquidity; most prudential regulations are in line with the Basle principles, and an action plan to modernize the payment system is being implemented. Notwithstanding this progress, the banking system remains a concern, as weak banks remain in operation awaiting the completion of measures to restructure them through mergers, liquidation, or sale. The slow progress in this area keeps the banking system vulnerable and the monetary transmission mechanism sluggish. The staff would encourage the government to take decisive action in this area. The adoption of new regulations on open foreign exchange positions, and their strict enforcement, is also a high priority.  

63. Ghana is a heavily indebted poor country. However, it has never had recourse to concessional debt-rescheduling agreements and has avoided sustained external arrears. Moreover, based on the last debt sustainability analysis, its external debt is deemed to be sustainable without recourse to debt relief.14 With contimued strict adherence to the financial policies envisaged in the program, including the moratorium on nonconcessional external borrowing, Ghana should be able to meet its external debt-service obligations, including those to the Fund. Ghana nevertheless needs foreign assistance to improve its social indicators; timely and reliable information on prospective assistance would help the authorities build a macroeconomic program that maximizes the benefits from this assistance.  

64.In view of the above appraisal, the staff recommends that the waivers be granted for the nonobservance of the end-June I998 structural performance criteria regarding the setting up of the central revenue authority and the offers for sale of the Tema Oil Refinery and the Ghana Oil Company Ltd.  

65. Ghana has consented to its quota increase under the Eleventh General Review of Quotas. The staff urges the authorities to complete procedures for the acceptance of the Fourth Amendment of the Articles of Agreement.  

66. The staff recommends that the next Article IV consultation take place on the standard   
12-month cycle.

# Memorandum of Economic and Financial Policies of the Government of Ghana for 1998  

# I. INTRODUCTION  

1. This memorandum reviews Ghana's performance to date under the economic program being supported by the Fund's second annual enhanced structural adjustment facility (ESAF) arrangement, and describes the additional measures taken by the government to accelerate the structural reform process and adjust the policy stance. Despite drought-related energy shortages in early 1998, the government has remained fully committed to the program objectives and was able to observe all quantitative performance criteria for June l998. It has also made progress in carrying out its structural reform agenda, although some temporary delays occurred.  

# II. PERFORMANCE IN THE FIRST HALF AND PROSPECTS FOR 1998  

2. In the first half of 1998, Ghana's economic performance was adversely affected by an unforeseen electricity shortage that curtailed production in some sectors such as cement, aluminum, and mining. However, the negative impact of the energy crisis was mitigated by energy management measures and by the decline in world prices for oil. Agriculture, which accounts for 37 percent of GDP, was largely unaffected. Therefore, real GDP growth in 1998 is now projected at 4.5 percent, significantly higher than forecast at the onset of the crisis, but lower than the 5.6 percent target.  

3. While the inflation rate in the first half of l998 showed a tendency to rise as a result of the energy crisis, by September the end-of-period annual inflation rate had declined to 17 percent, and the government's expectation is that it willbe only slightly above the program target (ll percent) by year-end. Helped by higher-than-projected cocoa exports, the cedi remained relatively stable, depreciating by 3 percent from January to June 1998.  

# A. Fiscal Performance  

4. The government was able to maintain the fiscal stance broadly in line with the program. The primary domestic fiscal surplus for the half-year exceeded its floor by 0.5 percent of GDP. Tax revenue was slightly below its nominal benchmark, but exceeded its target in GDP terms. Company taxes fellin the second quarter because of the impact of the energy crisis, but petroleum taxes were higher owing to the increased use of thermal power to meet energy demand. Grants were also below their target for the half-year by the equivalent of 1.3 percentage points of GDP.  

5. To compensate for the weakness of revenue, noninterest recurrent and foreignfinanced capital expenditures were curtailed below original projections. The shortfall in noninterest recurrent expenditure in the first half of 1998 was due, for the most part, to prolonged wage negotiations, which delayed the annual wage increase to July. Domestic interest payments, however, were significantly above projected levels as interest rates declined more gradually than expected. The effect of this increase was partly compensated by lowerthan-budgeted external debt service payments, as the budget included debt service of stateowned enterprises (with government guarantees), which these enterprises were able to pay from their own funds. In mid-l998, expenditure arrears in the road sector were found to be nearly 0.7 percent of GDP higher than reported at the beginning of the year, indicating a need to improve expenditure control in this sector. Net domestic financing of the government at end-June l998, a performance criterion, remained below its ceiling.  

6. In the second half of the year, the government is further tightening the fiscal stance to mitigate the effects of the energy crisis and higher-than-programmed interest rates. Total domestic revenue is expected to be slightly better than the program, with higher collections of sales and petroleum taxes and cocoa export duties compensating for the shortfallin direct taxes.  

7. For l998 as a whole, domestic interest payments are likely to be 1.2 percentage points of GDP more than forecast, raising recurrent expenditure above programmed levels. Wage payments are expected to be in line with program assumptions. The government reduced road-related arrears to 0.5 percent of GDP by end-July, and willtake measures to eliminate all road arrears by 20o0. To ensure that new arrears do not arise, the government, with World Bank assistance, is conducting an audit of road contracts, and future road expenditures will be planned in the context of a medium-term expenditure framework.  

8. With revenue collections expected to turn out slightly better than programmed and with the pressure on tax administration from the imminent reintroduction of the VAT, it would not be realistic to entertain measures to raise revenue beyond what is now expected. Therefore, continued expenditure restraint will be needed to permit the government to increase the domestic primary surplus above the level projected under the program.  

9. Budget for 1999. Preliminary work on the 1999 budget has been completed within the context of the new medium-term expenditure framework. The 1999 budget envisages a further increase in government savings based on improved domestic revenue collections, relying in part on further reform and rationalization of the tax system. Noninterest domestic recurrent expenditure relative to GDP is projected to decline, with social spending expected to rise as a share of GDP. The projected decline in domestic interest payments as a percentage of GDP follows from the programmed decline in interest rates.  

# B. Monetary Policy and the Financial Sector  

10. The 1998 economic program envisages a tight monetary policy to support the government's inflation target. During the first half of 1998, the growth in broad money was 5.4 percent, considerably higher than envisaged under the program (l percent).' This development was due to an increase in the money multiplier as the Bank of Ghana has continued to maintain reserve money within the limits of the program. The higher-thanexpected multiplier results from increased deposit mobilization efforts by the banking system as reflected in the decline of the currency-to-deposit ratio.  

11. Interest rates have been slowly declining since the beginning of the year. The interest rate on 9l-day treasury bills, a key reference rate for the money market, has fallen from $42\%$ percent at the end of 1997 to $33\%$ percent at end-July 1998. The real interest rate on the 9l-day treasury bill rates has declined from 18 percent at the end of 1997 to 10 percent at end-September 1998.² The Bank of Ghana expects that the interest rates will show a declining trend during the remainder of the year, and is prepared to reduce its rediscount rate (currently at 45 percent) as monetary conditions permit.  

12. The Bank of Ghana has continued to take steps aimed at facilitating the smooth operation of financial markets. As net foreign assets of the banking sector declined in the first half of 1998, the Bank of Ghana encouraged transactions in foreign currency to temporarily redistribute foreign exchange working balances among market participants. A new instrument was introduced in September 1997, which took the form of repurchase operations in foreign currency colateralized by treasury bills and intermediated through the central bank. The Bank of Ghana is gradually disengaging itself from its role as intermediary for these transactions. The Bank of Ghana is also promoting the use of repurchase operations to permit better management of liquidity between treasury bil auctions. A centralized book-entry system for treasury bills was completed in July and a master repurchase agreement was formally introduced in August 1998 setting the stage for a more active use of repurchase operations by the Bank of Ghana and market participants.

# C. External Sector  

14. Ghana's external situation is gradually improving, but remains vulnerable to exogenous factors, such as terms of trade changes and weather patterns. The current account deficit (including oficial transfers) is estimated to have increased from about 5 percent of GDP in 1996, to almost 9 percent in 1997, but is expected to decline in 1998 to about 6 percent owing to an improvement in Ghana's terms of trade. Ghana's export receipts are projected to increase by about l7 percent in l998, owing mainly to a recovery in cocoa exports, and notwithstanding the expected weakness in the international gold price. Imports are expected to increase by $6\%$ percent in 1998. External financing requirements for 1998 are expected to be covered by the concessional assistance pledged by donors at the Consultative Group meeting held in November 1997 and disbursements associated with the second annual ESAF arrangement. Donors will be contacted to ensure that the foreign assistance shortfalls observed in the first half of the year are at least partially reversed in the rest of the year. The government will promptly resolve remaining issues regarding bilateral arrangements with a Paris Club creditor. In the first half of 1998, gross international reserves declined by US\$56 million; for the year as a whole gross international reserves are expected to increase beyond the program target, but at 2.6 months of imports (c.i.f.), willremain modest.  

15. The external debt service ratio increased from 27 percent of exports of goods and nonfactor services in 1996 to 32 percent in 1997, reflecting the impact of large non-concessional borrowing in 1996. Under present assumptions, Ghana's external debt service ratio would fall below 20 percent in 20O1. With strict adherence to the financial policies envisaged in the program, and to the moratorium on nonconcessional borrowing, Ghana should be able to meet its external debt service obligations including those to the Fund. In this context, the government has withdrawn its commitment to guarantee any nonconcessional borrowing by the Ghana National Petroleum Company (GNPC) for the intended Tano Field Development and Western Power projects. These projects will now only proceed if significant private equity participation is secured. To further improve its external debt management, the government will embark on a program to strengthen monitoring of the external debt.  

16. The cedi has been appreciating in real effective terms since 1995 raising some concerns about the long-term competitiveness of the economy. The Bank of Ghana is committed to maintaining a floating exchange rate system and will continue to aim its foreign exchange operations at attaining the net foreign assets target of its economic program. It believes that exchange rates can only be affected in a sustainable fashion by a change in monetary and fiscal stance. Therefore, it will continue to focus its efforts on vigorously pursuing price stability, a strategy that will eventually result in more stable real effective exchange rates. Nevertheless, the government will continue monitoring developments in the foreign exchange markets and will conduct studies of export competitiveness to help guide future Bank of Ghana policies in this area.  

# III. STRUCTURAL REFORMS  

17. Implementation of structural reforms has not been without difficulties owing to the wide-ranging nature of the reforms being pursued in an open and democratic system. The need for parliamentary debate and the broadening of public support for measures have resulted in delays in carrying out the program policies. The government believes, however, that this consensus-building process yields more sustainable and long-lasting reforms as well as strengthened ownership. The main areas of structural reforms are the tax reforms, the enhancement in expenditure controls, trade liberalization, public sector reforms, and modernization of the energy and cocoa sectors. The divestiture program will be pursued vigorously.  

18. The government is implementing tax reforms with technical assistance from several multilateral and bilateral donors. A major measure is the reintroduction of a value-added tax (VAT) to replace the current sales and services taxes. The VAT law was adopted by parliament in February 1998, and the tax will take effect in December 1998. It will have a single rate of 10 percent, with limited exemptions and an effective refund mechanism for exports. Consistent with the intended coverage of the VAT, the government extended the services tax in 1997 to cover a broad range of professional services. To avoid a repetition of the difficuities following the short-lived introduction of the VAT in 1995, the government has launched an intensive public information campaign, which includes visits with new taxpayers by officials to explain procedures for complying with the tax. The assignment of taxpayer identification numbers (TINs) to the targeted group of current sales, service, and import taxpayers, about 2,5O0 in number, was completed in July. In addition, TINs have been assigned to about 5,oo0 new taxpayers. TIN assignment for new taxpayers is basically completed, which puts the VAT implementation on a tight, but achievable, schedule for introduction of the tax in December.  

19. The implementation of a Central Revenue Authority (CRA) has progressed more slowly than anticipated under the program. The objective of establishing the CRA was to improve the efficiency of tax administration by merging services---such as legal support and audit—and by alocating resources among the revenue collection agencies to achieve greater compliance. The bill to establish a CRA could not be submitted to parliament in light of legal opinion that pointed to constitutional provisions that preserve the existing tax agencies as separate institutions. Instead of setting up the CRA as a new agency, the government has decided to appoint a central revenue board that will take over the functions of the governing boards for each agency, and set up a secretariat under it to coordinate activities between the revenue agencies. The legal framework for the new central revenue board will be in place by end-November and the Board and Secretariat will be established thereafter. In the meantime, the nominated members of the Board are meeting in regular sessions as members of the VAT Oversight Committee to ensure coordination of activities between the revenue agencies. The government believes that this alternative approach can achieve the same administrative improvements expected from the creation of a central revenue authority, while avoiding the delays that a constitutional amendment would entail.  

20. The Cabinet approved in May the issuance of the circular for preparation of the 1999 budget and provisional ceilings based on the methodology of the medium-term expenditure framework (MTEF). Workshops on the methodology were conducted in June-August for about 5O0 senior managers and officials from ll ministries and various agencies. The MTEF was initially to be completed for the priority sectors of education, health, and roads. Further, as a result of the workshops, a decision was made (and implemented in September) to extend the use of this methodology to all sectors ahead of schedule. The next step wil be to develop a macroeconomic modeling capability for the MTEF to ensure that economic trends and assumptions are fully taken into account when drawing up the budget.  

21. A new tariff structure was introduced in March 1998 with a view to reducing tariff dispersion and achieving a more uniform treatment of imports. Particular attention was given to limiting by as much as possible the number of items that were zero-rated or exempted. In order to facilitate the shift away from zero-rating, a new rate of 5 percent was added to the existing positive rates of 10 and 25 percent. Imports will be exempt from the VAT only if they are on the list of exempted goods in the VAT law. The government is reviewing tariffs on imports that are not subject to sales tax with a view to rationalizing them in line with the forthcoming VAT schedules.  

22. The government is enhancing its capacity to implement public sector economic reforms. The National Oversight Committee of the National Institutional Renewal Programme $\left(\mathsf{N I R P}\right)$ is consolidating job functions with a view to streamlining and downsizing the public service. The government is negotiating with the social partners a comprehensive long-term wage policy, aimed at reforming the pay structure based on a detailed job evaluation across the entire public sector.  

23. An important part of this reform is the rationalization of the regulatory framework for subvented agencies. In December 1997, Cabinet approved a comprehensive strategy for public service reform over the next 10 years. Approximately 70 percent of the civilian public service is employed in 174 subvented agencies, including the Education Service, Health Service, and Highways Authority. A review of activities and staffing levels of these agencies is ongoing with a view to making them more eficient and, where appropriate, incorporating them in the budget, closing them down, or commercializing them prior to privatization. The Cabinet approved a pilot project for the reform of subvented agencies, which would restructure at least seven agencies, commercialize at least five, and close down five others. The draft legal framework and procedures for closing down or commercializing subvented agencies are to be prepared by the end of December 1998. The Cabinet is expected to approve the specific subvented agencies for the pilot project by the end of 1998 and the project is to be completed by the end of 1999. The reform of the subvented agencies will be supported by a World Bank credit, which encompasses assistance for the pilot project and preparation of a medium-term program to reform the sector.  

24. Key measures to deregulate the petroleum sector were introduced in mid-1996, including the liberalization of crude oil imports, and the adoption of an“import parity" pricing mechanism to ensure transparency in the setting and adjustment of ex-refinery prices of petroleum in line with import costs. The government is continuing to make progress in this area by introducing uniform ex-regional depot wholesale prices in February 1998, and by taking steps to phase out the setting of uniform marketing/retail margins for oil marketing companies that result in a uniform national pump price for petroleum products. By endDecember 1998, the government, through the Energy Commission, will introduce new regulations that will liberalize margins for petroleum product marketing and retailing activities. The GNPC completed the repayment of its long-standing debt to the Bank of Ghana in April 1998.  

25. In November 1997, the government created an independent Public Utilities Regulatory Commission (PURC) to regulate tariffs and customer service standards in the electricity and water sectors. The PURC adopted a program to restore electricity tariffs to economic levels by end of 2000. Accordingly in February 1998, the PURC increased tariffs by 90 percent, and in September l998, announced a further 100 percent increase in electricity tariffs to enable the power utilities to recover the relatively higher costs of procuring thermal power to fillin the deficit in hydropower generation that had emerged. The new tariffs of September 1998 will also ensure that power utilities at least cover the cash operating costs of transmission and distribution seryices and a portion of current debt service obligations. Furthermore, as part of the government's efforts to restore the financial viability of the power utilities, it has adopted a Financial Recovery Plan under which, by end-1999, all outstanding arrears in debt service and power supply up until December l997 are to be converted into government equity, and subsequently recovered through sale of its shares in the power utilities. The Financial  

Recovery Plan also provides for some debt service payment obligations for 1998 through the end of 1999 to be deferred until the year 2000.  

26.The government has continued to implement a strategy to rationalize and increase the efficiency of the cocoa sector while improving the return to farmers and enhancing the quality premium of the Ghanaian cocoa. Incentives for cocoa farmers were improved through an increase in the share of the f.o.b. price of cocoa that accrues to farmers from 51 percent in the 1996/97 crop year to 54 percent in the 1997/98 crop year and further to 56 percent in the crop year that started October 1, 1998. The government will identify further cost-cutting measures for the Cocoa Board to support future increases in the share of the farmers. The government will proceed with its already announced plan to divest the Produce Buying Company (PBC), a subsidiary of the Cocoa Board, and will offer it for sale by March 1999. The divestiture process was delayed by a discussion regarding splitting the PBC into two companies so as to increase competition in the sector. The unification of the extension services of the Ministry of Agriculture and the Cocoa Board were slowed down by the existence of salary scales that were significantly different. While a common salary scale is being considered, the government has gone ahead with the first phase of the unification covering selected producing areas. Full unification is expected early in 1999. The government intends to request assistance from the World Bank to finance possible retrenchment costs associated with the reforms in the extension services and the PBC. Various options for eliminating the Cocoa Board's export monopoly will be assessed while safeguarding the quality of the cocoa crop. The government, with World Bank support, has scheduled a workshop on cocoa in January 1999 to discuss ways to deepen the ongoing reforms and to consolidate the future prospects for the sector.  

27.During 1998, the government will continue its privatization program with the divestiture of at least two large and an additional 20 smal- and medium-size enterprises. Of the large enterprises, three quarters of the government's share in Barclays (4O percent) and one half of its shares in Twifo Oil Plantation (80 percent) were divested during the first half of 1998. At the same time, the State Insurance Corporation was privatized. By end-1998, the Mim Timber Company and the Ghana Reinsurance Corporation will be offered for sale. Regarding the small-scale enterprises, government divestiture initiatives remains on track.  

28. GNPC divested its nonenergy-related assets in March 1998. The government appointed international consultants to prepare comprehensive prospectus for the sale of shares in Tema Oil Refinery Ltd. and the Ghana Oil Company Ltd. The request for bids for Ghana Oil Company Ltd. is expected by end-October, 1998, and the sale should be completed in early 1999. For the divestiture of Tema Oil Refinery Ltd., the process is expected to involve negotiations with creditors to convert their debts into equity in the company by end-1999. Subsequently, government will arrange sale of a second batch of shares to reduce its shareholding to a minority stake.  

29. The government does not intend to (i) introduce multiple currency practices; (ii) impose new or intensify existing restrictions on payments and transfers for current international transactions; (ii) for balance of payments purposes, introduce new or intensify existing restrictions on imports; or (iv) conclude any new bilateral payments agreements that are inconsistent with Article VII of the Fund's Articles of Agreement. The government willnot accumulate external payments arrears, on a continuous basis, apart from those arising from debt-service payments pending the conclusion of debt rescheduling agreements.  

# IV. STATISTICAL ISSUES  

30. The government recognizes the need to improve the quality and timeliness of the economic and social data, and, to that end, is seeking technical assistance. The Ghana Statistical Service (GSS) has been publishing a new national consumer price index but publication of monthly export and import statistics on the basis of the Harmonized System has been irregular. Following technical assistance from the Fund in July 1997, the Bank of Ghana is implementing the agreed action plan to improve monetary data. The government plans to reduce the two-month lag in the publication of the consumer price index to no more than a month, and make the import and export statistical bulletins available regularly with a lag of at most six weeks. The GSS will reconcile its import data with that from customs by end-1998, and will publish the quarterly digest of statistics regularly.  

31. In order to ensure effective monitoring of the program, the government will make available to Fund staff all core data on a timely basis.

# Ghana—-Domestic Debt Sustainability  

1. Domestic debt has risen sharply in recent years, in particular, from 13.3 percent of GDP in 1996 to 17.4 percent of GDP in 1997. Excessive spending and financing in the 1996 budget were major causes. High interest rates have.also contributed, with domestic interest costs reaching 16 percent of total expenditures and 31 percent of total revenue in 1997. To a degree, accumulation of domestic debt reflected capitalization of high interest costs, creating the potential for an upward spiral of debt. This possibility can be examined in the mediumterm framework which includes the calculation of domestic debt and interest cost in a consistent macroeconomic and budgetary framework based on the program.  

2. The size and maturity structure of domestic debt in the third quarter of 1998 reported by the Bank of Ghana provide the starting point for the domestic debt analysis incorporated in the medium-term framework. The information on debt stocks and their associated interest rates are used to project domestic interest payments in the budget. Projection of the budget yields the amount of net domestic financing to be added to the stock of domestic debt, and permits the calculation to be carried forward.  

3. Domestic debt stocks, interest rates, and interest costs are presented in Table 1, at their actual levels at end-l997 and the thrid quarter of 1998, and projected levels for the fourth quarter and in subsequent years. Through 1997, the main instrument for financing the budget was 9l-day treasury bills, which comprised over 60 percent of the outstanding stock of treasury bills. This share has decreased as banks shifted to l8o-day and one-year treasury notes with the decline in interest rates in 1998. In the table, projected net domestic financing in the fourth quarter is covered entirely by that period's increase in one-year treasury notes. The use of treasury bills for liquidity purposes by the Bank of Ghana accounts for the increase in 9l-day treasury bills. These allocations comport with the Bank of Ghana's expectation for treasury bill issuance for the remainder of the year. In subsequent periods, net domestic financing is allocated among the different types of treasury bills so as to preserve the structure of maturities at the end of l998. The Bank of Ghana has decided to discontinue the use of Bank of Ghana bills for liquidity management. Therefore, after 1998, the projection assumes that the amount of BoG bills will remain constant.' The government overdraft and long-term stock are assumed to remain constant.  

4. The calculation of the interest cost associated with each type of treasury and BOG bill is based on an average of its interest rates in the current and previous periods, extending as far back as its maturity. The analysis varies little with changes in the calculation of average  

interest rates or, as well the allocation of net domestic financing among treasury bill maturities. On the other hand, projected levels of interest rates are important for the results.  

5. Based on the third-quarter domestic debt stocks and projections of interest rates, net domestic financing, and the use of treasury bill for liquidity purposes for the remainder of the year, the total stock of domestic debt is projected to be 20.9 percent of GDP at the end-1998. Although stilla significant increment over 1997, it nonetheless represents a decline in the rate of increase in the domestic debt ratio, reflecting the tight financial policies adopted in 1997 and continued in 1998.  

6. Table l presents domestic debt and interest costs in the medium-term framework. The key result is the leveling off of domestic debt between 1998 and 1999, followed by a steady decline to 16.7 percent of GDP by the end of the medium-term projection period. The drop in interest rates in 1998 and the projected decrease in 1999 contribute to this decline, and point to the importance of reducing inflation and interest rates for bringing domestic debt under control. Maintaining a primary domestic surplus over the medium term is also critical to reducing the domestic debt ratio.  

7. The medium-term projection of domestic debt is understated to the extent it does not include the financing cost of the Bank of Ghana's liquidity operations, which are inherently difficult to project. More broadly, the need to strengthen the banking system may imply additional financing costs as in the past. Still more broadly, the definition of domestic debt as the stock of assets whose service is born by the budget may be too narrow and could arguable be extended to include state enterprise debt, expenditure arrears, intergovernmental loans, and possibly other forms of debt.

# Exchange Rate Arrangement  

Ghana maintains a flexible exchange rate system, using the U.S. dollar as the intervention currency. The exchange rate in the interbank market at end-August 1998 was C 2,310.8 per U.S. dollar for buying and C 2,340 per U.S. dollar for selling, while the average bureau buying rate was $\pmb{\emptyset}$ 2,335 per U.S. dollar and the average selling rate was $\pmb{\tau}$ 2,360 per U.S. dollar.  

Ghana has liberalized the making of payments and transfers for current international transactions, and with effect from February 21, 1994, has accepted the obligations of Article VIlI, Sections 2, 3, and 4.

# Ghana-—Relations with the World Bank Group  

(As of September 30, 1998)  

1.The objective of the World Bank's Country Assstance Strategy for FY98-FY00 is to support the key elements of the government's development agenda, which seeks to improve the quality of life for all Ghanaians, generate employment and growth, and reduce poverty. The Bank's broad strategy objectives include: restoring macroeconomic stability, promoting private investment, supporting social and rural development, and implementing direct poverty alleviation measures.  

2. Over the three-year period covering FY98-FYO0, the Bank's base case-lending program has a range of US\$430 million to US\$660 million. Within this range, the Bank willfollow a graduated response to changes in key indicators with respect to fiscal sustainability, portfolio performance, and structural reforms. In addition, the IFC's portfolio totals 23 projects, for total commitments of US\$55.9 million and outstanding disbursements of US\$50.7 million.  

3.Recent and proposed Bank Group projects have broad sectoral coverage, including roads and other infrastructure, health, education, agriculture, environment, private sector development, and public sector management. These projects emphasize rehabilitation, maintenance, and institutional strengthening. More reliance is being put on supporting sector investment programs and nonlending services. The Bank is moving away from infrastructure investments in telecommunications, power, ports, rails, and large urban water systems, while providing greater nonlending support (enhanced by IFC and MIGA) to promote private participation in these sectors.  

4. Since the formulation of Ghana's Economic Recovery Program in 1983, the Bank has provided a series of policy-based credits to help finance urgently needed imports and support structural reforms through the phased liberalization of the exchange rate, trading system, prices, rationalization of capital and recurrent expenditures, privatization, and parastatal reform. In FY92, two adjustment credits were approved--a second financial sector adjustment credit and an agriculture sector adjustment credit--to further deepen reforms in these two sectors. In FY95, a private sector adjustment credit was approved in support of the government's accelerated divestiture program. Sector investment credits to finance investments in roads and highways and to improve basic education were approved in FY95. In FY98, a one-tranche adjustment credit, Economic Reform Support Operation, was also approved. A third sector investment credit in health also took place in FY98.

# Ghana-—Statistical Issues  

1. Although the quality and timeliness of the reporting of Ghana's core economic and statistics improved in some respects, many weaknesses remain, notably, delays in the availability of data on international reserves, interest rates, and the CPI. Ghana is in the bottom quartile of Fund members in the timeliness of reporting core surveillance data. Additional areas where improvements need to be made are identified below. The central bank is also in the process of computerizing its databases, particularly with regard to economic indicators, in consultation with the Fund's Bureau of Computing Services.  

# Real sector  

2. GDP estimates have been reported with considerable delay, in September 1998 a complete set of national accounts for the years 1995 and 1996 was published. However, only three major sectors of economic activity are accounted for and a review of the methodology used in preparing the data is still underway. Sectoral weights used in the calculation of aggregate GDP are to be revised on the basis of the l992 Ghana Living Standards Survey (GLSS) and expenditure data are to be revised to improve the consistency of the national accounts data, which are produced by Ghana Statistical Service (GSS), with the balance of payments data produced by the Bank of Ghana.  

3. In January 1998, a new CPI was introduced. The weights are computed according to the standard consumption basket estimated from the 1992 GLSS. The new basket reduced substantially the weight for clothing from 19 to 9 percent, while the weights for medical care, housing and transport were correspondingly increased. A set of comparable indexes based on the new consumption basket is available only after September 1997, and consequently the authorities use a spline method by compounding inflation, in order to estimate the 12 month inflation rate from January to September 1998. In addition, the reporting lag for the CPI increased from one to two months and longer. The wholesale price index was discontinued in 1991. Trade data are reported with gaps and the most recent figures for total exports and imports in the IFS relate to the year 1996. Rather large discrepancies exist in the reported data for imports between the GSS and the customs statistical services. Labor statistics in Ghana are also produced with a considerable lag and wage statistics are nearly nonexistent. To reduce delays, the UNDP and ILO are providing technical assistance to the Ministry of Employment. Unit value and volume indexes for total exports and imports are no longer supplied to Statistics Department (STA). Industrial production and wage indicators are not available.  

# Public finance  

4. The timeliness of the reporting of public finance statistics was improved by the introduction in 1994 of a new expenditure coding system that allows data to be generated on a cash basis from the central bank records. However, an economic classification of expenditures is available with a lag of at least two months. Expenditure data on a commitment basis are not available, but recent efforts have been made to get large ministries to report their expenditure commitments and arrears to the Ministry of Finance. Expenditures financed by externally funded project aid are produced with a considerable lag and are subject to estimation errors owing to timing differences, foreign exchange valuation, and the recording of the technical assistance component. However, staff estimates of these expenditures are derived from balance of payments data. Consolidated fiscal accounts are not compiled because data on autonomous government agencies (subvented agencies) and public enterprises are not reported. A World Bank mission in August 1998 reviewed Government's domestic debt data.  

5. Annual government finance data are reported for publication in the Government Finance Statistics Yearbook (GFSY), although the coverage and timeliness of the data need to be improved; the latest available data cover the central government only and relate to the fiscal year ended December 31, 1993. These data include allthe major components of the GFS tables, with the exception of debt, for which the latest data published in the GFSY relate to 1985. The authorities reported fiscal data for the 1996 GFSY, but the data could not be published as they were not sufficiently detailed to allow conversion to the GFS format. No monthly or quarterly data are reported for publication in IFS.  

# Monetary accounts  

6. Monetary data are compiled on a regular basis. In December 1997 the Monetary Survey was extended to include 6 additional banks, it thus currently covers all17 banks in the system. Not withstanding the technical assistance provided since 1985, several problems persist in Ghana's monetary statistics. In particular, foreign currency deposits of banks are classified without regard to the holder's residency. In addition, there are large deviations between data reported by commercial banks' with respect to their deposits with the central bank and those reported by the central bank, which are mainly due to reporting time lags. Moreover, foreign-currency accounts are not valued at the end-of-period market exchange rates. The money and banking statistics mission (July 1997) proposed a plan of action and a timetable to resolve these problems.  

# Balance of payments and external debt  

7. Balance of payments are published only annually. Trade data are available on a more frequent basis, but those for imports are impaired by unreliable customs statistics. No breakdown is available either for imports or exports of goods. Recent computerization resulted in some improvements in the customs records, but there is scope for further improvement. In July 1998, balance of payments estimates up to 1996 were provided to STA. With technical assistance from the Commonwealth Secretariat, there have recently been significant improvements in the debt reporting system though reconciliation of data between the Bank of Ghana and the Ministry of Finance still presents problems; nevertheless, efforts are being made to achieve further progress in this area.  

# Publication  

8. The reporting of data to the Fund for purposes of IFS publication has recently improved. However, there are still lags of about four to five months in reporting exchange rates, prices, and international transactions data. In general, statistical information from offcial sources is not disseminated to the public on a timely basis. Even though the Quarterly Economic Bulletin of the Bank of Ghana is now published more regularly, the data show lags ofup to six months. The Quarterly Digest of Statistics is usually published by the GSS with a longer lag.  

9. Deficiencies in data dissemination have been brought to the attention of the Ghanaian authorities at the level of the Minister of Finance and the Governor of the Bank of Ghana.
