# EXECUTIVE SUMMARY  

# Overview  

Economic performance weakened during the second half of 1999, owing to an external terms of trade shock and delays in taking adequate measures to deal with it. However, in early 2000, the authorities adopted a number of fiscal, monetary, and structural measures to keep the adjustment effort on track. Ten prior actions were undertaken, some of them to move ahead stalled structural reforms, while fiscal and monetary performance in the first five months of 2000 was in line with agreed monthly benchmarks. Despite the risks involved in implementing reforms in an election year, the staff believes that the program for 2000 represents a credible response to the conditions facing Ghana. The government continues to make strides in implementing its poverty reduction strategy, including through the presentation of an interim PRSP (EBD/00/66; 8/7/00).  

# 1999 outturn  

Ghana experienced a terms of trade decline beginning in l999, which continued in 2000 and led to a sharp depreciation of the exchange rate. In addition, there was a shortfall in external financial assistance and significant slippages in the fiscal program. This combination of factors caused some resurgence in inflation and a worsening of the external current account deficit. At end-December 1999, nine performance criteria for the PRGF-supported program were not met, although measures underlying some of these criteria were subsequently carried out.  

# The 2000 program  

Fiscal measures, including an increase in the VAT rate combined with expenditure restraint and the substantial exchange rate depreciation, are expected to turn the fiscal situation around, resulting in a primary domestic surplus on the order of 4 percent of GDP. The government has accelerated the privatization program in order to reduce domestic debt accumulation and interest payments. Monetary policy should continue to be tight to halt the decline of the cedi and limit the pass-through of the depreciation and oil price increases into the inflation rate. The increased exchange rate flexibility and the substantial depreciation since the third quarter of 1999 are both a positive sign of adjustment and a cause for concern: even with the adjustment policies in place, there will be a negative impact on inflation expectations in the short run. The program also contains a commitment to structural reform, notably in the ongoing liberalization of the cocoa sector and the creation of a competitive and sound banking sector.

# I. INTRODUCTION  

1. A mission visited Accra from February 24 to March 9, 2000 to conduct a review of the first year program under the Poverty Reduction and Growth Facility (PRGF) and begin discussions on a second-year program; these discussions were concluded in Washington during April 14-19, 2000.' The government's letter of intent (Appendix I) requests an augmentation of access and waivers for the nonobservance of nine performance criteria for December l999, and a memorandum of economic and financial policies (Appendix I, Attachment I) defines policies to be pursued during the second year of the arrangement. The Ghanaian authorities also presented their poverty reduction strategy to the Consultative Group meeting in November 1999 and have prepared an interim poverty reduction strategy paper (EBD/00/66). In December 2000, Ghana will hold presidential elections.  

2. On May 3, 1999, the Executive Board approved a three-year arrangement under the PRGF in the amount of SDR 155 million (EBS/99/57). During the 1999 Article IV consultation, Executive Directors noted that Ghana's strong macroeconomic performance in the first half of the year had weakened in the second half, owing to a major terms of trade shock and policy slippages. Directors emphasized the importance of allowing the exchange rate to play a role in containing the terms of trade shock and of returning international reserves to adequate levels. They regretted the slow pace of the divestiture program and encouraged the authorities to.address the problem of high domestic debt. Directors aiso acknowledged the need for exceptional foreign assistance in 2000 to help cushion the adverse effects of the external shock on the poorest segments of the population, and they highlighted the need for appropriate policies in 2000, when the brunt of the external shock was expected to be felt.  

3. Owing to the disappointing outcome of the first-year program, the staff has delayed initiation of the second-year program to allow the government to demonstrate its commitment to program implementation by undertaking a number of prior actions and meeting monthly benchmarks. There was a further delay after this monitoring period to ensure that financing for the program from divestiture receipts would be available, and then to allow for reporting on a multiple currency practice and noncomplying purchase. These delays, combined with the terms of trade shock and the paucity of foreign inflows during the first half of the year, raised uncertainty in the foreign exchange markets, which led to an accelerated depreciation of the cedi that reached 50 percent per annum at end-May. Monetary policy was tightened in June, but exchange markets remain extremely sensitive. Taking the delays into account, the program period has been extended into 200l with benchmarks set for March. The staff believes that the real depreciation of the currency will be beneficial and that the 20o0 economic program represents a credible response to the problems facing Ghana, and therefore recommends approval of the augmentation of access requested, the granting of waivers for nonobservance of the December 1999 performance criteria, and the completion of the second review under the three-year PRGF arrangement.  

# II. 1999 PERFORMANCE AND RECENT ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENTS  

# A. Overview  

4. Beginning in mid-1999, world market prices for Ghana's two principal exports-- cocoa and gold-fell by over 30 percent and 5 percent, respectively, while the world price of petroleum more than doubled (Figure 1). In addition, a shortfall in fiscal revenues and delays in external disbursements caused an overrun in domestic credit expansion and a decline in international reserves. The program's fiscal policy stance was tightened in the fourth quarter of 1999, but the fiscal situation did not improve rapidly, and five quantitative performance criteria, some interrelated, were not observed. Structural reforms also stalled, resulting in noncompliance with four structural performance criteria (see Box 1). A number of corrective prior actions were carried out in the first five months of 2000 to put the program back on course: measures underlying most of the unobserved structural performance criteria have been implemented, the monthly fiscal outcomes are in line with the program, and monetary policy has been tightened.  

# B. Real Growth and Prices  

5. Real GDP grew by 4.4 percent in 1999, I percentage point below the original program forecast (Table 1). Value added in agriculture, the largest sector, grew by an estimated 3.9 percent, limited by weak cocoa prices and inclement weather conditions in some areas, while the industrial and services sectors grew by at about 5 percent.  

6. Overall consumer price inflation declined to an annual rate of 13.8 percent at end1999 from l5.8 percent at end-1998, but was still above the revised target of 9 percent. The rise in petroleum product prices, compounded by the depreciation of the cedi, fueled inflation for nonfood items, which doubled to 24 percent at year's end (compared with end-1998), while food price inflation dropped sharply to 6 percent at year's end (Figure 2). The trend of rising nonfood prices continued into 2000 but should have peaked in the second quarter if monetary policy remains tight.

7. Fiscal performance in l999 fell short of projected targets reflecting slippages in expenditures, weak revenue performance, and the authorities' delay in implementing the necessary adjustment measures.  

8. Total expenditures were 0.7 percentage point of GDP below projections. Although significant cuts were made in capital expenditure (Tables 2-3 and Figure 3), there was still an accumulation of domestic arrears of 0.8 percentage point of GDP. Despite the successful introduction of the value added tax (VAT), total revenue was 1.4 percentage points of GDP below target. # D. Monetary and Financial Sector Developments  

11. Despite domestic credit expansion of 70 percent during 1999, monetary policy succeeded in moderating the growth of broad money to 16 percent during the year, continuing the deceleration over the past two years, and holding reserve money (performance criterion) on target with a 12.9 percent growth rate (Table 4). However, these monetary targets were achieved in part by allowing the net foreign asset position (performance criterion) of the central bank to decline by US\$94 million (US\$143 million short of target).  

12. Despite the fall in cocoa prices during the first half of l999, the authorities were able to keep the cedi depreciation at less than the rate of inflation by intervening in the foreign exchange market, thereby allowing a continued real appreciation (Figure 4). However, the loss of international reserves, which reached 1.5 months of imports in midyear, could not be sustained and the cedi depreciated sharply during the last four months of 1999, bringing the depreciation for the whole year to 34 percent. As oil prices moved sharply higher, the pressure on the rate continued into 2000, resulting in a further 29 percent nominal depreciation by end-May. At end-May, the real effective exchange rate had depreciated by an estimated 40 percent from its peak in March 1999, erasing all of the real effective  

appreciation since mid-1994. Despite the substantial movement of the exchange rate, large and growing queues indicated that the foreign exchange market was not working effectively.  

13. Credit to the private sector recorded an overall growth of 6l percent during 1999; borrowing abroad provided the resources to finance almost two-thirds of this credit expansion.4 Bankers explained that this lending was directed to foreign exchange earners. The staf noted that a number of banks were in breach of the 15 percent foreign exchange exposure limit and encouraged the Bank of Ghana to be more proactive in enforcing this prudential rule. The authorities explained that they lacked adequate enforcement mechanisms until the new banking act was passed, but they would monitor the situation closely.  

14.As foreign reserves were depleted and intervention in the foreign exchange market halted in the fourth quarter of 1999, interest rates increased sharply, as the Bank of Ghana strove to keep reserve money on track through a more aggressive placement of government securities. The treasury bill rate, which normally was not very responsive, rose from $24\%$ percent in mid-1999 to $31\%$ percent at year's end, where it remained until May 2000 despite the continued depreciation of the cedi and the rise in the overnight interbank rate (Figure 5). The staff regret that the discount rate was not similarly raised as market rates rose, but notes that banks are no longer allowed to borrow at this rate. An MAE mission visited Ghana in May 20O0 and concluded that the lack of flexibility in treasury bill rates exacerbated pressures on the exchange rate.

15. The current account deficit (including transfers) for 1999 is estimated at US\$815 million (10.6 percent of GDP), substantially larger than originally anticipated (Table 5 and Figure 6). Actual cocoa prices fell by only 13 percent in 1999 and gold prices by 5 percent, owing to the use of forward contract sales. These price declines translated into a loss of US\$110 million (2 percent of GDP) on existing export volumes for the year.

# F. Structural Reforms  

17. Progress has been achieved in restructuring the banking sector, albeit with some delays. The new draft Bank of Ghana and Banking Acts (performance criterion) were prepared by end-1999 and submittd to parliament in March and May 2000, respectively. The liquidation of the problem banks (performance criterion) followed the designated plan but with some delay; the Bank for Housing and Construction and Cooperative Bank were closed on January l7, 2000, and the Bank of Credit and Commerce on May 31, 2000. All liquidations proceeded smoothly, and deposits have been transferred to sound banks with no adverse consequences. In the area of divestiture, the govermment's shares of the Produce Buying Company were offered for sale on the Ghana Stock Exchange on December I0, 1999, and the target for divestiture receipts for the year was met. The Tema Oil Refinery was not offered for sale (performance criterion) by the end of l999, but a financial restructuring was accomplished and the government has now reached an agreement with a strategic partner (see paragraph 37). A decision was made not to proceed further with the divestiture of Ghana Airways; instead, the government intends to meet its financial divestiture target through the sale of part of its remaining shares in Ghana Telecom.  

# HI. MEDIUM-TERM ADJUSTMENT STRATEGY  

19. Ghana's development goals emphasize improving overall social welfare, expanding employment opportunities, and improving social indicators, particularly in rural areas.7 The strategy is based on a market economy with strong private sector participation. To achieve the welfare gains anticipated in Ghana-Vision 2020, which documents the government's plans for improving all Ghanaian living standards and substantially reducing poverty, the economy is projected to grow at about 6 percent per annum, although the tighter policies required by the current terms of trade shock may reduce the average rate of growth over the 2000-02 period to around 5 percent. To realize the growth target, gross fixed investment wil need to remain high, with private investment becoming increasingly important over time (Table 6).9  

20. For the government to achieve its goals of poverty reduction and rising living standards, it will maintain a sustainable fiscal deficit and direct expenditures to priority areas: infrastructure, education, health, and agricultural extension. The medium-term strategy will aim at reducing the domestic debt burden, which willabsorb about 6 percent of GDP in 2000 in interest costs, to make room for priority expenditures. Domestic debt will be reduced by maintaining primary surpluses on the order of 4.5 percent of GDP and by reducing domestic borrowing. The primary surpluses will be attained by increasing the total . revenue-to-GDP ratio (excluding grants) to 19 percent, by broadening the tax base and improving tax administration, and by restraining the growth of noninterest expenditures to 10.3 percent of GDP. Moreover, domestic financing will be sharply reduced over the PRGF arrangement period to an average of 1.5 percent of GDP.  

21.Monetary policy will aim at reducing inflation to not more than 5 percent in 2002 and at stabilizing the real exchange rate, to consolidate the competitiveness gains of the recent depreciation.  

22. Foreign savings will be needed to finance the desired level of investment, although the current account deficit in 1999 is temporarily high and would have to decline to 5 percent of GDP in 2002. As the economy strengthens, domestic savings should replace foreign savings. As Ghana's terms of trade improves, foreign reserves will be gradually rebuilt to a more secure level.  

23.The medium-term strategy will focus on the completion of a number of structural reforms that will support the poverty reduction strategy (Section V). In particular, the government will implement the Accelerated Agricultural Growth and Development Strategy and the next phase of the cocoa sector reform, which is expected to eliminate Cocobod's export monopoly (paragraph 39).

# A. Fiscal Policies  

26..The fiscal strategy remains focused on maintaining a domestic primary surplus sufficient to reduce net domestic borrowing and future interest obligations of the government to sustainable levels and prevent the crowding out of the private sector. An acceleration of the privatization program will be used as a further mechanism to control domestic debt trends. Implementation of this strategy will help relieve the previous tension between the restrictive monetary policy and lax fiscal stance, which resulted in rising interest rates.  

27. The rise in the VAT rate from 10 percent to 12.5 percent, the increase in petroleum excise duties and the higher customs revenues from improved administration will contribute to a rise in the domestic primary balance to 4.0 percent of GDP in 2000. Increased grants and a cut in noninterest expenditure will help offset the increase in interest expense, as well as the onetime costs for the census and elections this year.'' Nonetheless, the cash deficit will not decline in 20o0, owing to a substantial clearing of road and other arrears.  

28. Immediate measures taken to strengthen the fiscal situation include the folowing: restrictions on official travel; a reduction in fuel allocation to government officials; stricter controls on procurement; and more aggressive prosecution for fraud. Under the ongoing revenue management program, all agencies have been asked to submit proposals for expanding their revenue bases. Special emphasis will be placed on the collection of customs revenues through tightened controls on bonded warehouses, including computerization of inventory and the conducting of unannounced audits. Implementation of the contingent expenditures (equivalent to about 1 percent of GDP) will depend on satisfactory fiscal performance, and particularly the realization of divestiture receipts.  

29. The authorities are actively implementing programs to enhance expenditure control and avoid the accumulation of arrears. Notably, all new projects will require a certificate of commencement of work to control commitments. Progress is expected under the PUFMARP, particularly on technical assistance to improve the Ghana Audit Service; development of the Budget and Public Expenditure Management System; and fiscal decentralization. As part of the efforts to improve expenditure control mechanisms in the near term, the authorities received a technical assistance mission from FAD in June. The findings indicate a systemic problem of management control. The mission discussed with the authorities the need to improve the measurement and audit of the arrears stock, to strengthen commitment controls, and to reconcile data on commitments, Ministry of Finance authorizations, and financing operations. Implementation of the key recommendations will reduce the risk that continued expenditure restraint efforts could be translated in part into expenditure arrears. Importantly, the authorities will undertake an early audit of the stock of nonroad arrears in light of the current limited recording mechanisms.

# V. POVERTY REDUCTION  

40. The poverty reduction strategy is an outgrowth of Ghana—Vision 2020, which sets out Ghana's long-term goal to improve the living standards of all Ghanaians and substantially reduce poverty. According to the fourth Ghana Living Standards Survey (1999), overall poverty dropped by 8.2 percentage points between 1992 and 1999. Still, 42.6 percent of the population live below the poverty line, and poverty has actually increased in some regions.2 According to the Demographic Health Survey (1998), life expectancy is now 59 years, while infant and child mortality rates have fallen significantly over the last 10 years to 71 and 110 per thousand respectively (Table 8). In primary and secondary education, preliminary 1999 data indicate that gross enrollment rates improved over the first half of the 1990s.  

41. While the approach to poverty strategy is multidimensional, a key priority is the Accelerated Agricultural Growth and Development Strategy, designed to increase the sector's annual growth rate from 2-3 percent to 5-6 percent in the long run. The agricultural program will focus on establishing food security, ensuring adequate financing of the sector, and improving the infrastructure for agriculture through increased public investment under the Agricultural Services Sector Investment Program. Given the smallholder structure of agriculture in Ghana, the strategy of using the private sector as the source of growth has direct benefits for some of the poorest elements of society. Recent reforms in the cocoa sector and growth in nontraditional exports appear to have benefited the poor, and both of these developments will continue and be deepened.  

42.The full PRSP will be compieted by April 2001. Among other things, this will allow a clear delineation of responsibility for advice and monitoring of structural areas between the Bank and the Fund, and a corresponding shift in the focus of conditionality for the remainder of the PRGF arrangement.

VII. FINANCING REQUIREMENTS; ACCESS, CAPACITY TO REPAY, AND RISKS  

47. External financing requirements are projected to total about $\$2.4$ billion over the 2000-02 period (Table 9). For 2000, total requirements amount to $\$874$ million, of which US\$432 million is program assistance. Additional donor financing has been obtained from the World Bank and Japan, and should cover the financing needs of the balance of payments and budget. Given the pressures on the balance of payments, the amount of real depreciation of the exchange rate already incurred, and the need to maintain an adequate level of reserves, a modest increase in access to PRGF resources of 10 percent of quota has been assumed, although only one-fourth of it would be released this year. These magnitudes do not seem excessive, and Ghana has maintained a good record of meeting its obligations to the Fund.  

49.Despite the measures taken to put the 200O economic program on track, serious risks remain. First, the exchange rate continues to depreciate sharply, and some overshooting of the equilibrium rate looks likely. If the rate moves far beyond its equilibrium level, there is increased danger of initiating a damaging cycle of inflation and depreciation and reducing growth. Second, during this election year domestic groups may put strong pressure on the fiscal authorities to increase various elements of expenditure. In 1992 and 1996, election year pressures to implement expansionary financial policies derailed the adjustment process. If fiscal policy loosens in the face of tight monetary policy, real interest rates would likely rise further and could place the budget for a prolonged period in a debt trap, in which increasing interest costs either crowd out priority expenditures or cause the deficit to grow.  

# VIII. STAFF APPRAISAL  

50. Faced with sharp declines in cocoa and gold prices-and increases in oil prices--the Ghanaian authorities took a number of positive steps to cushion the shock, but did so only after some hesitation, which caused most performance criteria for end-1999 to be missed. During this process, the mismatch between a loose fiscal policy and a restrictive monetary policy resulted in a marked increase in interest rates. Since then, the government has raised the VAT rate and the excise tax on petroleum and taken steps to sell 10-l5 percent of the capital of Ghana Telecom, but again delays to implement these measures sustained a further depreciation of the cedi. Monetary policy was once again tightened to stabilize the exchange rate and inflation. The staff emphasizes the importance of smooth and timely implementation of agreed policies.  

51. The substantial depreciation of the exchange rate will have a positive impact on budgetary revenues and foreign financing. Nevertheless, the authorities must not relax their efforts in implementing the measures taken to raise taxes and improve customs, tax administration, and expenditure controls. The staff urges the authorities to maximize privatization proceeds and use them for the reduction of domestic debt.  

52.Following the recommendations of the recent MAE technical assistance mission, the staff urges the authorities to allow the interest rate and exchange rate to be more responsive to market pressures, thereby serving as a signal to guide policies and as a mechanism to prevent an undue decline in reserves and/or excessive expansion in private sector credit. To avoid confusion over the level of external reserves, it is important to ensure that the monetary authorities properly record foreign exchange transactions with domestic banks.  

53.The staff encourages the authorities to use their MTEF to prioritize expenditures in consultation with stakeholders. In particular, budgetary execution should become increasingly transparent to ensure that poverty reduction strategies are closely monitored by civil society and do not fall victim of efforts to gain electoral advantage. The government should encourage wage raises that reflect productivity trends, while avoiding excessive wage increases similar to those seen in previous election years. The government has committed itself to a marked reduction of domestic and external arrears in the first half of 200O, and to a gradual elimination of road-related arrears during 200o-0l. The staff urges the government to rapidly improve the budget management control system, undertake an audit of arrears, and prevent new arrears from developing.  

54. Decentralization efforts are important to increase accountability, to better target poverty reduction efforts, and to give practical meaning to a participatory approach to economic development. However, decentralization is not without its pitfalls, particularly where local administrative capacity is low. Setting limits on the borrowing ability of local authorities and on the percentage of resources applied to paying salaries should be considered to avoid shortcomings often seen in other countries' programs. It is also crucial that budget execution at the local level be made fully transparent and consolidated into the public sector accounts provided to the public and parliament.  

55.The government has taken the necessary steps to move ahead with its medium-term cocoa strategy. While the decision to keep producer prices constant could have been questioned initially, the subsequent sharp depreciation of the cedi has restored price parity vis- $\dot{\mathbf{a}}$ -vis neighboring cocoa producers. The share of cocoa farmers in the f.o.b. price of cocoa is estimated to be about 66 percent, or in line with commitments under the World Bank's ERSO II. Further continued reform of the cocoa sector will be critical to the success of the medium-term framework and poverty reduction strategy.  

56. The staff urges the government to reduce average tariff rates to enhance the competitiveness of local industries, harmonize regional practices, and remove distortions. The staff, concerned about attempts to protect local industries through import surcharges, calls for the immediate elimination of all surcharges. Efforts should also be made to curtail tariff exemptions and strengthen customs procedures, particularly those related to bonded warehouses.  

57. The external shock suffered by the Ghanaian economy in 1999 highlights the vulnerability of its balance of payments. Delays in donor assistance and the cyclical nature of export earnings argue for a buildup in foreign reserves as conditions permit. Nontraditional exports need to be supported by trade liberalization, macroeconomic stability, and a realistic exchange rate policy. A debt sustainability analysis in November 1999 indicated that Ghana qualified for debt relief under the HIPC Initiative, but it has chosen not to avail itself of such assistance.  

58. Assistance from the World Bank and Japan, as well as from other development partners, willhelp cushion the adverse effects of the external shock on the poorest segments of the population, on social expenditures, and on growth, by closing most of the financing gap for 2000. The staff believes that a modest increase in access to PRGF resources is justified in the circumstances and should eliminate the remaining financing gap. However, the reliability of foreign financing in 20oo will be particularly crucial.to the successful implementation of the government's economic program.  

59.The staff is encouraged by the government's willingness to consult with a wide range of stakeholders while developing its poverty reduction and growth strategy. It noted the commitment expressed in the budget documents to reconvene the National Economic Forum. The staff commends the government on its decision to request World Bank assistance in conducting a survey of corruption perceptions.  

60. Several data and reporting issues have arisen since the last staff report, including the use of and the accounting for special swap transactions and the monitoring and measurement of domestic arrears. The authorities have abolished the use of all swap transactions and have taken steps to resolve the associated accounting issues in the measurement of net foreign assets. To strengthen expenditure management, the government will undertake an audit of domestic arrears. The staff commends the authorities for their cooperation in resolving these matters, which serve as a reminder of the importance attached to transparency in reporting.  

61. To increase liquidity in the foreign exchange market and reconcile the seasonal nature of foreign inflows with reserve management, the Bank of Ghana introduced special swaps in November 1999 (see paragraph 45), which resulted in a multiple currency practice (MCP). The MCP has not entailed discrimination against other members. The discontinuation by the Bank of Ghana to enter into any new special swaps since end-May 2000 will result in the abolition of this MCP after all outstanding balances from previous swaps are redeemed, which is expected to be in March 2001. In view of the above, the staff recommends the approval of the retention of the MCP until March 31, 200l or the next Article IV consultation with Ghana, whichever is earlier.  

62.Despite poor performance in adhering to quantitative and structural performance criteria for 1999, the government of Ghana has signaled its commitment to macroeconomic stability in 2000. The fiscal and monetary trends in the first five months of 2000 are encouraging, and prior actions have been implemented to recover the reform momentum. In view of these developments, the staff recommends that (l) the augmentation of access requested by the authorities be approved; and (2) waivers be granted for the nonobservance of the performance criteria on domestic primary balance of the government; net domestic financing; reserve money; net foreign assets of the Bank of Ghana; the offer for sale of the Tema Oil Refinery; the hiring of sales advisors for the Electricity Company of Ghana, Ghana Railways, and Ghana Airways; the submission of the new Bank of Ghana Act and Banking Act to parliament; the withdrawal of licenses of banks not complying with the capital adequacy requirement; and the temporary accumulation of external arrears. These waivers would allow Ghana to complete the second review of the current PRGF arrangement.

# Memorandum of Economic and Financial Policies of the Government of Ghana for 2000  

# I. INTRODUCTION  

1. In November l999, the government presented its poverty reduction strategy to the Consultative Group meeting. This strategy has been the object of wide consultation with civil society and represents a central element in our approach to raising Ghana's living standards significantly within a generation. The poverty reduction strategy has been summarized in an Interim Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper (EBD/00/66; 8/7/00). This memorandum of economic and financial policies provides an overview of Ghana's performance in 1999, and sets out the government's objectives for 2000-2002 and the program for 2000, including quantitative and structural benchmarks to guide policy implementation during the year.  

2. In 1999, Ghana suffered a severe terms-of-trade shock, delays in external disbursements, and a shortfall in fiscal revenues. World prices for Ghana's two principal exports--cocoa and gold-fell by over 30 percent and 5 percent, respectively, and the world price of petroleum roughly doubled. These developments posed a serious threat to the implementation of the PRGF-supported economic program. In response, the government tightened monetary and fiscal policies in the fourth quarter of 1999 and the 2000 program was adjusted accordingly. However, the shift to tighter policies faced implementation delays and fell short. While structural reforms were for the most part carried out, albeit with delays, quantitative performance criteria on fiscal performance and net foreign assets were not observed.  

3.The government will continue to act resolutely to achieve macroeconomic stability and implement structural reforms. Despite the problems faced in 1999, the government remains determined to achieve its poverty reduction and sustainable growth objectives and has taken the steps reported below to deal with this difficult situation.  

# II. PERFORMANCE IN 1999  

4. In 1999, real GDP grew by 4.4 percent, which was below the original program projection of 5.5 percent, owing to domestic capital expenditure cuts and tightened monetary policy necessitated by the external shock. Inflation declined to 13.8 percent at end-1999 as compared with 15.8 percent at end-1998. Sharp declines in food prices from increased agricultural production helped keep inflation down, outweighing increases in the growth of non-food prices caused by higher oil prices and the pass-through effect of a sharp exchange rate depreciation (34 percent on an end-of-period basis).  

5． The overallbudget deficit on a commitment basis, including grants, was 8.2 percent of GDP, 2 percentage points of GDP higher than projected for the year, as total expenditure was cut by 0.8 percentage points of GDP, while total revenue and grants fell short of target by 2.8 percentage points of GDP. The domestic primary surplus was 1.4 percent of GDP, which was below the 3.4 percent target. The domestic financing of the budget was about  

1.3 percent of GDP above the program target. Of the observed deviation, most was due to lower-than-expected tax revenue.  

6. The shortfall in import tax collections reflected delays in correcting problems in customs procedures—particularly leakages from bonded warehouses. The slow rate of adjustment of petroleum prices to reflect the depreciation of the cedi and higher world oil prices reduced petroleum tax collections below their projected amount. Company taxes were also lower than expected, reflecting weaker-than-expected performance in key enterprises, such as VALCO, an aluminum producer, whose production recovered sluggishly from the effects of the 1998 energy crisis.  

7. Total expenditures were 0.7 percentage points of GDP below programmed levels, reflecting mainly shortfalls in foreign project assistance (1.4 percentage points of GDP). Payments of arrears in the road sector were 0.3 percentage points of GDP above their programmed level. Nevertheless, outstanding arrears remained above programmed levels on account of interest charges, and extensions of some projects beyond the expected termination date.  

8. Net domestic assets of the banking system expanded by 40 percent, reflecting higher lending to both the government and private sector. This expansion was made possible by a significant contraction in net foreign assets while money expansion, at 16 percent, remained in line with the program. The Bank of Ghana maintained reserve money on target for the year by conducting aggressive open market operations, particularly in the last four months of 1999. In doing so, it pushed the 9l-day treasury bill rate to 34 percent at year-end from 27 percent in September. However, it also used sales of foreign exchange to control reserve money. As a result of these sales and of the external balance of payments shock, the net foreign assets of the central bank contracted by US\$94 million, instead of increasing by $\mathtt{U S S50}$ million as programmed. The 34 percent depreciation in the nominal exchange rate during l999 was a significant and needed response to the terms of trade shock, but it has contributed to a rise in the inflation rate in the latter part of 1999.  

9. The current account deficit for 1999, including official transfers, is now estimated to have been higher than originally projected by almost US\$500 million. This is attributed mainly to higher imports. Substantial shortfals in official transfers and delays in external financing have adversely affected the external accounts, although this has been partly offset by unexpectedly high short-term inflows. Ghana continues to observe a moratorium on nonconcessional external borrowing. Temporary external arrears were incurred in late-1999, but they have been regularized in the first half of 2000.  

10. Regarding structural reforms, good progress was achieved in reducing government involvement in financial sector institutions. The National Investment Bank has been sold to investors, and the Bank for Housing and Construction (BHC) and Cooperative Bank (COOP) was closed on January 17 with minimum disruption to banking activities; deposits were transferred to sound banks. In 2oo0, the cost of these closures is presently estimated at about $\pmb{\mathcal{C}}60$ billion, which is less than originally estimated. The government willbe issuing treasury bills as needed to cover liabilities to depositors. The Bank of Credit and Commerce, a private bank, was also closed on May 31, 2000. Two new banks have entered the market: Stanbic Ghana Ltd. and Amalgamated Ghana Ltd. Draft central bank and banking laws have been finalized and submitted to parliament in May 2000.  

11. Shares of the Produce Buying Company (PBC) were offered for sale on the Ghana Stock Exchange on December l0, 1999. A sales advisor for the Electricity Company of Ghana will be appointed by end-September 20o0. The appointment of the transaction advisor. for Ghana Railways has been delayed and is now expected in September 2000. The government decided not to move ahead with the divestiture of Ghana Airways for the moment. The financial restructuring of the Tema Oil Refinery (TOR) was completed in 1999. In June 2000, a memorandum of understanding was signed with Samsung (Korea), which is financing the modermization of the refinery, to take up to a 15 percent equity stake in TOR.  

12. The government issued a report for the Consultative Group (CG) meeting in November 1999 on the measurement of poverty, both its incidence and distribution, based on results of the fourth Ghana Living Standards Survey (GLSS4) completed in June. It also presented reports on the elements of a national program for reducing poverty at that meeting.  

13.In the area of governance, the government is reinforcing its fight against corruption. The Serious Fraud Office investigated 37 cases of alleged contract fraud, embezzlement, tax evasion, and misappropriation involving an amount of $\mathbb{C}9.5$ billion, and, as a result, has been able to recover about $\mathbb{C}2.2$ billion. Government has extended an invitation to the World Bank to collaborate in the conduct of a survey on corruption perceptions in Ghana, a first step in determining how this problem can be mitigated, if not eradicated.  

# III. OBJECTIVES AND POLICIES FOR 2O0O-2002  

14. Objectives. The government's overall objective is to increase growth and reduce poverty within a stable macroeconomic environment. In view of the severe external shock suffered by the economy in 1999-2000 the government intends to reconvene the National Economic Forum to consult stakeholders on appropriate adjustments to its development strategy. In the meantime, Ghana's vulnerable balance of payments position is likely to constrain economic growth. The government is appealing to bilateral and multilateral donors to help Ghana by committing to disburse additional assistance, particularly program assistance to finance poverty reduction measures.  

15.In view of the terms-of-trade shock, the real GDP growth target has been set at a minimum of 4 percent in 2000, 4.5 percent in 200l, and 5.5 percent in 2002, as tighter policies are likely to reduce economic activity and constrain imports. The program recognizes a temporary rise in inflation to 20 percent (average period) in 2000 owing to the substantial depreciation of the cedi during the year, but is expected to decline to 5 percent in 2002. The external current account deficit, including grants, will be targeted at 10.4 percent of GDP in 2000 and at an average of 6 percent for 2001-02. Gross official reserves, which declined in 1999 to 1.5 months of imports owing to the external shock, are expected to increase to 1.8 months in 2000.  

16.The central objective of the government's medium-term fiscal policy is to move toward a balanced overallbudget. The government aims at maintaining the domestic primary surplus in the 3.5-4.5 percent of GDP range, while ensuring that the stock of domestic debt as a share of GDP begins to decline by 200l. These fiscal objectives are expected to be achieved by increasing the total revenue-to-GDP ratio to about 19 percent by broadening the tax base and improving tax administration, notwithstanding the planned reduction of cocoa taxation and tarif reform. Discretionary expenditures will be curtailed, but expenditures in health and education will be protected. Interest payments are expected to increase to 7.7 percent of GDP (43 percent of recurrent expenditures) in 2000, crowding out other needed expenditures. Government intends to reduce interest payments through improvements in domestic debt management, primary surpluses, and the use of divestiture receipts to reduce the domestic debt stock. Monetary policy will aim at limiting monetary expansion to that consistent with targeted nominal GDP growth so as to contribute to a stable exchange rate.  

17. Structural reforms. During 2000-2002, the government will accelerate the implementation of structural reforms that directly support its poverty reduction strategy. In particular, the government will begin the implementation of its Agriculture Services Sector Investment Program, an integrated program of expenditures to be financed by the government and a consortium of donors. Regarding cocoa, a workshop involving all stakeholders took place in March and submited its recommendations to the government. In June, the government introduced new cocoa regulations for the 20oo/Ol crop year. Private Licensed Buying Companies that meet Cocobod standards willbe allowed to directly export 30 percent of their cocoa purchases. An ad valorem tax projected at l7.8 percent will be imposed on cocoa exports. A producer price will remain in effect whereby farmers will receive an estimated 67 percent of the f.o.b. export price.  

18.The government will deepen its divestiture program during 2000-2002, with the sale of the Electricity Company of Ghana, sale of additional shares in Ghana Telecom and the Ghana Commercial Bank, and concessioning of the Ghana Railways. Efforts will also be made to collect amounts due to government on account of previous divestitures. The government will continue to implement public sector reforms aimed at refocusing its priorities on core areas, in particular agriculture, the social sectors, and basic infrastructure. Ghana's decentralization policy is expected to devolve power, competence, and resources to the district level.  

IV. PROGRAM FOR 2000  

# A. Financial Policies  

19. Fiscal policy. The overall deficit in the 20o0 program, on a commitment basis, is projected at 6.5 percent of GDP, a 1.7 percentage point decline from 1999. The domestic primary balance is expected to show a surpius of about 4 percent of GDP in 2000. Helped by significantly higher divestiture receipts, the net domestic financing of the budget will be limited to 0.9 percent of GDP in 2000, and it will be maintained below 2 percent of GDP in 2001-02 to help lower inflation and interest rates and avoid crowding out the private sector.  

20. Revenue measures. Revenue is projected to rise to 18.0 percent of GDP in 2000. The value-added tax rate was increased from 10 percent to 12.5 percent and the petroleum excise tax has been raised by $\mathfrak{C}80$ per liter. Despite government's policy of cushioning the impact of the decline in world cocoa prices on poor farmers by keeping producer prices in cedi constant, cocoa revenues will be only slightly below last year's as the strong cedi depreciation has helped prevent a marked decline in revenue. The government does not intend to introduce new tax incentives, particularly those contingent on export and import performance, until the revenue impact of the existing incentives has been assessed.  

21. Revenue administration. The government has proposed the setting up of a Parliamentary Oversight Committee on the performance of revenue collecting agencies, with special powers to deal with delinquent and corrupt officials. The Ministry of Finance will make the release of funds to spending units that collect nontax revenue conditional on the presentation of quarterly returns and the transfer of these resources to the consolidated fund. Tariff exemptions will be tightened by allowing only nongovernmental agencies specifically exempted by parliament to import goods free of duty. Controls over bonded warehouses will be tightened by requiring computerization of inventory, conducting unannounced audits, limiting warehousing time and type of commodities allowed, and requiring breakage reports to be filled with customs not more than 48 hours after the goods reach the warehouse.  

22. Tariff reforms. The government will continue to review the tariff structure, with a view to enhancing the external competitiveness of local industry, harmonizing tariff rates with regional practices, and removing distortions. Trade developments in the West African region will be closely monitored to ensure that Ghana takes full advantage of existing regional arrangements, while positioning itself as the gateway to West Africa. The government will complete by September 2000 a study of its tariff structure to determine the best strategy in response to world and regional trends. Specifically, it will assess the effects of a possible reduction of the average tariff rate, which now stands at 13.6 percent, to 10 percent by end-20o2. It wil also impose a standstill on new exemptions and will review all exemptions and prepare a report on the review by end-October 2000. To facilitate the review of exemptions, the government will request technical assistance from the Fund.  

23. Expenditure. Noninterest recurrent spending will be cut by 0.7 percent of GDP, despite the inclusion of exceptional expenditures in 2000 comprising the national census l $\pmb{\mathcal{C}}26$ billion) and the elections (C 37 billion). Domestically financed capital expenditure will be maintained broadiy constant as a share of GDP, with spending on education and health increasing as a share of both total expenditure and GDP under the program. The wage bill is expected to increase broadly in line with nominal GDP.  

24.Expenditure controls. The government is implementing a number of expenditure control measures aimed at avoiding the accumulation of arrears (see paragraph below) and improving cash flow projections. These include the introduction of a certificate of commencement of work, which willbe required for any payments to be made with respect to ongoing and new projects in 2000. Also, certificates for payment submitted every fourth quarter will be thoroughly scrutinized to ensure that the services have been provided or goods received before effecting payment. Strict controls on procurement will be set up for all spending units. As part of the measures to minimize fraud, prosecution will now be an initial option in all cases of malfeasance revealed in the Auditor-General's report. As part of its efforts to strengthen expenditure controls and cash flow monitoring, the government has received Fund technical assistance in May/June and will respond to the mission's final report.  

25. Domestic arrears. Road arrears amounting to $\pmb{\mathbb{C}}$ 130 billion were outstanding at the end of l998, owing to contracts that allowed road construction to be carried out even in the absence of full budgetary allocation. To resolve this situation, the government terminated a number of contracts, scaled down domestically financed road projects, and revised procedures for initiating projects and awarding new road contracts in line with World Bank procurement procedures. Nevertheless, extensions of some of these road contracts to allow the completion of partly constructed road sections and underestimation of late interest charges resulted in outstanding road arrears remaining at $\pmb{\mathbb{C}}$ 277.5 billion in December 1999, despite payments in excess of program. A new program of payments aimed at eliminating road arrears by end-200l has been approved by cabinet in the first quarter of 2000. In addition to road arrears, other outstanding liabilities at the end of l999 are estimated to $\pmb{\mathbb{C}}$ 15( billion. The government will monitor these outstanding liabilities monthly and will eliminate them by mid-2000.  

26. Monetary policy. A key challenge for monetary policy in 2ooo will be to ensure that the passthrough effect on prices of the large depreciation in late l999 and early 2000 is minimized. The inflation target for 2000 is 20 percent (average period), given the large currency depreciation. Consistent with this objective and the expectation that real GDP will grow by 4 percent, the Bank of Ghana will aim at limiting monetary growth to 20 percent. The monetary growth target will be achieved by controlling reserve money growth to 15 percent by slowing down the growth of net credit to government. Pressures on the exchange rate and inflation have limited the Bank of Ghana's ability to reduce its interest rates in 2000. Bank financing of the budget is projected to expand by no more than $\pmb{\mathbb{C}}$ 213 billion, or the equivalent of about 0.8 percent of GDP, which would allow credit to the private sector to grow by 23 percent in 2000.  

27.To confront the instability in the foreign exchange market, the Bank of Ghana has taken steps to defend the exchange rate using monetary policy to remove excess liquidity from the financial markets and encourage the holding of domestic currency. In June 2000, the Bank of Ghana announced that reserve requirements would be increased from 8 to 9 percent effective July 3. Treasury bill sales were increased during May and June and the yield on 91- day treasury bills rose about ten percentage points to about 44 percent by late June. To improve the exchange rate mechanism, the Bank of Ghana has begun using the weighted average transaction-based exchange rates for all official transactions, and has halted the use of special swap agreements. The government has not and will not implement any new payments restrictions.  

28. The Bank of Ghana will intervene in the foreign exchange market as necessary to achieve its net foreign assets target, but will otherwise continue to ensure that the exchange rate is market-determined, without administrative interference. The Bank of Ghana's expectation is that the real effective exchange rate will gradually stabilize during the course of 2000, as the terms-of-trade shock is absorbed, foreign exchange inflows increase in the second half of the year, and inflation moderates in response to continued tight monetary policies. For 20o0, the Bank of Ghana has targeted an accumulation of net foreign assets, and gross international reserves from about 1.5 months of imports to 1.8 months of imports.  

29. The Bank of Ghana will keep domestic and foreign exchange market developments under close review and will adjust policies as needed. It will continue to take steps to deepen its domestic and foreign exchange markets and has requested Fund assistance in this area. In this context, it intends to eliminate the surrender requirement on gold exports by September 2001.  

30. Balance of payments prospects. Ghana's external situation is very vulnerable. The Bank of Ghana gross international reserves were not more than 1.5 months of imports in 1999. Exports in 2000 are forecast to increase by 4 percent despite the decline in Ghana's export prices, owing primarily to a strong growth of nontraditional exports. Import volumes are projected to contract markedly, although import values willfall only 3 percent as a result of increased import prices. The current account deficit, including grants, is expected to remain at about $10\%$ percent of GDP in 2000.  

31. External financing requirements. External financing requirements are projected to total about US\$2.4 billion over the 2000-2002 period. For 2000, total requirements amount to about $\mathbf{US}\$90.9$ billion. The financing gap for 2oo0 was closed by exceptional assistance by a number of donors led by the World Bank and Japan. Additional access under the PRGF, amounting to 10 percent of quota, is also'being requested. Because of the large terms-of-trade shocks in 1999/00, Ghana's debt service payments experienced temporary delays, but the government is committed to meeting its external debt-service obligations, including those to the Fund.  

32. Statistical issues. The government continues to attach high priority to improving its statistics base. It intends to conduct a housing and population census in 2oo0. The Ghana Statistical Service (GSS) will continue its review of the methodology used to compile national accounts; it will publish updated national accounts (sources and uses) with a lag of at most six months. The GSS will collaborate closely with the Ministry of Trade and Industry, Customs and the Bank of Ghana to improve the reliability of trade and balance of payments statistics. In this context, the government has received technical assistance on balance of payments from the Statistics Department of the Fund. The government will report consumer prices, international reserves, and monetary accounts with lags of not more than one month, as well as budget execution data with a lag of not more than six weeks, and trade and travel receipts data with a lag of not more than 2 months.

# Technical Memorandum of Understanding  

1. This technical note contains definitions and adjustor mechanisms that are intended to clarify the measurement of the performance criteria designated in the Memorandum of Economic and Financial Policies (MEFP), paragraph 33 and Table 1B of the MEFP.  

2. The domestic primary balance is defined as the difference between total central government revenue (excluding foreign grants and divestiture receipts) and noninterest central government expenditure (excluding foreign-financed capital expenditure), measured on a commitment basis.  

3． Central government revenue, a benchmark, excludes foreign grants and divestiture receipts.  

4. Ceiling on domestic financing of the government. The net domestic financing of the government is defined as the change in net credit to government, as reported in the central governments accounts of the monetary survey, plus net financing from all other sources to the government, excluding the amount of bonds contracted to cover the cost of bank restructuring. The quarterly quantitative performance criteria and benchmarks for the ceiling on domestic financing of the government have been set in cedis. All quantitative performance criteria and benchmarks willbe evaluated in terms of cumulative flows from January 1, 2000.  

i. All quantitative performance criteria and benchmarks for the ceiling on domestic financing of the government will be adjusted for differences between projected and actual external program support for the government. Projected quarterly amounts in U.S. dollars of external program support for the government budget (program loans and grants) are presented in Table 1. Quarterly differences between projected and actual support, both excesses and shortages, will be converted to cedis based on programmed average quarterly exchange rates. The ceiling on domestic financing will be reduced by the excess of actual over projected external program support and will be increased by 50 percent of the shortfall of actual below projected external program support. A temporary increase in the ceiling of 100 percent of a shortfall will be allowed if the shortfall is generated by a disbursement programmed within one quarter of a test date other than the last.  

ii. Divestiture receipts. The government's fiscal program for 2000 projects the following flows of divestiture receipts for 2000: US\$100 million in July, and US\$17 million in October.  

iii. The ceiling on domestic financing of the government willbe reduced by any excess in divestiture receipts over the programmed amounts given in item ii above expressed in cedis. The ceiling on domestic financing will not be increased for a shortfall in divestiture receipts.  

iv. Domestic arrears in sectors other than the road sector. Such arrears comprise all accrued liabilities on purchases, wages, interest, and transfers in sectors outside the road sector. All such arrears are expected to be eliminated by the end of June 2000.  

v.The ceiling on domestic financing of the government will be reduced by the amount of new net domestic arrears at any test date.  

5. Reserve money is defined as the sum of currency in circulation, commercial banks deposits at the Bank of Ghana in cedis and private sector demand deposits at the BoG in cedis. It will be measured by the indicated stock at end of month and will be adjusted downward to the extent of any reduction in, or shortfall in compliance with, the legal reserve requirement (8 percent of average weekly bank deposits until July 6 and 9 percent thereafter).  

6. Floor on net foreign assets of the Bank of Ghana. Until December 2000 or the completion of the STA technical assistance mission if earlier, net foreign assets will be defined as the difference between gross foreign assets and gross foreign liabilities based on the currency criterion presently utilized by the Bank of Ghana. Subject to the results of technical assistance, foreign assets and liability transactions will be based on the residency criterion, which will exclude swap arrangements with and foreign currency deposits from domestic banks from foreign assets and liabilities, respectively. These data will be provided as memoranda items until the final revision of accounts. The quarterly quantitative performance criteria and benchmarks for the floor on net foreign assets of the Bank of Ghana have been set in U.S. dollars and will be monitored on an end-of-quarter basis. All quantitative performance criteria and benchmarks will be evaluated in terms of cumulative flows from January 1, 2000.  

i. All quantitative performance criteria and benchmarks for the floor on net foreign assets of the Bank of Ghana will be adjusted for differences between projected and actual external program support for the government budget. Differences, both excesses and shortages, will be expressed in U.S. dollars. The floor on net foreign assets of the Bank of Ghana will be increased by the excess of actual over projected external program support and will be reduced by 50 percent of the shortfall of actual below projected external program support. A temporary decrease in the floor of 100 percent of a shortfall will be allowed if the shortfall is generated by a disbursement programmed within one quarter of a test date other than the last.  

ii. The floor on net foreign assets of the Bank of Ghana will be increased by any excess in divestiture receipts paid in dollars over the programmed amounts given in paragraph 4, item ii, but will not be decreased for any shortfall in divestiture receipts.  

7. Short-term external debt contracted or guaranteed by the government or the Bank of Ghana (with an initial maturity of less than one year) will exclude normal import-related credits. For the purposes of the program, government is defined to comprise central and local authorities and special funds. Public enterprises, agencies and public corporations are excluded from the definition of government. As such, cocoa financing by Cocobod is excluded.

# Ghana: Relations with the World Bank Group (As of May 18, 2000)  

1.The World Bank Group's Country Assistance Strategy (CAS) for Ghana for the period of FY01-FY03 was presented to the Bank's Board on March 30, 2000. The CAS outlines the Bank's business plan to support the Government's development strategy aimed at eliminating hard-core poverty in Ghana. Within this broad goal, the Bank Group would help the Government to: (i) raise the growth rate of the economy; (ii) redefine the role of the state; and (iii) implement their strategy more effectively on the ground. Ghana is one of the pilot countries for the Comprehensive Development Framework (CDF) and the preparation of the CAS was an integral part of close donor coordination under the CDF initiative.  

2. As of May 18, 2000 total commitments to Ghana amounted to US\$1,233 million with total disbursements of US\$692 million. In addition, the IFC's portfolio totals 20 projects, for total commitments of US\$18.4 million, of which US\$14.3 million have been disbursed. Over the three-year period covering FY01-FY03, the CAS proposes a lending program of US\$490 million for the base case and US\$640 million for the high case. Under the low case, lending would be 40 percent below the base case. Triggers include: the public sector domestic borrowing requirement, portfolio performance, cocoa reform, private participation in infrastructure, and education sector performance.  

3. Recent and proposed Bank Group projects have broad sectoral coverage, including roads and other infrastructure, health, education, agriculture, environment, private sector development, and public sector management. These projects cmphasize rehabilitation, maintenance, and institutional strengthening. More reliance is being put on supporting sectorwide programs and nonlending services. The Bank is moving away from infrastructure investments in telecommunications, power, ports, rails, and large urban water systems, while providing greater nonlending support (enhanced by IFC and MIGA) to promote private participation in these sectors.  

4. Since the formulation of Ghana's Economic Recovery Program in 1983, the Bank has provided a series of policy-based credits to help finance urgently needed imports and support structural reforms, including trade liberalization, rationalization of public expenditures; privatization, and parastatal reform. In FY98, a one-tranche adjustment credit, the Economic Reform Support Operation, was approved. In FY99, the Bank approved a second Economic Reform Support Operation (ERSO I) adjustment credit in the amount of US\$180 million. In view of the recent sharp decline in Ghana's terms of trade, the Bank is considering providing a supplemental to ERSO II within the next months.

# Ghana: Statistical Issues  

1. Ghana is in the bottom quartile of Fund members in timeliness of reporting core surveillance data. This situation is a source of great concern. Improvements in the quality and timeliness of Ghana's statistics should be given high priority by the government. Areas where improvements need to be made are identified below.  

# National accounts  

2. The Ghana Statistical Service (GSS) is the agency responsible for compiling national accounts statistics, which were published in the Quarterly Digest of Statistics until 1992, when publication was halted. From 1992 until the fallof 1998, only estimates of GDP by sectors were provided to the staff. In the absence of official GDP data by expenditure category, the Fund staff developed its own estimates on the basis of available information on the fiscal accounts and the balance of payments.  

3. In 1996, the GSS received technical assistance from the United Kingdom's Department for International Development (DFID) through the assignment of a resident national accounts statistician. The objective of the assistance was to prepare new national acounts estimates using 1993 weights, based on information collected from the third Ghana Living Standards Survey (GLSS), instead of the 1975 weights that had been used. The resident statistician was also expected to help with the estimation of GDP by expenditure category.  

4. In September 1998, a complete set of national accounts for the years 1993 to 1996 was published by the GSS. However, the methodology used in preparing the data has not been published. Estimates of GDP by sectors had been revised to account for use of 1993 weights, which resulted in downward revisions by about 5 percentage points and 20 percentage points of the sector weights for agriculture and services, respectively, and an upward revision of 12 percentage points for industry. The national accounts series was not revised backward beyond l993, resulting in large discontinuities in the series between 1992 and 1993.  

5. The staff also noted large discrepancies between the import data used for the national accounts and those used for the balance of payments. For 1997, the discrepancy in merchandise imports was close to US\$1 billion in c.i.f. terms. Moreover, the behavior of some deflators did not seem consistent with available price indicators. For example, the deflator for investment, described to the staff as a weighted average of the building and construction price index and the consumer price index (CPI), was found to significantly exceed both of these indices. In this context, the staff asked the Ghanaian authorities to (a) reconcile the trade data between the national accounts and the balance of payments; (b) review the deflators for errors in their calculation; and (c) provide the staff with a description of the methodology used to compile the national accounts. The authorities  

completed the first item (see below for details). The revision of the deflators resulted only in a change for the l997 investment deflator, which does not resolve the staff's concerns.  

6. To improve the reliability of national accounts at current prices, a comprehensive overhaul of the basic sources of data would be needed, as the existing sectoral surveys that form the basis for the estimations are very much outdated. A review of the method of compilation of GDP by expenditure categories led to a number of recommendations: (i) private household expenditure should be estimated independently using detailed information from the household expenditure surveys rather than as a residual; (ii) total consumption should be split into private and government consumption, deflating the former by the CPI and the latter by a price index for government output such as the average wages paid to civil servants; and (ii) the constant price estimates for gross fixed capital formation in machinery and equipment should be calculated by deflating current price estimates by the various import unit values of that particular type of imported good. The staff also assisted the GSS in selecting better deflators for the construction sector. The approach involved deflating gross output and intermediate consumption for three main types of construction activity-- non-permanent housing, permanent housing, and roads, bridges and other construction mainly undertaken by the government.  

7. The staff believes that additional technical assistance in the compilation of national accounts statistics is urgently needed; furthermore, this assistance should focus primarily on developing and implementing updated statistical surveys that could be used to compile better estimates of value added by sector and ensuring that appropriate deflators are used in the compilation of the national accounts.  

# Prices  

8. A revised national CPI was introduced in January l998 with a base of September 1997. The weights are computed based on the standard consumption basket which was estimated from the 1992 GLSS. Most notably, the weight of clothing in the new basket was reduced from 19 percent to 9 percent while the weights for medical care, housing, and transport were increased. GLSS4, completed in June 1999, will serve as the basis for a revision of the CPl's current weights to take account of the substantial shifts in relative prices since the 1992 GLSS. The authorities have reduced the lag in reporting the CPI to one month. The wholesale price index was discontinued in 1991.  

# Labor statistics  

9. The paucity of labor statistics is a cause for concern; while wage statistics are almost nonexistent, some wage indicators are available from the Social Security National Insurance Trust (SSNIT). The Ministry of Employment has been receiving technical assistance from the UNDP and the ILO in the design and compilation of labor statistics.  

# Trade and Balance of Payments Statistics  

10. The staff recommended that the GSS collaborate with the CEPS, the Ministry of Trade and Industry (MoT), and the BOG in identifying and reducing the discrepancies in trade statistics between the various sources. Besides setting up regular inter-agency procedures to foster collaboration, the data collection procedures of the CEPS need to be improved. For instance, there is significant scope for improving trade volume data collected by the CEPS through customs invoices, which would help the GSS to develop meaningful import and export unit values for balance of payments and national accounts purposes. Another area of deficient trade data concerns the accurate recording of VALCO's aluminum exports, which tend to exhibit sharp fluctuations from one year to the next. The staff recommended that the GSS and the MoT seek the co-operation of VALCO in reconciling discrepancies in aluminum exports data.  

11. The staff recommended that the GSS develop export unit values for major export commodities such as gold and cocoa. A high coverage of the country's export bundle can be obtained by including just three major exports-cocoa, gold, and unwrought aluminum. In contrast, the deflation of imports is likely to involve an iterative procedure in order to strike a balance between coverage of the index and its stability due to heterogeneity of the basket. In case import unit values exhibit sharp fluctuations owing to product mix, the GSS was advised to use the export prices of particular groups of commodities published by Ghana's major trading partners with proper adjustments of these prices for exchange rate changes.  

12.A technical assistance mission on balance of payments statistics in March 200o found that improvements in the quality of these statistics would necessitate the introduction of surveys of key establishments, the training of staf in the BOP unit of the BOG, improved collaboration between the various government agencies responsible for collecting related data, and additional computer resources for the agencies involved. Moreover, survey data are also needed for the services account of the BOP, in particular for travel statistics through the implementation of surveys of various travel agents and tour operators. The mission also assisted the authorities in completing the transition to accounting under the fifth edition of the Balance of Payments manual.  

13. The BOG has been unable to directly estimate foreign direct investment flows into Ghana for want of direct data based on surveys of foreign direct investment (FDI) companies. Thus, good survey data need to be extracted from the FDI companies and from the banks and foreign exchange bureaus. The surveys should focus on tracking direct investment flows, loans, interest payments and receipts, and foreign holdings. Moreover, it was suggested that the BOG work closely with the Ghana Investment Center and the Attorney General's Office in setting up a register of FDI companies in Ghana and initiate surveys of these establishments.  

# Debt statistics  

14. The Aid and Debt Management Unit of the Ministry of Finance and Economic Planning (MOF) maintains the external debt database. The BOG, as the financial agent of the government, services the external debt upon order of the MOF after review and approval of the Office of the Accountant General. The MOF makes use of the Commonwealth Secretariat Debt Reporting System and the World Bank provides technical assistance with helping to set up and manage the system. The Debt Management Unit is now able to provide data on new debt contracted, interest and principal payments due and paid as wellas arrears two weeks after the end of the quarter. With technical assistance from the Commonwealth Secretariat, significant improvements have been made in the debt-reporting system, although reconciliation of data between the Bank of Ghana and the Ministry of Finance still presents problems.  

# Public finance  

15. The timeliness of the reporting of public finance statistics was improved by the introduction in 1994 of a new expenditure coding system that allows data to be generated or a cash basis from the central bank records. Further accounting improvements now allow a summary budget statement to be available with a lag of only one month. However, an economic classification of expenditures is available only with a lag of at least two months. Expenditure data on a commitment basis are not available, but efforts have recently been made to get large ministries to report their expenditure commitments and arrears to the Ministry of Finance. Expenditures financed by externally funded project aid are produced with a considerable Iag and are subject to estimation errors, owing to timing differences, foreign exchange valuation, and the recording of the technical assistance component. Staff estimates of these expenditures are derived from balance of payments data. Consolidated fiscal accounts are not compiled because data on autonomous government agencies (subvented agencies) and public enterprises are not reported. A World Bank mission reviewed the government's domestic debt data in August 1998.  

16.Annual government finance data were reported for publication in the Government Finance Statistics Yearbook (GFSY), but the coverage and timeliness of the data need to be improved; the latest available data cover the central government only and relate to the fiscal year ended December 31, 1993. These data include all the major components of the government finance statistics tables, with the exception of debt, for which the latest data published in the GFSY relate to l985. The authorities reported fiscal data for the 1996 GFSY, but the data could not be published, as they were not sufficiently detailed to allow conversion to the government finance statistics format. No monthly or quarterly data are reported for publication in IFS. In April 2000, the authorities contacted STA to resume reporting of fiscal data for the GFSY. Data are expected by August 2000, in time for inclusion in the 2000 edition of the GFSY.  

# Monetary Statistics  

17. The monetary authorities report monetary data on a regular basis and have reduced the reporting lag for most components to one month. In December 1997, the monetary survey was extended to include 6 additional banks; it thus currently covers all 17 banks in the system. Notwithstanding the technical assistance provided since l985, several problems persist in Ghana's monetary statistics. In particular, there are significant deviations between data reported by commercial banks with respect to their deposits with the central bank and those reported by the Bank of Ghana. Moreover, foreign currency accounts are not valued at . the end-of-period market exchange rates. A STA money and banking statistics mission (July 1997) proposed a plan of action and a timetable to resolve these problems. Among the issues that need to be addressed is the classification by residency of foreign currency deposits in commercial banks, as well as the proper treatment of repurchase agreements that are conducted between the Bank of Ghana and the commercial banks. A follow-up STA mission is scheduled for the latter part of 2000.  

# Publication  

18. The reporting of data to the Fund for purposes of publication in the IFS has recently improved. However, there are still lags of about four to five months in reporting exchange rates, prices, and international transactions data. In general, statistical information from official sources is not disseminated to the public on a timely basis. Even though the Quarterly Economic Bulletin of the Bank of Ghana is now published more regularly, the data show lags of up to six months. The Quarterly Digest of Statistics is usually published by the GSS with a longer lag. The government has agreed to take steps to ensure that both quarterly bulletins will be published regularly, with a lag of not more than 60 days.  

19. Deficiencies in data dissemination have been brought to the attention of the Ghanaian authorities at the level of the Minister of Finance, the Governor of the Bank of Ghana, and the Government Statistician.

# Coverage of Performance Criterion on the Short-Term Debt Ceiling  

1. The staff has been asked to review whether the crop financing by the Ghana Cocoa Board (Cocobod), using cocoa receivables as guarantee, was a breach of the performance criterion on the short-term debt ceiling, and, as a result, a noncompliant purchase was made in November 1999. LEG has not found grounds for the above, but some background explanation is provided below.  

2. Since Ghana's third annual arrangement in 1991 under the Enhanced Structural Adjustment Facility, the program has maintained a quantitative performance criterion on "short-term external debt outstanding contracted or guaranteed by the government or the Bank of Ghana (with an initial maturity of less than one year)." The criterion carried a qualifying footnote “excluding import-related credits." Performance under this target was measured against data on new short-term debt contracted by the Bank of Ghana or central government. Short-term cocoa financing by the Ghana Cocoa Board (Cocobod), which was seasonally in excess of the ceiling, was viewed as secure and self-liquidating and not a target of this criterion. A question was recently raised, however, whether short-term cocoa financing should have been considered when assessing compliance with this performance criterion, given Ghana's external debt situation.  

3． This type of short-term cocoa financing has been taking place since 1991. In 1999, Cocoa exports were US\$550 million and were financed by a syndicated loan totaling US\$300 million, which was known to the staff and announced in the international financial press. The size of the loan was determined by the expected crop size, the world price and the domestic producer price. The loan was guaranteed by cocoa receivables, as some 90 percent of Cocobod's exports were carried out under forward contract. Cocobod first sells the foreign exchange from its foreign loan to the Bank of Ghana; the cedi proceeds are then onlent with commercial bank guarantees to licensed buying companies. The income from this domestic lending has been exceeding the cost of borrowing abroad. Until now, all exports of cocoa in Ghana were done by Cocobod. After deducting its marketing and other cost, Cocobod's profits were transferred to the government budget in the form of the cocoa export tax.  

4. LEG reviewed the 1994 Cocobod Law and concluded that the statute does not provide for a statutory guarantee of any cocoa financing by the government. The staff also requested from the authorities copies of documents pertaining to the syndicated loan for review. Due to a confidentiality clause in the syndicated loan agreement between Cocobod and the lenders, the authorities were unable to provide the staff with a copy of the agreement to review. Though the staff would prefer to review the agreement, in the letter dated June 16, 2000 to the IMF resident representative, the Minister of Finance confirmed that the government and the Central Bank of Ghana have been neither obligors nor guarantors of any cocoa financing agreements entered into by Cocobod during the first year of the three-year PRGF arrangement.  

5. The question of whether Cocobod falls within the meaning of "government," is less clear. Cocobod is empowered to manage and regulate the cocoa industry but requires government permission to set the producer price of cocoa. LEG has interpreted the term "government" to mean the central government and those entities whose financial operations form part of the government budget. Given the fact that Cocobod has been self-financing on its current activities, it does not fall within “government."  

6. To avoid possible confusion in the future, a Technical Memorandum of Understanding has been attached to the MEFP to clarify definitions and measurement of performance criteria. Cocoa financing will be explicitly excluded from the short-term debt ceiling, but will be monitored by the staff.
