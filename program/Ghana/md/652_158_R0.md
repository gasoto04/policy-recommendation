# I. Introduction  

The l995 Article IV consultation discussions with Ghana, together with negotiations on a program to be supported by an ESAF arrangement, were held between November 1994 and March 1995. l/ The Ghanaian representatives included Dr. Botchwey, Minister of Finance, Dr. Agama, Governor of the Bank of Ghana, and other senior officials concerned with economic and financial matters.  

In the attached letter dated April 28, 1995, the Government of Ghana is requesting a three-year ESAF arrangement in an amount equivalent to SDR 164.4 million (60 percent of quota), in support of its economic program for 1995-97. A total of SDR 54.8 million (20 percent of quota) would be available in the first year (Table 1). After taking into account the payment of scheduled obligations, Ghana's outstanding use of Fund credit, which amounted to SDR 477.6 million (l74 percent of quota) at end-March 1995, would decline to SDR 366.6 million (134 percent of quota) by endDecember 1997.  

Ghana's economic program is to be implemented in the context of a medium-term strategy, which has been formulated by the Government in close collaboration with the staffs of the Fund and the World Bank. The program is set out in a policy framework paper (PFP), which was circulated to the Fund's Executive Board on May --, l995 (EBD/95/--) and issued to the Executive Board of the World Bank at the same time. 2/  

Ghana is on the standard 12-month Article IV consultation cycle, and the last consultation was concluded on June 24, 1994. 3/ Ghana formally accepted the obligations of Article VIII, Sections 2, 3, and $4$ of the Fund's Articles of Agreement on February 2l, 1994.  

Ghana's relations with the Fund and the World Bank Group are summarized in Appendices I and II, respectively.  

# II． Background and Recent Economic Performance  

Following a protracted period of serious economic decline, the Government of Ghana launched an economic recovery program (ERP) in 1983., which aimed at reviving economic growth through the pursuit of tight demand management policies and the easing of structural impediments to expanding economic activity. The ERP was supported by a series of Fund arrangements, the most recent of which was a three-year ESAF arrangement that expired in March l992. Ghana enjoyed considerable success under the ERP; inflation was reduced from l42 percent in l983 to l0 percent by the end of 199l, real GDP growth averaged about 5 percent a year, and the balance of payments registered sizable overall surpluses throughout most of the period.  

In l992 the Ghanaian authorities sought to continue the process of economic reform in the context of a program under the Fund's arrangements for enhanced consultations. However, serious difficulties were encountered in policy implementation, and the program slipped off track. In an effort to quell labor unrest and maintain a stable political climate during the run-up to the first multiparty elections under the new constitution, large wage increases and other benefits were awarded to the civil service. In addition, government revenue performance was undermined by shortfalls in cocoa tax receipts and delays in passing through higher oil import costs to retail petroleum prices. As a result, the Government's overall budget balance reverted from a surplus of 1.5 percent of GDP in 199l to a deficit of 4.8 percent in l992, and strong price and balance of payments pressures re-emerged (Chart 1). Private investment slumped, and real GDP growth dropped to below 4 percent.  

The authorities made efforts to bring economic performance back on track during 1993-94, under macroeconomic adjustment programs that were monitored informally by the Fund staff. Although performance under these programs generally fell short of expectations, particularly in the area of monetary policy, some progress was made in reducing fiscal and external imbalances and laying the basis for increased growth of economic activity (Tables 2 and 3). The government budget recorded a surplus of 2.2 percent of GDP in l994, bolstered by proceeds from the sale of public assets,  

amounting to the equivalent of 5 percent of GDP (Tables 4 and 5), $\yen12$ and increased revenue from taxes on cocoa exports. On the other hand, plans to impose import duties on a range of zero-rated or exempted goods were not carried through, leading to shortfalls in such receipts, while overruns occurred on personal emoluments, other labor-related expenditures, and domestic interest payments.  

The implementation of monetary policy in l994 was severely undermined by large arrears incurred on oil-related credits by the national oil company (GNPC) to the Bank of Ghana. As a result, despite a step-up in open market operations by the Bank of Ghana and the net repayment of debt to the banking system by the Government, broad money grew by 46 percent in l994, as against an original target of 5 percent (Table 6). 2/ Inflationary pressures intensified in response, with the consumer price index increasing by 34 percent on an end-of-period basis in l994. This inflation rate compares with a three-month treasury bill rate of 30 percent at the end of December 1994 and bank lending rates in the range of 29-33 percent.  

Ghana's external position deteriorated sharply in l993 as a consequence of the expansionary financial and incomes policies and a worsening in the terms of trade. The current account deficit (including official transfers) widened from 3.6 percent of GDP in 199l to 9.2 percent in 1993, while reserves fell from the equivalent of 4.1 months of imports to 2.7 months. In response, the Bank of Ghana allowed the cedi to depreciate sharply in real terms from around the middle of l993 (Chart 2). This action helped contain import demand and, in conjunction with a terms of trade improvement and a much stronger export performance, contributed to a marked reduction in the current account deficit, to 4.9 percent of GDP in l994 (Table 8). Ghana experienced a major shortfall in long-term concessional aid in 1994, equivalent to some $4$ percent of GDP, as a result of difficulties encountered in fulfilling macroeconomic conditions for disbursements under multilateral and bilateral aid programs. However, the impact of this shortfall on the overall balance of payments was more than offset by receipts from the divestiture program and short-term foreign borrowing by the Cocoa Board that had not been built into the program. l/ Accordingly, Ghana's gross reserve position improved to 4.l months of imports by the end of l994, but this was well below target after upward adjustment for Cocoa Board borrowing and excess divestiture proceeds.  

Real GDP growth recovered to 5.0 percent in l993, primarily on account of the impact of favorable weather conditions on agricultural output. However, growth slackened off to 3.8 percent in l994, as a result of the uncertainties surrounding the macroeconomic situation, uneven rains, and ethnic disturbances in the north of the country.  

III. Policy Issues and Economic Program for 1995-97  

# 1. Medium-term policy framework  

# a. Overall strategy  

Despite the reduction in macroeconomic imbalances and structural distortions under the ERP during l983-9l, inflation remained comparatively high during most of the period, while private sector saving and investment performance was weak. Real per capita GDP growth averaged 2 percent a year, but at this rate Ghana's average poor would not cross the "poverty line" for well over 30 years. The Government has therefore targeted an acceleration in growth, to 5.5 percent by l997 and to 6 percent by the end of the decade. Its program for achieving this goal is based on reversing the poor record on investment by creating a sound and predictable financial environment within which Ghana can maintain a stable nominal exchange rate, while enjoying a strong and sustainable external position that is less vulnerable to external shocks. Consistent with these objectives, policies have been developed with a view to reducing inflation to 5 percent by l997 and the external current account deficit to 2.7 percent of GDP.  

The authorities are of the view that savings from domestically generated sources will not alone be sufficient to generate the volume of investment needed to raise the growth rate. Moreover, Ghana cannot depend on any significant increase in foreign aid from the levels recorded in the early l990s. It will therefore need to attract new inflows of foreign investment and concentrate on policies designed to improve the quality of investment. The experience of the ERP suggests that, while much was accomplished in removing impediments to private investment, economic initiative was retarded by the absence of clear signals from the Government regarding the role it expected the private sector to play in promoting growth. The economic program for l995-97 seeks to provide such signals in a clear and unambiguous manner by removing state involvement and intervention in all areas of economic activity that can be handled more efficiently by the private sector. Specific actions focus on an acceleration and expansion of the divestiture program, as well as deregulation of the petroleum and cocoa sectors. Those enterprises that remain in the public domain are to be placed on a sounder commercial basis. The program also includes policies aimed at raising productivity in agriculture, which still contributes over 40 percent of output, but which has performed poorly over the past decade. 1/  

# b. Stabilization policies  

The authorities' medium-term fiscal strategy is geared to generating the additional government savings needed to help meet the program's investment and external current account objectives. The overall budget balance, excluding divestiture proceeds, is expected to move from a deficit of 2.9 percent of GDP in l994 to a surplus of 1.3 percent by 1997. It is anticipated that the bulk of this adjustment, or about 3 percent of GDP, will emanate from lower spending relative to GDP. In particular, laborrelated expenditures, which consist primarily of civil service pay and end-of-service benefits, 2/ are expected to decline by nearly 1 percent of GDP during 1995-97, while reductions in outlays on goods, services, and transfers are also planned. After an initial increase, the domestic interest burden should also shrink in line with lower interest rates and a marked reduction in the Government's domestic debt through the medium term. Major new tax initiatives have been launched in l995, but in light of the relatively high prevailing level of taxation and the need to reduce dependence on export taxes, the Government believes that there is only limited scope for undertaking additional new measures during the rest of th program period, if the recovery of economic activity is not to be impeded.  

Within the overall objective of reducing the budget deficit, the fiscal strategy calls for a realignment in the structure of both revenues and expenditures. The Government remains concerned about the distortionary impact of income-based taxes on private investment, labor, and savings decisions. As an important first step in shifting the tax burden toward expenditure-based sources of revenue, a value-added tax (VAT) was introduced on March l, l995 (see below). Similarly, the tax on cocoa exports gives rise to considerable disincentives to production, and the Government is exploring ways of taxing cocoa producers, and agriculture more generally, in a manner that does not act as a disincentive to producers. An embryonic  

profits tax, grower licenses and fees, and property or land taxes are possible sources of revenue that are being considered. The Government also plans to restructure its expenditures by using some of the savings generated from lower outlays on labor and interest to fund improvements in the country's economic infrastructure and the provision of social services, notably in the areas of education and health. 1/ The projections do not incorporate any net costs that may emanate from a forthcoming civil service restructuring exercise.  

The Government recognizes that bringing inflation firmly under control is a necessary precondition for achieving enhanced growth. Its antiinflation policy is based upon a two-step strategy. In the first stage. which will cover at least the first year of the ESAF arrangement, money will provide the nominal anchor for prices, and accordingly broad money growth will be reduced sharply. Monetary restraint will be accompanied by a flexible exchange rate policy aimed at protecting Ghana's external competitiveness and foreign reserve position while inflation is subsiding. In implementing its monetary policy, the central bank will monitor targets that have been established for reserve money. In the event that these targets are exceeded because of \*overperformance\* in the balance of payments, the appropriate policy response will depend primarily on the extent to which the monetary impulse is perceived as inflationary. 2/ The authorities will remain in contact with the staff on this matter, but it would in any event be a focus of the midterm review under the first annual ESAF arrangement.  

Subsequently, once sufficient progress has been made in establishing a tight and credible demand-management posture and inflation has been convincingly brought under control for a reasonable period, consideration will be given to using the exchange rate as the instrument for holding down inflation. Use of the exchange rate as a nominal anchor, and a commitment to maintain the rate at a stable and predictable level, should provide added discipline in the implementation of fiscal policy and help bolster business and investor confidence. Under this regime, monetary policy would be directed toward meeting the program's overall balance of payments targets and, in particular, ensuring that Ghana accumulates sufficient foreign reserves to provide a strong cushion against unanticipated external shocks.  

Control of the money supply will continue to be effected through open market operations, which are currently conducted by the central bank in the form of weekly auctions of official debt. Less reliance will be placed on the use of a reserve price in the auctions and interest rates, which will continue to be market-determined, will probably rise further in real terms, thereby providing support for the program's inflation and savings targets. However, open market operations are still a relatively recent monetary policy instrument, and until confidence in them has been firmly established, liquid asset requirements will continue to be used as and when necessary. The intention is eventually to lower these requirements to the level necessary for prudential purposes only. l/ To promote the development of a secondary market, the central bank is planning on rediscounting bankers' acceptances.  

# c. External sector policies  

As indicated above, during the first year of the program, Ghana will maintain a flexible exchange rate policy, whereby net sales of foreign exchange by the Bank of Ghana will be guided by its foreign reserve targets. The acceptance by Ghana of the obligations of Article VIII underscores the Government's intention of achieving its external objectives through marketbased instruments rather than recourse to quantitative controls. Ghana continues to maintain certain capital controls in the form of surrender requirements and restrictions on overseas lending and the acquisition of foreign investments by residents. 2/ The authorities will consider the removal of these controls, and the adoption of full currency convertibility, once inflation is brought under control and foreign reserves are raised to more comfortable levels than at present.  

During the ERP, Ghana undertook a number of important changes in its import protection system, most notably the dismantling of all quantitative restrictions on imports and their replacement by tariffs. The degree of effective protection has also been lowered by reductions in the dispersion of tariffs, the most recent being those announced in the l995 budget. Other countries in the region have not moved as quickly as Ghana in undertaking tariff reform, and the Government is of the view that the structure of tariffs is appropriate at the present time. In l994 a system of minimum presumptive duties was applied to a specific list of imports in order to address difficulties being experienced in customs collection. The Government is aware that presumptive duties are very much a second-best solution to the problem of duty evasion, and intends to retain the system only until it is satisfied that the quality of preshipment inspection has been raised to satisfactory standards and that the imports concerned attract the correct amount of duty. This should be accomplished well within the program period.  

# d. Structural policies  

The discussions with the authorities on structural policies revolved around the objective of limiting or removing the remaining state involvement in economic decision-making and providing strong market-based incentives for private sector initiative. The authorities explained that they had already set up various channels of dialogue with the private sector and that a number of important policy actions, including a new Investment Act, had resulted. In addition, a legal framework has been established for converting state enterprises into incorporated companies in readiness for privatization.  

The privatization program, which aims at selling or liquidating virtually all state enterprises that are not public utilities, is being accelerated and extended to cover additional companies. The sale of nearly one half of the Government's share in the largest state enterprise, Ashanti Goldfields, was successfully concluded in l994. The Government now intends to sell several large enterprises, such as Ghana Telecommunications, three financial institutions (including the country's largest commercial bank), and 48 small- to mid-scale enterprises during l995-97. 1/ Disbursements under World Bank lending programs are conditional upon performance in this area. The financial operations of those state enterprises that remain in the public domain are being strengthened and placed on a profit-making basis under a performance contract system. No goverrment or central bank financial support, including loan guarantees, will be provided to these enterprises, which will be expected to cover their financing needs from internal sources or private capital markets.  

The government wage bill absorbs over 5 percent of GDP, but uncompetitive pay levels in the civil service have given rise to difficulties in attracting and maintaining high calibre staff, particularly in managerial and executive positions. Efforts in the past to cut the size of the civil service have not proven particularly successful and, to provide a comprehensive and feasible solution to the problem, the Government has established a high-level committee to draw up recommendations for reducing the size of, and restructuring pay scales in, the civil service and subvented organizations. The recommendations will be implemented from early 1996.  

# e. Social and environmental issues l/  

Moderate progress was made under the ERP in reducing poverty, mostly in rural areas where it remains most prevalent. 2/ The Government recognizes that economic growth is a necessary, but not sufficient, condition for poverty reduction, and is continuing in its efforts to expand and strengthen the social safety net and protect the most vulnerable groups. In particular, increased farm incomes arising from structural reforms in the agricultural sector should help reduce poverty further in the rural areas, while the Government's efforts to curb inflation will also benefit the most needy and those on fixed incomes. The l995 budget provides for a real increase in spending on health and education, and also for an increase of over 50 percent in the minimum threshold for personal income tax. In addition, basic foodstuffs, livestock, and agricultural inputs have been exempted from the new VAT.  

Environmental problems arising from inappropriate cultivation practices and widespread deforestation pose serious threats to Ghana's rural economic base. A new forest and wildlife policy was approved in late l994 to deal with illegal timber felling and to promote a sustainable utilization of forest resources. Cocoa farming, in particular, stands to benefit from this policy, since cocoa trees require a forest canopy to produce maximum yields. Mining regulations have also been revised in an effort to prevent the discharge of heavy metals and cyanide into local water supplies, and the Government is imposing standards on the use of chemicals and the cleaning up of mined-out areas. Attempts are being made to ensure that the cost of repairing environmental damage is borne by the private sector through the imposition of concession fees on timber and mine operators. In l994 the Government established an environmental fund, which is financed directly by the budget, largely for addressing problems arising from urban-based pollutants.  

# 2. The program for 1995  

A cut in money supply growth from 46 percent in 1994 to 14 percent in 1995 lies at the core of the Government's anti-inflation policy." However, the inflationary repercussions of the rapid growth in broad money in late 1994 and early l995 will likely continue to be felt during much of the first half of 1995, and inflation performance will also be affected by the costpush impact of the tax measures announced in the l995 budget. The program targets an inflation rate of just under 20 percent for 1995, which is based on the conservative assumption of a strong increase in velocity. l/ If inflationary expectations are rapidly dampened and the velocity assumption is not borne out, then inflation performance should be correspondingly stronger than presently envisaged. The stabilization program also provides for an overall fiscal surplus of 1.2 percent of GDP, or a broadly balanced budget when divestiture receipts are excluded. This improvement in the public finances will help release the resources needed to support the program's investment target and, together with continued adherence to a flexible exchange rate policy, should contribute to a further narrowing of the external current account deficit in 1995 and a reduction in Ghana's debt service burden.  

# a. Fiscal policy  

The reduction envisaged in the overall budget deficit for l995 amounts to 2.8 percentage points of GDP, excluding divestiture proceeds. Most of the revenue effort needed to achieve this. target will come from strengthened customs collections, reflecting the introduction in the budget of an import duty of 10 percent on about one half of the goods that were previously zerorated or exempt from tax. 2/ This measure complements a constitutional provision that no discretionary duty exemptions may be granted without parliamentary approval. The budget also raised the rate of the sales tax, from l5 percent to 17.5 percent. 3/ Subsequently, on March 1, the sales tax was converted into a VAT and its coverage extended to include retail sales and a range of services, such as electricity supply, telecommunications, and professional services. To provide a safety margin in the program, however, the fiscal projections do not incorporate the impact on revenues of any broadening of the tax base or improvement in the efficiency of tax administration that could be associated with implementation of the VAT.  

With regard to other fiscal revenues, retail petroleum prices were raised by an average of 20 percent in the budget in order to offset the impact on the effective tax rate of expected exchange rate movements during the course of 1995. From September 1995, however, the Government will impose an ad valorem tax on petroleum products and allow retail prices to be set by the oil marketing companies, which will protect duties from delays in adjusting prices to unexpected increases in oil import costs. Increases in petroleum duties will constitute contingency action in the event of slippages in fiscal policy performance during l995. Despite a larger cocoa crop and a real depreciation of the currency, tax receipts from cocoa are expected to decline slightly relative to GDP in 1995 because of a 127 percent increase in producer prices at the beginning of the l994/95 crop season.  

Total government spending is targeted to be reduced by l.2 percent of GDP in l995. The budget provides for a modest reduction in the civil service wage bill relative to GDP, and the Government will strive to ensure that the l995 pay agreement with the trade unions is consistent with this target. Other economies have been identified in noninterest recurrent expenditure; these cuts are reflected in quarterly ministerial allocations and are expected to generate savings of some 1.3 percent of GDP. The repayment of debts owed by state enterprises will also help improve expenditure performance. l/ However, domestic interest payments are expected to continue to rise as a result of increased net sales of central bank debt, the interest on which is borne by the Government. 2/  

Arrears to domestic suppliers were incurred on development spending during 1992-93. An effort to quantify these arrears is proceeding, but the exercise has proven difficult in the absence of a properly functioning system of expenditure monitoring at the commitment level. In 1994 a pilot project was established in several key ministries with a view to strengthening the tracking and control of expenditure commitments and reconciling cash spending with bank statements. This system will be extended to all line ministries during the course of 1995. In the meantime, the Government will make every effort to avoid accumulating new arrears, and funds have been allocated in the budget for partial payment of outstanding amounts.  

Proceeds from the sale of public assets are a nonrecurrent source of fiscal revenue and represent an important part of the country's stock of wealth. 3/ As a baseline projection, the 1995 program assumes that $\mathtt{U S S73}$ million in proceeds will be received from the sale of public assets, although actual receipts are likely to exceed this projection by a large margin. 4/ The program provides for any excess divestiture receipts over projections to be used for the repayment of domestic or external debt or the acquisition of foreign assets. The program's fiscal, monetary, and balance of payments targets will be adjusted appropriately, and the Government's  

interest payments could be somewhat less than presently projected as a result.  

b. Monetary policy and financial sector reforms  

The repayment of domestic debt by the Government will be critical in helping the Bank of Ghana in its efforts to curtail monetary growth, while ensuring that sufficient bank credit is made available to the nongovernment sector. The central bank intends to expand the scale of its open market operations in order to ensure that the targets established for its net domestic asset position are observed, and it is prepared to allow interest rates to rise in the short run, if necessary. The Bank of Chana has already raised the rediscount rate by 6 percentage points since the beginning of this year to 39 percent.  

Virtually all the expansion in broad money in l994 was attributable to the impact on the monetary base of borrowing by GNPC from the Bank of Ghana. This source of funding has now been cut off and a ceiling placed on GNPC's indebtedness to the central bank; observance of this ceiling constitutes a performance criterion under the program. Until September 1995, by which time GNPC will have ceased its oil trading operations, the company will need to borrow approximately $\yen123,456,7$ million to finance the importation of oil and the repayment of existing oil credits. The program has assumed that this borrowing will be undertaken domestically, but in the event of external financing, the Bank of Ghana's net foreign and domestic asset targets will be adjusted accordingly.  

The public's holdings of currency relative to bank deposits rose significantly in l994, reflecting in part the exceptionally large increase in cocoa producer prices. The program is built on the expectation of a decline in the currency/deposit ratio during l995, as farmers' cocoa earnings find their way back into the banking system. As a consequence, reserve money expansion will need to be limited to $4$ percent in 1995. The central bank is aware that it will have to monitor movements in the money multiplier very carefully in the coming months, and will tighten monetary policy if it appears that the currency/deposit ratio has fallen below the levels envisaged in the program.  

The first phase of the reform of Ghana's financial sector, which consisted of a restructuring of the commercial banks' nonperforming assets and a strengthening of their capital base, is now essentially complete. The second phase is under way, and it aims to promote the process of financial intermediation and to increase competition through privatization and the licensing of new banks. In this connection, the divestiture of 60 percent of the Government's share in three commercial banks is scheduled to be completed by March l996. l/ The Government also plans to divest its  

ownership in the country's largest insurance company. The activities of the Ghana Stock Exchange (GSE), which provides a means of raising equity capital for 17 publicly traded companies, were boosted in 1994 with the local issue of shares from the sale of the Government's stake in Ashanti Goldfields. The GSE seeks to deepen the equity market by floating shares in newly divested public enterprises, and recent interest has been expressed by domestic and foreign investors in participating in local bond financing.  

# “ Deregulation of the petroleum and cocoa sectors  

GNPC's serious financial problems reflect losses on oil trading operations and investments undertaken outside its oil-trading activities. These problems prompted the Government to review the issue of state involvement in the procurement, refining, and pricing of petroleum, and to develop an action plan to deregulate the sector. Once an in-depth examination of the financial operations of GNPC has been completed, which is scheduled for June l995, the company's operations will be confined to hydrocarbon- and energy-related exploration activities. GNPC will be relieved of all other functions, and its assets sold in order to repay outstanding debts, including those to the Bank of Ghana. After September l995, the private oil marketing companies will be permitted to purchase crude oil for processing at the Tema Oil Refinery, and they will also be allowed to import refined products when demand exceeds the viable capacity of the refinery. In preparation for its sale, the refinery will b run on a commercial basis under a private management contract. The stateowned oil marketing company, GOIL, which accounts for one third of oil distribution, will also be privatized.  

In principle, GNPC is authorized to set petroleum prices at the retail level, but in practice this function has been assumed by the Government. As a consequence, the price-setting process has become politicized, and the resulting price rigidity has weakened the Government's revenue performance and contributed to a squeeze on marketing margins. It is the Government's intention to fully liberalize the pricing of petroleum products by June l996, and allow retail prices to be set by the oil companies in accordance with prevailing market conditions. The only constraint on pricing behavior will be the requirement that each company maintain a geographically uniform schedule of prices. In the meantime, to ensure a smooth transition to the new system, retail prices will be set by the oil companies, and monitored by the Government, on the basis of a formula that automatically passes through changes in oil import costs. The deregulation process will be accompanied by the adoption of a legal framework designed to safeguard fair trading practices and maintain adequate environmental, health, and safety standards.  

The process of deregulating cocoa marketing is well under way. Since March l992 private traders have been permitted to compete directly with the Cocoa Board in the purchase of cocoa from farmers; during the 1994/95 crop season, they purchased some 25 percent of the crop. Furthermore, the Cocoa Board is implementing a downsizing and cost-cutting program, which has  

involved a reduction of over 40 percent in its labor force in the past two years and the sale of some of its concerns, including a cocoa processing factory. These actions have been accompanied by a significant strengthening of producer price incentives, and have contributed to a jump in the share of cocoa export revenue received by farmers to around 50 percent in l995 from 29 percent last year.  

This progress notwithstanding, there remains much room for further efficiency gains in cocoa marketing. The access of private traders to local bank credit has been constrained by sectoral exposure limits, which the banks use to guide their lending behavior. l/ Moreover, the Cocoa Board continues to fix delivery prices and to enjoy a monopoly over exports of the crop. To advance the decontrol process, the Government will complete by end-September 1995 a study to review the deregulation experience in other countries and formulate the modalities and safeguards necessary to ensure success of the deregulation exercise. The Cocoa Board will be divested of its export monopoly by end-March l996, once the next crop purchasing season is completed. The objective will be to create a "level playing field\* in cocoa marketing and, depending on the outcome of the study, the Cocoa Board may be allowed to continue to participate in the purchase of the crop until the private sector becomes fully established. It will not have any powers to license private traders. The Cocoa Board will also retain its quality control function in order to ensure that Ghana's cocoa continues to command a substantial premium in the international market; this function, however, may also be undertaken in conjunction with private sector bodies.  

3. Balance of payments and external financing  

# a. Outlook and financing reguirements for l995-97  

The strengthening of the public finances in l994 led to a significant reduction in the external current account deficit and debt service burden. The program seeks to narrow the current account deficit (after official transfers) further, to 3.7 percent of GDP in l995 and to 2.7 percent by 1997. Export proceeds are expected to grow by about l2 percent a year in U.S. dollar terms during l995-97. 2/ This should permit a sufficient

The balance of payments projections for l995 assume a recovery in longterm concessional assistance under multilateral and bilateral aid programs, largely reflecting the carry-over of undisbursed commitments from 1994. However, the reduction in the current account deficit will enable Ghana to become less dependent on external assistance over the medium-term, particularly that extended on less concessional terms; total long- and medium-term inflows are expected to decline from the equivalent of 9 percent of GDP to less than 7 percent between l995 and 1999. Similarly, multilateral debt, including that to the Fund, should drop from 59 percent of GDP in 1994 to 52 percent by l999; and the debt service ratio should decline from 27 percent to 18 percent during this period. l/ Debt service to the Fund is projected to peak at under 10 percent in 1997 and to fall rapidly thereafter. The capital account forecasts also incorporate the impact of the winding down of the divestiture program, but anticipate a strong pick-up in direct investment. It is assumed that the cocoa crop will continue to be financed by external borrowing, but that there will be no new oil credits. 2/  

At the end of l994, the Government's external payments arrears to official and private creditors amounted to ${\tt U S S92}$ million. These arrears were incurred over several years and largely reflect technical factors rather than balance of payments difficulties. 3/ The Government recognizes that the regularization of financial relations with its external creditors is necessary to ensure unimpeded access to new credits. Just over half of the arrears are owed to a single official creditor, and discussions on the rescheduling of this debt are expected to be completed shortly. The remainder will be cleared through cash payments, the bulk of which will be made in 1995, and the program includes an injunction against the accumulation of new external arrears. 4/ Ghana does not need, or intend to seek, debt relief under the auspices of the Paris Club.  

Ghana's foreign exchange reserves are projected to rise to the equivalent of 4.5 months of imports by end-l995 and to 5.6 months by end-l997. While this represents a healthy international liquidity position, Ghana's reserves include funds from sources such as the divestiture program and short-term Cocoa Board borrowing, which, strictly speaking, should not be regarded as freely available for balance of payments financing purposes. l/ On the basis of disbursements under commitments indicated by Ghana's donors and creditors, the financing requirement during the first annual ESAF arrangement is projected to be fully covered. The balance of payments projections for l996-97 incorporate the volume of aid disbursements that Ghana can reasonably expect to receive, although not all these funds have been committed. 2/ On this basis, a relatively small gap is expected for l996-97, and commitments to fill this gap, and to cover the remainder of Ghana's financing needs, will be sought in the context of a Consultative Group meeting scheduled for mid-June (Table 9).  

# b. Longer-term outlook and capacity to repay the Fund  

The current account deficit is expected to continue to decline beyond the program period, to 1.9 percent of GDP by 2000. This forecast is based on the assumption that export prices rise with world inflation and that volumes grow by about 6 percent a year, which is just slightly higher than the growth rate projected for real GDP. Nontraditional exports are forecast to increase their share in total exports from l6 percent in 1994 to 23 percent by 20oo. On the expectation that the Government's efforts to raise public and private savings and promote import substitution activities are successful, the volume of imports is projected to grow by a little less than real GDP during this period. The Government's stabilization program and the effort to provide an environment conducive to private initiative should provide the basis for a strong increase in foreign investment, and this impact is reflected in the projections. It has been assumed that foreign reserves will reach the equivalent of almost seven months of imports in 2000. The time paths envisaged for the current account, direct investment, and foreign reserves should allow Ghana to considerably reduce its dependence on foreign aid and nonconcessional borrowing, and should permit: a moderate reduction in the external debt service burden (Table 10).  

Ghana's external prospects nevertheless remain somewhat vulnerable to the concentration of export earnings in relatively few primary commodities and to the continuing, albeit diminishing, dependence on foreign aid. This vulnerability underscores the importance for Ghana of building up a healthy stock of foreign reserves during the program period. # IV. Program Monitoring and Technical Assistance  

Performance under the first annual ESAF arrangement will be monitored on the basis of the following financial performance criteria and benchmarks (a) the net domestic assets of the Bank of Ghana, with subceilings on claims on the Cocoa Board and the GNPC; (b) net domestic financing of the government budget; (c) the net foreign assets of the Bank of Ghana; (d) new nonconcessional external borrowing of l-l2 years' maturity, with a subceiling on debt of l-5 years' maturity; (e) the stock of external short-term debt; and (f) the elimination of external payments arrears, and an injunction against the incurrence of new external arrears (Table 13). Structural performance criteria and benchmarks will include the following: (a) cocoa producer price adjustments; (b) a timetable for the termination of the Cocoa Board's export monopoly; (c) a timetable for the deregulation of the pricing and marketing of petroleum products; and (d) progress in implementing the divestiture program (Table 14).  

Benchmarks will apply to end-June l995 and performance criteria to end-September 1995. Indicative benchmarks have been established for end-December l995. The second disbursement under the first annual ESAF arrangement will be conditional upon observance of the performance criteria at end-September l995 and completion of the midterm review, which will take place no later than December l5, 1995. The review will set indicative benchmarks for end-March and end-June 1996 and pay particular attention to the progress being made in deregulating the cocoa and petroleum sectors, including the sale of GNPC assets. Delays have been experienced in the past in the furnishing of data, and the Government has undertaken to ensure that all data necessary for monitoring performance under the program will be collected and made available on a timely basis (see Appendix V).  

Ghana is making use of technical assistance from the World Bank, the Fund, and a number of other multilateral and bilateral agencies to help supplement its capacity to implement macroeconomic and structural reforms. Several ongoing projects provide funding for technical support, primarily in the areas of fiscal and parastatal management, financial sector development, and private sector development. With regard to budget execution, technical support is being utilized for the implementation of the VAT, expenditure management and control systems, and poverty analysis to determine effective expenditure targeting. Technical assistance has also been directed at monetary policy and foreign exchange operations. Additional technical assistance requirements have been identified in a number of areas. These  

include the implementation of the divestiture program; strengthening nonbank financial institutions, such as the stock exchange, insurance, and pension funds; implementation of public sector reforms, including contracting out of public services and the civil service restructuring program; and the promotion of investment in nontraditional exports. A number of World Bank and other donor technical assistance projects are being initiated and coordinated to cover these needs.  

# V. Staff Appraisal  

Ghana's economic reform program suffered a serious setback in 1992, when major slippages in fiscal policy emerged during the run-up to the first multiparty elections under the new constitution. Since then, the Government has made efforts to correct the slippages and bring economic performance back on track. The results have been rather mixed, however, and the macroeconomic situation remains fragile. Fiscal and external imbalances were reduced during l993-94, thanks in part to higher cocoa prices and receipts from the sale of public assets, and Ghana's international reserve position improved significantly, despite shortfalls in external assistance. On the other hand, acute financial difficulties experienced by the national oil company (GNPC) led to a rapid expansion in the money supply in 1994, and inflationary pressures intensified as a result. Private saving and investment remained weak, and economic growth performance was uneven.  

The Government is intent on pushing forward with the process of reform, and has formulated an economic program for l995-97, which seeks to establish a low inflation environment conducive to private sector initiative and foreign investment. The removal of state involvement in activities that can be better conducted by the private sector will be critical to achieving the objectives of raising per capita GDP growth to 3 percent by the end of the decade and accelerating the process of poverty alleviation. In support of its adjustment efforts, Ghana has requested financial assistance from the Fund in the form of a three-year ESAF arrangement. The staff believes that the Government's program embodies objectives and policies that represent a realistic and well-designed strategy for addressing existing macroeconomic imbalances and structural distortions, and as such it warrants the full support of the international financial community. There remain, however, several areas of program implementation where careful monitoring by the Government is called for, and where timely corrective action will have to be taken if necessary.  

First, labor unrest was reported earlier this year, and the industrial relations situation may deteriorate further as the trade unions attempt to recoup real earnings lost as a result of recent increases in inflation. The Government must take a strong stand in resisting excessive pay demands in the civil service, not only to maintain strict fiscal discipline, but also to help contain labor costs in the parastatal and private sectors and protect Ghana's international competitiveness. Presidential and parliamentary elections are scheduled for l996, and any repetition of the  

pay awards granted under similar circumstances in 1992 could seriously undermine the adjustment process. Receipts from the sale of public assets, which are expected to be considerable, represent a nonrecurrent source of revenue and should not be used for funding higher civil service pay, or any other expenditure overrun. The Government intends to restructure the civil service and improve pay relativities once the deliberations of a high-level committee examining the issue have been completed. The staff would urge that the forthcoming elections not be allowed to interfere with the design of the civil service restructuring program or the speed of its implementation.  

Second, the program has set aside budgetary funds for the repayment of arrears on capital projects that have been incurred vis-a-vis domestic and foreign suppliers. The Government should make every effort to settle these debts as quickly as possible on a nondiscriminatory basis. In this connection, the staff welcomes the progress that has been made in installing machinery for the tracking and control of government expenditure on a commitment basis. High priority should be attached to the completion of this exercise for all ministries in l995. With regard to fiscal revenue, the staff would urge the Government to move forward with its intention of reducing dependence on cocoa export taxes as quickly as possible.  

Third, at the time of the l994 Article IV consultation, Executive Directors called upon the Government to decontrol petroleum prices. This action now constitutes an important element of the plan to deregulate the petroleum sector, the political consensus for which has been galvanized by the gravity of the GNPC's financial difficulties. The success of the deregulation program will depend on its being implemented expeditiously. The transfer of the GNPC's oil trading functions to the private sector and the liquidation of its assets will be key mileposts in this regard. The process of deregulation has been designed to dovetail with the divestiture program, progress under which will be critical in establishing the environment needed for private sector initiative to flourish.  

Finally, the central bank will need to pay close attention to movements in the money multiplier, which has been subject to relatively large swings in recent months. The central bank should be prepared to tighten monetary policy in the event that the multiplier exceeds the levels presently envisaged in the program.  

During the early stages of the program at least, the exchange rate will continue to be directed at achieving the overall balance of payments targets, while monetary policy will be assigned the task of bringing inflation under control. This policy configuration should allow the program's stabilization objectives to be met, as well as enable Ghana to establish a comfortable cushion of foreign reserves and maintain its external competitiveness. Once substantive and demonstratable progress has been made in stabilizing prices and building up reserves, the Government will consider as a possible option use of the exchange rate as a nominal anchor to keep inflation down. The staff supports this strategy, but would emphasize that it is the strength and credibility of financial policies that will be critical in determining the success of the stabilization program, irrespective of whether money or the exchange rate is adopted as the anchor for the price level.  

While the successful completion of the program supported by an ESAF arrangement in March l992 should have placed Ghana in a potentially strong position to continue to attract concessional aid and non-debt-creating inflows, slippages in policy implementation have led to growing resistance on the part of donors to extend the necessary financing. The new ESAF arrangement seeks to strengthen Ghana's external position as a basis for achieving enhanced and sustainable growth. In doing so, it should boost donor and creditor confidence in Ghana's economic program, and help catalyze the concessional aid and direct investment needed to cover Ghana's external financing requirements for l995-97. These considerations, together with Ghana's present level of indebtedness to the Fund and past use of ESAF funds, is reflected in the level of access to Fund resources being sought under the arrangement. The access level is also intended to send a clear signal to the international financial community that renewed Fund assistance does not indicate a major relapse in economic performance, and that Ghana is resuming with determination its efforts to maintain the momentum of economic reform. On the basis of financial commitments already made, the staff considers that there is sufficient assurance that Ghana's financing needs during the first annual ESAF arrangement will be covered. Resource requirements for the remainder of the arrangement will be met at the forthcoming Consultative Group meeting. The staff would urge that the remaining external payments arrears be cleared on a timely basis.  

Ghana's long-term balance of payments prospects depend on the success of policies to promote export growth and diversification and to mobilize the savings and investment needed to reduce the dependence on aid, including that extended by multilateral institutions. Sensitivity tests based upon various export and aid flow scenarios indicate that the external position is relatively robust, although risks do remain. Overall, the staff does not expect Ghana to experience any major difficulties in meeting its debt service obligations, including those to the Fund.  

It is recommended that Ghana remain on the standard 12-month consultation cycle.

# Contingency Mechanisms  

# 1 Excess net foreign financing - balance of payments.  

The floor on the net foreign assets (NFA) is to be adjusted upuard, and the ceiling on net domestic assets (NDA) downward, by 50 percent of excess net foreign financing (nonproject loans minus cash debt service payments passing through the budget) over the amounts envisaged in the program.  

# 2. Excess net foreign financing - budget  

The ceiling on net domestic financing of the budget (NDF) will be lowered by the excess of net foreign financing (nonproject loans minus cash debt service payments passing through the budget) over the amounts envisaged in the program.  

# 3. Other external borrowing  

NFA will be adjusted upward, and NDA downward, by any net oil credits and any net external borrowing by the Cocoa Board in excess of the amounts envisaged in the program.  

# 4. Divestiture proceeds  

In the case of proceeds from the sale of public assets that are not used for the purchase of foreign investments by the Government or the prepayment of nonconcessional medium-term external debt, the following adjustments will apply. NFA will be adjusted upuard, and NDA dounward, by any divestiture proceeds received in foreign exchange above the amounts assumed in the program.NDF will be adjusted downward by the total amount of divestiture proceeds received, in foreign exchange and local currency, above the amounts assumed in the program.

# I. Introduction and Background  

1.  The Government of Ghana is intent on quickly stabilizing the economy and laying the basis for accelerated economic growth and poverty alleviation. In support of these objectives, the Government is seeking financial assistance from the Fund in the form of a three-year ESAF arrangement. The program to be supported by the ESAF arrangement has been cast within a medium-term framework for l995-97, which is described in a policy framework paper. The macroeconomic and structural objectives and policies for the first year of the program beginning April 1, 1995 are set out in this memorandum.  

2. During l983-91, Ghana successfully implemented an economic recovery program, which was supported by a series of SAF and ESAF arrangements. However, slippages in policy implementation occurred during the run-up to multiparty elections in l992, and the overall budgetary position reverted from a surplus of l.5 percent of GDP in l99l to a deficit of 4.8 percent. The Government has since made strenuous efforts to correct these slippages. As a result of the receipt of some 5 percent of GDP in proceeds from the sale of public assets and strong performance in the collection of income and import taxes, the budget recorded a surplus of 2.2 percent of GDP in 1994. Despite the improvement in the public finances, the Bank of Ghana experienced difficulties in controlling monetary growth because of a sharp deterioration in the financial position of the national petroleum company (GNPC), which necessitated extensive borrowing from the central bank. As a consequence, the growth of broad money rose sharply, and the annual rate of inflation rose to 34 percent by the end of 1994.  

3. In order to protect its international reserve position in the face of mounting inflationary pressures, the Bank of Ghana allowed the cedi to depreciate in real terms. The demand-dampening effect of this exchange rate policy, together with favorable export performance, contributed to a contraction in the external current account deficit, from 9.2 percent of GDP in l993 to 4.9 percent in 1994. Ghana experienced a large shortfall in long-term concessional loan inflows during l994, arising largely from policy-related difficulties encountered by the Government in meeting macroeconomic and structural conditions under World Bank and bilateral aid programs. This shortfall was covered by proceeds from the divestiture program and prefinancing of cocoa exports.  

4. Despite strong gains in the mining and services sectors, poor performance elsewhere as a result of the macroeconomic slippages, inadequate rains, and the impact of the ethnic disturbances in the northern regions, prompted some slackening in economic growth from 5.0 percent in 1993 to 3.8 percent in 1994.  

# II. Policy Framework for 1995-97  

5. The economic recovery program achieved a significant measure of success in correcting macroeconomic imbalances and structural distortions. Private sector saving and investment remained comparatively weak, however, and real GDP growth averaged only about 5 percent a year, or 2 percent on a per capita basis. At this rate, Ghanaian average poor would not cross the "poverty line\* for well over 30 years. Consequently, the central objective of the Government's economic adjustment program for l995-97 will be to generate the saving and investment necessary to raise real growth to 5.5 percent by l997 and to around 6 percent by the end of the decade. Inflation is targeted to fall to 5 percent in 1996 and beyond, which would allow Ghana to maintain a stable and predictable exchange rate, while enjoying a strong externally competitive position.  

6. The strategy envisaged will be based on expenditure-dampening policies aimed at bringing down inflation and reducing Ghana's external debt service burden. Private and public savings alone, however, are unlikely to generate the volume of investment necessary for enhanced growth, while there is little prospect of any significant increase in foreign aid from the levels recorded in the early 1990s. The program will therefore focus on attracting additional foreign investment and on policies designed to improve the quality of investment itself.  

7. The Government is of the view that, while much has been accomplished in removing impediments to investment, incentives to save and invest can be further strengthened by the dissemination of clear signals by the Government concerning the role it expects the private sector to play in promoting economic growth. The medium-term policy framework therefore lays out a plan of action for the withdrawal of state involvement in all areas of economic activity that can be better handled by the private sector. Specific action focuses on an acceleration of the divestiture program and on deregulation of the petroleum and cocoa sectors, while the economic functions that remain in the public domain will be streamlined and rendered more efficient.  

# III. Economic Program for 1995  

8.The most pressing economic policy goal facing the Government is to quickly stabilize the economy and bring the rate of inflation firmly under control. Further fiscal adjustment and a considerable tightening of monetary policy will be undertaken to achieve this goal. However, in view of the strong expansion in the money supply that occurred toward the end of  

1994 and the entrenched state of inflationary expectations, the rate of inflation is expected to continue to rise during the first few months of this year. The rate of inflation in l995 will, moreover, be affected by the cost-push impact of various tax increases announced in the budget. Realistically, therefore, it is unlikely that inflation can be reduced to much below 20 percent (on an end-of-period basis) in l995. Real GDP growth is expected to recover to 5.0 percent in 1995.  

9. In support of these targets, the program seeks to achieve a fiscal surplus of 1.2 percent of GDP in l995 (or a broadly balanced budget, excluding divestiture proceeds) and a sharp reduction in money supply growth, to l4 percent from 46 percent in l994. This tight demand-management stance, in conjunction with a flexible exchange rate policy, should allow for a further narrowing of the external current account deficit and a replenishment of Ghana's foreign exchange reserves. Important structural policy initiatives will be undertaken in the petroleum and cocoa sectors.  

# (i) Financial policies  

10. To meet the overall fiscal target, the budget for l995 announced a range of measures aimed at raising revenues, excluding divestiture proceeds and grants, to 19.l percent of GDP from 18.4 percent in l994. An import duty of 1O percent was imposed on about one half of the goods that were previously zero rated or exempt from tax. The rate of sales tax was lifted from 15 to l7.5 percent and, on March l, expanded into a value-added tax (VAT) that covers the retail and services sectors. l/ In addition, petroleum prices were raised by an average of over 20 percent in order to pass through changes in world oil prices and the exchange rate, while various user fees and charges were also increased.  

1l. Economies have been identified that will enable government expenditures to be cut by about l percent of GDP in l995. As announced in the budget, the civil service wage bill will be allowed to rise by no more than 25 percent, which would represent a modest contraction relative to GDP. Savings will also be forthcoming in other areas of recurrent spending, and these are reflected in ministerial allocations. Domestic interest payments, however, are expected to rise as a result of increased open market operations by the Bank of Ghana. 2/ Measures are also being taken in the critical area of expenditure control. In l994, a pilot project was established in several key ministries, aimed at strengthening expenditure monitoring and control mechanisms at the commitment level and reconciling  

cash spending with bank statements; the pilot project proved successful and will be extended in l995 to all other line ministries. 1/  

12. The Government recognizes that proceeds from the sale of public assets are a nonrecurrent source of fiscal revenue and believes strongly that the divestiture program should not lead to any depletion of the nation's stock of wealth. Consequently, while the budget for l995 provides for the use of a specified amount of divestiture proceeds for budgetary purposes, any receipts in excess of this amount will be used for domestic or external debt reduction or for the acquisition of foreign assets. The program's fiscal and balance of payments targets will be adjusted accordingly.  

13. Monetary control will continue to be effected through open market operations, which are conducted by the central bank in the form of a weekly auction of official debt. The central bank intends to reduce its reliance on the use of a reserve price in the auction. In the short run, liquid asset requirements will continue to be kept at a high level as a second line of attack in containing credit expansion, but the intention will be to subsequently lower the requirements to levels where they are needed for prudential purposes only. To encourage the development of a secondary market, the central bank is now rediscounting bankers' acceptances. Meeting the program's monetary targets in l995 will require a considerable expansion in open market sales by the central bank, and this may entail increases in interest rates, at least in the short term. 2/ Despite the pick-up in inflation in l994, lending rates have remained broadly positive in real terms; the maintenance of positive real rates is a critical element in the: effort to restrain aggregate demand, mobilize private savings, and encourage net inflows of capital.  

14. The borrowing by GNPC from the central bank has largely been a consequence of poor investments undertaken in areas outside the petroleum sector. This borrowing was the major cause of the rapid monetary expansion that occurred in l994, and substantive steps are being taken to address GNPC's financial problems. These steps are elaborated below.  

# (ii) Structural and sectoral policies  

15. Macroeconomic stabilization alone will not in itself guarantee accelerated growth in Ghana, and continuing structural reform will be necessary to limit state involvement in economic decision making and to provide strong market-based incentives for private sector initiative. Considerable progress has already been achieved since l983 in removing structural impediments to growth: the country's economic infrastructure has been rehabilitated and expanded; all commodity price and credit controls (except on petroleum products and cocoa) have been lifted; no quantitative restrictions on trade remain; the import tariff structure has been rationalized; and the exchange system is free of current restrictions. However, while these actions have exposed the economy to greater competition both at home and from abroad, obstacles to greater factor mobility and improved resource mobilization have persisted, particularly with regard to capital investment. As a first step in removing these obstacles, a new investment code was recently introduced to provide investors with a clear and simplified regulatory framework and with incentives aimed at stimulating the pace of foreign and domestic investment.  

16. The sale of Ashanti Goldfields in $1994$ constitutes the successful conclusion of the single most important element of the Government's divestiture program. The remainder of the program, however, has proceeded somewhat slowly, and the Government intends to accelerate the pace of divestiture in l995. l/ With this in mind, it has introduced procedures with assistance from the World Bank whereby foreign and domestic technical expertise will be subcontracted to administer the divestiture program. In addition, the list of enterprises to be divested has been expanded (more banks have recently been included in the list), and the Government has accorded particular priority to the quick sale or liquidation of a large number (over l00) of small and medium-size enterprises, primarily in the manufacturing sector. Disbursements under World Bank lending programs are conditional upon performance in this area.  

17. The Government intends to deregulate the pricing and marketing of petro leum. In addition to the macroeconomic repercussions of GNPC's borrowing from the central bank, public management of the procurement, refining, and distribution of oil has given rise to dead-weight losses and operating inefficiencies. Administrative regulation of petroleum prices has, moreover, politicized the price-setting process, and delays in fully passing through increases in imported oil costs have resulted in losses in fiscal revenue. To redress these problems, GNPC's access to new credits at the Bank of Ghana has been terminated and its operations will be confined to hydrocarbons and energy-related activities. Its assets will be sold to repay outstanding debts, including those to the Bank of Ghana. 2/ GNPC has also been placed on the list of companies to be privatized. The oil marketing companies will be free to import crude oil for processing by the state-owned oil refinery and to import products in excess of the refinery's capacity. The refinery will be operated on a commercial basis in preparation for its divestiture. During a transitional period ending June 1996, retail petroleum prices will be subject to a ceiling, which will be adjusted in line with changes in world oil prices and the exchange rate. This will help depoliticize the price-setting process and protect government revenues from unanticipated increases in imported oil costs. After the transitional period, retail prices will be fully decontrolled.  

18. Much has been accomplished recently in removing impediments to the efficient functioning of cocoa marketing. Private traders are now allowed to compete with the Cocoa Board for the purchase of the cocoa crop, while the Cocoa Board itself has made considerable economies in its operating expenses. However, the access of private traders to commercial bank credit has been limited, and only around 25 percent of the crop is being traded privately. l/ To provide scope for further efficiency gains in cocoa marketing, the Government intends to remove the Cocoa Board's export monopoly, upon the findings of a government review to study the modalities of deregulation, which will be completed by September l995. The Cocoa Board will continue to participate in the marketing and export of cocoa until private sector traders become fully established. The quality control function will remain in the public domain.  

19. Emoluments to civil servants amount to over 5 percent of GDP. At the same time, uncompetitive pay levels in the civil service have contributed to difficulties in attracting and retaining quality staff in managerial and executive positions. In recognition of these problems, the Government has set up a high-level National Oversight Committee (NOC) with a mandate to implement institutional reforms aimed at increasing the efficiency of the public service and downsizing and restructuring the civil service and subvented organizations. The decisions of the NOC will be based on the findings of an integrated personnel and payroll management system, which is presently being used to review the structure of the civil service salary grades. They will be implemented beginning in early 1996.  

20. Under new industrial relations legislation to be considered shortly by Parliament, wage settlements negotiated with civil service trade unions on a two-year cycle will be delinked from wage awards settlements in the nongovernment sector. In January 1995, the minimum wage was increased by 52 percent, although this should not affect pay levels in the formal sector. During the policy framework period, minimum wage policy will be guided by the need to ensure that wage levels in Ghana remain competitive and compatible with the quest for increased investment and growth.  

2l. Government will reorganize the subvented agencies with a view to eliminating those agencies performing nonessential functions and contracting out those functions that can be performed more efficiently by private vendors on a commercial basis. The financial operations of those state enterprises that remain in public hands are being strengthened under a  

performance contract system. Rather than depend on government investment, lending, or loan guarantees, these enterprises will now be expected to generate funds from internal sources, the sale of equity, or the floatation of corporate bonds in domestic and foreign capital markets.  

# (iii) External sector policies and financing  

22. Ghana will continue to pursue a flexible exchange rate policy in 1995, whereby net sales of foreign exchange by the Bank of Ghana are guided by the need to meet its foreign reserve targets. A review of this strategy will be undertaken at the time the program for l996 is formulated. If sufficient progress has been made in adhering to tight financial policies and lowering inflation, and if this progress is considered credible and sustainable, consideration will be given to using the exchange rate as an instrument to hold down inflation; monetary policy would be directed at meeting the overall balance of payments objectives.  

23. Various tariff rate adjustments have been made in recent years, which have led to a reduction in the effective rate of protection. Other countries in the region have not moved as quickly as Ghana in undertaking tariff reform and, in view of this, the Government believes that the existing level and dispersion of tariffs is appropriate for the time being. In 1994, a system of minimum presumptive duties was applied to a specific list of imports in order to address problems in the underinvoicing of imports and other areas of customs collection. This system will be retained until the Government is satisfied that the quality of preshipment inspection has been raised to satisfactory standards and that the imports concerned attract the correct amount of duty. This should be accomplished well within the program period.  

24. The program envisages further progress being made in attaining a viable external position over the medium term. The current account deficit is expected to narrow to 3.0 percent by 1997, from 4.9 percent in l994, in response to a strengthening in the public finances, the mobilization of additional private saving, and favorable market conditions for Ghana's exports. This should permit a diminution of Ghana's debt service burden over this period and a modest decline in external interest payments relative to export earnings from 8 percent to 7 percent.  

25. The balance of payments projections incorporate conservative assumptions regarding external financing from multilateral and bilateral sources, and are also based on the expectation that the cocoa crop will continue to be financed externally. The program provides for the repayment or rescheduling on a bilateral basis of external arrears that have been incurred by the Government against certain bilateral creditors; these arrears were incurred for technical reasons and do not reflect balance of payments difficulties. The Government attaches the highest priority to the clearance of these arrears as a means of ensuring access to new credits from bilateral sources. No new external arrears will be incurred. Ghana will not seek debt relief under the auspices of the Paris Club. The program  

will target an increase in gross reserves to just over five months by the end of l997. The entire amount of these reserves should not be regarded as freely usable, however, since the equivalent of over $13$ months of imports represents proceeds from the divestiture program and continued short term external borrowing by the Cocoa Board. l/ Ghana's external financing requirements during the period of the first annual ESAF arrangement are expected to be covered on the basis of disbursements under aid commitments already made. 2/ A modest financing gap is envisaged for l996-97, which is expected to be filled in the context of a Consultative Group meeting to be held in mid-l995.  

26. The ESAF arrangement will play a primarily catalytic role in mobilizing the bilateral funds necessary to support Ghana's adjustment efforts, and this consideration has governed the level of access to ESAF resources that the Government is requesting. The Government is also intent on ensuring that the level of access being sought in no way suggests that there has been a major relapse in Ghana's economic policy performance.  

# IV. Program Monitoring  

27. Performance under the first annual ESAF arrangement will be monitored on the basis of the following financial performance criteria and benchmarks; (a) the net domestic assets of the Bank of Ghana; (b) net domestic financing of the government budget; (c) the net foreign assets of the Bank of Ghana; (d) new nonconcessional external borrowing of l-l2 years maturity, with a subceiling on debt of l-5 years' maturity; (e) the stock of external shortterm debt; and (f) the elimination of external payments arrears, and an injunction against the incurrence of new external arrears (Table 1). Structural performance criteria and benchmarks will include: (a) cocoa producer price adjustments; (b) a timetable for the termination of the Cocoa Board's export monopoly; (c) a timetable for the deregulation of the pricing and marketing of petroleum products; and (d) progress in implementing the divestiture program (Table 2).  

28. Benchmarks will apply to end-June l995 and performance criteria to end-September l995. Indicative benchmarks have been established for end-December l995. The second disbursement under the first annual arrangement will be conditional upon observance of the performance criteria at end-September 1995 and completion of the midterm review, which will take place no later than December l5, l995. All data necessary for monitoring performance under the program will be collected and made available on a timely basis. The review will set indicative benchmarks for end-March and end-June l996 and pay particular attention to the progress being made in deregulating the cocoa and petroleum sectors, including the sale of GNPC assets.

# Contingency Mechanisms  

# . Excess net foreign financing - balance of payments.  

The floor on the net foreign assets (NFA) is to be adjusted upward, and the ceiling on net domestic assets (NDA) downward, by 50 percent of excess net foreign financing (nonproject loans minus cash debt service payments passing through the budget) over the amounts envisaged in the program.  

# 2. Excess net foreign financing - budget  

The ceiling on net domestic financing of the budget (NDF) will be lowered by the excess of net foreign financing (nonproject loans minus cash debt service payments passing through the budget) over the amounts envisaged in the program.  

# 3. Other external borrowing  

NFA will be adjusted upward, and NDA downward, by any net oil credits and any net external borrowing by the Cocoa Board in excess of the amounts envisaged in the program.  

# 4. Divestiture proceeds  

In the case of proceeds from the sale of public assets that are not used for the purchase of foreign investments by the Government or the prepayment of nonconcessional medium-term external debt, the following adjustments will apply. NFA will be adjusted upuard, and NDA downward, by any divestiture proceeds received in foreign exchange above the amounts assumed in the_program.NDF will be adjusted downward by the total amount of divestiture proceeds received, in foreign exchange and local currency, above the amounts assumed in the program.

Commentary on Lending Operations  

1. The main objective of the Bank's assistance strategy is to help the Government achieve its goals of rapid economic growth, macroeconomic stability, and poverty alleviation. There are three strategic elements in the Bank's assistance program over the medium term. The first is an emphasis on achieving enhanced growth in Ghana, through an expanded role for the private sector, and to achieve this in a way that preserves macroeconomic stability. The second is improved management of public sector resources to allow the Government to be an effective partner in promoting growth. The third is to sustain the achievements envisaged through more effective human resource development and physical resource preservation.  

2.  As of March 3l, l995, the World Bank Group had approved operations for Ghana totaling US\$3,547 million, consisting of 10 loans, 92 credits (including African Facility), and 34 IFC operations (Table attached). Over the three-year period FY 1996-98, the Bank's base case lending program for Ghana, would be ${\tt U S S790}$ million. More reliance will be put on sector adjustment credits.  

3.  Recent and proposed Bank Group projects emphasize rehabilitation, maintenance, and institutional strengthening. These cover the transport, telecommunications, energy, water, and urban sectors. Other operations are intended to increase output and efficiency in agriculture and mining. The Bank program also includes support for enterprise and technology development.  

4.  Since the formulation of Ghana's ERP in l983, the Bank has provided a series of policy-based credits to help the Government arrest the deterioration in the economy and lay the basis for sustained growth. These credits financed urgently needed imports and supported the Government's strategy for economic recovery through the phased liberalization of exchange, trade, and price controls, the rationalization of capital and recurrent public expenditures, improvements in producer prices, privatization, and parastatal reform. In FY 92 two credits were approved, a second Financial Sector Adjustment Operation, and an Agricultural Sector Adjustment Credit, to further deepen reforms in financial and agricultural sectors. Three more adjustment operations are envisaged: in support of private sector development, a Private Sector Adjustment Credit which will focus on accelerated privatization, is expected to be approved in May l995. Two other sector credits are planned to finance investments in highways, and in the health and education sectors. Increased attention is being paid to longer-term issues, including human resource development, agriculture, infrastructure, and the environment.

# General comments  

While there are some weaknesses in Ghana's overall economic statistics and certain gaps remain in coverage, the systems designed to generate monetary, balance of payments, external debt, and government finance statistics have functioned reasonably satisfactorily for the purpose of monitoring performance under successive Fund-supported programs. The areas where improvements need to be made are identified below.  

# National accounts  

Data on the output and expenditure sides of the national accounts are prepared by the Statistical Service. Annual output estimates are produced on a timely basis, but expenditure data are prepared with a lag of several months. With donor assistance, the Service is currently reviewing the methodology used in preparing expenditure data. The review aims at improving the consistency of the national accounts with the balance of payments produced by the Bank of Ghana, particularly with regard to the export and import deflators. Estimates of private savings and investment are also subject to data discrepancies that affect their consistency with other sources, such as the government accounts and the balance of payments. The methodology for compilation of the series on fixed capital formation is under review.  

# Prices and labor costs  

Comprehensive consumer price index (CPI) statistics are available with reasonable currentness. However, the weights presently used in the CPI basket are based on a l974 survey. The Statistical Service has conducted several Living Standards Surveys (l987/88, 1988/89, and 1991/92) and it has reported that the weights in the consumption basket have not changed significantly over time. Nonetheless, a new CPI series based on revised weights is planned. The wholesale price index has been discontinued since September 1991, and no indication has been given as to when it will be resumed. Labor statistics in Ghana are produced with a considerable lag, of over two years in some cases; to reduce delays, technical assistance from UNDP and ILO is being used by the Ministry of Employment.  

# Public finance  

For purposes of monitoring on a monthly basis the main elements of the fiscal program, the timeliness of public finance statistics was improved by the introduction in l994 of a new expenditure coding system that allows data to be generated on a cash basis from the Bank of Ghana records. However, a more comprehensive breakdown of expenditures is only available with a lag of about two months. The authorities are currently introducing an expenditure tracking system which will improve the quality of expenditure data and reduce the lags involved. Expenditure data on a commitment basis are not available. Official estimates relating to expenditures financed by externally funded project aid are produced with a considerable lag and are subject to estimation errors owing to timing differences, exchange valuation, and recording of the technical assistance component. However, staff estimates of these expenditures are derived from balance of payments data. Fiscal indicators are generally available on a monthly basis, but the fiscal accounts are published only on an annual basis. Data on the core public enterprises are collected by the State Enterprise Commission, but are not published regularly. Data on performance of some key enterprises, including the Ghana National Petroleum Corporation, are not available. However, for some enterprises a partial indication of their financial position can be derived from their outstanding credit balances with the banking system.  

# Monetary data  

Monetary data are compiled routinely, and the timeliness of the Bank of Ghana balance sheet is good. Trial balance data are available weekly, and end-of-month data are reported to the Fund with a lag of 5-1O days. Monthly commercial bank accounts are reported with a lag of one month. Following long-term technical assistance from the Fund's Statistics Department, the compilation of the net foreign assets of the Bank of Ghana has recently showed significant improvements. The Bank of Ghana is in the process of expanding its computerization program.  

# Balance of payments and external debt  

Balance of payments estimates are compiled by the Bank of Ghana and are only published annually. Data for exports are available on a more frequent basis, but import data are usually subject to considerable lags, owing to unreliable customs statistics. Recent computerization resulted in some improvements in the customs records, but there is scope for further improvements. External debt data are prepared by the Debt Unit at the Ministry of Finance. With technical assistance from the Commonwealth Secretariat, there have recently been significant improvements in the debt reporting system, but efforts are being made to realize further progress in this area.  

# Publication  

Reporting to the Fund for purposes of IFS publication has recently improved. However, there are still lags of 4-5 months in reporting monetary, price, and international transactions data, and the lag in reporting exchange rates is l-2 months. The frequency of publication of the Quarterly Economic Bulletin of the Bank of Ghana has recently improved, but: data are still published with lags of up to 6 months. The Quarterly Digest:  

of Statistics is usually published by the Statistical Service with a longer lag. In general, statistical information from official sources is not made available on a timely basis to the public.
