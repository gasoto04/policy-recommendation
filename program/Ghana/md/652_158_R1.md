# I. Introduction  

The l996 Article IV consultation discussions with Ghana, together with policy discussions for the midterm review under the first annual ESAF arrangement, were held between November l995 and March 1996. l/ The Fund staff missions met with Mr. Peprah, Minister of Finance; Dr. Agama, Governor of the Bank of Ghana; and other senior officials and representatives of the private sector.  

The Executive Board approved a three-year ESAF arrangement on June 30, 1995. 2/ It was originally envisaged that the midterm review under the first annual arrangement would be held by mid-December l995, but completion of policy discussions was delayed pending action to correct the slippages that had occurred in program implementation. In recommending completion of the review, this report assesses performance to date under the program and outlines the macroeconomic and structural policies to be implemented during 1996. The Government of Ghana has set out its policy intentions in the attached letter of intent and accompanying memorandum (Appendix I). These documents provide a disciplined framework to guide policies for the remainder of the year, which covers the run-up to presidential and parliamentary elections. The Government is seeking waivers for the nonobservance of three financial and two structural performance criteria for end-September 1995.  

Ghana is on the standard l2-month Article IV consultation cycle, and the last consultation was concluded on June 30, 1995. 3/ Ghana formally accepted the obligations of Article VIII, Sections 2, 3, and 4 of the Fund's  

Articles of Agreement on February 21, 1994. Ghana's relations with the Fund and the World Bank Group are summarized in Appendices II and III, respectively.  

For purposes of program monitoring, the reporting of Ghana's core macroeconomic data has generally been satisfactory, although some weaknesses in the quality and timeliness of the national accounts and fiscal data persist (see Section III and Appendix V).  

# II. Adiustment Program, 1995-97  

# 1. Overall strategy  

Despite the progress made during l983-9l in reducing macroeconomic imbalances and structural distortions under the Economic Recovery Program (ERP), inflation remained comparatively high for most of the period, while private sector saving and investment performance were weak. Real per capita GDP growth averaged 2 percent a year; at this rate Ghana's average poor would not cross the \*poverty line" for well over 30 years. l/ Accelerated growth therefore was to be the central objective of the Government's ESAF-supported program, with the growth rate rising from less than 4 percent in 1994 to 5 percent in 1995 and to 6 percent by the end of the decade. The strategy for achieving this goal was to reverse the poor record on investment by creating a sound and predictable financial environment within which Ghana can maintain a stable nominal exchange rate, while enjoying a sustainable external position. Consistent with these objectives, policies were developed with a view to reducing inflation from 30 percent in 1994 to 5 percent by 1997. The external current account deficit was targeted to contract from about 5 percent of GDP to less than 3 percent over this period.  

The economic adjustment program recognizes that savings from domestically generated sources alone will not be sufficient to generate the volume of investment needed to raise the growth rate to the desired level. Moreover, since the scope for attracting significantly larger external assistance is limited, Ghana has to concentrate on attracting considerable new inflows of foreign investment and focus on policies designed to improve the quality of investment. In support of this strategy, the program seeks to remove state involvement and intervention from those areas of economic activity that can be handled more efficiently by the private sector.  

The program for l995 was formulated against the background of intensifying inflationary pressures that had emerged in l994 and the early part of 1995.. These pressures primarily reflected excessive monetary growth, which arose as a result of slippages in fiscal policy and severe financial difficulties experienced by the Ghana National Oil Corporation (GNPC). 1/ The program therefore accorded high priority to the restoration of fiscal discipline and measures aimed at addressing GNPC's financial situation.  

During the early stages of the ESAF arrangement, delays were experienced in putting together a financial restructuring package for GNPC, and its overdraft at the central bank increased further. Monetary growth was also boosted by larger-than-expected foreign exchange inflows from gold and timber exports, and the Bank of Ghana was slow in taking measures to absorb the liquidity impact of these inflows. A concerted effort was made in September to bring the stabilization effort back on course. Steps were taken to reduce GNPC's overdraft at the central bank and cut off its access to new central bank credits, while its oil trading transactions were brought under direct control of the Ministry of Finance. In addition, open market operations by the Bank of Ghana were stepped up, the interest rate structure was allowed to rise by about l0 percentage points and, by the end of September, reserve money had been reduced to below program target. 2/ These remedial measures helped turn the corner in the fight against inflation; the annualized seasonally adjusted inflation rate fell in September to 70 percent from a peak l20 percent in July (Chart 2). Domestic financing of the government budget exceeded target at end-September, but this was entirely attributable to a shortfall in aid and divestiture receipts and did not therefore contribute to monetary expansion; this shortfall has since been reversed. 3/  

1/ The origin of GNPC's financial difficulties lies primarily in its attempts to finance various long-term projects through successive extensions in the maturities of oil-import credits extended or guaranteed by the Bank of Ghana. By early 1994, GNPC was financing most of its oil imports under one-year credits, even though the \*turnaround time" on the importation of crude and products was less than 60 days and 20 days, respectively. In 1994, the Government insisted that the company confine its financing to shorter-term credits and, being unable to meet its naturing obligations, GNPC built up a large overdraft position at the central bank. Considerable interest costs and foreign exchange losses were incurred on these credits. 2/ Consequently, the end-September performance criteria relating to GNPC borrowing from the central bank and the net domestic assets of the central bank were observed. The end-September performance criterion for net foreign assets of the Bank of Ghana was also met, despite delays in disbursements of foreign aid and divestiture proceeds (Table 2).  

3/ The other performance criteria that were not observed related to the stock of external short-term debt, external arrears payment, and actions to deregulate the petroleum sector. These are discussed further below.  

During the fourth quarter of l995, monetary pressures reemerged, largely in response to capital inflows associated with the financing of a large cocoa crop. l/ These inflows, together with a strong current account performance, further increased Ghana's foreign exchange reserves, and the Bank of Ghana's net foreign assets position exceeded the end-1995 target by the equivalent of 30 percent of reserve money. As a consequence, broad money grew by 37 percent in 1995, which was well above the program target of l4 percent. 2/ Inflation, however, continued to fall, reaching 54 percent at an annualized seasonally adjusted rate in the final quarter of the year. For the year as a whole, the rate of inflation was 70 percent on an end-of-period basis, which also reflected the cost-push impact of sharp increases in imported rice and wheat prices that had occurred earlier in the year.  

The budget recorded an overall deficit on a commitment basis of 0.1 percent of GDP in 1995, compared with the program target of a surplus of 1.4 percent. This slippage was attributable to overspending on roads and highways, which amounted to more than 2 percent of GDP and contributed to excess development outlays of over 3.2 percent of GDP. The overspending reflected the initiation of a number of large new projects and a bunching of work contracted some years previously, and was indicative of weaknesses in the expenditure control system. However, since large arrears to domestic contractors were incurred in the process, the Government was able to meet the program objective of repaying the equivalent of nearly 1 percent of GDP to the banking system.  

Total governnent revenues were some 1.8 percent of GDP above target in 1995, because of the receipt of very large dividend payments from public enterprises and royalties from the mining sectors. Performance in other revenue areas, however, fell short of program expectations. Petroleum tax collections were lower than expected, owing to reduced consumption and the failure to pass through increases in the cedi cost of oil imports to pump prices. Import duty collections suffered as a result of a real appreciation of the cedi and delays in implementing import tariff adjustments earlier in the year. There was also a shortfall in budgetary grants, which amounted to 0.7 percent of GDP. The large cocoa crop harvested toward the end of 1995 was not reflected in higher tax receipts until early 1996.  

Recurrent expenditures were held in check during l995. The government wage bill, which had been a source of najor overruns in the past, was  

maintained at 5.7 percent of GDP, despite intense pressures for higher pay from the trade unions and a prolonged strike by university teachers. Domestic interest payments were somewhat lower than programmed. l/ In addition to road spending, the capital budget was higher than projected because of additional outlays undertaken on social services.  

Progress in the implementation of structural reforms was generally slower than anticipated and, in particular, delays were encountered in plans to deregulate the petroleum and cocoa sectors (see Section 3.d). The divestiture program, on the other hand, proceeded well. The Government offered for sale 46 small- to medium-scale enterprises, of which 17 have already been sold. It also sold shares in the Social Security Bank, the Ghana Commercial Bank, and l2 mining companies, including Ashanti Goldfields (Table 3).  

Real GDP growth recovered from 3.8 percent in 1994 to 4.5 percent in 1995, but this was somewhat short of the program target of 5.0 percent. Private investment remained comparatively weak as a result of uncertainties arising from persistently high inflation and the uneven nature of policy implementation. On a sectoral basis, mining recorded strong growth in 1995, but manufacturing output remained sluggish. The agricultural sector, which grew by only 1.3 percent on average over 1992-94, expanded by 4.5 percent, despite a late rainy season, principally because of the large cocoa crop.  

# 3. Economic program for 1996  

The underlying policy strategy set out in the original ESAF program will continue to guide policy implementation in l996. However, the authorities are proposing some tightening in the stance of domestic financial policies in l996 over that originally envisaged in order to correct for the policy lapses that occurred last year. The most pressing policy priority is to bring down inflation, and to achieve this target the authorities have lowered the target for noney supply growth from 9 percent to 5 percent (Table 4). To provide further support in the fight against inflation, and to help consolidate gains that have been made in reducing the external current account deficit, the fiscal program for l996 will seek an overall budgetary surplus on a commitment basis of 2.4 percent of GDP. This would represent an underlying fiscal adjustment, excluding divestiture proceeds, of 2.2 percent of GDP from 1995.  

Notwithstanding the tightening in demand management policies, it will take some time for the strong monetary expansion that occurred in 1995 to work its way through the system, while the entrenched state of inflationary expectations will also tend to militate against any dramatic reduction in inflation. 1/ The program is therefore based on an inflation target of 25 percent for l996; any rapid dampening of expectations would permit stronger inflation performance, and thus it is imperative that the stabilization program be quickly perceived by the public to be effective and sustainable. A reversal of the sharp increase in imported rice prices experienced in l995 could also contribute to moderating price pressures.  

The program will continue to target real economic growth of at least 5 percent in l996; this objective should be achievable because of the impact of sharply lower inflation on transactions costs and savings incentives. Moreover, the critical part of the wet season has now passed and, as a result of the excellent rains that Ghana has enjoyed, strong growth in agricultural is anticipated.  

Pending conclusion of the discussions for the medium-term review, and to ensure that policy implementation is consistent with the overall targets for l996, informal monthly and quarterly targets were established for the key monetary and fiscal aggregates during the first half of 1996. Performance relative to these targets has been satisfactory. In particular, steps taken to control fiscal expenditures (see below) appear to have been successful, and development outlays were held at around 10 percent of the annual allocation during the first three months of the year. Petroleum duty revenue growth was somewhat weak, but this should be corrected by the increases in duty rates that were subsequently effected in early June. As a result of intensified open market operations and some foreign exchange sales by the Bank of Ghana, reserve money contracted by 7 percent in the first quarter, although broad money grew by 2.7 percent, because of a sharp seasonal fall in the public's currency/deposit ratio. Consequently, the improvement in inflation performance has been sustained, and the increase in the consumer price index fell to 32 percent at a seasonally adjusted annualized rate during the first four nonths of l996. The interest rate on treasury bills rose to positive levels in real terms around the beginning of the year.  

# a. Fiscal policy  

The budget for l996 calls for an overall budget surplus of 2.4 percent of GDP on a commitment basis, which corresponds to a surplus of 0.6 percent of GDP, excluding divestiture proceeds, and which is essentially the same as that targeted under the original progran (Tables 5 and 6). 2/ Additional revenue effort and expenditure conpression are being undertaken to achieve this objective.  

On the revenue front, a number of measures have already been implemented. Petrol prices were raised in February and again in May, while large increases in licenses and user fees were announced in the budget. 1/ Import duty collections will benefit from the full-year impact of tariff adjustments made in mid-1995. 2/ In addition, corporate income taxes should be boosted by the payment of large back taxes from a major commercial bank (equivalent to about 0.6 percent of GDP), and by the recent strong financial performance of the aluminum company, which is Ghana's largest corporate taxpayer. Because of the nonrecurrent nature of certain large dividend receipts in 1995, revenues from \*incomes and fees" are expected to drop sharply in l996. Despite some softening recently in international cocoa prices, tax receipts from the sector will be higher by 0.6 percent of GDP in l996, as a result of the large harvest this past crop year and the success of the Cocoa Board in its efforts to trim its operating and marketing costs. The divestiture program has been well managed and considerable funds are expected to be raised during the course of l996 from the sale of public assets; about one-third of these proceeds (or 1.8 percent of GDP) are to be used for budgetary purposes, while the remainder will be directed at domestic or external debt reduction or the acquisition of foreign assets. 3/  

The authorities are aware of the strains imposed on the stabilization effort as a result of the capital expenditure overruns that occurred last year. The capital budget swelled to an unsustainably high level in 1995, and the share of government resources devoted to investment in roads, which rose to 54 percent, was unduly large. The l996 program therefore calls for a sharp reduction in capital outlays to 3.9 percent of GDP from 6.0 percent in l995, with the brunt of the cuts concentrated in road spending.  

The Government has expended a great deal of effort over the past several months in restoring financial discipline in the area of roads. With assistance from the World Bank, all outstanding road contracts have been reviewed, and a special joint committee representing the Ministries of Finance and Roads and Highways has completed the renegotiation of each contract. Work programs with contractors have been drawn up that limit the aggregate value of physical work to be undertaken during l996 to the  

equivalent of 2.0 percent of GDP. l/ To remain within this ceiling, no new major contracts for locally funded roads will be entered into unless existing contracts of comparable value are canceled. 2/ At the same time, all outstanding arrears to road contractors have been regularized. 3/ Measures have also been taken, such as the setting up of monthly commitment ledgers, that will enable the Ministry of Finance to monitor road spending more effectively. Looking further ahead, the World Bank has assisted the authorities in completing a detailed multiyear plan for the road sector, which involves rationalization of the roads investment program and coordination of financing of both locally and donor-funded projects. 4/  

Considerable fiscal savings will be realized in l996 on wage-related outlays. Most notably, payments for end-of-service benefits, which reached a peak of 0.8 percent of GDP a year during l993-94, will be terminated by mid-l996. 5/ Furthermore, the Government recently concluded negotiations with the trade unions for a civil service wage increase in l996 of 30 percent and an increase in the national minimum wage of 42 percent (see below). A salary restructuring exercise will be considered for implementation in early l997, once the necessary preparatory study has been completed. In addition, strict limits have been placed on non-wage current outlays of ministries, which must now prioritize their spending in favor of operations and maintenance, while constraining non-essential activities, including travel.  

# b. Monetary and anti-inflation policies  

To help guide the Bank of Ghana in its efforts to rein in the growth of liquidity and to underscore the role of money as the nominal anchor for inflation, reserve money will be used as a program benchmark. The exchange rate will be assigned the role of meeting the program's overall balance of payments objectives and will therefore be allowed to respond to conditions of supply and demand in the foreign exchange market. However, in the event of strong unanticipated foreign exchange inflows, intervention by the central bank may prove necessary to avert an undue appreciation of the cedi, if an assessment is made that these inflows are not demand-induced and are therefore potentially inflationary in their impact. Under such conditions, additional monetary and, if feasible, fiscal policy action would be required in order to sterilize the domestic liquidity impact of this intervention.  

The Bank of Ghana has established a quarterly profile for the major monetary aggregates through end-l996 (Tables 7 and 8). In developing this profile, it was assumed that the money multiplier would follow a typical seasonal pattern, whereby the public's holdings of currency relative to deposits would decline during the first six to nine months of the year and rise sharply toward the end of the year. l/ On this basis, in order to meet the broad money growth target for l996, reserve money is targeted to contract by ll percent during the first half of the year. Information available through end-April indicates that reserve money fell by 6 percent from the beginning of the year, which is in line with the monetary program.  

Monetary management will continue to rely largely on the use of indirect instruments of control, principally open narket operations by the Bank of Ghana. To improve the effectiveness of these instruments, in March l996 the Bank of Ghana streamlined procedures for the weekly wholesale auction system and took steps to expand secondary market trading in official debt instruments. The central bank has also allowed more frequent adjustments in interest rates. The maintenance of positive real interest rates will be critical in the Government's efforts to restrain aggregate demand and mobilize private savings.  

# c. Financial sector reforms  

Since the completion in l990 of the first phase of the bank restructuring program, under which most of the nonperforming portfolios of commercial banks were taken over by the Government, stringent prudential regulations have been enforced by the Bank of Ghana. This has resulted in a significant improvement in the soundness of the financial sector. 2/ The banking system has recorded large profits in the past few years, and loan recovery rates have been very healthy. The Government has therefore recently embarked on implementation of the second phase of reforns, which entails divestiture of the najority of its shares in the banking sector. Since the approval of the ESAF arrangement, the Government has offered for  

sale 60 percent of its shares in the Social Security Bank and in Ghana's largest financial institution, the Ghana Commercial Bank. About one-half of the Ghana Commercial Bank share offer has been reserved for potential foreign strategic investors, while the rest was traded domestically in early 1996 through the local stock exchange. The domestic issue was oversubscribed and, in response, the Government sold an additional l2 percent of its shares to local investors.  

# d. Petroleum and cocoa sector deregulation  

Stabilizing the economy and reducing inflation will not by itself be sufficient to achieve sustained and broad-based growth, and the Government is moving ahead with the pursuit of structural reforms. The removal of the remaining impediments to private sector initiative will constitute the core of this effort. Much has already been accomplished by establishing strong market-related price incentives and liberalizing domestic and external trade. The public enterprises, however, still play an important role in a number of sectors and scope remains for realizing further efficiency gains through deregulation as well as divestiture.  

The financial difficulties experienced by GNPC, and their repercussions for macroeconomic performance more generally, prompted the Government to develop an action plan aimed at removing state involvement in the procurement, refining, and pricing of petrol. The plan called for the implementation of two important measures in the deregulation program by endSeptember l995. These measures, which are structural performance criteria under the program, are: (a) removal of GNPC's monopoly over the importation of crude oil and adoption of a system of open bidding for oil procurement contracts; and (b) introduction of a formula to allow retail petrol prices to fully and automatically reflect movements in international crude oil prices and the exchange rate. Delays were experienced in implementing these measures, but actions were eventually taken in May and early June l996, and the Government is requesting that the nonobservance of the relevant performance criteria be waived. l/ The authorities explained that, because of concerns over the potential inflationary impact, they wished to wait until the wage negotiations with the trade unions had been completed before they pushed ahead with deregulation. Serious dissensions had also arisen within the administration regarding the merits of deregulation, and re-establishing the necessary political consensus had taken longer than expected. The delays mean that full price decontrol will be pushed back to the end of l996, when an ad valorem tax will be imposed.  

In the meantime, efforts to restructure GNPC are proceeding. The Government intends to draw up by mid-l996 a timetable for the sale of a number of companies that are whole subsidiaries of GNPC or in which GNPC holds shares. Proceeds from the sale of these assets, and from any private equity participation in GNPC or its subsidiaries, will be used to repay outstanding debts to the Bank of Ghana and the Government. GNPC plans to develop major energy projects, which are currently being evaluated by the World Bank; certain important financial considerations are associated with these projects, and are discussed in Section 4.a. The Government is also actively seeking potential investors to purchase the oil refinery and a network of storage facilities.  

The process of cocoa sector liberalization began in l992, when private traders were permitted to compete with the Cocoa Board in marketing the domestic crop. Private purchases now account for about 25 percent of the total crop sold. However, certain impediments to greater efficiency in cocoa marketing remain. Because the Cocoa Board holds a monopoly over exports of cocoa, private traders have faced fixed selling prices and encountered problems in securing adequate bank credit for their operations. 1/  

Under the ESAF-supported program, the Government had indicated its desire to remove the Cocoa Board's monopoly over the export of cocoa. The Cocoa Board would, however, retain its quality control function and, for a transitional period, also compete in crop marketing. A study commissioned by the Government and financed by the World Bank was to be undertaken to examine the external marketing of cocoa, including issues relating to the modalities of the deregulation process. A first draft of the study, which was completed in February, concluded that Ghana would benefit from retention of the Cocoa Board's monopoly, because of the impact of deregulation on quality control and the reliability of deliveries. Participants in the international cocoa market have expressed similar concerns. In light of these considerations, but while remaining aware of the benefits inherent in a deregulated system, the Government has asked the consultants to undertake a more in-depth analysis of the issues involved. On the basis of that report and after further consultations with World Bank and Fund staff, the Government will make a decision by end-Septenber l996 on how to proceed with the design of a more fully articulated cocoa sector reform strategy.  

Significant increases in producer prices (in U.S. dollar terms) have been made possible in the past two years by reductions in the effective tax rate on cocoa exports and by economies realized in the operations of the Cocoa Board. These price increases contributed to the success of the crop for the l995/96 season, which was the largest in more than 20 years. Announcement of an appropriate producer price, consistent with world market price trends, fiscal revenue requirements, and adequate producer incentives, is a structural benchmark for end-June 1996.  

# e. Institution building  

Steps are being taken to strengthen the institutional and administrative capacity of the Government to effectively carry out the reform program. In the area of tax administration, it remains committed to the concept of a VAT-type tax, and a unit has been established in the Ministry of Finance to help introduce a tax that retains the attributes of the VAT, but is more generally acceptable to the business community. l/ In the meantime, the computerized taxpayer registers that were developed for the VAT are being utilized to strengthen collections and combat tax evasion. The Government has also initiated a comprehensive overhaul of its expenditure monitoring and control system with assistance from the World Bank and other donors. While the full system is not expected to be completed before mid-1997, transitional control measures have already been taken. These include improvements in the monitoring of expenditure commitments in large spending agencies (including the Ministry of Roads and Highways), the extension of quarterly budget reviews to all spending agencies, and introduction of new procedures for contracting and procurement to achieve transparency and value for money. Further transitional measures are planned during l996, including the monthly monitoring of cash and commitment spending on capital projects, a thorough review of ongoing contracts, and quicker reporting by spending agencies.  

A major study has been commissioned by the Governnent to develop a comprehensive strategy on public sector pay structure and wage policies, and its recommendations are expected to be completed by end-September l996, with implementation scheduled for early next year. In addition, a high level committee, the National Oversight Committee, is developing a program aimed at downsizing the civil service and subvented organizations by consolidating functions within the civil service, eliminating duplication, and hiving off those services that can better be delivered by the private sector on a fullcost recovery basis. These reviews will also aim at identifying ways to improve efficiency of manpower utilization throughout the civil service.  

The composition of government spending has become skewed in recent years in favor of physical, relative to human, capital, and the Government is keenly aware that this imbalance needs to be corrected. An overall public investment program (PIP) for 1996-2000 is currently being prepared, which will concentrate on limiting the start of new projects to those that yield the highest rates of return and that are more likely to attract foreign funding. In the meantime a nunber of projects that have remained incomplete for long periods will be pruned back. l/ This should provide room to improve the mix of public investment spending and allow for the rehabilitation of the social infrastructure, particularly in health and education sectors.  

# f. Social policies  

Social safety net considerations have been addressed within the context of changes in the minimum wage, tax and expenditure provisions in the 1996 budget, and agricultural sector policies. The Government granted an increase in the minimum wage of 42 percent in April 1996, the first such adjustment in two years. 2/ This measure will have no direct effect on incomes in the formal sector where the lowest pay levels are well above the minimum wage rate. The minimum threshold for income taxes was also raised in the l996 budget, while the price for kerosene, which is used by low income families, has declined over tine relative to other petroleum products. In support of the Government's ongoing poverty alleviation program, the budget for l996 has kept noninterest recurrent spending, the bulk of which is absorbed by the social sectors, at the same level relative to GDP as in l995. The level of transfers has been increased in real terms, mainly to improve the provision of rural education and health services at the district level. Furthermore, as indicated earlier, the reductions in the effective tax rate on cocoa exports over the past two years have contributed to an enhancenent in rural incomes and helped discourage urban migration.  

4. Balance of payments outlook and debt sustainability  

# a. External sector developments and policies for 1996  

External sector performance was generally strong in l995 with regard to both current and capital transactions, and all key program targets were met by wide margins. The current account deficit narrowed by more than 2 percent of GDP, mainly because of strong growth of gold and timber exports (gold shipments rose by l8 percent) and because of sluggish import demand reflecting increases in import taxes and the relatively tight posture of demand and credit policies. Delays in the receipt of aid disbursenents were more than offset by higher short-term capital inflows, primarily in the form of oil credits and prefinancing of l996 cocoa exports. As a result, the overall balance of payments recorded a surplus of $15\$284$ million, and  

Ghana's foreign exchange reserves rose to the equivalent of 5.l months of imports, from 4.l months in l994 (Table 9).  

The balance of payments outlook for l996 remains favorable. Export proceeds are expected to grow by l2 percent in U.S. dollar terms, mainly because of the strong expansion in cocoa production during the 1995/96 season, which should offset some recent softening in international prices. Gold production is expected to continue to grow at a healthy pace in response to the considerable increase in direct foreign investment that has taken place in the mineral sector over the last few years. The strong export performance should provide some room for a rebound in imports, while helping maintain the original program target for the current account deficit at just above 3 percent of GDP.  

The program had envisaged a large buildup of gross reserves to the equivalent of 5.2 months of imports in 1996. In reviewing the program's external objectives, however, the authorities considered that somewhat less recourse to external borrowing and a correspondingly lower reserve buildup might be desirable as a means of minimizing net debt service costs. In addition, they noted that proceeds from the divestiture program not explicitly factored into the balance of payments projections were expected to be higher than previously anticipated; these proceeds would be added to reserves pending their disposition under the Governnent's debt-reduction program. l/ In light of these considerations, the Government is now targeting an overall balance of payments surplus of Us\$ll5 million in 1996, which would translate into an end-of-year international reserve stock equivalent to 4.7 months of imports.  

At end-l995, Ghana had external arrears to three bilateral creditors, which amounted to Us\$loo nillion; agreements have since been or are about to be signed for a deferral of these arrears over a period of $4-1/2$ years. 2/ The arrears in question relate to specific debts contracted prior to 1983, when Ghana embarked on its ERP. The ESAF arrangement includes, as a performance criterion for end-September 1995, a targeted reduction of Us\$30 million in external arrears through cash payments. In the event, less than this amount was paid because agreement on the repayment plans had not been concluded at that time. Subsequently, additional arrears payments were made, the end-December benchmark was observed, and the Governnent is requesting a waiver for noncompliance with the performance criterion. No new external arrears have been incurred under the program.  

The program's ceiling on short-term external debt for end-September 1995, which is a performance criterion, was exceeded primarily because the Bank of Ghana took out a three-month bridge loan as a contingency against further delays in the receipt of official aid and divestiture funds. l/ With the subsequent receipt of these funds, the short-term debt stock was reduced to a level only slightly above the end-December 1995 benchmark, and the Government is requesting a waiver for the nonobservance of the performance criterion.  

Loans totaling Us\$393 million have been approved by an official export credit guarantee agency to support a natural gas-fired power project being undertaken by GNPC. These loans have a maturity of slightly more than 12 years, bear a market rate of interest, and carry Bank of Ghana guarantees, which were a precondition for approval of the credits; they have not yet been signed by the GNPC Board. GNPC has agreed not to draw down the loans until a World Bank study verifies the viability of the projects and GNPC's capacity to service the loans. 2/ To eliminate exposure of the Bank of Ghana to the risks entailed in the project, GNPC will be making efforts to refinance these loans with funds raised through private capital markets over the next two years.  

A Consultative Group meeting held in June 1995 reviewed Ghana's external financing requirements. On the basis of commitments made and prospective debt deferral, the financing requirements for the period through 1997 are expected to be fully covered (Table 10).  

# b. Long-term outlook and debt sustainability  

An analysis conducted by the staff of the Fund and the Bank in consultation with the Ghanaian authorities indicates that Ghana's present debt position is sustainable. 3/ The net present value of debt relative to exports is expected to decline in l996 to less than 200 percent, and should continue to fall through the medium term. The debt service ratio is projected to drop to 27 percent in l996 and decline further, to less than 20 percent by the end of the decade. The analysis also indicates that Ghana's debt capacity is relatively robust and can readily sustain modest adverse external stocks. Ghana should not, therefore, experience problems in meeting its debt service obligations, including those to the Fund. 4/ Ghana's debt burden will be further reduced as a result of the Government's plans to use proceeds from the sale of public assets to prepay expensive commercial debt.  

# III. Program Monitoring. Prior Actions. and Data Reporting  

Performance under the program will be monitored on the basis of the following indicative benchmarks: (a) reserve money; (b) the net domestic assets of the Bank of Ghana; (c) net domestic financing of the government budget; (d) the net foreign assets of the Bank of Ghana; (e) new nonconcessional external borrowing, with a subceiling on debt of l-5 years' maturity; (f) the stock of external short-term debt, excluding normal import-related credits; and (g) external payment arrears, with an injunction against the incurrence of new arrears (Table l4). Indicative structural benchmarks will cover (a) cocoa producer price adjustments; (b) a decision on the modalities of cocoa sector deregulation; (c) a tinetable for the full decontrol of petroleum product prices; (d) progress in implementing the divestiture program, including the sale of GNPC assets; and (e) progress in the installation of expenditure monitoring and control machinery (Table 15).  

Certain actions have been taken since the beginning of the year that have enabled the program to be brought back on track and that will provide a basis for effective program implementation for the renainder of 1996. Reserve money growth has been running within program targets; all road contracts have been successfully renegotiated; agreement has been reached with the trade unions on national minimum and civil service pay awards for 1996; and key petroleum sector reforms have been implemented.  

The reporting of core economic statistics required to undertake effective surveillance and monitor program performance has generally been satisfactory (Appendix V). Nonetheless, although the monthly fiscal data on a cash basis, as recorded in the banking system, are reliable for the major aggregates, certain details on receipts or payments sometimes remain unidentified for several months pending supporting data from the Accountant General's office. Expenditure data on commitments are also not readily available because of weaknesses in the expenditure monitoring system. As an integral element in the program, however, the authorities will be providing additional data required to nonitor commitments and compile the fiscal accounts on a commitment basis. The authorities are taking measures to improve the expenditure reporting system, but these improvements are likely to take some time until a reliable budgetary tracking and control system can be installed. Progress in this area will be monitored by structural benchmarks under the program.  

There is also scope for improvements in other areas of statistical compilation and reporting: inadequate data on public enterprises preclude derivation of public sector accounts; labor market data remain scanty; the national income accounts on an expenditure basis have not been produced for some time; and the weights in the basket for the consuner price index have not been updated for more than ten years. l/ To address these weaknesses, technical assistance from a number of bilateral sources and the World Bank is being provided to strengthen data compilation, revise methodologies, and improve the timeliness of data reporting and publication.  

# IV. Staff Appraisal  

Ghana's performance in stabilizing and restructuring its economy under the ESAF arrangement has been mixed. Money supply growth was well above target in l995, and inflation rose to unacceptably high levels. In addition, very large overruns in road spending were recorded, and arrears to domestic suppliers were incurred in the process. Inflation and uneven macroeconomic policy implementation have continued to retard the recovery in private investment, and economic growth fell slightly below target in 1995. On the other hand, balance of payments performance has been strong and significant progress was made in achieving a sustainable external position; the current account deficit shrank sharply to 2 percent of GDP in 1995 and Ghana's international reserves rose to the equivalent of over 5 months of imports. In the area of structural reform, the divestiture program has proceeded on track and, after some delay, important action has been taken to deregulate the petroleum sector.  

Of the l2 financial and structural performance criteria that applied at end-September l995, 5 were not observed. In three of these cases, the targets have since been achieved, while in the case of the short-term debt ceiling, the outstanding stock was reduced to a level very close to the benchmark for end-December. The performance criterion for net domestic financing of the budget was not observed because of a shortfall in aid receipts that has since been reversed. Convincing policy action has also been taken in other areas where slippages occurred in l995. The staff therefore believes that there is now a firm basis for concluding the midterm review and supports the Government's request for a waiver of the performance criteria for end-September 1995 that were not observed.  

In the staff's view, the program for 1996 represents a determined effort by the Government to adhere as closely as possible to the original targets of the ESAF arrangement. It provides, in particular, a disciplined framework to guide economic policy during the run-up to presidential and parliamentary elections scheduled for later this year. The Government is urged to resist election-related pressures in implementing the budget for 1996 and to maintain the program firmly on course. The staff welcomes the strong stand taken by the Government during the recent wage negotiations with the trade unions and considers this indicative of the strength of its commitment to the goals of the economic program. The stance of domestic  

management policies for 1996 is tight, but the more forceful and convincing is the Government's anti-inflation policy perceived to be, the more quickly will inflation be reduced, without imposing undue strain on the economy. Lower inflation will also help enhance savings incentives and reduce uncertainties in decision-making, thereby setting the economy on a course of growth-oriented stabilization. In this regard, the decline in inflation to 32 percent in the first four months of 1996 is most encouraging. This inflation performance needs to be sustained, and the Government should not hesitate to allow nominal interest rates to rise, if necessary, to meet its stabilization objectives.  

The Bank of Ghana observed the limit on its net domestic assets at the end of l995, but the broad money target was overshot because of unanticipated balance of payments inflows. In keeping with the strategy of assigning money the role of nominal anchor in the fight against inflation, reserve money is to be made a program benchmark, with the exchange rate allowed to adjust in order to meet the program's international reserve objectives. The staff supports this policy mix as a means of dealing with the sources of excessive monetary growth, such as larger than projected cocoa export earnings, that undermined the monetary program in 1995. However, the authorities need to implement these policies carefully, bearing in mind prevailing money demand and inflation developments. In particular, additional foreign exchange inflows that may arise in response to strongerthan-expected money demand should not necessarily prompt tighter credit conditions or a currency appreciation, since these inflows are unlikely to have inflationary repercussions.  

Deficiencies in the machinery to track and control expenditures became particularly apparent in l995 with the emergence of large spending overruns on road construction and the incurrence of arrears to local road contractors. A major overhaul of the expenditure monitoring system is presently under way with technical assistance from the World Bank and donors. The Government should accord this exercise, which will not be completed until 1997, the highest priority. In the meantime, progress in implementing transitory control measures will be monitored through the use of program benchmarks. Furthermore, in preparing its revised public investment program, the Government needs to achieve a better balance in the structure of capital spending, by controlling the level of spending on physical construction projects so as to allow for rehabilitation investments in the social infrastructure, particularly in health and education.  

Steps to complete deregulation of the cocoa sector have been halted pending further review of the situation. The staff is sympathetic to the concerns that have been expressed over the consequences of deregulation. It nevertheless feels that these concerns can be adequately addressed by retaining the Cocoa Board as a quality control body and allowing it to compete with private traders in crop marketing for a trial period. A decision by the Government on the Cocoa Board's export monopoly has been postponed until September l996, which would still provide time for new marketing arrangenents to be introduced before the beginning of the  

crop marketing season. Over the mediun-term, there is a need to reduce reliance on cocoa export taxes, which have strong disincentive effects on production, as a source of revenue; the intention to introduce a VAT-type tax should facilitate the Government's efforts in this area.  

Financial problems experienced by GNPC constituted a major force destabilizing policy implementation in 1994 and the early part of 1995. Important steps have since been taken to address these problems, and they have so far proven effective. Nevertheless, GNPC operations remain a potential threat to the economic adjustment effort, and the Government is urged to monitor the company's financial position carefully in the coming months. On a related matter, the staff notes that GNPC may shortly contract nearly Us\$40o million in new loans to finance a gas-fired energy project. The World Bank is assessing the viability of the project and GNPC's capacity to repay the debts, and no drawdown of the loans will be made unless the Bank's assessment is positive. Staff calculations indicate that the loans, if disbursed, will not materially affect Ghana's external debt sustainability prospects. The staff is, however, concerned that, upon the insistence of the credit guarantee agencies involved, the loans will carry Bank of Ghana guarantees. These guarantees threaten to reverse the progress made over the past year in disengaging GNPC from its financial relationship with the Bank of Ghana. The staff has therefore discussed with the Government a plan for GNPC to refinance the loans with a nonguaranteed debt offering. In light of Ghana's fledgling status in international capital markets, this exercise will not be easy, and considerable effort on the part of the authorities will be required to ensure its success.  

As an integral element in its external debt management strategy, Ghana has avoided recourse to debt relief, and this has provided it with access to new credits that would not otherwise have been available. Ghana is seeking a deferral on nonconcessional terns of arrears incurred to three creditors on specific debts contracted before l983; this is a highly limited operation and should not be interpreted as representing a departure from Ghana's ongoing debt strategy.  

On the basis of the debt deferral and commitments made at a Consultative Group neeting in June l995, Ghana's external financing requirements through l997 are expected to be fully covered. Moreover, the external debt outlook appears viable. Both liquidity and solvency debt indicators suggest that the present debt burden is manageable and that it will continue to shrink over the nedium term, particularly if plans to repay expensive debt with divestiture proceeds are successful. Sensitivity tests also reveal that Ghana's balance of paynents position is sufficiently robust to sustain modest adverse shocks. The staff does not therefore expect Ghana to experience any difficulties in neeting its debt service obligations, including those to the Fund.  

It is recommended that Ghana remain on the standard 12-month consultation cycle.

# I. Introduction and Recent Economic Performance  

1. This memorandum provides a progress report on Ghana's economic performance in connection with the Fund's midterm review under the first annual arrangement under the enhanced structural adjustment facility (ESAF). It also sets out the Government's policy objectives for l996 and presents a profile of financial and structural benchmarks that are intended to guide and monitor policy implementation.  

2. The Government's economic program is being supported by a three-year ESAF arrangement, which was approved in June 1995. It was formulated against the background of intensifying inflationary pressures, which arose in l994 and early 1995 as a result of slippages in fiscal policy implementation and financial difficulties experienced by the Ghana National Petroleum Corporation (GNPC). The program sought to reduce monetary growth, which had risen to nearly 50 percent by mid-l995, through tightening financial policies and by curtailing GNPC's access to central bank credit and restructuring the company's activities.  

3. In the early stages of the program, delays were experienced in putting together a financing package for GNPC, and its overdraft at the central bank continued to increase. A stronger than anticipated overall balance of payments position provided a further monetary impulse.  

4.In response to these developnents, external financing was arranged for GNPC so that its overdraft position at the central bank could be reduced, and its oil trading transactions brought under the direct control of the Ministry of Finance. In addition, the pace of open market operations was accelerated, the structure of interest rates allowed to rise by around ten percentage points, and reserve money was reduced to below target for end-September. As a consequence of these actions, the end-September performance criteria relating to GNPC borrowing from the central bank and the net domestic assets of the central bank were observed. Despite delays in the receipt of divestiture proceeds and aid disbursements, the endSeptember performance criterion for net foreign assets of the Bank of Ghana was also met. These delays, however, resulted in failure to observe the performance criterion for domestic financing of the budget, and the Government is requesting that this be waived.  

5.Subsequently, during the final quarter of 1995, an excellent cocoa crop contributed to large foreign exchange inflows, and the central banks net foreign assets target for end-l995 was exceeded by the equivalent of 30 percent of reserve money. The Bank of Ghana experienced problems in sterilizing the liquidity impact of these inflows and was able to reduce broad money growth by end-l995 to only 37 percent, which was well above the target of 14 percent. The inflationary repercussions of this excess monetary  

growth were further fuelled by the cost-push impact of sharp increases in imported rice and wheat prices, and inflation rose to the unacceptable level of 70 percent by the end of 1995.  

6. Because of the shortfall in external grants in l995, the Government budget recorded a surplus of 0.9 percent of GDP on a cash basis, which compares with a program target of l.2 percent; however, considerable arrears were incurred to local road contractors, reflecting in part weaknesses in the expenditure control and budget management system. Lower-than-expected petroleum tax revenues due largely to reduced consumption levels were more than offset by the receipt of unusually large dividend payments from the parastatal sector and royalties from the mining sector, while wage expenditures and development spending on non-road projects were kept in check relative to GDP.  

7. The structural reform program has proceeded generally satisfactorily, although delays have been encountered in certain areas. Two key reforms in the deregulation of the petroleum sector--the removal of GNPC's monopoly over the importation of oil and the adoption of a market-related mechanism for setting retail prices--were not implemented until May and early June 1996, which was well behind the original target date of September l995. 1/ The Government is requesting a waiver for nonobservance of the two structural performance criteria in this area. The Government has offered for sale 46 small-to-medium-scale enterprises (of which 17 have been sold), and 60 percent of its shares in the Social Security Bank and Ghana Commercial Bank. In addition, half of the Government's 10 percent shares in eleven mining companies, as well as 5 percent of its shares in Ashanti Goldfields, were also sold.  

# I1. Program for 1996 (a) Financial Policies  

8. The most pressing econonic policy challenge facing the Government is to quickly bring inflation under control, and financial policies will be tightened to attain this goal. In particular, the growth of broad money will be reduced to 5 percent in l996, while the fiscal program seeks an overall budgetary surplus of 2.4 percent of GDP on a commitment basis; this would represent fiscal adjustment, excluding divestiture proceeds, of 2.2 percent of GDP from l995. Additionally, the Government will take a strong stand in the area of wage policy. The Government nevertheless believes that it will take some time for inflationary expectations to dampen sufficiently, and it is unlikely that the inflation rate can be reduced much below 25 percent in 1996. However, inflation performance could be considerably stronger than this if the sharp increase in imported cereals prices experienced in l995 is reversed, as recent trends indicate may occur. The program will continue to target real economic growth at 5 percent in 1996.  

9. The fiscal adjustment envisaged for l996 is to be realized through large increases in petroleum duty rates, which were announced in the 1996 budget, the full year impact of import duty adjustments made in mid-1995, and large increases in licenses and certain fees. In addition, company tax collections will be boosted by the payment of large back-taxes by a major bank and the recent strong financial performance of the aluminum company, VALCO, which is Ghana's largest taxpayer. Despite some softening of international cocoa prices, tax receipts from the sector will be higher in 1996, mainly as a result of the large crop and the success of efforts by the Cocoa Board to reduce its operating and marketing costs.  

10. The Government felt compelled to suspend the Value Added Tax in June 1995 soon after its introduction because it became evident that sufficient work to prepare the public for the tax had not been undertaken. The Government remains convinced, however, of the fairness and efficiency of the VAT, and a unit has been created in the Ministry of Finance to help introduce a tax that retains the attributes of the VAT, but is more generally acceptable to the business community.  

1l. Substantial proceeds are expected from the sale of public assets in 1996, but less than one-third (or 1.8 percent of GDP) will be used for budgetary purposes. The remainder will be directed at domestic or external debt reduction for the acquisition of foreign assets on the basis of a plan that is being developed in consultation with Fund staff.  

12. Steps have been taken to enable the Ministry of Finance to regain firm control over locally funded expenditures on roads and highways. In consultation with the World Bank, a detailed multi-year plan for the road sector has been prepared which involves rationalization of the roads investment program. In the meantime, contractors have been informed of the maximum amount of work that can be undertaken each month under existing contracts, while monthly commitment ledgers have also been established to help track and control expenditures. These ledgers will be examined monthly by a special joint committee represented by the Ministries of Finance and Roads and Highways. Cash allocations for road construction will be administered through the Accountant-General's office. No new contracts will be entered into in 1996 for locally funded roads, except one very small project that has already been initiated, l/ unless existing contracts of comparable value are canceled. Around one-half of the arrears incurred to road contractors in l995 have since been paid and the remainder will be consolidated and repaid under agreements reached with the contractors. No new net arrears will be allowed to arise. The l996 budget calls for a sharp  

reduction in locally funded outlays on roads and highways relative to GDP in order to make up for some of the overruns that occurred last year.  

13. Considerable fiscal savings will be realized in 1996 on wage-related outlays. Most notably, the payment of end-of-service benefits is being drawn to a close, while the increase in the wage bill will be contained to 30 percent. This target will include the cost of a salary restructuring program, the implementation of which will commence early in 1997 (see below). Strong pressures are developing in the labor movement for sizeable wage increases, but the Government is determined to observe the budget allocations for wages and is intent on avoiding a repetition of l992, when large pay awards seriously undermined the stabilization effort.  

14. Control of the money supply will provide the nominal anchor necessary to reduce inflation, while the exchange rate will be assigned to meeting Ghana's balance of payments objectives. Monetary management will continue to rely largely on indirect instruments through open market operations conducted by the central bank. The Bank of Ghana will allow more frequent adjustments in interest rates; the positive real interest rates presently prevailing are a critical element in the Government's effort to restrain aggregate demand, mobilize private savings, and encourage capital inflows. To improve the effectiveness of monetary management, the Bank of Ghana has recently taken measures to streamline the procedures and operations of the weekly wholesale auction system and to encourage secondary market trading ir official debt instruments.  

# (b) Structural Reforms  

15. The Government realizes that creating a low-inflation environment will not by itself provide a sufficient basis for sustained and durable growth, and that structural reforms need to be directed at encouraging private sector initiative and improving resource allocation. It is therefore moving ahead with its plan to deregulate the petroleum and cocoa sectors and to divest interests in the mining, manufacturing, and financial sectors.  

16. In April 1996, GNPC's monopoly over the procurement of crude oil and oil products was removed. In early June, a flexible and automatic pricing mechanism was introduced on a transitional basis in order to ensure that retail prices reflect prevailing market conditions and that government revenue and narketing margins are protected from movements in the exchange rate and international oil prices. Full price decontrol will be effected by end-December l996 and an ad valorem tax introduced at that tine. A number of GNPC's assets are to be sold, and the proceeds from this source, and from any private equity participation in GNPC or its subsidiaries, will be used to repay debts to the Bank of Ghana and the Government. In the meantime, GNPC's plans to develop certain major energy projects will be reviewed by the World Bank; credit lines to fund these projects will not be drawn upon until the review is completed and unless a positive assessment is provided. GNPC will be making efforts to refinance these credit lines, which bear marked rates of interest and carry Bank of Ghana guarantees, with funds  

raised from private capital markets within the next two years. The Government has also indicated to potential investors its intention to sell the oil refinery and the network of storage facilities.  

17. Good progress has been achieved in recent years in improving incentives for cocoa farmers. As a result of significant increases in producer prices in US dollar terms, the sector registered strong growth in 1995 and the crop for l996 is expected to be the largest for over 20 years. The producer price increases were made possible by reductions in the effective tax rate on cocoa exports and by the success of cost-cutting measures undertaken by the Cocoa Board. These measures included a cut in the size of the labor force by one-half over the past two years. Economies have also been realized with the opening up of domestic marketing to private traders, who purchased around one-quarter of the crop during the 1995/96 season. The Government had previously indicated its desire to remove the Cocoa Board's monopoly over the export of cocoa, although the Cocoa Board was to retain its quality control function and, during a transitional period, also compete in crop marketing. A study commissioned by the Government and financed by the World Bank was to be undertaken to examine the external marketing of cocoa, including the modalities of the deregulation process. A first draft of this study was completed recently, and it concludes that Ghana would benefit from retaining the Cocoa Board's monopoly. The Government intends to make a decision on how to proceed on this matter by end-September 1996, after consultation with Bank and Fund staff.  

18. As well as strengthening incentives for the private sector, the Government is taking steps to enhance its own capacity to effectively implement economic reforms. Government, through the Public Services Commission, has engaged the services of a consulting firm to develop a comprehensive long and medium-term strategy on public sector salaries and wages policy for Government. The consultant is to identify all aspects of the current public sector salary regine and deternine those that should be modified, deleted or maintained with a view to reforming the pay structure generally. The consultant is also to undertake a detailed job evaluation and job classification in the entire public sector, as well as a mininum wage policy, and a salary administration structure. The consultant's report is expected to be completed by the end of the third quarter of l996. Also, the National Oversight Committee of the National Institutional Renewal Program, NIRP, is engaged in a program aimed at consolidating job functions with a view to streamlining and down-sizing the civil service and the subvented organizations to become more efficient and effective in the delivery of public services. This major new challenge calls for an urgent and critical review of available capacity and the identification of constraints to the effective utilization of manpower throughout the entire public service of Ghana.  

19. The Government has also embarked on a comprehensive overhaul of its expenditure monitoring and control systen with assistance from the World Bank and donors. The revamped system is not, however, likely to be fully installed until mid-l997, but transitional control measures will be introduced in the meantine; these include improvements in the monitoring of expenditure commitments and the extension of quarterly budget reviews to all spending agencies. In addition, with World Bank assistance, the Government is implementing new procedures for contracting and procurement.  

20. The Government recognizes that its overall expenditure program, particularly capital spending, has been weighted in favor of investment in physical relative to human capital. An overall expenditure review and a new PIP are currently underway as part of the five-year plan (l996-2000), and the strategy will concentrate on the completion of ongoing projects and limiting the start of new projects to funding that yields a positive net rate of return. In the meanwhile, projects that have been inconplete for six years or more and are still not close to completion will be liquidated. All parastatal projects will be included in the PIP, and their financing will be evaluated within the medium-term macroeconomic framework of the ESAF.  

# (c) External sector  

21. The performance of Ghana's external sector was generally good in 1995. The current account deficits declined to 2.2 percent of GDP from 4.9 percent in l994, largely as a result of strong growth in cocoa and gold exports. Delays in the receipt of aid disbursements were offset by higher short-term capital inflows, primarily in the form of cocoa pre-financing and oil credits. As a result, the overall balance of payments target for l995 was exceeded comfortably, and Ghana's foreign exchange reserves rose to the equivalent of 5.1 months of imports at the end of 1995, from 4.l in the previous year.  

22. The program envisaged, as a performance criterion for end-September 1995, a reduction of US $\$30$ million in external arrears through cash payments. In the event, slightly less than this was paid because agreenent on repayment plans with certain official bilateral creditors were not concluded in time; these plans should be finalized shortly. 1/ In the meantime, additional cash payments have been made, and the end-December benchnark was observed. Agreenents to consolidate these arrears and defer payment over a $4-1/2$ year period have been, or are about to be, concluded. No new external arrears have been incurred during the program period. The short-term debt ceiling, which is a performance criterion for end-September, was also not observed because a credit line was drawn upon as a contingency against further delays in the receipt of foreign aid and divestiture funds. The short-term debt stock was subsequently reduced to just above the endDecember benchmark level. The Government is therefore seeking a waiver for the nonobservance of the performance criteria relating to external arrears and short-term debt.  

23. Ghana's external outlook for l996 remains favorable. Continued expansion of cocoa production should offset some recent softening in international price conditions, while gold exports are expected to continue to display healthy growth in response to considerable foreign investment that has taken place in the mineral sector. These developments should allow some recovery in imports, and the external current account is expected to register a deficit of 3.4 percent of GDP, which is in line with the original program target. After taking into account anticipated inflows of long- and medium-term capital and foreign investment and divestiture proceeds, the balance of payments is expected to register an overall surplus of nearly US\$ll5 million in 1996; this would translate into an inprovement of $\yen123,456,7$ million in the net foreign assets of the Bank of Ghana after the repayment of arrears. Once the debt deferral agreements are concluded, and on the basis of donor commitments made at a Consultative Group meeting in June l995, Ghana's external financing requirements for the remainder of the three-year ESAF period should be covered.  

24. On the basis of liquidity and solvency indicators, Ghana's external debt position appears sustainable. The debt service ratio is projected to decline from 27 percent in l996 to 15 percent by the end of the decade, while the net present value of debt relative to exports at end-l995 was 219 percent and falling.  

# III. Program Monitoring  

25. Performance during the remainder of 1996 will be monitored on the basis of the following benchmarks: (a) reserve money; (b) the net domestic assets of the Bank of Ghana; (c) net domestic financing of the government budget; (d) the net foreign assets of the Bank of Ghana; (e) new nonconcessional external borrowing, with a sub-ceiling on debt of 1-5 years' maturity; (f) the stock of external short-term debt, excluding normal import-related credits; and (g) external payments arrears, with an injunction against the incurrence of new arrears (Table 1). Structural benchmarks will include (a) cocoa producer price adjustments; (b) a decision on the modalities of cocoa sector deregulation; (c) a timetable for the full decontrol of petroleum product prices; (d) progress in implementing the divestiture program, including the sale of GNPC assets; and (e) progress in the installation of expenditures monitoring and control machinery (Table 2).  

26. In summary, the Government is seeking waivers for the three financial and two structural performance criteria that were not observed for end-September 1995.  

27. In order to ensure effective monitoring of the program, the Government will make available to Fund staff all core data on a timely basis.

# Commentary on Lending Operations  

1.  The main objective of the Bank's assistance strategy is to help the Government achieve its goals of rapid economic growth, macroeconomic stability, and poverty alleviation. There are three strategic elements in the Bank's assistance program over the medium term. The first is an emphasis on achieving enhanced growth in Ghana, through an expanded role for the private sector, and to achieve this in a way that preserves macroeconomic stability. The second is improved management of public sector resources to allow the Government to be an effective partner in promoting growth. The third is to sustain the achievements envisaged through more effective human resource development and physical resource preservation.  

2.As of March 31, 1996, the World Bank Group had approved operations for Ghana totaling Us\$3,786 million, consisting of 9 loans, 60 credits and 38 IFC operations (Table attached). Over the three-year period FY 1996-98, the Bank's base case lending program for Ghana, would be Us\$790 million. More reliance will be put on sector adjustment credits.  

3. Recent and proposed Bank Group projects emphasize rehabilitation, maintenance, and institutional strengthening. These cover the transport, telecommunications, energy, water, and urban sectors. Other operations are intended to increase output and efficiency in agriculture and mining. The Bank program also includes support for enterprise and technology development.  

4. Since the formulation of Ghana's ERP in 1983, the Bank has provided a series of policy-based credits to help the Government arrest the deterioration in the economy and lay the basis for sustained growth. These credits financed urgently needed imports and supported the Government's strategy for economic recovery through the phased liberalization of exchange, trade, and price controls, the rationalization of capital and recurrent public expenditures, improvements in producer prices, privatization, and parastatal reform. In FY 92 two credits were approved, a second Financial Sector Adjustment Operation, and an Agricultural Sector Adjustment Credit, to further deepen reforms in financial and agricultural sectors. Two more adjustment operations are envisaged: in support of private sector development, a Private Sector Adjustment Credit which will focus on accelerated privatization, is expected to be approved in FY95. Three other sector credits are planned to finance investments in roads and highways, and in the health and basic education sectors. Increased attention is being paid to longer-term issues, including human resource development, agriculture, infrastructure, and the environment.

# 1. Introduction  

This appendix extends the analysis of the medium-term prospects and assesses Ghana's debt sustainability over the next 20-year period. The assessment indicates that, although Ghana's economy is vulnerable to adverse external shocks, its external debt situation is sustainable over the projection period. The projected debt service burden remains manageable and is consistent with the envisaged rates of economic growth. The analysis assumes that the thrust of the economic and financial policies to be pursued by Ghana would remain the same as set out in the medium-term policy framework. This analysis has been undertaken in collaboration with the World Bank staff, which is in agreement with its conclusions.  

# 2. Structure of external debt and debt strategy  

At the end of l994 nominal value of Ghana's total external debt stock was $\cos5.4$ billion, l/ which was equivalent to about l04 percent of 1994 GDP and the ratio of present value of debt to exports of goods and services was 249 percent (Table 16). Nearly 63 percent of this debt was owed to multilateral creditors (including the IMF), of which almost 65 percent was owed to IDA/World Bank, 2l percent to the IMF, and nearly 10 percent to the African Development Fund. More than 60 percent of Ghana's external debt is on concessional terms. Its nonconcessional debt is owed mainly to the African Development Bank, the International Fund for Agricultural Development, and some to the IBRD.  

Debt service obligations in l994 were equivalent to almost 30 percent of exports of goods and services of the same year, of which approximately 48 percent were to multilateral institutions. Debt service to IDA/IBRD accounted for nearly 3 percent of exports of goods and services. The debt service ratio rose to nearly 40 percent in l995, owing to payment of a government-guaranteed loan by the Ashanti Goldfield Company. 2/ Ghana is among the 4l \*heavily indebted poor countries.\* On the basis of the threshold ranges for debt sustainability of 20o-250 percent for the ratio of present value of total debt stock to exports of goods and services, and 20-25 percent for the debt service, the current levels of these indicators for Ghana are high; however, its debt burden is mainly official, particularly multilateral debt.  

The burden of the external public debt on the budget (external public debt service/government revenue) was 35 percent in l994, and rose to  

approximately 4l percent in l995, owing to the above-mentioned payment of a government-guaranteed loan by the Ashanti Goldfield Company.  

Since the beginning of the Economic Recovery Program (ERP) in 1983, Ghana has not rescheduled its external debt--except for a recent deferral of limited amounts in arrears--and intends to pursue a debt management strategy that will not necessitate a future rescheduling. l/  

3． Baseline scenario: Economic policy framework and projections  

It is projected that in 1996 the debt-service ratio will be 27 percent and the ratio of present value of debt stock to exports of goods and services will be 196 percent. These indicators are projected to decline to 15 percent and l29 percent, respectively, by the end of the decade. The fiscal burden is projected to decline to 31 percent in 1996 and would remain on average approximately the same for the next two years.  

The main assumptions underlying the baseline scenario are that real GDP will grow on average during l996-2005 at a rate just under 6 percent and thereafter will gradually increase to 7 percent by 2015. This trend of GDP growth is predicated on continued fiscal consolidation and structural reforms that will facilitate increases in savings and investment and an expansion in the role of the private sector, particularly in agriculture, mining, and services. It is assumed (Table 17) that during 1996-2000 the volume of exports would grow on average by 7-8 percent; Ghana has since 1986 been able to achieve an average export growth rate of 9 percent in real terms. It is further assumed that the volume of imports would grow on average by nearly 7 percent per annum during l996-2005 and by l2 percent thereafter, owing to a projected increase in the demand for capital goods. These assumptions would imply a current account deficit (excluding official transfers) equivalent to less than 5 percent of GDP on average for the period l996-2000, declining to less than 3 percent of GDP thereafter. 2/ International reserves would remain in the region of 5-6 months of imports, before declining slightly after 2005.  

It is assumed that new lending from IDA and other multilateral organizations would remain on average at about Us\$430 million per annum (6 percent of GDP) during 1996-2000, but would decline thereafter to about $\yen5390$ million per annum (3 percent of GDP). Official grants are projected to decline from about US\$180 million (2.4 percent of GDP) to about Us\$110 million (l percent of GDP) during the corresponding period. 1/ This decline in grants is projected to be offset by an increase in private financing in the form of direct investment as the benefits of structural adjustment take hold, and very modest recourse to borrowing on commercial terms toward the end of the projected period. 2/ The Government plans to meet its future financing requirements of about $\tt t s s2.5$ billion over 1996-98 with the help of external borrowing on highly concessional terms and with grants.  

On the basis of current expectations about future levels of external financing and continued strong policy performance, which would support the projected export and GDP growth rates, the net present value (NPV) of debt/exports and the debt service ratios are projected to decline to 129 percent and 15 percent, respectively, by the end of the decade.  

# 4. Sensitivity analysis 3/  

Some alternative scenarios were examined in order to assess the sensitivity of Ghana's debt sustainability to external shocks (Table l8). In Scenario 2 it is assumed that from 1996 onward concessional aid flows will be l0 percent lower throughout the projection period (1996-2015) and that the shortfall will be made up by recourse to nonconcessional financing; the level of reserves would remain unchanged from the baseline scenario. Results of this analysis show that, compared with the baseline scenario, the NPV of Ghana's debt as a ratio of exports of goods and services would rise on average by 2 percentage points during 1996-2000. The debt service ratio would increase by l percentage point over the same period.  

Scenario 3 assumes that earnings from export of goods would be 5 _percent lower from 1996 onward and that the shortfall would be made up by borrowing on commercial terms. Under this scenario, the NPV of the debt/exports ratio for the period 1996-2000 would rise on average by 10 percentage points, compared with the baseline scenario, while the debt service ratio would be 3 percentage points higher.  

Scenario 4, which is the most pessimistic case, is based on Scenario 3, but also assumes that GNPC will take out Us\$400 million in new nonconcessional loans during 1996-97. Results of this scenario reveal that the ratio of the NPV of debt to baseline exports will be higher on average by 28 percentage points over the period 1996-2000 when compared with the  

baseline scenario. The debt service ratio would be 4 percentage points higher, assuming no increase in exports from these projects.  

In Scenario 5 it is assumed that Ghana pays off nearly Uss500 million (US\$250 million in 1996 and 1997 each) of its nonconcessional bilateral debt and some of its short-term debt through the use of divestiture proceeds. This action would result in a sharp decline of 19 percentage points in the NPV of the debt/exports ratio.  

# 5. Summary  

The results of the debt sustainability exercise for Ghana reveal that its debt burden is sustainable in the long run provided that Ghana perseveres with the reform program. The country's external debt position is also relatively robust in the face of a variety of adverse external shocks. Ghana's sustainability will be further strengthened by its intention to use divesture proceeds to prepay expensive commercial debt. At the same time, however, Ghana will need to rely on substantial concessional assistance for some years to come in order to support its reform efforts.

Despite some weaknesses, Ghana's overall economic statistics have functioned reasonably satisfactorily for the purpose of monitoring performance under successive Fund-supported programs. The areas where improvements need to be made are identified below.  

# National accounts  

GDP estimates by activity and expenditure are available, but with substantial lags. The most recent data available are for 1993. With donor assistance, the authorities are currently reviewing the methodology used in preparing expenditure data. The review aims at improving the consistency of the national accounts with the balance of payments produced by the Bank of Ghana, and to improve the export and import deflators and the estimates of private savings and capital formation.  

# Prices_ and labor costs  

The weights presently used in the CPI basket are based on a 1974 survey. The Statistical Service has conducted several Living Standards Surveys (1987/88, 1988/89, and 1991/92) and it has reported that the weights in the consumption basket have not changed significantly over time. Nonetheless, a new CPI series based on revised weights is planned. The wholesale price index was discontinued in September l99l. Labor statistics in Ghana are produced with a considerable lag, of over two years in some cases; to reduce delays, technical assistance from UNDP and ILO is being used by the Ministry of Employment.  

# Public finance  

For purposes of monthly monitoring, the timeliness of public finance statistics was improved by the introduction in 1994 of a new expenditure coding system that allows data to be generated on a cash basis from the Bank of Ghana records. However, a more comprehensive breakdown of expenditures is only available with a lag of about two months. The authorities are currently introducing an expenditure tracking system, which will improve the quality of expenditure data and reduce the lags involved. Comprehensive expenditure data on a commitment basis are not available, but recent efforts have been made to get large ministries to report their expenditure commitments, and domestic arrears to the Ministry of Finance. Official estimates relating to expenditures financed by externally funded project aid are produced with a considerable lag and are subject to estimation errors owing to timing differences, exchange valuation, and recording of the technical assistance component. However, staff estimates of these expenditures are derived from balance of payments data. Consolidated fiscal accounts are not compiled because data on the core public enterprises are not reported regularly. Data on performance of some key enterprises, including the Ghana National Petroleum Corporation, are not available. However, for some enterprises a partial indication of their financial  

position can be derived from their outstanding credit balances with the banking system.  

Annual government finance data are reported for publication in the Government Finance Statistics Yearbook (GFSY) on a relatively regular basis. although the coverage and timeliness of the data could be improved. (The latest available data cover the central government only and relate to the fiscal year ending December 3l, 1993.) These data include all the major components of the GFS tables, with the exception of debt, for which the latest data published in the GFSY relate to l985. No monthly or quarterly data are reported for publication in IFS.  

# Monetary data  

Monetary data are compiled routinely, and the timeliness of the Bank of Ghana balance sheet is good. Following long-term technical assistance from the Fund's Statistics Department (STA), the compilation of the net foreign assets of the Bank of Ghana has recently showed significant improvement. The Bank of Ghana is in the process of expanding its computerization program.  

# Balance of payments and external debt  

Balance of payments estimates are only published annually. Data for exports are available on a more frequent basis, but import data are usually subject to considerable lags, owing to unreliable customs statistics. Recent computerization resulted in some improvements in the customs records, but there is scope for further improvement. With technical assistance from the Commonwealth Secretariat, there have recently been significant improvements in the debt reporting system, but efforts are being made to realize further progress in this area.  

# Publication  

Reporting to the Fund for purposes of IFS publication has recently improved. However, there are still lags of about four to five months in reporting exchange rates, monetary, price, and international transactions data. In particular, there are still serious delays in the reporting of international trade statistics. The latest detailed export and import data reported are for 1989. However, STA has been able to obtain more recent data from the UN database. The frequency of publication of the Quarterly Economic Bulletin of the Bank of Ghana has recently improved, but data are still published with lags of up to six months. The Quarterly Digest of Statistics is usually published by the Statistical Service with a longer lag. In general, statistical information from official sources is not made available on a timely basis to the public.
