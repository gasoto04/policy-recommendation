# EXECUTIVE SUMMARY  

# Background  

The Ghanaian authorities are requesting a new three-year ESAF arrangement as the current one, which was approved on June 30, 1995, expires in June l999 and cannot be extended under the new ESAF procedures.  

During 1998, fiscal and monetary policies were implemented broadly in line with the program. All midterm quantitative performance criteria were observed, except for the nominal target for the domestic primary surplus, which was, nevertheless, observed in terms of GDP. Quantitative benchmarks for the end of the year were also met, except in the fiscal area where higher-than-expected domestic capital expenditures and interest payments caused the fiscal targets to be missed. The value-added tax was reintroduced at the end of 1998 and the separate governing boards of the tax agencies were replaced by a central revenue board. The Ghana Oil Company was offered for sale in October. Progress was made on structural reforms, but key measures in the energy and cocoa sectors, such as the privatization of the Tema Oil Refinery and the Produce Buying Company (PBC) were delayed.  

# .The 1999-2001 program  

Ghana has made significant progress in reducing macroeconomic imbalances since early 1997. While macroeconomic stability will remain an important element of the government's medium-term program, a key objective for 1999--200l will be to accelerate the pace of structural reform. The government intends to take significant steps to liberalize the cocoa and energy sectors, accelerate the divestiture of state enterprises, and strengthen the banking system. Public sector reforms will be carried out to limit the scope of the government to core areas, in particular, agriculture, the social sectors, and basic infrastructure. The diversification of exports, in general, and of agricuitural exports, in particular, willbe crucial for improving agricultural growth, a key condition for poverty reduction. Further trade liberalization will help increase the efficiency of the economy.  

+ Over 1999-200l, the government intends to reduce inflation to no more than 5 percent, lower the external current account deficit, including grants, to $2\%$ percent of GDP, and bring gross oficial reserves to three months of imports. Real GDP growth is projected to increase to 6 percent in 2000-2001.  

The objectives of the l999 program are end-period inflation of 9 percent, an external current account deficit of 2.9 percent of GDP, and a US\$77 million increase in net foreign assets of the central bank. Real GDP is expected to grow by $5\%$ percent in 1999. Domestic budget financing willbe limited to about 2.8 percent of GDP and the primary domestic balance will accumulate a surplus of about $3\%$ percent of GDP. Reserve money growth will be limited to ll/ percent so as to constrain monetary growth to $14\%$ percent.  

# I. INTRODUCTION  

1. A mission visited Accra during February 11-25, 1999 to discuss a medium-term structural adjustment program covering the period January 1999-December 200l that could be supported by a new three-year arrangement under the Enhanced Structural Adjustment Facility (ESAF).' The midterm review of the second annual arrangement under the current three-year ESAF arrangement and the l998 Article IV consultation were concluded by the Executive Board on December 2, 1998. The government's letter of intent in Appendix I requests a new three-year ESAF arrangement and a reduction of the extension of the commitment period of the current three-year arrangement (EBS/95/76, Sup. 1) to the time elapsed at the time of the Board's approval of the new three-year ESAF arrangement. The commitment period of the current three-year arrangement was extended by one year on March 23, 1998 and cannot be extended any further under the ESAF Trust Instrument, as amended, which now limits to one year the period of extension of a commitment period. The current commitment period, which is due to expire on June 29, 1999, would not allow for the approval and disbursement of allthe loans under a third annual arrangement. It would also prevent the approval of a new three-year arrangement before June 29, 1999, since the existing and new commitment periods cannot overlap under Section II, 1(b) of the ESAF Trust Instrument. Relations with the Fund are summarized in Appendix II and relations with the World Bank Group are described in Appendix III. A debt sustainability analysis is presented ir Appendix IV, and statistical issues are covered in Appendix V.  

2. In concluding the 1998 Article IV consultation and midterm review, Executive Directors commended Ghana's macroeconomic performance under the program for 1998. They noted the importance of continued financial discipline and the need to accelerate the pace of structural reform. They emphasized the importance of a successful introduction of the value-added tax (VAT) and the need to solve the problem of expenditure arrears in the road sector. Directors urged the implementation of productivity-enhancing initiatives in health and education to improve Ghana's social indicators and external competitiveness. They supported substantive measures to strengthen the banking sector, including decisive action to merge, liquidate, or privatize weak banks still in operation. Directors also encouraged decisive action on reforms in the cocoa and energy sectors, and warned against intervention in the foreign exchange market to slow the depreciation of the cedi.  

# II. PERFORMANCE UNDER THE CURRENT ESAF-SUPPORTED PROGRAM  

# A. Overview  

3. The second three-year ESAF arrangement, approved on June 30, 1995, aimed at reducing the inflation and financial imbalances triggered by a large increase in public spending in 1992, when Ghana held its first free elections under a new democratic constitution. This arrangement also supported major structural reforms designed to reduce government involvement in the economy.  

4. Performance in 1996, however, was mixed. On one hand, the economy grew by almost 5 percent and inflation was halved from 71 percent at the end of I995 to 33 percent at end-1996 (Table 1). On the other hand, in the second half of l996, large slippages in fiscal performance in the runup to the elections took the program off track. The external situation deteriorated partly because the terms of trade worsened, but also because donors became concerned with the increasingly expansionary policies and reduced foreign aid flows. While not all structural performance criteria were observed, structural reforms progressed as state enterprises were privatized, farmers received a larger share of the export price for cocoa, and private buyers were allowed to purchase cocoa from farmers.  

5. Ghana's macroeconomic performance improved in 1997 as budgetary discipline was restored and current account deficits contained, with the inflation rate declining to 2l percent from 33 percent in the previous year. The budget showed a significant domestic primary surplus of 3.2 percent of GDP, the highest level since commencement of the economic reform program in 1983. The automatic pricing mechanism for setting ex refinery prices of petroleum products with import prices was reinstated. These positive developments cleared the way for the second annual arrangement, which focused on further reductions in macroeconomic imbalances and the deepening of structural reforms.  

# B. Performance in 1998  

6. During 1998, fiscal and monetary policies were implemented broadly in line with the program. All midterm quantitative performance criteria were observed, except for the nominal target for the domestic primary surplus, which was, nevertheless, observed in terms of GDP. Quantitative benchmarks for the end of the year were also met, except in the fiscal area where higher-than-expected domesticaly financed capital expenditure and interest payments caused the fiscal targets to be missed. Progress was made on structural reforms, but key measures in the energy and cocoa sectors, such as the privatization of the Tema Oil Refinery and the Produce Buying Company (PBC), the state enterprise that purchases 50-60 percent of the cocoa crop, were delayed.  

# C. Real Growth and Prices  

7. Real GDP growth increased from 4.2 percent in 1997 to 4.6 percent in 1998 despite the drought-related energy crisis in the first half of the year. Because of the crisis, growth was about l percentage point less than originally projected. The crisis caused inflation to increase in March and April, owing to adverse effects on production and food supply. However, inflation resumed its declining trend in May and fell below 16 percent by the end of the year (Figure 1).  

# D. Fiscal Developments  

8. The overall budget deficit on a commitment basis, including grants, was 8.1 percent of GDP in 1998, a decline of almost 2 percentage points of GDP from the previous year. Total revenue was below its projected level for the year as taxpayers waited for the reintroduction of the VAT in late December before rebuilding stocks. Domestic primary expenditure was broadly as programmed. As a result, the domestic primary surplus was 3.6 percent of GDP, which was less than targeted but an improvement from the 3.2 percent surplus in 1997 (Table 2 and Figure 2). Total expenditure exceeded its programmed level as domestic interest payments ran 1.4 percent of GDP higher than projected under the program (Table 3).2 Also, the government, confronted with large road sector arrears, made an effort to reduce them beyond what had been contemplated in the program.3 Consequently, net domestic financing exceeded its target under the program by almost 2 percent of GDP.  

9. Revenue reached 18.3 percent of GDP in 1998, an increase of l percentage point of GDP over 1997. Tariff measures werc implemented in March 1998 to reduce the number of zero-rated goods and exemptions. To facilitate the shifting of zero-rated goods to a positive rate, a 5 percent rate was added to the existing rates of 10 percent and 25 percent. The VAT was reintroduced at the end of the year. The govermment replaced the separate governing boards of the tax agencies by a central revenue board to better coordinate activities and facilitate tax administration.

# Box 1. Medium-Term Erpenditure Framework  

The medium-term expenditure framework (MTEF) is a comprehensive approach to budget preparation that integrates investment and recurent expenditure into a unified three-year roling budget.The MTEF is based on a planning exercise that combines top-down funding discipline and a decision-making process in whichall govemment agencies participate. It clarifies the procedures,costs, and potential outcomes ofresource allcation, thus requiring policy-makers to make choices based on comparisons of costs and benefits.  

The MTEF processin Ghana started formally in June with cabinet approval for issuing the l999 budget circular with preliminary ceilings for l99-200l derived from the policy framework paper. This was followed by a review of the responsibilities and objectives of ministries and their supervising departments ahd agencies (MDAs), the outputs produced to a chieve these objectives, and the activities required to produce the outputs.MTEF workshops spanning the months of July and August provided senior managers and personnel from ll MDAs and selected statutory institutions hands-on experience in applying the MTEF methodology.The process entailed the preparation of a strategic plan byeach MDA to formthe basis of budget estimates.Both at the aggregate and sectoral levels, an attempt was made to capture all expenditures, including those financed by MDAs' internally generated funds and external sources. Although the MTEF was to be impiemented initially in the priority sectors of education, health, androads,the authorities realizedthat its success depended on including allMDA's,rather than trying to pursue a dual budget process, and therefore decided to implement it for the entire budget.  

The implementation of the MTEF approach to budgeting in Ghana is encouraging. In addition to the early expansion of its budget coverage,other developments include preparation of misson statements for all MDAs, the basing of budget estimates on the explicit costing of activities that are required to meet MDAs'objectives, and the withdrawal ofthe Ministry of Finance from involvement in the details associated with incrcmental budgeting by MDAs.  

# E. Monetary and Financial Sector Developments  

11. The Bank of Ghana continued to tighten monetary policy by reducing the growth rate of reserve money to 17 percent during 1998 from 33 percent during 1997. As a result, broad money expanded by 18 percent, as compared with 4l percent during 1997, in line with its target (Table 4). Net domestic financing of the government by the banking system declined from about 4 percent of GDP in 1997 to 2 percent of GDP in 1998, which allowed credit to the private sector to expand by 31 percent, well in excess of nominal GDP growth.  

12.The 9l-day treasury bill rate declined to 26 percent in 1998 after remaining above 40 percent from the end of 1995 to the end of 1997. The decline in interest rates and inflationary expectations allowed the central bank to reduce its rediscount rate to 37 percent in November 1998 and to 32 percent in January 1999. In an effort to improve secondary market operations, the Bank of Ghana introduced a centralized book entry system in July and a master repurchase agreement in August.  

13.During 1998, the cedi depreciated 4.3 percent against the U.S. dollar, an appreciation of 4.1 percent in real effective terms (Figure 1). While the real effective exchange rate has appreciated during 1998 as a whole, it has depreciated since August 1998. The foreign exchange bureau rate remained within $2-2\%$ percent of the interbank rate. The real effective appreciation of the cedi has raised concerns among some market observers about possible excessive intervention to support the cedi and a loss of external competitiveness. Regarding central bank intervention in the foreign exchange market, although gross international reserves did not increase in l998, a year in which exports were growing strongly, net foreign assets of the central bank increased by US\$100 million, $\ensuremath{\mathbf{US}}\$60$ million in excess of the program, mostly as a result of large repayments of foreign liabilities in the Bank of Ghana's balance sheet, including debt to the Fund. Regarding loss of competitiveness, the analysis of a broad range of indicators shows a mixed picture with no clear-cut evidence of loss of competitiveness in 1998 (Figure 2). Moreover, nontraditional exports continued to grow strongly (54 percent) in 1998, albeit from a low base.  

# F. External Sector Developments  

14. Ghana's current account deficit, including official transfers, improved markedly in 1998 to $3\%$ percent of GDP from $9$ percent of GDP in 1997, reflecting strong export growth led by a good cocoa crop (Table 5). Net foreign assets of the Bank of Ghana increased by US\$100 million during 1998, despite smaller official capital inflows than in 1997 and higher amortization of external public debt. Nontraditional exports continued to grow sharply, expanding their share in Ghana's export value. Ghana met its external payments obligations, although small payments to Italy were delayed. Amortization payments for the large nonconcessional borrowing contracted in 1996 and 1997 weakened the capital account, but these were outweighed by the current account improvement.

The PURC also raised water tariffs to eliminate operating losses. # HI. MEDIUM-TERM ADJUSTMENT STRATEGY  

16. Ghana has made significant progress in reducing macroeconomic imbalances since early 1997. While macroeconomic stability is an important element of the government's medium-term program, the objective of making Ghana a middle-income country by 2020 cannot be attained without decisive structural transformation. Consequently, another key objective for 1999-200l will be to accelerate the pace of structural reform. The government intends to take significant steps to liberalize the cocoa and energy sectors, and strengthen the banking system. Public sector reforms will be carried out to limit the scope of the government to core areas, in particular, agriculture, the social sectors, and basic infrastructure. Achieving solid export growth is also crucial to ensure that Ghana's external position remains sustainable. The diversification of exports, in general, and of agricultural exports, in particular. will be crucial for improving agricultural growth, a key condition for poverty reduction.  

17. Over l999-2001, the government intends to reduce inflation to no more than 5 percent, lower the external current account deficit, including grants, to 2/2 percent of GDP, and bring gross official reserves to three months of imports. Real GDP growth is projected to increase to 6 percent, thus alowing output per capita to increase by 3 percent on average in the next three years. The share of private investment in GDP is expected to rise by over 3 percentage points, as private sector activity is boosted by rising confidence and expanding business opportunities, such as in nontraditional exports (Table 9).  

18. The government's macroeconomic objectives will be supported by adherence to prudent financial policies. The government will move toward overall budget balance, achieving domestic primary surpluses of at least 3/ percent of GDP, thereby reducing interest payments and ensuring that the ratio of domestic debt to GDP begins to decline by 2001. The net domestic financing of the government is projected to fallto 1.3 percent of GDP in 2001. The strengthening of the fiscal position is to be achieved by restraining the growth of expenditures and by increasing tax revenue colections to about 16/ percent of GDP in 1999- 2001—notwithstanding the planned reduction of cocoa taxation and the tariff reform-through a broadening of the tax base and an improvement of tax administration. However, expenditures in health and education will be protected.  

19. Ghana's external situation will improve gradually over i999-2001, with the current account narrowing from $3\%$ percent of GDP in 1998 to $2\%$ percent of GDP in 2001, and afterward declining slowly to about 2 percent over the medium term. Growth in the value of imports will be slow because of weak prices, in particular, of oil. Exports are projected to increase moderately because of an expected decline in the cocoa crop in l999 and lower world prices. Gross international reserves willrise to three months of imports by 200l and remain at this level over the medium term.  

20. Ghana is one of the heavily indebted poor countries (HIPC) that has not fully used traditional debt relief mechanisms. An updated debt sustainability analysis, carried out in collaboration with the World Bank staff and the authorities, is reported in Appendix IV. It concludes that Ghana's external debt burden, though vulnerable to strong adverse external shocks, remains sustainable over the medium term without recourse to debt relief, provided Ghana adheres to a prudent external debt management strategy and adjustment efforts envisaged in the proposed program.  

21. Ghana's public external debt, albeit held mostly on concessional terms, is relatively large at almost US\$6 billion (79 percent of GDP) at the end of 1998. On a net present value basis, the outstanding external debt of Ghana was equivalent to 198 percent of exports of goods and nonfactor services. The debt service ratio under the baseline scenario was 28 percent, but was expected to fall below 20 percent by 200l without the use of traditional debt-relief mechanisms. These results, however, presuppose strict compliance with a program of macroeconomic adjustment and structural reform, including adherence to limits on nonconcessional borrowing and strong export growth, as well as the absence of external or production shocks.  

22. Over the medium term, foreign aid is projected to decline but to remain at levels consistent with the program's import and growth targets. An alternative scenario has been developed to show the effects of a shortfallin foreign aid, which may cause Ghana to resort to nonconcessional loans. Another two scenarios cumulatively add reductions in exports growth and declines in cocoa prices to the first alternative scenario (Appendix Table 12). While in all of these scenarios the net present value of debt-to-exports ratio remains below 200 percent in the medium term, the debt-service ratio would be high in all alternative scenarios and, in the least favorable cases, would not decline below 20 percent even in the medium term.

24. This revenue level will need to be achieved in spite of expected declines in revenue owing to tariff reform and lower taxes on cocoa exports. Although the VAT was set at a relatively low rate of 10 percent, the authorities expect that it will produce higher revenues than the previous sales tax because of the expansion of the tax base to cover the retail sector and because of improvements in tax administration, including the taxpayer identification system and the establishment of a large taxpayers unit.7 Also, the authorities are making efforts to curtail tax and tariff exemptions. Moreover, the decline in world oil prices has made it possible for the government to extend the tax on petroleum products to more products. # B. Monetary Policy  

31.Monetary policy in 1999 will be directed at reducing inflation to 9 percent by the end of the year. Consistent with this objective and with projected real GDP growth of $5\%$ percent, the Bank of Ghana will aim at limiting monetary growth to $14\%$ percent. The monetary growth target will be achieved by limiting the growth of reserve money to lll/2 percent. An increase in the money multiplier is expected as the financial deepening continues. Bank financing of the budget is projected to be C210 billion, about 1 percent of GDP, allowing credit to the private sector to expand by $17\%$ percent. The monetary authorities intend to improve liquidity projections, which have in the past caused problems for monetary control. In that context, the government will step up its efforts to prepare accurate weekly cashflow projections. The projected reduction in inflation and domestic financing of the budget, along with improvements in the operation of the treasury bill market, including greater use of repurchase agreements, is expected to support a further decline in interest rates.  

32. The market wil continue to determine Ghana's exchange rate, with the Bank of Ghana's interventions directed at dampening short-run volatility of the cedi and achieving the program target of increasing its net foreign assets by US\$77 million during 1999. Gross international reserves are projected to rise to about 2.7 months of imports. The Bank of Ghana will take steps before end-1999 to deepen the foreign exchange market, and has requested Fund assistance in achieving this objective. In this context, surrender requirements will be assessed and, if needed, modified.  

# C. External Policy  

33. Ghana's external position in 1999 is expected to improve.only marginally as exports increase slightly, owing to the projected decline in the export volume and price of cocoa, and to modest growth in gold export receipts. The slow growth in the value of imports reflects in large part the projected decline in oil prices. The volumes of both oil and non-oil imports (other than those tied to externally financed projects) are expected to expand broadly in line with real GDP. The projected current account deficit for 1999 is 2.9 percent of GDP, lower than the 3.5 percent of GDP recorded in 1998.  

34. Ghana's total external financing requirements during 1999-200l are estimated at about $\mathrm{\bfUSS2.5}$ billion (Table 8). For 1999, the program is fully financed, while for 2000-01 financing will need to be firmed up at the next Consultative Group meeting, planned for late 1999.  

# D. Structural Issues  

35. The government's program of structural reforms is described in the memorandum of economic and financial policies for 1999 (Appendix I) and policy framework paper (EBD/99/53, 4/14/99). Key reform areas in 1999 include the cocoa and energy sectors, the banking system, and divestiture program. The medium-term strategy for the cocoa sector is described in Box 2. It has announced that it will ensure equal access to Cocobod's (the cocoa marketing board) crop financing and warehousing facilities for all licensed buying companies. The government has also defined clear targets for its divestiture progran and rescinded the freeze on the licensing of new banks.

# G. Access, Capacity to Repay, and Risks  

45. Proposed access under the ESAF arrangement will be 42 percent of Ghana's quota under the Eleventh General Review of Quotas, which translates to about 57 percent of the quota under the Ninth General Review of Quotas (equivalent to SDR 155 million), to be disbursed in seven equal installments. As this will be Ghana's third three-year ESAF arrangement, access is appropriately lower than in the earlier three-year arrangement, which was approved at 60 percent of quota (of which 50 percent of quota was drawn). At the same time, the arrangement allows some protection from uncertainties in the cocoa sector (where prices have been plunging in recent months) and recognizes the fact that Ghana was not in a position to benefit fully from the commitment under the earlier three-year ESAF arrangement owing to a change in the ESAF Trust Instrument, which now limits to one year the period of extension of a commitment period.  

46. Ghana made payments to the Fund of US\$173 million in 1997 and US\$148 million in 1998, representing 32 percent and 26 percent of total debt service due, respectively. Starting from 1999 these will taper offto 6 percent of total debt service due in 2003, before rising subsequently to over 16 percent in 207, assuming the request for the new three-year ESAF arrangement is approved (Table 7). Given this profile and also Ghana's past excellent record of meeting its debt-service obligations to the Fund, it is expected that the government will discharge these future obligations in a timely manner.  

47. Ghana's macroeconomic stability and structural reforms were adversely affected by election year pressures both in 1992 and 1996, when expansionary financial policies derailed the adjustment effort. Similar events cannot be allowed to happen in 20o0, when new  

elections are expected to take place. While the government seems firm in its resolve to carry out the much-needed reforms, it is imperative that strong progress be achieved early in the arrangement period to generate momentum and discourage backtracking in 200o. The program therefore emphasizes prior actions and policy announcements, as well as close monitoring of macroeconomic trends and medium-term budgetary procedures, such as the MTEF.  

48. There is significant consensus within the Ghanaian civil society in favor of focusing government priorities on core issues, including agriculture, education, health, and basic infrastructure. Progress in these areas should strengthen support for the program. The authorities believe strongly in consensus building, and the cocoa strategy, in particular, has been widely discussed with stakeholders. While consensus building reduces the likelihood of policy reversals, it sometimes results in delays and in overly cautious approaches to problems. A strong leadership role by government will therefore continue to be needed to strike a reasonable balance, particularly in areas that may prove controversial, such as in divestiture. While divestiture is accepted by a majority, there is no consensus about the approach to reach that objective: either share flotation in the stock exchange or direct sales. If the government is not able to stand firm in individual divestiture cases, it is possible that the process will continue to show stop-and-go patterns.  

49. Ghana has better than average human resources when compared with other subSaharan countries. However, technical assistance is stillneeded to improve program implementation, although the government is sometimes reluctant to request early assistance before problems become serious.13 Technical assistance in the form of short-term visits by experts, leaving policy implementation clearly in the hands of Ghanaian nationals, could be a way to resolve the problem, while at the same time increasing program ownership.  

# V. STAFF APPRAISAL  

50. Ghana's economic performance in 1998 was strong despite serious energy shortages early in the year. Real GDP growth increased, inflation fell, real interest rates plummeted, the overall budget deficit declined, the external current account improved, and gross international reserves rose. Quantitative benchmarks for December 1998 were observed, except for the domestic primary surplus, which nevertheless increased by about $\nu_{2}$ of l percentage point of GDP, and the net domestic financing of the government, which was missed because of higherthan-expected interest payments. The Bank of Ghana kept reserve money within its target, and broad money expansion was in line with the program.  

51. The structural reforms appear to be picking up speed and the commitment to reforms strengthening. While the offrs for sale of the PBC and the Tema Oil Refinery were postponed by one year, a number of important structural measures were undertaken: the VAT was reintroduced; a central revenue board was set up to coordinate revenue agencies; tariff dispersion was reduced; the medium-term expenditure framework was introduced ahead of schedule; electricity and water tariffs were increased sharply; the Ghana National Petroleum Corporation debt to the central bank was repaid; the Ghana Oil Company was offered for sale; and the producers' share in the f.o.b. price of cocoa was raised.  

52. On the downside, the accumulation of domestic arrears to road contractors and the continued real effective appreciation of the cedi raised questions about the effectiveness of existing expenditure controls and the operation of the floating exchange rate system, respectively. Regarding road arrears, the government has already moved to terminate problem-prone contracts and has put in place a plan for eliminating existing arrears. Regarding exchange rates, the macroeconomic policies in place are consistent with a greater stability of the real effective exchange rate in 1999. The staff has found little evidence of the use of the use of administrative measures in support of the cedi. As part of the program, the Bank of Ghana has agreed to review existing foreign exchange market procedures, including surrender. requirements, with a view to deepen the market, and has requested Fund assistance in this area. It reiterated that its market presence will continued to be guided by the net foreign assets target with only minimal intervention to avoid exchange rate volatility.  

53. In his address at the opening of the parliament in January 1999, President Rawlings highlighted the commitment of his government to the “Ghana--Vision 2020" strategy. The strategy calls for a commitment to an open and liberal market economy, underpinned by measures to foster private investment, human development, basic infrastructure, and a supportive legal and administrative environment. The structural adjustment program in support of which the Ghanaian authorities are requesting a new three-year ESAF arrangement is fully consistent with the Ghana-Vision 2020 strategy, but it sets shorter-term objectives to ensure that this development strategy is implemented without delay.  

54.The staff agrees that the program's focus on cocoa sector reforms, bank restructuring, and divestiture is appropriate. Without significant progress in these areas, the objective of transforming Ghana into a middle-income country within one generation cannot be attained. Strong macroeconomic performance will, however, continue to be crucial and will depend on further fiscal consolidation, including the success of the VAT, strengthening of expenditure controls, particularly on road projects, and public sector reforms. Limits on net government borrowing both domestically and externally, and particularly on nonconcessional terms, will also be important to ensure that interest costs and government indebtedness remain sustainable.  

55.Proper administration of the VAT willbe critical. The staffis encouraged by the resources, collection enforcement, and audit plans of the VAT Service. However, the staff believes that equity and eficiency considerations will require reductions in cocoa export taxes and average tariff rates over the medium term. These measures are likely to cause revenue losses and require an increase in the VAT rate--by 20Ol, at the latest. The authorities agree in principle with the staff but would prefer to increase the VAT rate only after taxpayer acceptance of the tax is secure. The staff is wiling to wait another year before making a final assessment of the need for a VAT rate increase.  

56. The staff is encouraged by the progress made in the implementation of the mediumterm expenditure framework and associated improvement in budget preparation and decision making. The continued success of the MTEF in Ghana will depend on making improvements in several critical related areas, including timely release of funds for budget execution (including foreign assistance), accurate cash flow forecasting, effective expenditure management and control, and macroeconomic performance monitoring for ongoing review of MTEF assumptions. The government shouid, therefore, persist in its efforts in this area, with the assistance of the Fund and World Bank.  

57. The government is taking a deliberate approach to the public sector reforms, consulting extensively with stakeholders and the World Bank. However, the implementation calendar cannot continue to be extended into the future. The staf supports the introduction of the new and streamlined wage structure, but it is concerned that the new structure will result in an increase, albeit modest, of the wage bill as a proportion of GDP. While the wage billis expected to decline in subsequent years, this development is conditional on progress in commercializing, restructuring, and liquidating subvented and central management agencies. Since the pilot project for reform of subvented agencies is moving slower than originally planned and is now expected to be completed in 2001 only, the staff urges the authorities to accelerate completion of this task.  

58.The medium-term cocoa strategy is cautious, but the authorities have indicated their willingness to take additional measures in response to developments in cocoa markets and as they gain experience with more open marketing and export arrangements. Extensive consultations with stakeholders should ensure that the announced reforms command considerable support in the cocoa sector and will not be easily reversed.  

59. The staff is encouraged by the authorities' commitment to take swift actions, including bank closures, to remove existing weaknesses in the financial sector while fostering competition. The staff believes that the financial sector measures scheduled for implementation in 1999, including the withdrawal of licenses from banks not complying with prudential requirements, modernization of the central bank and banking laws, strengthening of bank supervision, tightening of prudential regulations, divestiture of state-owned banks, greater use of repurchase agreements, and strengthening of the payments system, represent a comprehensive plan of action for achieving a strong, efficient financial sector.  

60. The divestiture work program proposed by the authorities is appropriately ambitious covering a large number of enterprises, including a critical mass of large enterprises and financial institutions. In the past, however, the program has experienced difficulties in  

completing divestitures, owing to lack of investor interest--in some cases, as a result of due diligences that exposed deficiencies in the enterprises offered for sale. The staff urges the authorities to engage experienced sales advisors as a way of generating additional investor interest and to be realistic in their estimation of the value of the enterprises offered for sale. The staff believes that the measures aimed at increasing the transparency of the divestiture process will help make the program successful.  

61. The improvement in Ghana's balance of payments in 1998 was significant, but estimates for l999 show a slowdown in export growth, owing to the cocoa production cycle and a weakening of world prices for Ghana's exports. In 1999-—and even more markedly in 2000—there will be a bulge in debt-service payments. These trends underscore the continuing vulnerability of the external situation of Ghana, a conclusion that was confirmed by the debt sustainability analysis. The government willbe approaching donors late in the year with requests for assistance in 20oo 0l. As long as the authorities maintain their commitment to sound economic policies, the assistance would be justified, and early indications of support would be helpful for planning purposes.  

62. In summary, the staff is of the view that the proposed adjustment program is appropriate, containing an ambitious but achievable agenda of structural reforms. In light of the actions already taken and the strength of the program, the staff recommends approval by the Executive Board of the authorities' request for a new three-year ESAF arrangement. The staff also supports the request for a reduction of the commitment period of the present threeyear ESAF arrangement (EBS/95/76, Sup. 1; 6/27/95).

# Memorandum of Economic and Financial Policies of the Government of Ghana for 1999  

# I. INTRODUCTION  

1． In January 1995, the President of Ghana presented to parliament “Ghana—-Vision 2020 (The First Step: 1996-2000)," a coordinated program of economic and social development policies aimed at making Ghana a middle-income country within one generation.1 The program cals for sustained economic growth to be achieved through an open and liberal market economy, supported by measures to foster private investment, human development, and basic infrastructure, and a supportive legal and administrative environment. Rural and urban development issues are also emphasized to help sustain a rise in the standard of living of the population. This memorandum of economic and financial policies provides an overview of Ghana's performance in 1998, and sets out the government's objectives for 1999-2001 and the program for l999, including quantitative and structural benchmarks to guide policy implementation during the year.  

2. In June 1995, a three-year Enhanced Structural Adjustment Facility (ESAF) arrangement was approved by the Fund in support of the government's program. This arrangement focused on reducing macroeconomic imbalances and redirecting government efforts away from activities that could be provided more efficiently by the private sector. Despite temporary setbacks, the program has broadly achieved its objectives. The inflation rate was brought down from 71 percent at end-1995 to 16 percent at end-1998. Real growth averaged 4.5 percent per year in the period 1996-98, and the domestic primary surplus increased from 1.5 percent of GDP in 1995 to 3.6 percent of GDP. On the structural front, the farmers' share of the f.o.b. export price of cocoa was increased, private buyers were allowed to purchase cocoa from farmers, access of the national petroleum company to central bank credit was eliminated, and steps were taken to deregulate petroleum product marketing. Moreover, 16 medium-sized public enterprises and 34 smallpubiic enterprises were privatized or liquidated in the context of the ESAF-supported program and the World Bank private sector adjustment credit (July 1995--February 1998).  

3． The government recognizes that, irrespective of the progress achieved since 1995, Ghana cannot afford a lapse in its adjustment effort. Therefore, the government will act resolutely to consolidate macroeconomic stability and strengthen its structural reforms, particularly in the cocoa and banking sectors, which are critical for raising real GDP growth rates toward poverty-reducing levels  

# II. PERFORMANCE IN 1998  

4. In 1998, real GDP grew by 4.6 percent, or by about l percentage point less than originally projected, as a result of the drought-related energy crisis early in the year. The inflation rate fell to 16 percent at end-1998 from 21 percent at end-1997. Most quantitative benchmarks for December I998 were observed, except for those in the fiscal area. Total revenue was below its targeted level as taxpayers waited for the reintroduction of the valueadded tax (VAT) before rebuilding their stocks. The domestic primary surplus fell short of its floor but stilincreased to 3.6 percent of GDP from 3.2 percent in 1997. Net domestic financing of the government exceeded its program benchmark, mainly because of higher-thanexpected interest payments and road arrears payments. Deviations from fiscal targets did not cause reserve money and broad money to exceed their original targets, and net foreign assets of the Bank of Ghana comfortably exceeded their target level, helped by strong cocoa exports. The external current account deficit (including grants) fell by about 5 percentage points of GDP, narrowing the deficit to $3\%$ percent of GDP. The cedi depreciated by 4.3 percent against the U.S. dollar during 1998, an appreciation of 4.1 percent in real effective terms.  

# III. OBJECTIVES AND POLICIES FOR 1999-2001  

5． Objectives. The government's overall objective is to consolidate the gains achieved so far in stabilizing the macroeconomic environment, and to accelerate structural reforms. During the next three years, real GDP growth rate is projected at 5.5 percent in 1999 and at least 6 percent in 2000 01. The program aims at reducing the inflation rate to 9 percent in 1999 (end of period) and to not more than 5 percent in the following two years; and limiting the external current account deficit, including grants, to 2.9 percent of GDP in 1999 and $2\%$ percent in 2000/01. Meanwhile, the government will endeavor to increase net foreign assets of the central bank during l999-200l so as to attain gross official reserves of at least three months of imports by 2001.  

6. Financial policies. The central objective of the government's medium-term fiscal policy is to move toward a balanced overall budget, achieving primary surpluses to reduce the interest burden. The government aims at maintaining the domestic primary surplus above $3\%$ percent of GDP while ensuring that the stock of domestic debt begins to decline by 2001. These fiscal objectives are expected to be achieved by increasing the total revenue-to-GDP ratio to about 19 percent by broadening the tax base and improving tax administration notwithstanding the planned reduction of cocoa taxation and tarif reform-—and by restraining the growth of expenditures. However, expenditures in health and education will be protected. Monetary policy will aim at reducing inflation to not more than 5 percent in 20Ol by limiting monetary expansion to that consistent with nominal GDP growth.  

7. Structural reforms. During 1999-200l, the government will accelerate the implementation of structural reforms so as to encourage private savings and investment, and to foster efficient resource allocation. It willtransfer the purchase of the cocoa crop to private licensed marketing agents, while allowing them to begin exporting a significant share of the crop. The government will also increase farmers' income by reducing the taxation of cocoa and the Cocobod's (cocoa marketing board) share. To improve the allocation of financial savings, the government will foster the development of a sound and competitive banking system. It will take steps to'complete the divestiture of its shares in financial institutions other than the Agricultural Development Bank.² Banks that by end-September 1999 fail to meet the capital adequacy requirements of the Banking Law of 1989 will have their banking licenses withdrawn. Also, the government will be vigorously implementing a divestiture program, which will cover about 80 state-owned enterprises during the first two years of the ESAF arrangement. In so doing, it will enhance the transparency of the divestiture program through timely publication of audited financial statements of the Divestiture Implementation Committee. The government will continue to implement public sector reforms aimed at refocusing its priorities on core areas, in particular agriculture, the social sectors, and basic infrastructure. Finally, actions to overhaul the energy sector will remain crucial, was highlighted by the early 1998 crisis.  

# IV. PROGRAM FOR 1999  

# A. Financial Policies  

8. Fiscal policy. The overall deficit in the 1999 budget, on a commitment basis, is projected at $5\%$ percent of GDP, a $2\%$ percentage point decline from 1998. The domestic primary balance is expected to show a surplus of at least $3\%$ percent of GDP in 1999. Revenue is projected to rise to almost 19 percent of GDP, driven by the curtailment of tax exemptions and improvements in administration, including the introduction of the VAT and the taxpayer identification system. Noninterest recurrent spending and domestically financed capital expenditure willbe maintained broadly constant as a share of GDP, with spending on education and health increasing as a share of both total expenditure and GDP under the program. The net domestic financing of the budget will be limited.to 2.8 percent of GDP in 1999, but it will be reduced to below 1'/ percent of GDP in 2001 to help lower interest rates and avoid a crowding out of the private sector.  

9. Domestic arrears. Road arrears amounting to C130 billion were outstanding at the end of 1998, owing to contracts that allowed road construction to be carried out even in the absence of full budgetary allocation. To resolve this situation, the government has decided to terminate a number of contracts, to scale down domestically financed road projects, and to revise procedures for initiating projects and awarding new road contracts in line with World Bank procurement procedures. Outstanding road arrears wil be reduced by C60 billion in 1999 and will be eliminated by end-2000.  

10. Tax measures. The VAT was successfully reintroduced in December 1998 as a first step in the government's strategy to increase the tax base and the eficiency and equity of the tax system. Other elements of this strategy include reductions in the cocoa export tax, implementation of a tariff structure that supports eficient production and competitiveness, significant reductions in tax and tariff exemptions, and steps to improve tax administration through closer collaboration among tax agencies.  

11. Tariff reforms. The government will continue to review the tariff structure, with a view to enhancing the extemal competitiveness of local industry, harmonizing tariff rates with regional practices, and removing distortions. To prevent smuggling, the government will reduce the top tariff rate to the target set by the West African Economic and Monetary Union (20 percent) in the context of the 200o budget. While taking steps to curtail exemptions, the government will gradually reduce the average tariff rate over the next three years to less than 10 percent. The special tax of 17.5 percent on certain imported goods was abolished in March 1999.  

12. Monetary policy. The main goal of monetary policy in 1999 will be to reduce inflation to not more than 9 percent by the end of the year. Consistent with this objective and the expectation that real GDP will grow by $5\%$ percent, the Bank of Ghana will aim at limiting monetary growth to 14/ percent. The monetary growth target wil be achieved by limiting reserve money growth to $11\%$ percent through the use of indirect monetary policy instruments, including repurchase agreements. The Bank of Ghana expects that price trends and the performance of the economy will allow interest rates to continue to fall during the year, but it will adjust its policies as needed to meet its monetary targets. Bank financing of the budget is projected to expand by no more than C210 billion, or the equivalent of about 1 percent of GDP, which would allow credit to the private sector to grow by $17\%$ percent in 1999, broadly in line with the growth of nominal GDP.  

13.The exchange rate will continue to be determined by market forces. The Bank of Ghana's expectation is that the exchange rate will move in tandem with price trends in Ghana and in partner countries. Nevertheless, the Bank of Ghana's intervention policy will be directed at meeting the program target for net foreign assets of the central bank while smoothing short-term fluctuations. For 1999, the Bank of Ghana has targeted an accumulation of net foreign assets of US\$77 million, which is expected to raise its gross international reserves to about 2.7 months of imports.  

14. The Bank of Ghana will keep domestic and foreign exchange market developments under close review and willadjust policies as needed. It will continue to take steps to deepen its domestic and foreign exchange markets and has requested Fund assistance in this area. In this context, it will also assess the scope for future modification of the surrender requirements.  

15. Balance of payments prospects. Ghana's external situation remains vulnerable. Exports in 1999 are forecast to expand only marginally, owing to an expected decline in the size of the cocoa crop and world price. Moreover, gold prices are expected to decline in 1999. Imports are also forecast to show modest growth, about 2 percent in value terms. However, given the expected decline in import prices (in particular oil prices) the volume of imports will still exceed growth in real GDP. The current account deficit, including grants, is expected to narrow from 3.5 percent of GDP in 1998 to 2.9 percent of GDP in 1999.  

16.Debt sustainability analysis. The external debt-service ratio, which had increased to about 32 percent of exports of goods and nonfactor services in 1997, because of the impact of earlier nonconcessional borrowing, declined to about 28 percent in l998, a trend that is expected to continue. Under present assumptions, Ghana's debt-service ratio would fall further to about 15 percent in 2002, underpinning Ghana's ability to service the debt, including obligations to the Fund. Ghana's external debt, albeit largely concessional, is large, with the ratio of external debt stock to exports of goods and nonfactor services ratio at about 300 percent-or 200 percent on a net present value basis-as at end-1998. Cognizant of the vulnerability of the external sector, the authorities willimplement policies oriented toward keeping debt at sustainable levels. Indeed, under present policies the level of debt in relation to exports is expected to fall to about 250 percent—or 165 percent on a net present value basis--by 20ol. The government will neither contract nor guarantee nonconcessional external loans.  

17. External financing requirements. External financing requirements are projected to total about US\$2.5 billion over the 1999-2001 period. The external financing requirement for 1999 of USs874 million is expected to be fully financed. Financing for the following two years will be firmed up in a Consultative Group meeting expected to take place in late 1999. With adherence to the financial policies envisaged in the program, Ghana should not have difficulty in meeting its external debt-service obligations, including those to the Fund, provided it is not confronted with major external shocks.  

# B. Structural Reforms  

18.The government realizes that creating a low-inflation environment will not by itself provide a suficient basis for sustained and durable growth, and that structural reforms need to be directed at encouraging private sector initiative and improving resource allocation. It is therefore moving ahead with its plan to deregulate the energy and cocoa sectors, to further divest interests in the manufacturing, services, and financial sectors, and to promote private participation in infrastructure.  

19. Agriculture. Without agricultural development, the objective of achieving sustained growth rates and reducing poverty willbe unattainable. To strengthen its agricultural policies, the government is preparing the Agricultural Services Sector Investment Program (AGSSIP), an integrated program of expenditures to be financed by the government and a consortium of donors committed to pooling their resources for this purpose.  

20. Cocoa. Within agriculture, the cocoa sector is paramount, and reforms are needed to ensure that Ghana remains a leading producer of cocoa in the next decade. In September 1998, a task force was established with wide representation from stakeholders; its report was issued in December 1998, and a workshop was conducted in January 1999. As a result of this broad-ranging consultation process, a medium-term strategy has been drafted and submitted for cabinet approval.  

21.Key elements of the government's medium term strategy for cocoa include: unifying the extension services of Cocobod and those of the Ministry of Agriculture; increasing competition in internal marketing by giving licensed buying companies (LBCs) equal access to Cocobod's warehouses and crop financing; allowing qualified LBCs to export at least 30 percent of their domestic purchases, starting with the 20o0/2001 crop; maintaining quality-control services in a public sector institution; eliminating price discounts on exportable cocoa to domestic processors in the 1999/2000 crop season; increasing the producer price to 60 percent of the f.o.b. price of cocoa for the 1999/2000 crop, and thereafter by at least 2 percentage points in each ofthe next two years; reducing the share of the Cocobod and the tax on cocoa to allow for increases in the farmers' share of the f.o.b. price; and outsourcing the Produce Buying Company for divestiture before end-March 1999 and offering it for sale before end-June 1999. Once the LBCs begin exporting cocoa, the Bank of Ghana will review surrender requirements.  

22.The government believes that the above cocoa strategy will best serve the interests of the country during the next three years, but it willbe prepared to take bolder steps, as needed, in the light of its experience and that of its competitors; it will assess the progress in the first phase of reforms with a view to moving toward a fully competitive system with open entry.  

23. Banking restructuring. The government believes that a sound and competitive banking system would greatly help to improve the allocation of resources in the economy. Since the mid-198Os, the government has taken a number of steps to clean up the existing portfolio of nonperforming loans, to divest its shares in banks, and to find strategic investors willing to manage or take over weaker banks. In this context, the government will streamline regulations on entry and exit in the industry. It will lift the freeze on applications for banking licenses and will so notify the public before end-March 1999. It will withdraw by endSeptember l999 the license of any bank that at that date, fails to meet capital adequacy requirements of the Banking Law of 1989; in doing so, it will ensure that depositors are protected and that debts are collected. By March 1999, the Bank of Ghana will establish  

monitorable quarterly targets for banks failing to meet capital adequacy requirements. These banks will be asked to invest any additional deposits in government securities.  

24. Both the government and the Bank of Ghana wil take steps to divest their shares in the banking industry. By September 1999, the government expects to complete the sale of at least 30 percent of the shares of the National Investment Bank to a strategic partner. Similarly, at least 40 percent of the shares in the Ghana Commercial Bank will be divested by December l1999. The Bank of Ghana will also sell all its shares in commercial banks, except those in the Agricultural Development Bank (ADB), by December 1999.  

25. Before end-1999, the government will place before parliament a new Bank of Ghana bill that will ensure the independence of the central bank in formulating monetary policy and supervising the financial system, while making it more transparent and accountable. A new banking law wil also be submitted to modernize regulatory practices. Banking supervision will be strengthened. A new foreign exposure regulation was introduced in December 1998 and will become effctive in mid-1999. The Bank of Ghana is encouraging the creation before December 1999 of an “apex institution" that will provide services to rural banks. The Bank of Ghana will however, retain final regulatory authority over these banks and, as demonstrated by its closure of 23 rural banks in late-1998, will not hesitate to withdraw the licenses of problem banks.  

26. Divestiture program. The government aims to create an environment in which private investment increases over the medium term, helped by a vigorous and transparent divestiture program. The government has prepared a divestiture work program for 1999--2000 covering about 80 companies. The government projects divestiture receipts of C100 billion in 1999, and $\mathbb{C}100$ billion and $\pmb{\mathbb{C}}50$ billion in 2000 and 2001, respectively. The government will seek to attract more investor interest by using experienced sales advisors and by improving the disclosure of information on the program. In that context, the government willreduce the publication lag of the annual audited financial reports of the Divestiture Implementation Committee (DIC) and will following its completion, disseminate a detailed impact assessment of the divestiture program.  

27. Public sector reforms will be carried out to refocus the government's efforts on core areas, particularly the social sectors and basic infrastructure. The government has begun to implement the public service reform program adopted by the cabinet and discussed at a conference of all stakeholders in 1998. This reform program is expected to be carried out in stages over the next decade. A pilot project for the reform of subvented agencies will commercialize, restructure, or close down at least 17 of them by the end of 2001. Legislation to support the commercialization or closure of agencies will be presented to parliament this year. The government also plans to reform the central management agencies over the 1999-200i period, putting a coherent and transparent regulatory environment in place and streamlining functions. It is negotiating with the social partners a comprehensive long-term wage policy, aimed at reforming the pay structure based on a detailed job evaluation of the entire public sector. The Central Management Board (CMB) appointed to implement the program has regraded current public service jobs according to the categories of the new remuneration system. The government intends to conclude the ongoing negotiations on the implementation of the new salary structure as soon as possible.  

28. Energy. Reforms in this sector wil remain important, as highlighted by the early 1998 crisis. The government is expected to complete the regulatory framework for the sector, including the Rules of Practice for the Operation of the National Grid, by end-1999. State-owned utilities will be made into separate corporate entities for the generation, transmission, and distribution of hydroelectric and thermal power. In anticipation of this, the government has already initiated the transformation of Volta River Authority (VRA) into four entities and is in an advanced stage of divesting its thermal generation plant. The Electricity Company of Ghana (ECG) is being restructured and willbe offered for sale by end-1999. Both utilities are also implementing financial recovery plans approved by the government to deal with past liabilities and to ensure their future viability. To guide the growing number of participants in the industry, including regulators and private operators, the Ministry of Mines and Energy is preparing a Statement of Sector Development Policy, to be discussed with stakeholders in 1999. The statement will outline the remaining reform agenda. The financial restructuring of the Tema Oil Refinery (TOR) will be completed by June I999, enabling the government to offer it for sale before end-December. The West Africa Gas Pipeline is under consideration by government in partnership with the private sector to address Ghana's longterm energy needs; feasibility studies of the pipeline are expected to be completed by mid1999.  

29. Education. As indicated in the “Ghana—Vision $2020^{\circ}$ document, the government's priorities in education are to achieve universal basic education and adult literacy, increase acess to secondary and tertiary education, and improve technical and vocational training. In line with these objectives, the government launched in 1996 a Basic Education Sector Improvement Program with the assistance of the World Bank. Implementation of the program is, however, experiencing dificulties as teaching and learning achievements are not improving. Measures are being taken to shore up the program. To monitor progress on the quality of basic education, the government is targeting significant improvements in pupils' achievements in the recently introduced Performance Monitoring Test, Criterion Reference Tests, and Basic Education Certificate Examination. Gross enrollment ratios for 200l have been targeted at 81.2 percent for primary schoois and 64.9 percent for junior secondary schools. Education expenditures are projected to rise from 3.8 percent of GDP in 1998 to 4.l percent of GDP in 2001.  

30. Health. The government's health sector reform program, which is based on implementation of the Medium-Term Health Strategy Toward Vision 2020, aims at providing universal access to basic health services, improving the quality and the efficiency of health services, and fostering linkages with other sectors contributing to health. The government has adopted a sector-wide approach to coordinate donors' efforts in the sector, including joint  

systems to monitor sector performance. Main strategies to achieve sector objectives include (a) strengthening primary health services; (b) improving the capacity for policy development and analysis, resource allocation, performance monitoring and evaluation, and regulation of services delivery and health professionals; (c) strengthening national support systems for human resources, logistics and supplies, financial management, and health information; and (d) promoting private sector involvement in the delivery of health services. A hospital development policy will be prepared in 1999.  

31.The impact targets of the health sector program for end-200l are (a) increase life expectancy from 58 years in 1997 to 60 years; (b) reduce the infant mortality rate from 66 to 50 per 1,000 live births; (c) reduce the under-5 years old mortality rate from 132 to 100 per 1,000 live births; (d) lower the maternal mortality rate from 214 to 100 per 100,000 live births; (e) bring down the total fertility rate from 5.5 to 5.0 births; and (f) reduce the proportion of children with severe malnutrition from 12 percent to 8 percent.  

32. Poverty. In collaboration with donors, the government is preparing a national poverty-reduction program for the period 1999-200l. In this context, poverty monitoring will be given high priority, including such poverty assessments as the Core Welfare Indicators Questionnaire (CWIQ) of 1997 and the fourth Ghana Living Standards Survey, which is to be completed in June 1999.  

33. Statistical issues. The government continues to attach high priority to improving its statistics base. It intends to conduct a housing and population census in 2ooO and will publish the results of the fourth Ghana Living Standards Survey by the third quarter of 1999. The Ghana Statistical Service will continue its review of the methodology used to compile national accounts; it will publish updated national accounts (sources and uses) with a lag of at most six months. The government willassess and explain before June 1999 the discrepancy between the trade data obtained from the Automated System for Customs Data (ASYCUDA) and from the Customs, Excise, and Preventive Services; it will ensure that national accounts and balance of payments data reflect the conclusions of that assessment. Finally, the government will reduce lags in reporting consumer prices and monetary accounts to not more than one month and budget execution data to six weeks.

The Bank's broad strategy objectives include: restoring macroeconomic stability, promoting private investment, supporting social and rural development, and implementing direct poverty alleviation measures.  

2.As of December 31, 1998 total commitments to Ghana amounted to US\$1036.1 billion with total disbursements of US\$5O9 million. # Introduction  

1. Ghana is a heavily indebted poor country (HIPC). However, it has not resorted to concessional rescheduling from the Paris Club nor fully utilized traditional debt relief mechanisms. The previous debt sustainability analysis carried out in March 1998 and reported in EBS/98/46 (3/9/98) concluded that Ghana would not exceed required threshold levels after exhausting traditional debt-relief mechanisms. Since the beginning of the Economic Recovery Program (1983), Ghana has had only one rescheduling agreement, in April 1996, which was on nonconcessional terms and pertained to pre-l983 debts to Brazil, Italy, and Norway. Even without further use of rescheduling operations, the debt situation of Ghana was considered sustainable, provided that sound macroeconomic and debt-management policies were put in place and no major shocks occurred. The debt sustainability analysis carried out in this appendix reiterates this conchusion.1  

2. Compared with the March 1998 debt sustainability analysis, the present analysis shows a lower ratio of debt stock, both in nominal and in net present value terms, to exports for end-1998. This lower level is explained both by the evolution of the exchange rate between the U.S. dollar and other currencies in which part of the Ghanaian external debt is denominated, and by a more complete debt-reconciliation exercise, done in conjunction with the World Bank and the authorities, which eliminated duplication of reported loans. The strong export performance in 1998 also played a role in making the external debt burden more sustainable..  

3. As in the previous analysis, the main conclusion is that, if Ghana is able to keep its structural adjustment program on track and avoid nonconcessional financing, it should have no serious problems in staying current on its debt service payments, except in case of major shocks. It should be emphasized, however, that Ghana's export performance is vulnerable to both weather and price shocks, and foreign assistance may decline faster than contemplated in the baseline scenario. Therefore, in order to guard against encountering difficulties under adverse conditions, Ghana needs to take a prudent approach to macroeconomic adjustment and external borrowing.  

'The present results are based on the same methodology as in EBS/98/46. The World Bank staff is in agreement with this analysis. A three-year moving average of export of goods and nonfactor services (excluding workers' remittances) is used as the denominator for the ratio of net present value of debt to exports, and a prorated net present value of debt disbursed and outstanding is calculated. Net present value was calculated using a discount rate based on the weighted average of currency specific commercial interest reference rates, with weights corresponding to the currency composition of Ghana's external debt. The aggregate debt data is based on information provided by the Ministry of Finance.  

# The level and composition of debt  

4. At the end of 1998, the nominal value of Ghana's total external debt was almost US\$6 bilion, equivalent to almost 80 percent of GDP. The ratio of net present value of debt to exports of goods and nonfactor services was 198 percent. About 6l percent of the debt was owed to multilateral creditors, including the IMF, and 2l per cent to official bilateral creditors (of which a litle more than 90 per cent is owed to Paris Club creditors). About 95 percent of the debt to multilateral and official bilateral creditors was on concessional terms. About half of the debt service in 1998 was owed to multilaterals and official bilateral creditors. However a relatively large share of debt service in 1998 is for servicing commercial nonconcessional debt that was assumed earlier and the payments on these amounts account for a temporary bunching of relatively large amortization payments.  

# Main results and assumptions of the baseline scenario  

5. In 1999, weaker cocoa prices and a smaller l999 crop will slow the improvement in debt-service indicators. Nevertheless, the net present value of debt-to-export and debt service ratios are projected to decline to about 164 percent and under 19 percent, respectively, by 2001 (Appendix Table 10).  

6. This profile of debt and debt-service ratios is predicated on certain assumptions. GDP growth is assumed to be 5.5 percent for 1999, and 6 percent in the outer years. As in the earlier debt sustainability analysis, the GDP growth trend assumes the continuance of sound financial policies and structural reforms, which will enhance savings and investment and increase the role of the private sector.  

7. The baseline scenario also assumes that, as a result of the medium-term cocoa strategy, the volume of cocoa exports will rise above 500,o00 metric tons by the year 2005 an increase of 30 percent from the volume of exports in 1998. Cocoa exports are projected to average about 700,000 metric tons over the 2007-1l period. These levels are in line with the production and export levels that are targeted in the government's cocoa strategy. The value of nontraditional exports are assumed to continue to grow strongly, at rates of about 20 percent in the initial years, a decline from the over 50 percent growth seen in 1998. In later years, these high growth rates are expected to gradually slow down. Given the softness in the gold market and environmental considerations, it is assumed that the volume of gold and timber exports will grow more modestly, on average by 6 percent a year, and 5 percent a year, respectively, in 1998-2003 and slow down to 4 percent and 2 percent, respectively, in later years. The volume of oil and non-oil imports are projected to grow in line with real GDP growth; and gross international reserve levels are targeted to stay at 3 months of imports. Under these assumptions, the current account, including official transfers would narrow to 2.2 percent in 2005 and improve a little thereafter to under 2 percent.  

8. On financing, grants rise in 1999 to $\mathbf{USS235}$ million, but then decline to about US\$175 millon by 2003 and remain at that level in the subsequent period. Concessional lending from multilateral and other organizations is assumed to decline modestly in the immediate years from about US\$578 million in 1999 to about US\$475 million in 2001, and then to about US\$430 million in the outer years.  

9. The implication of this set of assumptions is that aid flows as a share of GDP, which dropped from 13 percent to 10 percent of GDP in 1998, will hold steady at 10 percent in 1999 and decline by a percentage point in both 2000 and 200l, reaching 8 percent in the latter years. Subsequently, aid is assumed to continue to decline slowly in each year, such that aid flows as a share of GDP fals to about 3 percent of GDP in the outer years, by 2010, and to about 2 percent of GDP beyond 2010. Direct investment and other private flows are assumed to offset this steady decline in concessional loans.  

# Sensitivity analysis  

10. Some alternative scenarios were examined in order to assess the sensitivity of Ghana's debt indicators to various shocks. (Appendix Table 12). Scenario 2 assumes that the program grants and loans in the baseline scenario would not be forthcoming and that this amount would have to be made up by nonconcessional financing at the London interbank offered rate (LIBOR) plus 3 percentage points. The results show that by 2005 the net percent value of debt will be equivalent to 124 percent of exports, 8 percentage points higher than in the baseline scenario, and the debt-service ratio $5\%$ percentage points higher. Nevertheless, both the net present value of debt-to-exports ratio and the debt-service ratio will be below the threshold levels of 200 percent and 20 percent, respectively.  

11. Scenario 3 assumes that, in addition to the hypothesis in scenario 2, cocoa production and exports grow at rates of only 5 percent a year, with cocoa exports being lower by 5 percent and 10 percent by 2005 and 2010, respectively. Such a shock would further worsen the debt situation and the net percent value of debt. Indeed, by 20O5, the net percent value of debt in relation to exports would be about 18 percentage points higher than in the baseline. The debt-service ratio would also be higher and, if the shock persisted (as is assumed), the ratio would only fall below 20 percent in 2002.  

12. Scenario 4 assumes that prices for the metric ton of cocoa would also be 10 per cent lower throughout the period. The debt situation worsens further and the net percent value of debt in relation to exports is higher relative to the base case by over 25 percentage points by 2005 and the debt service ratio does not fall below 20 per cent even by 2007..  

# Conclusions  

13. The results ilustrate that despite the vulnerability of Ghana's balance of payments, the external debt is likely to be sustainable under sound policies. However, it is clear that additional nonconcessional borrowing in the early years would weaken the external situation  

over the medium term and increase the likelihood that shortfalls in cocoa exports and cocoa prices from the baseline scenario would make the debt situation vulnerable.

# Ghana: Statistical Issues  

1. Although the quality and timeliness of the reporting of Ghana's core economic and statistics have improved in some respects, many weaknesses remain, notably, delays in the availability of data on international reserves, interest rates, and the consumer price index (CPI). Ghana is in the bottom quartile of Fund members in the timeliness of reporting core surveillance data. Additional areas where improvements need to be made are identified below. The central bank is also computerizing its databases, particularly with regard to economic indicators, in consultation with the Fund's Bureau of Computing Services.  

# Real sector  

2. GDP estimates have been reported with considerable delay, in September 1998 a complete set of national accounts for the years 1995 and 1996 was published by the Ghana Statistical Service. However, only three major sectors of economic activity are accounted for and a review of the methodology used in preparing the data is still under way. Sectoral weights used in the calculation of aggregate GDP are to be revised on the basis of the 1992 Ghana Living Standards Survey (GLSS), and expenditure data are to be revised to improve the consistency of the national accounts data. The balance of payments data are produced by the Bank of Ghana.  

3. In January 1998, a new CPI was introduced. The weights are computed according to the standard consumption basket estimated from the 1992 GLSS. The new basket reduced substantially the weight for clothing from 19 percent to 9 percent, while the weights for medical care, housing, and transport were correspondingly increased. A set of comparable indexes based on the new consumption basket is available only after September 1997, and, consequently, the authorities use a spline method, by compounding inflation, to estimate the 12-month inflation rate from January to September 1998. In addition, the reporting lag for the CPI increased from one month to two months and longer. The wholesale price index was discontinued in 1991. Trade data are reported with gaps and the most recent figures for total exports and imports in International Financial Statistics (IFS) relate to 1996. Rather large discrepancies exist in the data reported for imports by the Ghana Statistical Service and the Customs Excises, and Preventive Services. Labor statistics in Ghana are also produced with a considerable lag and wage statistics are nearly nonexistent; to reduce delays, the United Nations Development Program (UNDP) and the International Labor Organization (ILO) are providing technical assistance to the Ministry of Employment. Unit value and volume indexes for total exports and imports are no longer supplied to Statistics Department (STA). Also, industrial production and wage indicators are not available.  

# Public finance  

4. The timeliness of the reporting of public finance statistics was improved by the introduction in 1994 of a new expenditure coding system that allows data to be generated on a cash basis from the central bank records. However, an economic classification of expenditures is available only with a lag of at least two months. Expenditure data on a commitment basis are not available, but efforts have recently been made to get large ministries to report their expenditure commitments and arrears to the Ministry of Finance. Expenditures financed by externally funded project aid are produced with a considerable lag and subject to estimation errors owing to timing differences, foreign exchange valuation, and the recording of the technical assistance component. However, staff estimates of these expenditures are derived from balance of payments data. Consolidated fiscal accounts are not compiled because data on autonomous government agencies (subvented agencies) and public enterprises are not reported. A World Bank mission reviewed the government's domestic debt data in August 1998.  

5． Annual government finance data are reported for publication in the Government Finance Statistics Yearbook (GFSY), although the coverage and timeliness of the data need to be improved; the latest available data cover the central government only and relate to the fiscal year ended December 31, 1993. Unless the authorities update the GFS data this year, the country page willbe scheduled for deletion in GFSY. These data include all the major components of the government finance statistics tables, with the exception of debt, for which the latest data published in the GFSY relate to l985. The authorities reported fiscal data for the 1996 GFSY, but the data could not be published as they were not sufficiently detailed to allow conversion to the government finance statistics format. No monthly or quarterly data are reported for publication in IFS.  

# Monetary accounts  

6. Monetary data are compiled on a regular basis. In December 1997, the monetary survey was extended to include 6 additional banks; it thus currently covers all 17 banks in the system. Notwithstanding the technical assistance provided since 1985, several problems persist in Ghana's monetary statistics. In particular, there are large deviations between data reported by commercial banks with respect to their deposits with the central bank and those reported by the Bank of Ghana. Moreover, foreign currency accounts are not valued at the end-of-period market exchange rates. A STA money and banking statistics mission (July 1997) proposed a plan of action and a timetable to resolve these problems. Among the issues that need to be addressed is the classification by residency of foreign currency deposits in commercial banks.  

# Balance of payments and external debt  

7. Balance of payments data are published only annually. Trade data are available on a more frequent basis, but those for imports are impaired by unreliable customs statistics. No breakdown is available for either imports or exports of goods. Recent computerization resulted in some improvements in the customs records, but there is scope for further improvement. Balance of payments estimates up to 1997 have been submitted to STA. With technical assistance from the Commonwealth Secretariat, significant improvements have been made in the debt-reporting system, although reconciliation of data between the Bank of Ghana and the Ministry of Finance still presents problems; nevertheless, efforts are being made to achieve further progress in this area.  

# Publication  

8. The reporting of data to the Fund for purposes of publication in the IFS has recently improved. However, there are still lags of about four to five months in reporting exchange rates, prices, and international transactions data. In general, statistical information from official sources is not disseminated to the public on a timely basis. Even though the Quarterly Economic Bulletin of the Bank of Ghana is now published more regularly, the data show lags of up to six months. The Quarterly Digest of Statistics is usually published by the GSS with a longer lag.  

9. Deficiencies in data dissemination have been brought to the attention of the Ghanaian authorities at the level of the Minister of Finance and the Governor of the Bank of Ghana.
