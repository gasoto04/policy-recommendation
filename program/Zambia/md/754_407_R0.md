# Executive Summary  

# Background  

Zambia's economic performance in 20oo improved substantially. All of the program's end-September quantitative and structural performance criteria and benchmarks were observed and most criteria for end-December were also observed. For the first time in recent years, real per-capita GDP growth was positive.  

Notwithstanding these gains, the economy experienced macroeconomic instability, particularly during the last quarter of 2000. The 12-month inflation rate reached about 30 percent in 2000 and the exchange rate depreciated by 37 percent in foreign currency terms with some 13 percent occurring in the last 2 months. In response, the authorities substantially tightened monetary policy, and the currency rebounded sharply in January 2o01 and has since stabilized. Real interest rates also rose markedly.  

On the political front, presidential and legislative elections are scheduled for November 2001. There is an on-going debate on whether President Chiluba should seek a third term.  

# The program for 2001  

The main objectives are macroeconomic stability, laying the basis for poverty reduction by increasing social and poverty-related spending, and strengthening investor confidence through structural reforms and governance measures. Accordingly, the program targets GDP growth of 5 percent, a reduction in inflation to 17.5 percent, and an increase in gross reserves to the equivalent of 2 months imports.  

Appropriate financial policies will help achieve the inflation and growth objectives while allowing for adequate investment in the social sectors and infrastructure, made possible by debt relief under the enhanced HIPC Initiative. The overall fiscal deficit is projected to narrow from 7.1 percent of GDP to about 5 percent, while broad money is projected to increase by 17 percent.  

Structural and governance reforms focus on: addressing the weaknesses of the ZNCB and privatizing the government's controlling interest; completing deregulation of the petroleum sector; strengthening expenditure control, including by beginning to implement an Integrated Financial Management Information System; and improving economic and financial governance.  

# Staff position  

There are a number of risks to the program, particularly wage and other expenditure pressures and the general risks to economic policy implementation in an election year. However, the authorities have demonstrated their commitment to achieve the program's major objectives and their efforts justify Fund support.

# Box 1. Dollarization in Zambia  

The dollarization ratio (the share of foreign currency deposits (FCD) in broad money) rose from 37 percent at end-1999 to 49 percent at end-2000, mainly owing to the rapid depreciation of the kwacha. 1/ The 58 percent depreciation in local currency terms during the year accounted for virtually all of the rise in the ratio of FCD to broad money-using the program exchange rate, the dollarization ratio would have risen from 37 percent at end-1999 to about 38 percent at end-2000. FCDs rose by some $\ensuremath{\mathrm{\ttUSS80}}$ million during 2000, in line with the increase in broad money, and much of this increase occurred in the immediate aftermath of the privatization of Zambia's copper mines in March 2000.  

Dollarization can come about through asset substitution, whereby market participants shift assets into foreign currencies, or currency substitution, in which they use foreign currencies for transaction purposes and as a unit of account. In general, asset substitution reflects a combination of low rates of return and high risk in holding domestic currency assets (relative to the foreign asset), while currency substitution reflects the high cost of holding domestic money balances (relative to foreign currency balances), usually under conditions of hyperinflation. Both conditions arise with increasing domestic inflation, declining confidence in domestic policies, and rising expected rate of currency depreciation. All of these factors seemed to be relevant in Zambia during 2000. Evidence on whether dollarization in Zambia reflects asset or currency substitution is mixed. On the one hand, foreign currency demand deposits account for about 95 percent of total FCDs suggesting currency substitution. On the other hand, given the high rate of inflation and high expected rates of a depreciation, especially toward end-20o0, part of the dollarization may be due to a substitution of kwacha-denominated assets for dollar denominated ones. On balance, the large share of foreign currency demand deposits seems to suggest that currency substitution could be a major cause of dollarization, and it would seem prudent to include these deposits in the monetary aggregate used as an intermediate target.  

Previous studies suggest that dollrization has had little impact on Fund program design in other countries. They have concluded that programs should avoid measures that discriminate against foreign currency deposits because of possible distortions. Dollarization -per se—should be neither encouraged nor discouraged. Rather, policies should focus on the fundamentals that make it attractive to hold kwacha assets.  

# Box 2. Foreign Exchange Measures  

Following the accelerated depreciation of the kwacha in late 20o0, the authorities tightened monetary policy substantially by raising the legal reserve requirement from 8 percent to 15 percent over the course of two months. The government also introduced certain foreign exchange measures in January 200l ostensibly to stop the depreciation of the kwacha and the dollarization that was taking place, and counter-balance the power of the mining companies in the foreign exchange market. Although these measures were not inconsistent with the Fund's Articles of Agreement, and many of them were prudential in nature, several measures raised concern, including the following:  

limiting foreign currency demand deposits to 25 percent of total foreign currency deposits (on a bank-by-bank basis); requiring prior approval of the Bank of Zambia before a Zambian bank could establish a new correspondent relationship with a non-resident bank; requiring that remittances, other than dividends, be made against invoices; and requiring that at least 75 percent of export proceeds be repatriated within 180 days.  

In February 2001, the authorities also introduced foreign exchange market arrangements targeted at large foreign exchange earners (over $\$200,000$ per week), reflecting concerns that the supply of foreign exchange was heavily concentrated in a few key export-oriented enterprises. Under these arrangements,  

large suppliers would be required to sell foreign exchange at daily auctions organized by the Bank of Zambia, and the Bank of Zambia could reject bids it felt did not reflect market fundamentals.  

Following the discussions with the staff in February 200l, the authorities in March eliminated the 25 percent limit on foreign currency demand deposits, replaced the requirement of prior approval of new correspondent relationships with a notification requirement, and clarified that remittances against invoice requirements would apply only to reporting by commercial banks for statistical purposes. In addition, the Bank of Zambia issued rules to market participants clarifying that it would not employ discretion in rejecting bids under the new foreign exchange market arrangements (or under its preexisting arrangements), and that foreign exchange would be allocated to all successful bidders at the cutoff rate. These steps were meant to ensure the continued smooth operation of Zambia's liberal foreign exchange market. The operation of the foreign exchange market and the recent foreign exchange measures will be an important part of the next review of the PRGF-supported program.

# Box 3. Zambia National Commercial Bank  

The Zambia National Commercial Bank (ZNCB) was established in 1975 as a state-owned commercial bank, and currently 99.8 percent of the bank's shares are owned by the government. The ZNCB is now the largest domestic bank in terms of asset size and deposits. The bank holds about 25 percent of banking sector deposits, with the government and statutory bodies accounting for 35 percent of these deposits and the private sector accounting for the rest.  

Nonperforming loans comprise approximately one-third of total loans of the banking system in Zambia, and the vast majority of these are accounted for by the ZNCB. A mission from the Fund's Monetary and Exchange Affairs (MAE) Department estimated that as at end-October 20OO, over 90 percent of the ZNCB's loan portfolio was nonperforming. The ZNCB's crisis began with the advent of the privatization program in 1991. With progress in privatization, it lost some of its deposit and customer base and was subject to increasing competition from private banks. At the same time, it suffers from an enormous bad-debt problem as it has, over the years, extended loans to parastatals at the direction of the government, for non-commercial reasons. A prime cause of the present problem of the ZNCB is its exposure to losses from credits extended to the state-owned ZNOC and a private mining company, Roan Antelope Mining Company of Zambia (RAMCOZ) which is currently under receivership. At end-October 2000, the ZNCB's exposure to ZNOC and RAMCOZ stood at K162 billion and K29 bilion, respectively, or 69 percent of the ZNCB's loan portfolio.Security for the ZNOC loan consisted of US\$28 milion guarantee from the government and pledges of real estate and other assets. Security for the RAMCOZ loan consists of $\mathbf{US9}$ million guarantee from the company's parent Banani group and the company's stocks and intangibles.  

The ZNCB's current position can be described at best as perilous. In addition to the large volume of non-performing loans, at end-October 200o the bank had negative balance sheet capital of K26 billion and a regulatory capital deficiency of K61 billion. The bank has a large proportion of its assets in cash and liquid assets, ofset by a large amount of government deposits that are drawn down with a very short notice. As a result, the bank frequently borrows from the inter-bank market and/or uses the overdraft facility at the BoZ. In recent years, the bank's net income after provision of losses has been negative--a situation that reflects a small proportion of earning assets on the bank's books, or, conversely, its large volume of problem assets. With insufficient capital and earnings, the bank is relying upon new deposits and borrowings to meet its cash needs.  

To halt the further deterioration of the ZNCB's asset position, in June 200O, as indicated in the government's letter of intent of June 30, 2000, the BoZ increased its direction of the bank's specific activities, including placing ZNOC and other nonperforming loans on a non-accrual basis, and putting limits on the growth of its assets and deposits. In the meantime, the ZNCB has received $\mathbf{US97}$ million from the government guarantee on behalf of ZNOC, and about $\mathbf{US}\$4$ million from RAMCOZ. On the basis of these payments, the BOZ has provided the ZNCB with exemptions from provisioning requirements on nonperforming loans to ZNOC and RAMCOZ until March 2001.  

The government recognizes that a long-term solution to the bank's problem lies in the privatization of its majority controlling interest. To this end, the Zambia Privatization Agency has submitted to the cabinet options for privatizing the bank.

# B. The Program for 2001  

21.The main objectives of the program for 2001 are to (i) increase real GDP growth to 5 percent, largely on the basis of a strong recovery in mining; (i) create conditions fostering exchange rate stability; (ii) reduce the 12-month rate of inflation to 17.5 percent by year's end; (iv) increase gross international reserves to the equivalent of two months of imports; and (v) start laying the basis for poverty reduction by increasing spending on key priorities in the social and economic sectors. External sector and financing  

28. Notwithstanding a substantial increase in exports, Zambia's external current account deficit is expected to widen from 18.5 percent of GDP to 19.9 percent in 2001, reflecting mainly higher imports, associated with investments in the mining sector and with HIPC Initiative-related spending and a marked increase in nonfactor service payments (including by the mining sector). Owing to recent efficiency gains in the wake of the ZCCM privatization in early 2000 and a modest increase in copper prices, metals exports would increase by 22 percent. Merchandise exports are projected to grow by 17 percent.  

29. Despite a widening of the current account deficit, balance of payments support and exceptional financing are expected to help the Bank of Zambia to more than double its foreign reserves, from US\$114 million to US\$249 million (or about 2 months of imports of goods and non factor services) in 20o1. Even with assistance under the enhanced HIPC Initiative (US\$278 million), Zambia remains heavily dependent on external assistance. Balance of payments support is expected from the World Bank (US\$95 million), the European Union ( $\mathbf{(USS79}$ million), the Netherlands ( $\mathtt{U S S20}$ million), the African Development Bank $\boldsymbol{\mathrm{US}}\$14$ million), and PRGF disbursements (US\$130 million). Following the December 2000 decision point under the enhanced HIPC Initiative, the program assumes that Paris Club creditors will agree a flow rescheduling on Cologne terms retroactive to January 2001 covering non-previously rescheduled pre-cutoff-date debt, and topping up previous concessional flow reschedulings, and that Zambia willseek comparable rescheduling terms with non-Paris Club bilateral and commercial creditors. The financing gaps in 2002-03 are expected to be filled by new commitments of grants and highly concessional loans from bilateral and multilateral sources. The larger gap envisaged for 2003 reflects the expiry of the current PRGF arrangement early that year.  

# PRSP, poverty reduction and social policies  

30. Progress on the development of the participatory full PRSP has largely been carried out by the eight working groups. The full PRSP will need to, inter alia, (i) establish a new three-year policy matrix with targets and indicators; (ii) develop a coordinated monitoring information system; (ii) produce costings and a budget for the policy priorities, in line with the annual budget and the MTEF; and (iv) submit allthis to public consultation (paragraph 14, LOI). The establishment of a high-level steering group will be important to provide political leadership and support for the process.  

31. While PRSP priorities are being developed, a list of programs to be supported by HIPC Initiative resources and estimated to cost US\$86 million was drawn up by the Ministry of Finance in consultation with the line ministries (see Box 4 and Appendix I). The selection was guided by knowledge of resource scarcity in existing poverty-focused programs (for example, where donor programs were stalled because of a lack of government counterpart funding); the need to complement existing government and donor programs; and absorptive capacity. Given the limited capacity to launch new programs, the government also chose to give priority to expanding existing poverty-oriented programs, where funds could be disbursed relatively quickly. Thus, 68 percent of the resources from the HIPC Initiative will go to these programs and the rest to new poverty-oriented programs. Because of uncertainties about when and how the HIPC Initiative resources would become available, the government also decided against using them for wages and salaries. As a result, the allocations are focused mainly on infrastructure (roads and water) and supplies (school supplies and drugs).  

Structural reforms, public expenditure management, and governance  

32. The structural reform and governance measures for 2001 are aimed at progress towards restoring investors' confidence and removing major bottlenecks to growth. The structural reform and governance agenda focuses on (i) addressing the weaknesses of the ZNCB and privatization of the government's majority controlling interest; (ii completing the deregulation of petroleum prices and imports and effectively privatizing the oil sector entities; (ii) strengthening information systems and expenditure control; and (iv) improving economic and financial governance. These measures, including the implementation of the PRSP and HIPC Initiative-related programs, will require close Bank-Fund collaboration. While the Fund staff will take the lead in areas such as banking reform, fiscal management, budget execution, and governance, the Bank staff will take the lead on issues relating to the privatization program and the energy sector, the MTEF, the direction of resources released by the HIPC Initiative to poverty reduction, and the PRSP process.  

33. The government recognizes that urgent action to address the issue of ownership and control of the ZNCB will be the key to the necessary reforms (see Box 3, and paragraph 28, LOl).15 Given the potential financial implications, the BoZ has instituted additional supervisory measures, including daily monitoring of the ZNCB's financial position. The authorities agreed with the staff that capital injection (including the 0.8 percent of GDP in the budget for honoring the government's loan guarantee to ZNOC) to bring the bank into compliance with the regulatory requirements should be done cautiously and in the context of a restructuring or privatization plan for the bank. Following a cabinet decision on the modality of the divestiture of government's majority controlling interest in the ZNCB, the Zambia Privatization Agency (ZPA) will take the necessary steps, including undertaking a due diligence study and completing all legal requirements with a view to implementing the cabinet decision by end-December 20o1. At this point, there is no firm estimate of the fiscal cost of restructuring ZNCB prior to its privatization. The 2001 budget includes a provision equivalent to $\mathtt{U S S24}$ million to cover a called guarantee on a nonperforming loan to ZNOC, and this should substantially strengthen the bank's position. Donor grants will be sought to finance any additional cost to prepare the ZNCB for privatization. The BoZ is cognizant of the importance of a sound banking system, and it is taking steps to strengthen its banking supervision and to address weak banks, including requiring recapitalization of some banks and taking over or liquidating others (paragraph 30, LOI). It is also considering improving the prudential regulation and banking supervision frameworks to bring them in line with Basel Core Principles.  

34.To promote private sector participation in the sector, the government, with assistance from the World Bank, plans to effectively complete the deregulation and privatization of the petroleum sector in 2001 (paragraph 31, LOI). Accordingly, the ZNOC will be assigned the task of maintaining a strategic reserve of petroleum products, and it will cease its operations in oil marketing and imports. Oil companies will not be required to inform the Energy Regulation Board (ERB) before changing prices, and the role of the ERB will be limited to ex-post review of prices, based on a transparent pricing formula that reflects movements in international prices and domestic costs. In addition, imports of crude oil will also be liberalized, and the ERB will issue import licenses automatically based on a set of transparent criteria. The government has also agreed to reduce its equity stake and management rights in the Indeni refinery, the TAZAMA pipeline, and the Ndola storage tank.  

35.The government recognizes the importance of public expenditure management and has begun efforts, with World Bank assistance, to develop an Integrated Financial Management Information System (IFMIS). This system should facilitate tighter control of commitments and expenditure, and promptly generate reports that can be used for day-to-day planning and operations (paragraph 21, LOl).% A project management team will prepare user requirements for the IFMIS by September 2001. A number of other complementary initiatives are also taking place, including Activity-Based Budgeting, consolidation of the payroll and establishment registers, and development of a MTEF. During the interim period when the IFMIS is not operational, the government will continue to improve current practices. In this context, the MoFED will constitute a task force to study ways in which expenditure reports can be produced more expeditiously. Finally, the authorities will continue their efforts to rationalize the number and structure of government bank accounts, and will consider tendering procedures for banking services, as well as adopting a zerobalance basis for commercial banking, so as to reduce the level of idle cash balances. Efforts will also be made to simplify the structure of government accounts at the BoZ, and to ensure that expenditures are processed with proper authority and through the appropriate channels.  

36. The government will continue to address governance issues, mainly by reducing incentives and opportunities for rent seeking, including deregulation of the economy, privatization, and the taking of demonstrable steps to enhance transparency and accountability in the management of public resources. In this respect, the government will finalize the draft report on ZCCM cobalt sales by end-June 200l after incorporating all outstanding information and the views of major ZCCM players. Following finalization of the study, the government intends to take appropriate follow-up actions, including possible referral to the Director of Public Prosecution or to the Attorney General for possible criminal/civil action (paragraph 33, LOl). To help strengthen parliamentary oversight of the management of public resources, the authorities will seek to increase the resources for the offices of the Accountant General and the Auditor General, so as to facilitate the submission of audited government accounts to the parliament within 12 months of the end of the year, as required by law. Governance issues will be a focus of the third review of the PRGFsupported program.  

# C. Technical Assistance  

37. Zambia has received extensive technical assistance (TA) in the areas of expenditure management and control, monetary management, and balance of payments statistics. A resident advisor in budget management has been in place since the beginning of 2001. The authorities welcome TA and will continue with efforts to improve financial control by implementing the recommendations of recent FAD TA missions. The HIV/AIDS epidemic continues to undermine the capacity of Zambia's civil service, including in the area of financial administration. In this regard, the Fund, the World Bank and other cooperating partners are providing increased TA. Such assistance is, however, a  

stop-gap and the development of long-term solutions will be critical to achieving effective and sustainable economic management. As indicated earlier, the medium-term goal is to develop an IFMIS and run the system, initially, on a pilot basis in three ministries with TA from the multilateral institutions, including the Fund. In the area of statistics, the authorities intend to intensify their efforts to improve the coverage, quality, and timeliness of fiscal, monetary, and balance of payments data by implementing the recommendations made by past STA technical assistance missions. The authorities willalso require TA in their efforts to simplify the numerous government accounts and operations with the BoZ. In addition, it will also need TA in the area of tax policy and monetary operations, including the treasurybill market.  

# IV. MEDIUM-TERM FINANCING REQUIREMENTS AND CAPACITY TO REPAY THE FUND  

38. With the reaching of the decision point under the enhanced HIPC Initiative, Zambia's external debt is expected to be sustainable, provided that the government maintains a sound macroeconomic policy framework and continues to strengthen structural reforms.17 With HIPC Initiative assistance, Zambia's external debt service should fall markedly from 16.8 percent of exports in 2000 to 11 percent in 2003. Payments to the Fund (before interim assistance) would have risen sharply from $\mathbf{US}\$9$ million in 2000 to an annual average of US\$226 million in 2001-03 (Table 8). However, with interim assistance, net repayments willamount to only US\$70 million a year in 2001-03, or 6 percent of exports of goods and services. Moreover, assuming Zambia's program remains on track, Fund disbursements under the PRGF arrangement willamount to US\$130 million in 2001-02 before the expiration of the current arrangement in March 2003.  

# V. PROGRAM MONITORING  

39. Policy implementation will be monitored on the basis of the quantitative and structural performance criteria and benchmarks shown in Appendix II, Tables 3 and 4. The quarterly phasing of net credit to government and net domestic assets of the BoZ was based on past seasonal patterns and the expected profile of revenue, expenditure, and donor support. The total number of quantitative performance criteria have been reduced from 10 to 7 through streamlining conditionality on the budget and on new external borrowing. Structural performance criteria have been reduced from 4 in the 2000 program to 2 in 2001, and these focus on areas that are central to the program's macroeconomic objectives and are critical for moving toward fiscal sustainability. The program no longer includes conditionality on the electricity parastatal, ZESCO, or the oil sector, although reform of these sectors remains a central element of the World Bank support. The definitions of the performance criteria and benchmarks are contained in the technical memorandum of understanding (Appendix III). The third review of the PRGF-supported program will be conducted based on performance through end-June 200l, and willinclude an assessment of progress in implementing the agreed budget, the monetary framework, the appropriateness of the remaining foreign exchange measures, the operation of the foreign exchange market, and governance issues.  

# VI. STAFF APPRAISAL  

40. Zambia's economic performance in 2ooo improved substantially. The privatization of the ZCCM in March 2000, which had proved to be a major political hurdle, improved the climate for private sector investments and helped boost Zambia's prospects for economic growth. While room remains for further strengthening, important gains were made in implementing fiscal policy and structural reforms. All of the program's end-September quantitative and structural performance criteria and benchmarks were observed and most criteria for end-December were observed. Real GDP growth was positive in per capita terms. Allthese developments were in marked contrast to Zambia's record of the past several years.  

41. Inflation in 20oo was significantly higher than programmed, albeit partly reflecting the impact of the sharp rise in world oil prices. Also, the kwacha showed some weakness, particularly in the last quarter of the year. Recognizing the importance of macroeconomic stability for sustained economic growth and poverty reduction, the authorities tightened monetary policy substantially in order to address any potential monetary overhang from 200o and to act forcefully to contain the pressures on prices and the exchange rate. As a result, the kwacha appreciated sharply in January 20o1, and has since stabilized. Also, for the first time in several years, real interest rates rose markedly in early 2001. Effective implementation of substantially tightened monetary policies will be essential to achieve the inflation targets. In this regard, the staff welcomes the authorities' resolve, as demonstrated in the first quarter of this year, and the actions included in the program, particularly the envisaged improvements in the operation of the treasury bill market. The authorities need to monitor developments closely, and stand ready to adopt corrective action as needed.  

42. The proposed fiscal program for 2001 tries to balance the need to support the program's macroeconomic objectives, especially on inflation, on the one hand, and the need to increase substantially spending in support of poverty reduction on the other. The staf welcomes the government's intention to maintain a firm line on expenditures despite one-off outlays related to elections, the OAU summit, and the elimination of domestic arrears. Political support at the highest level will be critical to enhance financial management and expenditure control, including resisting wage pressures from unions and in ensuring adherence to the agreed spending priorities in favor of social programs. In this connection, the staff welcomes the government's proposal to markedly increase expenditures for the social sectors and anti-poverty programs as a result of the increased resources from the HIPC Initiative. These policies should help build support for the program.  

43. The Fund and Bank staffs agree that the programs selected for HIPC Initiative support during 2001 are poverty-focused, and the criteria for selection are appropriate.  

The government recognizes the importance of being able to track HIPC Initiative-related expenditures and has proposed a set of objectives for an interim tracking system. Its implementation could, however, be hampered by a weak public expenditure information and management system. Therefore, it is also important that determined efforts be sustained in the implementation of the IFMIS, with the objective of substantially strengthening public expenditure management over the medium-term.  

44. The staff welcomes the progress made thus far in developing a participatory full PRSP, although a good deal of work remains if the target date of October 2001 is to be met. Enhancing and extending the degree of consultation with stakeholders, particularly in the provinces, will be important to ensure ownership of the final policy document. In this regard, the establishment of a high-level steering group will be particularly important to provide political leadership and support to the process.  

45.Regarding the foreign exchange measures introduced to relieve the pressure on the kwacha, the staff expressed concern about some of the measures and emphasized the need to maintain a market determined exchange rate. The staff, therefore, welcomes the reversal of the measures that raised most concerns. The rapid removal of these measures should help restore Zambia's reputation of having a liberal foreign exchange system. The third review of the PRGF arrangement will assess the appropriateness of the remaining measures.  

46.The staff remains concerned about the health of the banking system. While the staff commends the BoZ's efforts to deal with problem banks, it stresses the need for determined efforts to resolve the financial situation of the ZNCB. The staff welcomes the recent increased direction of the bank by the BoZ, including daily monitoring. However, given the financial difficulties facing the ZNCB and the potential implications for the budget and for the financial system, the staff is of the view that the most efficient way to resolve this issue on a sustained basis is to privatize the bank as soon as possible. Thus, following a cabinet decision on the modality for privatization, the ZPA should proceed expeditiously in implementing the cabinet's decision by end-December 2001. In the meantime, it is essential that the BoZ maintain tight oversight of the ZNCB's activities to ensure prudent financial management practices and to protect the assets of the bank.  

47. Actions to improve governance and enhance transparency and accountability in the management of public resources remain critical elements for creating an enabling environment for investments and for allocating efficiently the limited public sector resources. Governance issues, if not addressed forthrightly, could erode the confidence of the private sector and the donor community. In this context, the government's commitment to finalize the study on cobalt sales and to take appropriate follow-up actions is welcome, and staff will urge that the report be made available to the public. The authorities also seek to enhance transparency by submitting to parliament the audited government accounts within 12 months, as required by law.  

48. Zambia's quality and timeliness of data need to be improved, especially in the monetary, fiscal, and balance of payments areas. The staff urges greater efforts in these areas, including effective implementation of the recommendations of past technical assistance provided by the Fund. Further technical assistance and support from the Fund and other donors will continue to be needed for some time.  

49. There are a number of risks associated with the program, particularly expenditure pressures, including wage demands from the civil service unions, and the general risks to economic policy implementation in an election year. Notwithstanding these risks, the authorities' program for 2001 is appropriately ambitious and the authorities have demonstrated their commitment to achieve the program's major objectives. Thus, the staff recommends completion of the second review and approval of the third annual program under the PRGF.

# Government Proposals for Tracking  

# HIPC Initiative-Related Expenditures  

Expenditures in 2001, financed by resources from the HIPC Initiative, are budgeted to amount to K 352 billion (about US\$86 million, or 2.7 percent of GDP). The government has identified a priority list of some thirty poverty-focused programs for HIPC support (Table 1, Appendix I). These expenditures correspond not just to programs in the traditional social sectors, such as education and health, but also to more broadly defined poverty-related activities, including infrastructure, micro-finance schemes, the provision of inputs to poor farmers, and human rights/governance initiatives. Roughly 68 percent of the monies are being devoted to scaling up existing programs or projects, while the balance is to be used for entirely new ventures.  

The government recognizes the importance of public accountability in the use of its HIPC Initiative resources and, in the absence of a PRSP and MTEF, is in the process of setting up an interim system to track, monitor and report on HIPC Initiative-related expenditures. It is envisaged that this system will be operational by the first quarter of 2001 and will achieve statutory, political, managerial and public accountability.  

# The key features of the interim system are:  

a) The HIPC Initiative-funded activities are integrated in the Budget like all other budget items, (line items, code and amount). HIPC Initiative-related expenditures will be identifiable in all budgetary procedures (revenue, disbursement, expenditure, and reporting), supported with proper documentation.   
b) The programs to be supported by the HIPC Initiative have been determined in consultation with the line ministries. A summary table has been compiled that lists the HIPC Initiative-funded activities for 20o1, with the current budget codes and the amount allocated for each item, together with the total allocated to these items in the 2000 and 2001 Budget. For 2002, the programs will be determined according to the priorities set out in the full PRSP.   
c) In the recently published 2001 Budget (Yellow Book), HIPC Initiative-funded or co/funded programs have been identified with a footnote within the existing administrative subhead, and added to the list of donor-funded grants. However, as from March 2001, a separate HIPC Initiative subcode is to be introduced at the subhead level, which will enable the resources to be tracked through the disbursement, expenditure, reporting, and auditing processes--the new code will be made retroactive, and past funding sheets revised, so as to allow proper categorizatior of all HIPC Initiative-expenditures throughout the year. When the Integrated Financial Management Information System (IFMIS) is installed and information technology capabilities are improved, the HIPC Initiative coding system will be further modified.   
d) In order to identify the HIPC Initiative resources, a separate Ministry of Finance (MoFED) account has been created in the Bank of Zambia, as an integral part of the government's cash position. HIPC Initiative resources will be channeled into this account through the year, reflecting reductions in debt service due and inflows of cash refunds that materialize in the period.   
e) The line ministries will provide quarterly projections of expenditures to the Budget and Economic Affairs Division, who will use this information as the basis for allocating and releasing HIPC Initiative resources, as part of the normal execution of the Budget.   
f)From the HIPC Initiative account, the Budget Office will on a monthly basis, release resources to the line ministries, with a funding sheet indicating the HIPC Initiative programs to which the funds should be applied. As the HIPC Initiative code is at the subhead level, controlling officers (COs) will have no authority to transfer HIPC Initiative resources to non-HIPC activities.   
g) Budget Office willprepare a monthly report for the Accountant General, detailing sources, receipts and disbursement of HiPC Initiative resources.   
h)COs in the line ministries will continue to report monthly to the Accountant General on all expenditures, but will also report separately on HIPC Initiative-related expenditures, using the HIPC Initiative codes. The COs will ensure that the actual expenditures reported for HIPC Initiative programs are consistent with the amounts approved.   
i） In his monthly consolidated report to the Permanent Secretary (FMA), the Accountant General at MoFED will include a summary report on HIPC Initiative resources, disbursement and expenditures, following the accounting procedures and reporting currently in use. The summary of HIPC Initiative sources and utilization of funds will be published ona quarterly basis in at least three newspapers, beginning August 2001.   
j)HIPC Initiative transactions willbe subject to internal and external audit. They will be pre-audited internally by the Head of Internal Audit, to ensure accuracy, validity and authenticity of the transaction; conformity with the regulatory framework; and reporting of any misappropriation and wastage. The Auditor General will also audit HIPC Initiative transactions as part of the Financial Report (Blue Book), which is presented to Parliament.  

k) Semi-annual monitoring and inspections of HIPC Initiative expenditures will be carried out by a committee comprising officials from the Zambia Institute of Certified Accountants (ZICA), the Central Statistical Office, the Zambia Association of Public Finance and Auditing, the Institute of Purchasing and Supplies and representatives of civil society. Their reports to the Minister of Finance and the Secretary to the Treasury will be available to the public. Additionally, with donor support, MoFED will be carrying out some public expenditure surveys (PETS) at selected facilities (e.g., health clinics, schools) in order to track the flow of public resources to these facilities and to assess the delivery and quality of public services to the intended consumers.  

1)To promote transparency, public discussion and monitoring of the amounts and use of HIPC Initiative resources, and to improve the HIPC Initiative expenditure system, the Ministry of Finance will hold semi-annual public meetings for donors and civil society organizations. The monitoring and inspection reports, the PET reports, audit reports and other HIPC Initiative related-reports will be distributed prior to these meetings. A percentage of the HIPC Initiative resources will be retained to finance the semi-annual monitoring and inspections and public meetings.

# 1. RECENT ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENTS  

2. During 20oo, Zambia's economic performance continued to improve, with real GDP growth estimated at about 3.6 percent, well above the slow pace recorded in the past several years. However, the twelve-month rate of inflation accelerated to about 30 percent, compared to 19 percent in the program, as a result of a sharp depreciation of the kwacha, a steep rise in the cost of fuel, transport and other services, and higher than programmed money growth.  

3． The fiscal stance was weaker than programmed. Although overall expenditures were broadly in line with the program projection, revenue and grants fell short of the program target (largely because of 2.2 percent of GDP shortfall in grants) and as a result, the overall fiscal deficit was higher than anticipated. The domestic fiscal deficit, at 3.4 percent of GDP, was also higher than the program estimate.  

4. Broad money expanded by 68 percent during 2000, compared with the program target of 25 percent. This increase was primarily due to the sharp depreciation of the kwacha, a rise in foreign currency deposits (FCD) as the newly privatized copper companies placed their foreign exchange earnings in the domestic banking system, and from increased dollarization of the economy. During the year, the kwacha depreciated vis-a-vis the U.S. dollar by some 37 percent in foreign currency terms, with most of the depreciation taking place in the fourth quarter--this represents a significant real depreciation. The main factors contributing to this depreciation included: (i) a weaker than expected overall external position; (ii) an increase in demand for dollars stemming from a loss in market confidence in the kwacha, as inflation  

accelerated; (ii) monetary expansion beyond program estimates; and (iv) possibly, political instability in the region.  

5． To address the mounting pressures on the exchange rate and prices, in December 2000 the Central Bank undertook to tighten monetary policy by: (i) increasing the statutory cash reserve requirement on domestic and foreign currency deposits from 8 percent to 11 percent--and, subsequently, to 15 percent in mid-January 2001; (ii) reducing monthly limits on the rediscount of government paper from 15 percent to 10 percent; and (ii) raising the penalty rate for non-compliance with reserve and rediscount requirements. In addition, the BoZ began to pursue more aggressive open market operations. In view of the severe pressures in the foreign exchange market, in mid-January 2001 the government introduced measures on foreign exchange transactions in order to discourage foreign currency outflows and speculation. During January, the BoZ rate appreciated by 15 percent in foreign currency terms.

# HII. THE MEDIUM-TERM STRATEGY  

10. The government's medium-term strategy is outlined in the Interim-PRSP." For the period 2001-03, the government aims to achieve sustained economic growth with low inflation, a substantial strengthening of the external sector, and a reduction of poverty. To achieve these objectives, the main macroeconomic targets for the period 20o1-03 are to realize: (i) real GDP growth of around 5 percent a year; (ii) a substantial reduction of inflation to single digits by 2003; and (ii) a build-up of gross international reserves to the equivalent of about three-months of imports of goods and non-factor services by end-2003. This will be achieved by strengthening macroeconomic stability, and deepening structural reforms. This should help to mobilize both domestic and external resources, and provide an enabling environment for direct investment and for rapid and sustained economic growth, and poverty reduction.  

11. The medium-term fiscal objectives aim to enhance domestic resource mobilization for the social and poverty programs and for investment in infrastructure, and are consistent with lowering inflation to the single digit level and to moving toward a sustainable external current account deficit. Total revenues (excluding the clearance of ZESCO arrears) are projected to rise from 17.7 percent of GDP in 2000 to about 18 percent of GDP by 2003. Over the same period, total expenditures (after adjusting for relief on external interest obligations under the HIPC Initiative) are estimated to decline by around 4.5 percent of GDP. However, spending on social sectors is expected to increase from 4.5 percent of GDP in 2000 to 6.7 percent by2003, as aresult ofHIPCresources.4  

12. Notwithstanding a substantial growth in exports, the external current account deficit (excluding grants) is expected to widen slightly from about 18.5 percent of GDP in 2000 to  

19.9 percent in 200l owing to strong growth in imports. Nonetheless, external financing, including debt relief under the Enhanced HIPC Initiative, should permit a build-up of gross reserves to a comfortable level by 2003. The financing gaps in 2002-03 are expected to be filled by new commitments of grants and highly concessional loans from bilateral and multilateral sources.  

13. The government is cognizant of the importance of continuing to deepen the on-going program of structural reforms to ensure that resources are deployed efficiently. In this context, the government is committed to the divestiture of key state enterprises, in line with the PRGF and HIPC Decision Point documents. Moreover, to improve the monitoring, control and auditing of expenditure, and in order to prevent the accumulation of domestic arrears, the government has agreed to a timetable for the implementation of the IFMIS and development of a medium-term expenditure framework (MTEF).  

14. Some progress has been achieved in preparing the full PRSP, especially on reviewing existing policies and future policy development. The PRSP will: analyze the nature and causes of poverty in Zambia, taking account of the new dimensions of poverty (including HIV/AIDS); specify policies designed to accelerate growth and poverty reduction; establish a poverty monitoring system; and include costings for the priority sectors and programs. Eight working groups have been formed to work on these issues, and a synthesis document is expected to be completed by March 200l and sent to the provinces for consultations with civil society groups and provincial officials during April 2001. The technical secretariat has been expanded to include representatives from key line ministries, and a steering committe comprising ministers and high level officials has been set up to guide and endorse this process.  

IV. THE PROGRAM FOR 2001  

# A. Macroeconomic Policies  

15. Consistent with the medium-term framework, the main objectives of the program for 2001 are to: (i) increase real GDP growth to 5 percent largely on the basis of a strong recovery in mining; (ii) create conditions fostering exchange rate stability; (ii) reduce the 12- month rate of inflation to 17.5 percent by year-end; (iv) increase gross international reserves to the equivalent of 2 months of imports; and (v) start laying the basis for poverty reduction by increasing spending on key priorities in the social and economic sectors. These objectives will involve appropriate financial policies, a strengthening of structural reforms, and improvements in expenditure monitoring and control to ensure that the freed-up HIPC resources are used for poverty reduction.  

# The budget for 2001  

16.Fiscal policy will be geared toward promoting sustainable growth, and to supporting poverty-reducing expenditures. Total revenue and grants are projected to increase slightly by  

1.5 percentage points of GDP, while total expenditures (including the statistical discrepancy) are projected to decline by 0.5 percentage points of GDP.  

17.The government intends to adopt several revenue measures to address poverty and improve the efficiency of the tax system. To counter the accumulated effect of inflation since 1996, the lowest PAYE band has been widened and the basic tax credit increased, and allowable deductions for pension contributions have also been raised. Other measures intended to help the poor and the vulnerable include exempting from income tax severance packages for employees who are retired on medical grounds, and removing the duty on mosquito nets. The government aims to boost lagging nontax revenue, including, in particular, efforts to ensure that lottery proceeds are collected and go to the government. Proceeds from any training or tourism levies that may be under consideration will also be channeled through the budget. To improve efficiency and revenue from the VAT, a set of measures to reduce the administrative costs of refunds will be put in place. In addition, the insurance industry will be moved to VAT-cxempt status to reduce the exceptionally high level of VAT refunds to the industry. To preserve the excise revenue base, ZNOC will be registered as a licensed manufacturer under Section 93 of the Customs and Excise Act and its storage tanks will be designated as a licensed bonded warehouse. This will ensure that valuation of locally produced petroleum products is maintained at the wholesale price for the product ex-Indeni refinery. To maintain the integrity of the tax system, the government will refrain from introducing any new tax reductions, exemptions, rebates, or any other preferential tax treatment during 200l, except that hotel accommodation in the Livingstone area will be zero rated for VAT until end-2002. In addition, there will be no new accumulation of tax arrears by ZESCO and ZNOC to the central government.  

18. Total expenditures for 2001 are estimated at about 32 percent of GDP (including HIPC-related expenditures). Part of the expenditure reflect one-off allocations for: the presidential and parliamentary elections amounting to 0.7 percent of GDP; another 0.5 percent of GDP for the OAU summit; and 0.4 percent of GDP related to the elimination by line ministries of domestic arrears. Furthermore, the budget also includes alocations of 0.8 percent of GDP for repayment of a guarantee to ZNCB on behalf of ZNOC, a contingency reserve of 0.4 percent of GDP, and continued net lending to ZCCM of about 1.4 percent of GDP. The contingency will be used strictly for unanticipated outlays that were not foreseen at the time of budget preparation and for events such as natural disasters, and is significantly smaller than in 2000. Regarding the payments to ZNCB, government intends to make these payments slowly, cautiously and progressively. As for public sector wages, the government will limit the wage billto 5.7 percent of GDP in 2001 and will reduce the share of the wage bill in domestic noninterest expenditures over the medium term. Budgeted allocations for spending on the social sectors (excluding HIPC resources), along with improvements in the funding mechanism, will ensure that at least 36 percent of domestically financed discretionary expenditures go to these sectors.5 Government has decided to  

recapitalize the Development Bank of Zambia. However, in light of resource constraints disbursements for this initiative will be made when external funding for this purpose has been obtained.  

19. As noted above, given the additional resources released under the HIPC initiative, the government intends to boost substantially outlays on social sectors and other poverty-related programs. Accordingly, the government has identified a priority list of programs equivalent to 2.7 percent of GDP.% About 68 percent of these outlays would be used to scale-up existing programs and the rest would be for new programs. In addition to maintaining expenditures in the social sectors, the government intends to ensure rapid growth that would benefit the poor, through income-generating activities especially in rural areas. Thus, of the additional expenditure financed by the HIPC initiative, some 35 percent will go to rural development, and the rest will go to education (19 percent); health (14 percent); water and sanitation (10 percent); HIV/AIDS (9 percent); social safety nets (9 percent); low cost housing (2 percent); and human rights and governance (3 percent).  

20. The government realizes the crucial importance of effective tracking of HIPC-related expenditures, and of monitoring and controlling expenditures generally. An interim tracking system will be set up that involves: (i) establishing a separate HIPC budget code to be used in all disbursement, expenditure, reporting and auditing procedures; (ii) improving the timeliness and quality of monthly fiscal expenditure reports from the line ministries; (iii) publishing consolidated quarterly expenditure reports on HIPC-related activities; (iv) organizing independent semi-annual monitoring of HIPC expenditures and holding public meetings to promote public scrutiny of the use of HIPC resources; and (v) mobilizing external assistance to conduct independent public expenditure tracking surveys.

# Monetary and exchange rate policies  

23. Monetary policy willaim to (i) attain the target for inflation of 17.5 percent; (ii) attain relative stability of the kwacha exchange rate; (ii) build-up gross international reserves of the BoZ; and (iv) facilitate a substantial increase in credit to the private sector in support of productive activities. In light of the rapid expansion in broad money in 2000, and in order to guard against re-igniting inflationary pressures from a monetary over-hang and possibly the lagged effects of the depreciation on inflation, broad money is projected to increase by about 17 percent. Achieving this monetary target will be facilitated by a decline in credit to public enterprises (especially to ZESCO and ZNOC) as outstanding loans are repaid and the strict limits on lending by ZNCB are maintained, and by the net repayments by government to the banking system.  

24.The exchange rate will continue to be market-determined. Central bank intervention in the foreign exchange market will be limited to efforts aimed at meeting the international reserves target and to smooth short-term fluctuations without attempting to influence the underlying market trends. To achieve the monetary policy objectives, the BoZ willundertake indirect monetary policy measures, as necessary, and will refrain from entering into foreign exchange transactions that could expose it to undue risk. Accordingly, we intend to undertake specific measures (see paragraph 25) to improve the operations of the treasury bill market to strengthen implementation of the monetary program. Reaching the reserve target will be  

facilitated by substantial inflows of IMF disbursements under the PRGF (about US\$130 million) and interim assistance from the Fund under the HIPC initiative (US\$150 million).  

25. The government is committed to improving the operation of Zambia's treasury bill market. While recognizing that some steps in this direction will take time to implement, the BoZ intends to take the following actions in the near term: (i) in order to reduce government interest payments and strengthen the auction process, the modifications to the book entry system (provided with World Bank and IMF assistance) have been made that will enable the BoZ to effect a multiple-price format in which successful bidders pay their own bid price. The issuance of revised auction guidelines and the change to multiple pricing will be implemented by June 20o1; (ii) strengthen communications with banks and other market participants. In this regard BoZ staff will poll market participants on likely demand to better match volumes offered by maturity. This will help to ensure that auctions are fully subscribed. The poll will also enable the BoZ to determine the desired treasury bill maturities to offer each week; (ii) introduce repurchase agreements, initially between the BoZ and the commercial banks, as the main vehicle for open market operations by April 2001; (iv) prorate the marginal auction bid so that announced auction amounts will not be exceeded; and (v) the BoZ will strictly adhere to auction guidelines.  

# External sector policies  

26. Zambia's external current account deficit (excluding grants) is projected to widen to around 20 percent of GDP in 2001, as a robust growth in exports is offset by a sharp increase in imports. Total exports are projected to increase by 17 percent mainly on account of a 22 percent increase in metal exports. The latter reflects an improvement in the outlook for copper and cobalt prices and export volume, and is based on the full-year operation of the privatized ZCCM and the newly established mining companies. Nonmetal exports are programmed to grow by 8 percent reflecting, in part, the real depreciation of the kwacha exchange rate during 2000. Total imports are envisaged to increase by 15 percent as a result of increased financing (grant and loans) for projects, and higher foreign direct investments. The projected net capital inflows, together with balance of payments support and debt relief under the HIPC initiative, would allow an accumulation of gross reserves of some US\$135 million.

# B. Structural Reforms and Governance  

28.Clearly, resolving the situation of the ZNCB is a high priority. The recent ful-scope examination of the Bank revealed that a substantial part of its loan portfolio is nonperforming and the bank does not fully meet the minimum regulatory requirements. The government recognizes that addressing its ownership and control of ZNCB will be the key to the necessary reforms. In order to guard against further deterioration in the bank's financial condition that would increase the shareholders (government) financial liability, the Bank of Zambia has instituted additional supervisory measures, including daily monitoring, aimed at ensuring prudential operations and management of the bank. Consistent with this, exemptions from provisioning requirements on nonperforming loans to ZNOC and RAMCOZ will not be extended, unless significant payments (other than from government) have been received. Furthermore, ZNCB will be required to comply with alloan/other assetprovisioning instructions, and will publish by end-April its March 31, 2001 financial statement subject to specific instructions from the central bank.

Approval and hence authority for the ZPA to act will be granted by end of March 2001. Subsequently, ZPA willimplement the decision of Cabinet by end-2001. Progress towards the divestiture of ZNCB will be reviewed during the mid-term review of the program.  

30. In further efforts to strengthen the banking system, the BoZ has imposed a deadline of end-June 200l for the provision of new capital for the Investrust Merchant Bank. Also, Union Bank Zambia, Ltd. has been taken over by the Bank of Zambia and its operations suspended pending possible recapitalization and a takeover by another bank. In addition, Commerce Bank is under liquidation. The BoZ is also considering recommendations to improve, bank supervision, regulatory enforcement, and actions to bring Zambia into compliance with the Basel Core Principles.  

31. Under the petroleum sub-sector, government will implement actions as agreed with the World Bank under the Fiscal Sustainability Credit. The government will then determine what follow-up action may be required, including possible referral to the Director of Public Prosecution for consideration of whether a criminal investigation is warranted, or the Attorney General for possible civil action.  

34. In order to promote transparency, the annual audited government accounts will be prepared within the 12-month period mandated by the Constitution and submitted to parliament. To this end, funding will be sought to increase the resources for the offices of the Accountant General and the Auditor General (OAG), which willfacilitate among other things, interim audits, speedy auditing of the final accounts, and performance audits.

Efforts will be intensified to implement the recommendations of previous technical assistance missions. Zambia will continue to require technical assistance from the Fund and other development partners in the areas of public expenditure management (including IFMIS), tax policy, monetary operations, bank supervision, and treasury operations.

# Zambia: Technical Memorandum of Understanding  

March 29, 2001  

# I. INTRODUCTION  

This memorandum sets out the understandings between the Zambian authorities and the International Monetary Fund regarding the definitions of the quantitative and structural performance criteria and benchmarks for the program supported by the PRGF arrangement, as well as the related reporting requirements. The definitions are valid at the start of the program, but may need to be revisited during the program reviews to ensure that the memorandum continues to reflect best understanding of the Zambian and Fund staff to monitor the program.  

II. QUANTITATIVE PERFORMANCE CRITERIA: DEFINITIONS AND DATA SOURCES  

# A. Net Domestic Assets of the Bank of Zambia (BoZ)  

NDA of BoZ is defined as the monthly-average (based on daily data), during the month of the test dates, of the reserve money less net foreign assets calculated at end-December, 2000 BOZ mid-exchange rates (program exchange rates).' Reserve money includes currency in circulation, required reserves on kwacha deposits, required reserves on foreign currency deposits, positive current account balances of banks with the BoZ and deposits of nongovernment institutions. Net foreign assets of the BoZ are defined as gross international reserves (defined below) plus any other foreign asset, including the IMF interim assistance and the US\$25 million block reserves at the former Meridien Bank, minus foreign liabilities (defined below). The kwacha figures are derived from the U.S. dollar values using the program exchange rate of K 4,158 per U.S. dollar.  

Foreign liabilities are defined as short-term (one year or less in original maturity) foreign currency-denominated liabilities of the BoZ to non-residents and outstanding use of Fund credit.2  

The ceilings on NDA will be adjusted upward by the amount of the shortfall of balance of payments support net of debt service as indicated in Table 1 of performance criteria for the program, up to a maximum of US\$45 milion. The kwacha value of the cumulative shortfall will be calculated at the program exchange rate.  

The ceiling on NDA will also be adjusted downward/upward to reflect decreases/increases in the legal reserve requirements. The adjustor will be calculated as the percent change in the reserve requirement multiplied by the actual amount of reserves (kwacha and foreign currency denominated) at the end of the previous calendar month.  

# B. Net Bank Claims on Government (NCG)  

NCG (Appendix III, Table 1) refers to the net banking system's claims on Central government and is defined as:  

(i) the net position of the government with commercial banks, including: (i) treasury bills; (ii) bonds issued by the Government of the Republic of Zambia (GRZ); (iii) loans and advances; less (iv) support to Meridien Bank (MBZ); and less (v) Central government deposits (defined to include account balances under the authority of controlling officers); plus  

(ii) BoZ holdings of: GRZ statutory bonds; ordinary GRZ bonds; bonds in respect of loans to former parastatals; treasury bills on the trading portfolio of BoZ; treasury bills issued for interest on the BoZ foreign exchange bridging loan to GRZ; other government stock; and the BoZ Kwacha bridging loan to GRZ (including the GRZ's revolving fund under the Fiscal Sustainability Credit), less government deposits at the BOZ; plus  

(iii)The BoZ foreign exchange bridging (forex) loan to the GRZ less donor suspense acount balances. This will be calculated by taking the beginning period stock of the forex loan expressed in kwacha and then adding net external balance of payments support valued at current exchange rates.  

The ceiling on the increase in NCG will be adjusted upward by the amount of the shortfall in balance of payments support net of debt service, up to a maximum of US\$45 million. The kwacha value of the cumulative shortfall will be converted at the program exchange rate.  

The data source for the above will be the“Net Claims on Government Table" produced by BoZ Economics Department, submitted on a weekly basis, and reconciled with the monetary survey.  

# C. Domestic Arrears of Government  

Domestic arrears are defined as: (i) any bill that has been received by a spending Ministry from a supplier for goods and services delivered (and verified) and for which payment has not been made within 30 days; (ii) Wage and salary arrears that were due to be paid in a given month but remained unpaid on the $15^{\mathrm{th}}$ of the following month; and (ii) Interest or principal obligations which remain unpaid 30 days after the due date of payment. The information is to be compiled through audits of the accounts of spending Ministries and agencies, conducted by the Internal Audit division of MOFED. The audits will be completed and data submitted to Fund staf by the Commitments Monitoring Unit within 6 weeks of the end of each quarter.  

# D. Gross International Reserves of the BOZ  

Unless otherwise noted here, gross international reserves of the BoZ will be defined as reserve assets of the Bank of Zambia (Appendix III, Table 2). Reserve assets are defined in the IMF BOP manual ( $5^{\mathrm{th}}$ edition) and elaborated in the reserve template of the Fund's special data dissemination standards (SDDS). They exclude, for example, foreign assets not readily available to or controlled by the monetary authorities, and foreign currency claims on Zambia residents.  

Gross international reserves consist of (i) monetary gold; (ii) foreign currency in cash; (ii) Unencumbered foreign currency deposits at non-resident banks; (iv) foreign securities and deposits; (v) SDR holdings and Zambia's reserve position with the Fund; and (vi) balances in the BIS account related to debt service to Paris Club creditors. Gross reserves will exclude non-convertible currencies, pledged, swapped, or any encumbered reserves assets including but not limited to reserve assets used as collateral or guarantees for third party external liabilities, and will exclude the US\$25 million deposit in Meridien Bank which is under liquidation, and the IMF's interim assistance.  

The floor on gross international reserves will be adjusted: (i) downward by the amount in U.S. dollars of the shortfall in balance of payments support net of debt service, up to a maximum of $\tt U S S45$ million; (ii) downward/upward for any shortfall/excess in the U.S. dollar value of disbursements from the IMF under the PRGF arrangement; and (ii) for any increase in BoZ short-term foreign currency denominated debt (to resident and non-residents), using the definition of short-term debt below.  

For the purpose of this performance criterion, as well as those for external debt and arrears, valuation will be in U.S. dollars using the program exchange rates.  

Data on gross international reserves including its components will be reported by the BoZ on a weekly and end-month basis.  

# E. External Payment Arrears  

Official external payment arrears are defined as the stock of external arrears on debt repayments by the central government and BoZ, except on debts subject to rescheduling.  

Data on arrears are compiled jointly by the MoFED and BoZ and will be reported by MoFED on a quarterly basis.  

# F. Official Medium- and Long-Term Concessional Loans  

This is defined as all forms of official debt contracted or guaranteed by the central government and BoZ having a grant element of more than 40 percent, but excludes debts subject to rescheduling. This includes loans, supplier's credits and leases, that constitute current, i.e. not contingent liabilities, which are created under a contractual arrangement or guarantee through the provision of value in the form of assets (including currency) or services, and which require the government or BoZ to make one or more payment in the form of assets (including currency) or services, at some future point(s) in time; these payments discharge the principal and/or interest liabilities incurred under the contract. Under this definition, such debt includes arrears, penalties, and judiciary awarded damages arising from the failure to make payments under a contractual obligation that constitutes debt as specified above. The grant element is to be calculated by using currency-specific commercial interest reference rates (CIRRs) reported by the OECD); for maturities of less than 15 years, the grant element will be calculated based on six-month averages of CIRRs, and for maturities longer than 15 years, the grant element willbe calculated based on 1O-year averages. Adjustment lending from the World Bank and IMF will be excluded.  

The definition of debt, for the purposes of the limit, is set out in Executive Board Decision No. 6230-(79/140), Point 9, as revised on August 24, 2000 (see Annex below). This performance criterion applies not only to debt as defined in Point 9 of the Executive Board decision, but also to commitments contracted or guaranteed for which value has not been received.  

Detailed data on all new concessional and non-concessional debt contracted or guaranteed will be provided by MoFED on a quarterly basis.  

# G. Official External Short-Term Debt  

This is defined as external debt (as defined above) with a maturity of less than one year contracted or guaranteed by the central government, public enterprises and BoZ. For this purpose short term debt will include forward commodity sales but will exclude normal trade credit for imports. There will be no new official external short-term debt during the program period.  

The data will be reported by the BoZ on a quarterly basis.  

# H. New Loans Guaranteed by the Government or the BoZ for ZESCO and ZNOC  

This is defined as new loans (domestic or foreign) collateralized or guaranteed by the government or the BoZ for the electricity and oil parastatals ZESCO and ZNOC, respectively.  

The data will be provided by the BoZ on a quarterly basis.  

# III. STRUCTURAL PERFORMANCE CRITERIA AND BENCHMARKS  

# A. Divestiture of Zambia National Commercial Bank (ZNCB)  

The performance criterion on the divestiture of ZNCB will be met once Zambia Privatization Agency (or other designated agency) issues international bidding documents (or another acceptable mode of sale) for the sale of the government's majority controlling interest in the ZNCB. In the Enhanced HIPC decision point document (November 20, 2000), the “Issuance of international bidding documents for sale of majority (controlling) interest in the Zambian National Commercial Bank" is one of the triggers for reaching the Floating Completion Point under the Enhanced HIPC Initiative.  

# B. Approval of IFMIS User Requirements and Timetable for Implementation  

The internal project management team refers to the group of government officials charged with the day-to-day work relating to the design and development of the IFMIS, as well as with supporting the external consultants. As described in paragraph 21 of the Letter of Intent, this team will initially be expected to develop user requirements for the design of the IFMIS, as well as a timetable for implementation of the IFMIS. The IFMIS steering committee, chaired by the Secretary to the Treasury and including other high-level officials, will be in overall charge of IFMIS development and will be expected to approve the project management team's proposals.  

# C. HIPC Reporting  

The first quarterly HIPC report, covering the period January--June 2001, is expected to contain consolidated information on the disbursements and uses of HIPC resources for preidentified programs. The interim framework for HIPC tracking, as described in paragraph 20 of the Letter of Intent, involves: establishing a separate HIPC budget code; improving monthly expenditure reports; establishing quarterly HIPC expenditure reports; organizing semi-annual monitoring of HIPC activities; and beginning public expenditure tracking surveys.  

# D. Improving the Operation of the Treasury Bill Market  

The benchmark will be met after the BOZ has issued revised auction guidelines for multiple price auctions. Under the new guidelines, bids will be ordered in ascending order (of price) and winning bids will be the highest that allow for full absorption of the treasury-bills tendered, and the bidders willpay their offered price. Prorating, which occurs when the amount of the marginal (or lowest-price winning) bids exceeds the offered amount remaining in the auction (that is, the total amount offered less the amount awarded to the higher-price bidders), will be necessary to allocate awards to the lowest-price bidders without exceeding the total amount offered. In this case, the amounts awarded to the lowest-price bidders are prorated in proportion to the size of the bid by each respective bidder.  

# E. Preparation of Audited Government Accounts  

"Accounts for $2000^{\circ}$ are defined as the 20oo annual financial statements, as prescribed in the Finance Act and Regulations thereunder.“Timely preparation of final audited accounts as required by law" is taken to mean that the Auditor General will audit the accounts for 2000 in sufficient time to allow the Minister of Finance to present the Financial Report to Parliament within twelve months of the end of the year, i.e., by December 2001.  

# F. Abstention from Tax Reductions and Other Measures  

The government shall refrain from introducing any changes to the tax code, or to the application of the code, that will result in reduced tax collections. This prohibition will cover preferential changes (made in favor of a particular individual, firm, industry, region, or other category) as well as uniform changes, which do not discriminate between taxpayers.  

# G. New Tax Arrears by ZESCO and ZNOC  

This is defined as the tax arrears (PAYE, company income tax, VAT, Excise or import duties) accumulated by ZESCO and ZNOC as determined by the Zambia Revenue Authority.  

The data will be reported on a quarterly basis by the Ministry of Finance.

9. (a) For the purpose of this guideline, the term “debt" will be understood to mean a current, i.e., not contingent, liability, created under a contractual arrangement through the provision of value in the form of assets (including currency) or services, and which requires the obligor to make one or more payments in the form of assets (including currency) or services, at some future point(s) in time; these payments will discharge the principal and/or interest liabilities incurred under the contract. Debts can take a number of forms, the primary ones being as follows:  

(i)loans, i.e., advances of money to the obligor by the lender made on the basis of an undertaking that the obligor will repay the funds in the future (including deposits, bonds, debentures, commercial loans and buyers' credits) and temporary exchanges of assets that are equivalent to fully collateralized loans under which the obligor is required to repay the funds, and usually pay interest, by repurchasing the collateral from the buyer in the future (such as repurchase agreements and official swap arrangements);  

(ii) suppliers' credits, i.e., contracts where the supplier permits the obligor to defer payments until some time after the date on which the goods are delivered or services are provided; and  

(iii)）leases, i.e., arrangements under which property is provided which the lessee has the right to use for one or more specified period(s) of time that are usually shorter than the total expected service life of the property, while the lessor retains the title to the property. For the purpose of the guideline, the debt is the present value (at the inception of the lease) of all lease payments expected to be made during the period of the agreement excluding those payments that cover the operation, repair or maintenance of the property.  

(b) Under the definition of debt set out in point 9 (a) above, arrears, penalties, and judicially awarded damages arising from the failure to make payment under a contractual obligation that constitutes debt are debt. Failure to make payment on an obligation that is not considered debt under this definition (e.g., payment on delivery) will not give rise to debt.

# VII. Exchange Rate Arrangement:  

The currency of Zambia is the kwacha, which is market determined. The official exchange rate quoted by the central bank is calculated as the weighted-average exchange rate quoted by commercial banks through the Bank of Zambia's dealing window. The Bank of Zambia conducts daily foreign exchange dealing sessions at which it invites bids and offers from authorized dealers (i.e., registered commercial banks). On the basis of the bids and offers received, as well as other considerations, the Bank of Zambia determines the amount of foreign exchange to be sold to, or purchased from, the market. On February 28, 2001, the Bank of Zambia midrate was K3,659 per U.S. dollar.

# Zambia: Relations with the World Bank Group  

The World Bank Group has provided 97 loans and credits to Zambia since 1953, with total commitments amounting to US\$3.1 billion, of which about US\$2.5 billion had been disbursed by end-February 2001. Currently disbursing are 16 active IDA credits (see table below). World Bank operations have supported all the main sectors in the economy.  

The main elements of the Bank's strategy are to support efforts to achieve macroeconomic stability and sustainable and equitable growth; ensure suffcient access to imports, and an adequate reserve cushion through maintenance of an appropriate exchange rate and other measures to encourage exports; improve the climate for private sector development through deregulation, provision of infrastructure, and privatization of parastatals; strengthen human resource capacity by rehabilitating the education and health infrastructure and improving the quality of education and health services; implement an effective environmental strategy; and mobilize external resources to support the country's adjustment program.
