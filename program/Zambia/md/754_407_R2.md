# Executive Summary  

# Background  

● Zambia's economic performance continued to improve during 20o1. Provisional data indicate that real GDP grew by over 5 percent, while the inflation rate declined to 18.7 percent from 30 percent in 2000. However, notwithstanding the reduction in inflation, interest rates remained high as a shortfallin donor budgetary support led to a substantial increase in domestic borrowing by the central government. Despite the recent improvement in economic performance, Zambia is now faced with uncertain economic prospects following the decision by Anglo-American plc, the largest investor in the copper mines, to discontinue funding its mining operations in Zambia.  

● All the structural performance criteria and benchmarks for end-September and endDecember 200l were met, as were most quantitative performance criteria for end-September. However, because of fiscal slippages related to the end-year elections and the shortfall in donor support, several quantitative performance criteria for end-December 2001 were not met.  

# The Program for 2002  

● The 2002 program is based on the assumption that the copper mines will remain open and that the government willnot assume ownership of the mines. The main objectives, which are derived from the PRSP, include macroeconomic stability, poverty reduction, and strengthening investor confidence. The program targets real GDP growth of about 4 percent, a reduction in inflation to 13 percent, and an increase in international reserves to 1.7 months of imports. Achicving these objectives willrequire appropriate macroeconomic and structural policies.  

Financial policies are designed to help attain the macro-economic objectives on inflation and growth, while allowing for a reorientation of public expenditures toward social and economic sectors made possible by HIPC debt relief. The primary fiscal deficit is projected to narrow from over 3 percent of GDP in 2001 to virtual balance in 2002, while broad money is projected to increase by 13 percent.  

Structural reforms are geared toward increasing private sector participation and improving the investment climate. They focus on: privatization of the ZNCB and on improving the overall financial system, liberalization of the petroleum sector, strengthening expenditure management and improving governance.  

The program is subject to considerable risk owing to uncertainties in the copper sector. However, in light of the assurances given by the authorities on this issue, and the fact that they have demonstrated their commitment to the program, their efforts justify continued Fund support.

# II. PERFORMANCE UNDER THE PROGRAM AND RECENT ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENTS  

5． As indicated in paragraph 2 of the LOI, all the program's structural performance criteria and benchmarks for end-September and end-December 2001 were observed (LOI, Table 2). All quantitative performance criteria for end-September were met, except for the ceiling on the stock of domestic arrears, which, given the wide deviation in June, was not expected to be met (LOI, Table 1). Regarding end-December 2001 performance, the targets for net domestic assets (NDA) of the BoZ, net bank claims on government (NCG), and gross international reserves were not met, mainly because of a delay in the disbursement of $\mathrm{US}\$50$ million from the World Bank linked to oil sector reforms,² as well as fiscal slippages in the fourth quarter, owing to election-related spending. The issues related to the continuous performance criterion on external payments arrears have been resolved following a new agreement between Zambia and Russia (Paragraph 12). The authorities are requesting waivers for the nonobservance of the performance criteria mentioned above.  

6. During 2001, Zambia made considerable progress toward macroeconomic stability, characterized by sharply higher real GDP and positive per capita income growth, a significant reduction in inflation, and a relatively stable exchange rate (Table 3 and Figure 1). Provisional estimates indicate that real GDP grew by 5.2 percent, slightly exceeding the program target as a strong performance in mining, manufacturing, construction, and wholesale and retail trade more than offset weak agricultural production. Reflecting tight monetary policy, the 12-month inflation rate declined from 30.1 percent in 2000 to 18.7 percent in 2001, exceeding the program target of 17.5 percent because of a sharp rise in maize prices in December due to drought.  

7. Fiscal performance for 2001 was mixed (LOI, paragraph 4), as a significant overrun in aggregate expenditures was partially offset by revenues that substantially exceeded the program target partly on account of one-off factors (Tables 4 and 5 and Figure 2). The wage bill rose to 6.8 percent of GDP as a result of large increases granted to all public sector workers, and additional increases granted to the defense forces and the National Assembly shortly before the December elections. There were also overruns in domestically financed capital and other expenditures in the run-up to the elections. Despite a shortfall in HIPC spending, total cash releases to the social sectors were nearly 20 percent larger than budgeted. As a result, the overall fiscal deficit was 8.1 percent of GDP, compared with 7.4 percent under the program. This outcome, together with the shortfall in balance of payments loans (noted below), caused the government's domestic financing requirement to exceed the programmed level.  

8. The lower than programmed HIPC-financed spending reflected the initial difficulties in establishing an accounting framework, and subsequently the lack of implementation capacity at the line ministries. Nevertheless, overall spending on the social sectors increased. The quarterly cash releases to these programs were published in the local press, and a monitoring committee, including civil society and professional groups, was set up.  

9. During 2001, broad money rose by 10.7 percent, compared with 12.8 percent in the program and 74 percent in 2000, as monetary conditions were tightened by increasing the cash reserve ratio (Table 6 and Figure 3). At the same time, as a result of a substantial increase in the domestic borrowing requirement of the central government, stemming largely from shortfalls in donor disbursements, mainly from the European Union (EU),3 the stock of treasury bills rose from K 345 billion at end-2000 to K 677 billion at end2001. This increase, together with the tight monetary policy, contributed to a sharp rise in real interest rates: the interest rate on 91-day treasury bills rose from 34 percent at end-2000 to about 50 percent at end-2001, despite the significant drop in inflation noted above.  

10. Following a 58 percent depreciation (in local currency terms) against the U.S. dollar in 2000, the kwacha appreciated by about 8 percent during 2001, thereby reversing most of the real depreciation that took place in 2000 (Figure 4). Towards the end of 200l, as the kwacha came under pressure because of uncertainties in the lead-up to the national elections, the BoZ tightened monetary policy again and thus broadly stabilized the currency through the end of the year.  

11.Balance of payments developments during 2001 were mixed. The deficit in the trade balance was larger than envisaged, raising the deficit in the current account by 1.6 percent of GDP to 20.4 percent of GDP. Metal exports rose by 19 percent, slightly more than programmed, as a decline in world copper prices was offset by a 26 percent increase in total export volume. In addition, after a three-year decline, nonmetal exports grew by more than expected, as did tourism receipts. These favorable developments were offset by a significant increase in imports associated with mining activities. The larger deficit in the current account and the considerable shortfalls in balance of payments support were financed by higher-than-expected net capital inflows, mainly as counterpart to the copper sector imports. As a result, the overall deficit was broadly in line with the program target. The program's adjusted gross reserves target was, nevertheless, missed because of the lower donor support noted above.

13. The authorities made significant progress in implementing structural reforms in 2001 (LOI, paragraph 9). The Zambia Privatization Agency (ZPA) invited international tenders for the sale of a 35 percent share of the Zambia National Commercial Bank (ZNCB), with management control ceded to the buyer. In addition, the BoZ continued strengthening the overall financial sector and closely supervised the ZNCB based on a targeted inspection as at end-October 2001. The cabinet instructed the ZPA to prepare the state-owned electricity company, ZESCO, for concessioning, consistent with the triggers for the HIPC Initiative completion point. However, reform was slower than envisaged in the oil sector. While the state-owned Zambia National Oil Company (ZNOC) was put under receivership in November 2001, the concessioning of the Indeni refinery, the TAZAMA pipeline and the Ndola storage tank was delayed.

The government committed itself to incorporate the work of the summit into its medium-term policy framework. The new government completed the process and is submitting the final PRSP document to the Boards of the Fund and the Bank.  

II. REPORT ON THE DISCUSSIONS  

# A. The Medium-Term Framework  

15. The key medium-term challenge facing Zambia is to sustain the recent gains in growth of per capita income in light of the announcement by the Anglo American Plc (AA) in January 2002 that it would withdraw from KCM. This decision reflected financial losses linked to declining world copper prices and the AA's failure to obtain funding for the Konkola Deep Mining Project (KDMP), which was the main reason for its investment in Zambia in March 20oo. Given the potentially large negative economic and social impact of closing the mines (see Annex I), the government is determined to make every effort to keep the mines open. However, because of the potentially open-ended call on public resources, the government has made a commitment not to take over the mines (LOI, paragraph 18).  

16. In consultation with the private minority shareholders in the KCM, 5 the government, is conducting negotiations with AA, with a view to pursuing options that would ensure the mines continue to operate until AA's replacement by a new strategic investor. In the meantime, AA has agreed to leave the management team in place for up to a year, though the government prefers a longer period. The discussions with AA have also focused on an“exit fee" which would cover all future liabilities related to retrenchment and environmental costs. There have been some expressions of interest in the KCM, including requests for due diligence, though nothing firm has been agreed. The program's baseline assumption is that the mines will stay open, but with sharply reduced export volume growth, and that the KDMP is postponed indefinitely. The baseline scenario is described in Annex I, together with an alternative scenario showing the impact of closure and the medium-term outlook envisaged prior to the AA's announcement.  

17. In addition to the weaker outlook for copper exports, the program includes cautious projections of donor budgetary support for 2002-4, as shown below. Nevertheless, the government is firmly committed to taking all possible steps to sustain and enhance cooperation with donors in order to sustain and, to the extent feasible, increase the level of donor budget support.

# Box 2. Prospects for Diversification  

In view of the deteriorating prospects for the copper sector, the need to diversify Zambia's economy is urgent. The PRSP has identified agriculture, tourism, and textiles as offering the best opportunities for diversification and poverty reduction.  

Agriculture. The availability of cheap labor, high-quality land, and water offers scope for developing agro-based activities. Although the sector's growth has been sluggish, its potential is evidenced by the significant increases in agro-based nontraditional exports in recent years. These exports increased from US\$100 million in 1992 to $\mathrm{US}\$281$ million in 2001, of which the floriculture and horticulture industries accounted for about $\mathrm{USS50}$ million and employs about 15,000 people. To support the agriculture sector, the 2002 budget includes a number of initiatives such as: establishing a new agency to improve the distribution and marketing of inputs and output; introducing a modest subsidy scheme for fertilizer; supporting outgrower schemes for cash crops; reducing taxes on fuel and electricity; and making a commitment to improve rural roads and infrastructure.  

Tourism. Zambia offers numerous attractive destinations for tourists, including high-quality game parks and the Victoria Falls. In support of the sector, the government plans to allocate resources to improve critical infrastructure, such as roads, airports, telecommunications, and security. In addition, it has provided time-bound tax incentives for investment, including for establishment of hotels and lodges, and it has facilitated the provision of hunting licenses. The sector has the potential to grow by as much 10 percent a year over the medium term.  

Textiles. Ready-made garments geared for exports are another potential source of growth. A World Bank study indicated that textile manufacturing in Zambia based, mainly on low-labor costs and imported inputs, is cost competitive despite the relatively high land transport costs. Moreover, Zambia can take advantage of the African Growth and Opportunities Act and the Trade and Development Act, which give duty- and quota-free access to the U.S. market for African countries such as Zambia. In addition, South Africa has reduced barriers to Zambia's textile exports in the context of SADC.  

The government is also keenly aware of the heavy economic tollon Zambia arising from the HIV/AIDS pandemic and its impact on the skilled labor supply, savings and investments, and, therefore, growth. Given the difficulties noted above, the revised macroeconomic targets for the period 2002-4 include: (i) real GDP growth of about 4 percent per annum; (ii) a reduction in inflation to single digits by 2003; and (ii) a buildup of gross international reserves to about three months of import cover by end-2004. These targets are consistent with the PRSP.  

19. In view of the uncertainties about external donor financing flows, the mediumterm fiscal strategy aims to enhance domestic fiscal sustainability by maintaining a strong revenue effort and containing overall expenditures while reorienting them toward productive infrastructure and poverty-reducing programs. The authorities recognize the negative impact of the existing large stock of domestic debt on domestic interest costs in the budget and on real interest rates, and, thus, on investment and economic prospects. Indeed, if the trend observed in 2001 were to continue, it would rapidly lead to an unsustainable debt situation (Box 3). For this reason, it is essential to constrain domestic financing, which is programmed to decline from 4.5 percent of GDP in 2001 to 1.2 percent of GDP in 2002, and to eliminate such borrowing in 2003 and 2004 (Tables 4 and 5). In support of this goal, the domestic fiscal deficit is projected to narrow from 4.7 percent of GDP in 2001 to 2 percent in 2004. Even with this adjustment, there would be financing gaps in 2003 and 2004 if additional domestic borrowing were to be eliminated. These gaps could be covered by a combination of additional donor support and debt relief beyond that provided under the enhanced HIPC Initiative. The authorities are committed to covering any remaining gaps through additional fiscal measures (LOI, paragraph 16).  

20. As mentioned above, Zambia's medium-term external outlook has deteriorated substantially because of the difficulties in the copper sector. In the program's baseline scenario, the originally projected strong growth in copper exports has been scaled down considerably, and the healthy real GDP growth rates assumed in previous program projections and in the HIPC Initiative decision point document have been reduced accordingly. The external current account deficit narrows from over 20 percent of GDP in 2001 to about 16 percent by 2004 as imports associated with the development of KDMP are no longer envisaged (Table 8). Despite the lower current account deficit, however, the reduced net capital inflows are expected to lead to financing gaps averaging about $\mathrm{\us\$246}$ million a year during 20o3-4. As noted above, these gaps could be filled by additional grants and concessional loans from multilateral institutions and bilateral sources, and by additional debt relief over and above that provided under the enhanced HIPC Initiative.  

# Box 3. Domestic Debt Dynamics  

The government's rising domestic debt stock and interest costs raises the possibility that Zambia's fiscal stance could become unsustainable. Real interest rates are well above the rate of real GDP growth, and if this situation continues, Zambia will find itself on a fiscally unsustainable path, leading to explosive growth in the stock of debt. Under such conditions, the budget must generate a substantial primary surplus just to avoid an increase in debt arising from the interest on the existing stock of debt.

# B. The Program for 2002  

21. The key assumption underlying the program for 2002 is that the KCM will remain open and will produce the same amount of copper as in 2001 (paragraph 18 of the LOI). The government is committed not to assume ownership of the mines, and it is anticipated that the financial problems of the sector can be resolved without direct financial support from government. Therefore, the fiscal program does not include any provision for government support to the mining sector, other than a small amount for social safety nets. In the event that government support becomes unavoidable, the government has made a commitment to first seek external concessional financing, and, if such financing is unavailable, to implement further fiscal measures, including broad-based tax measures to ensure that such expenditures are fully financed.  

22. As noted in Appendix I, the staff has had discussions with officials at KCM, other mining companies, World Bank staff and government officials regarding the viability of KCM and the feasibility of the program's baseline scenario. An initial cash flow estimate provided by KCM in March 2002, and revised by the staff to reflect WEO copper prices and some success in KCM's cost reduction program, suggested a financing gap of the order of $\mathrm{US}\$30$ million for 2002 and a broadly breakeven position for 2003 if world copper prices recover as anticipated. The staff understands that provisional estimates prepared by the government's financial advisors indicate that KCM would be in a breakeven position this year if all nonessential capital expenditures are deferred. Moreover, according to the Zambian authorities, the negotiations are also concermed with other possible sources of funds, for example, shareholder contributions on amounts held back following AA's announcement, and a substantial insurance claim related to the landslide at the Nchanga open-pit mine. The staff further understands that some major donors are sympathetic to providing additional assistance related to the financial diffculties of the mining sector. Thus, while clearly subject to considerable uncertainty, available information supports the feasibility of the authorities mining sector policies. The staff will maintain close contact with the World Bank staff who concur with this view, and are continuing to assist the government in designing these policies.  

23. On this basis, the authorities' key macroeconomic targets for 2002, which are derived from the PRSP are (i) achieve real GDP growth of about 4 percent, as growth in the services sectors partially offsets lower growth of mining output and the effect of poor weather conditions on agriculture; (ii) reduce the 12-month inflation rate to 13 percent; (ii) increase gross international reserves by $\mathrm{USS116}$ million, to the equivalent of 1.7 months of imports cover; and (iv) continue to support poverty reduction by reorienting expenditure towards key priorities in the social and economic sectors. Achieving these objectives will require appropriate fiscal and monetary policies, a strengthening of structural reforms, and improving public expenditure management, along with an increase in transparency and accountability in government finances.  

# Fiscal policy  

24. The domestic fiscal deficit is programmed to decline from 4.7 percent of GDP in 2001 to 3.1 percent in 2002, in support of the monetary program and in moving toward medium-term fiscal sustainability (LOI, paragraphs 20-24). This implies that the domestic primary deficit will be reduced to zero from a deficit of 3.1 percent of GDP in 2001. Accordingly, the domestic financing need will be reduced to 1.2 percent of GDP. Revenues are expected to decline slightly to 18.1 percent of $\mathrm{GDP}^{7}$ as a result of various tax concessions introduced with the budget in March 2002 in support of the government's growth and poverty-reduction objectives (Tables 4 and 5). In particular, the government is reducing the excises on diesel and electricity; restructuring the pay-as-you-earn (PAYE) tax in favor of lower-paid workers; making antimalaria supplies tax exempt; and, to raise revenues, introducing new excises on cosmetics, perfumes, and motor vehicles, and making income from government bonds taxable (as is already the case for treasury bills). The government has also reduced the excise on beer in exchange for a revenue guarantee from the brewery. While some of these measures will have a desirable social impact, they have also lowered revenues and thus added to budgetary pressures.  

25. Given the already high revenue-to-GDP ratio, the burden of fiscal adjustment will fall on domestic expenditures, which will be limited to 21.2 percent of GDP, compared with 23.9 percent of GDP in 2001.8 Certain categories of spending, however, are projected to rise; these include the wage bill, which is expected to reach 7.l percent of GDP in 2002 on account of the pay increase awarded to the security forces shortly before the election and domestic interest payments, which are projected to reach 3 percent of GDP. The program also assumes a reduction in domestic arrears equivalent to 0.3 percent of GDP. The resulting compression of aggregate expenditures is mostly borne by recurrent departmental charges (RDCs), which are programmed to decline by more than l percent of GDP. This reduction in RDCs takes into account: the exclusion of the once-off expenses in 200l related to the OAU Summit and the elections, and the related higher security expenditures; a decentralization initiative in the education sector which has lowered expenditures; the lower diesel excise which should reduce sharply fuel costs for government; and sharply reduced funding for special security operations on Zambia's border with Angola. Domestically financed capital expenditures (excluding HIPC financed outlays) will also decline.  

26. The mission discussed alternative policy choices and the various constraints and trade-offs involved. Specifically, in light of the significant uncertainties relating to the mining sector and external budgetary support, as well as the need to ease the compression of recurrent departmental charges, the staff discussed the possibility of temporarily raising the VAT rate from 17.5 percent to 20 percent. However, the authorities instead proposed an import tariff surcharge, but the mission pointed out the need for Zambia to maintain its current liberal external trade regime, and to protect its external competitiveness. It was, therefore, agreed that raising the VAT would be considered as a contingency in the event that additional resources for the mining sector become unavoidable. The authorities felt that a staff proposal to raise the top PAYE rate to 35 percent (to align it with the corporate income tax rate) for the top l percent of taxpayers would raise little revenue and cause political difficulties. On the expenditure side, the authorities also argued that efforts to reduce the wage bill, including by rolling back the December 200l wage increases, would be  

impractical, and that the burden of adjustment must necessarily fall on recurrent departmental charges (RDCs). In the absence of election-year pressures, and with improved discipline in public expenditure management, the staff views the programmed expenditure restraint as ambitious, but attainable.  

27. The budget is substantially reoriented toward PRSP priority areas, such as education, health, and agriculture. The provisional functional classification indicates that appropriations for the key social and economic functions have increased from 56.4 percent of the total budget in 2001 to 61.5 percent in 2002 (Table 11). As part of its general commitment to publicly account for the use of HIPC Initiative debt relief, the government is also tracking a core list of priority subprograms as “HIPC Initiative-financed" spending, which for 2002 amount to 2.2 percent of GDP (Table 5 and Box 4). HIPC resources continue to be disbursed through the special HIPC account at the BoZ. Transactions from the HIPC account are tagged with a separate budget code, and the Accountant General prepares special reports showing the disbursement and utilization of HIPC funds. The poverty and social impact of the program is discussed in Box 5.  

# Monetary policies  

28. Monetary policy for 20o2 aims to achieve the program's inflation and growth targets and to bolster considerably the international reserves position (Tables 6 and 7). In line with these objectives, broad money expansion will be limited to 15 percent, and the BoZ will meet this target through the use of indirect monetary instruments (LOI, paragraph 25). The BoZ also intends to adhere to the treasury bill auction guidelines to ensure that interest rates reflect market conditions. Given the projected net credit to government and to the public enterprises, the monetary program provides room for a sufficient increase in credit to the private sector in support of the program's growth objective  

29. The government is concerned about the high real interest rates. As noted earlier, these reflect mainly higher domestic borrowing by the government over the past few years, and especially in 200l, persistent inflationary expectations, banking sector inefficiencies as well as the tight monetary policy. Thus, in addition to reducing its domestic borrowing requirement, the government intends to address the inflationary expectations by further lowering inflation. Moreover, the government has agreed that, any external budgetary support in excess of that included in the program, will be used to reduce government debt owed to the banking system provided that it is consistent with the monetary program. The central bank will also implement additional policies, generally aimed at reducing costs for commercial banks, with a view to facilitating a reduction in commercial bank lending rates (LOI, paragraph 26). One of the measures introduced was a pilot scheme that exempts lending to agriculture from commercial banks reserve requirements. The staff noted that the scheme would be distortionary and could be subject to substantial abuse. The staff, therefore, proposed and the government agreed to monitor the scheme closely and to revisit it during the next review of the program.

# Box 5. Poverty and the Social Impact of the Program  

# Expenditure policy  

A major focus of the PRGF-supported program for 2002 is on implementing public expenditurc rcforms to ensure that government resources are oriented toward PRSP priorities. The introduction of the IFMIS and other expenditure control measures will improve budget execution and monitoring, thereby increasing the likelihood that budgeted expenditures actually reach the poor.  

# Revenue measures  

On the revenue side, the basic PAYE tax credit, has been increased by 25 percent, giving direct relief to the poorest workers, while the 10 percent and 20 percent bands have been replaced with a flat 30 percent rate, thereby increasing the marginal tax rate for middle incomes. Other pro-poor measures include exempting all antimalarial supplies from VAT and customs duty. Malaria is still the number-one public health problem in Zambia (with a mortality rate higher than that of HIV/AlDS), and this exemption will help reduce the incidence of the disease among the poor. Finally, the revenue-raising measures proposed in the 2002 budget, such as excises on cosmetics, perfumes, and motor vehicles, have been chosen to avoid any adverse impact on the poor.  

# Privatization of the ZNCB  

A number of unprofitable rural branches of the ZNCB are likely to be affected by the privatization of the bank. Thus, the ZPA will conduct a feasibility study to identify options for creating a new institution to provide financial services in these areas.  

# External outlook and financing  

30. Zambia's exports are projected to increase only modestly in 2002 as a robust growth in nonmetal exports is ofset by lower growth in metal exports. The latter reflects a projected 4 percent decline in copper prices with only a marginal increase in copper volumes. As noted above, the large reduction in metal sector imports owing to the shelving of KDMP will cause the current account deficit to narrow from 20.4 percent of GDP in 2001 to 17.2 percent in 2002 (Table 8). Correspondingly, the improvement in the current account balance will be offset by a projected decline in private sector capital inflows and as a result, the overall balance of payments deficit would remain broadly unchanged. Nevertheless, with balance of payments support projected at about US\$160 million and debt relief under the enhanced HIPC Initiative of $\mathrm{US}\$267$ million, the program provides for a recovery of international reserves to $\mathrm{US}\$229$ million, or 1.7 months of imports, compared with 0.8 month at end-2001. Most of the balance of payments support will come from the World Bank (US\$107 million) and is linked to oil sector reforms ( $\mathrm{USS50}$ million), and the rest is mainly a supplement to the earlier Fiscal Sustainability Credit. EU disbursements are projected at US\$33 million and  

following the Zambian government's response to the EU's audit report on the use of funds during the 199Os, an EU delegation visited Lusaka in April with a view to resolving the outstanding issues. The remaining disbursements are expected from the African Development Bank and are tied to decentralization (see Box 6).  

31. The authorities reiterated their firm commitment to maintain a liberal trade and exchange regime. They also stressed that the exchange rate will continue to be determined by market forces, with BoZ intervention in the foreign exchange market limited to mccting the program's gross international reserves target and moderating sharp short-term fluctuations of the rate. The authorities noted that the real appreciation that took place in 2001 represented a reversal of the sharp depreciation in 2000, which, in their view, was out of line with prevailing market conditions, and that the current level of the exchange rate was broadly appropriate. Nevertheless, the authorities recognize that reduced expectations about future copper exports and the resulting need to improve the competitiveness of nontraditional exports could lcad to pressures on the exchange rate." They agreed to monitor closely exchange rate developments and reiterated their commitment to allow the exchange rate to be market determined and to depreciate. In further efforts to strengthen its status as an open economy, Zambia has accepted its obligations under Article VIl, Sections 2, 3, and 4.

On arrears, line ministries have submitted certificates of acceptance attesting to the validity of the figures produced in the latest quarterly audit by the Controller, Internal Audit. The Auditor General has also completed his audit of major contractors at the Ministry of Works and Supply, and his findings are broadly consistent with those of the Controller, Internal Audit. A joint task force consisting of representatives from the MoFNP and the BoZ has been constituted and is expected to prepare a proposal by July 2002 for streamlining the accounts and agreeing on the terms and conditions for the restructured BoZ's claims on the government. Subsequently, the government should decide on the strategy by September 2002 and implement the new system by end-December 2002.  

39. As indicated in the PRSP, efforts to fight corruption and improve governance are central to government policy. As part of this objective, transparency and accountability in the management of public resources are key areas of concern. Therefore, in addition to the PEM reforms, the agenda includes the timely preparation and submission to parliament of audited accounts as required by the law. In addition, government intends to publish the final report on ZCCM cobalt sales and refer it to the Attorney General for possible further action (LOI, paragraph 37). The government will also strengthen institutions including the office of the Auditor General and the Anti-Corruption Commission, by channeling more resources to them.

# IV. MEDIUM-TERM FINANCING AND CAPACITY TO REPAY THE FUND  

41.Because the revised copper exports are lower than envisaged at the time of the HIPC Initiative decision point, it will be more difficult than previously envisaged for Zambia to achieve a sustainable level of debt at the completion point. The ratio of the net present value of external debt to exports at the completion point is now projected at 218 percent based on end-2002 data, compared with the 168 percent projected at the time of the decision point (Table 10).l' The debt-service ratio is projected to be 15.4 percent in 2002 compared with the 10.5 percent projected at the decision point. Given the fundamental exogenous changes in Zambia's external position since the decision point, it is anticipated that Zambia will require significant debt relief beyond that provided under the enhanced HIPC Initiative. Should the program's assumptions on copper volume and prices not be borne out, the amount of debt relief needed to reach debt sustainability would be even higher (Annex I). Additional debt relief and further assistance from the Fund and donors will help Zambia to move toward a sustainable external position. Zambia's increasing fragile external situation has reduced the margin for error if Zambia is to avoid a build-up of external arrears, and strong program implementation is essential.

# VI. STAFF APPRAISAL  

43.Zambia's overall economic performance during 20o1 continued to improve. Provisional data indicate that, as in 20oo, real economic growth per capita was positive, and the 12-month rate of inflation declined in line with the program. These  

improvements are in marked contrast to Zambia's record over the previous two decades. Nevertheless, because of slippages in fiscal policy in the fourth quarter linked to election pressures, combined with large shortfalls in external support, the end-December 2001 performance criteria on net credit to the government, net domestic assets of the Bank of Zambia, and gross international reserves were missed. In contrast, all the program's structural performance criteria and benchmarks for end-September and end-December 2001 were observed.  

44.Despite this improvement in economic performance, Zambia now faces very difficult financial and economic prospects. The decision by Anglo American to discontinue funding for its mining operations has put in doubt the future of Zambia's copper sector. Given the implications that would follow closure of the mines, the staff supports the authorities' resolve to make every effort to keep the mines in operation by finding a new strategic investor. In this regard, the staff welcomes and fully supports the government's firm commitment not to take over ownership of the mines, and find a financially viable solution consistent with government finances. Moreover, the staff commends the government's intention to implement new fiscal measures if some short-term public support is essential and concessional funding is not available. However, the government should not assume commitments that go beyond such short-term support.  

45. Given that the contribution of copper to the economy can be expected to decline as the economic life of the mines is exhausted, policies to diversify the economy and to promote economic efficiency are essential and need to be implemented without delay. The staff, therefore, welcomes the recent increase in nontraditional exports and the general improvement in private sector economic performance and investment evidenced in the past two years. These developments must be reinforced. While some key factors are beyond the government's control, particularly regional political instability, creating a favorable investment climate must be at the core of Zambia's economic policy framework, and macroeconomic stability is central to attainment of this objective.  

46. Zambia's program for 2002 aims to lower the rate of inflation further, bolster the external position, and boost expenditures for poverty reduction. To that end, the proposed fiscal adjustment tries to balance macroeconomic objectives, with increased spending in support of poverty reduction. Sustained improvement in the implementation of fiscal policy will be critical. The staff notes with particular concern the overrun in the wage bill during 200l and urges the authorities to avoid further adjustments in salaries outside the context of a medium-term civil service pay reform. The projected increase in spending on poverty-reducing programs will necessitate strict limits on other discretionary outlays and this will require significant financial discipline. Given the difficulties experienced in obtaining external budgetary support in the past two years, it will be vital for Zambia to take the measures that will help ensure that pledged donor funds are disbursed in : timely manner in order to avoid a compression of social sector spending and to help maintair macroeconomic stability. Close monitoring of developments in this area will be essential.  

47.The staff supports the proposed monetary program which is aimed at reducing further inflation and bolstering the BoZ's low international reserves. Lowering the high level of real interest rates will require; continued pursuit of sound fiscal and monetary policies to address persisting inflationary expectations and to contain the recent increase in public sector debt; and measures to make the financial system more efficient. Appropriate external sector policies will also be crucial and the staff commends the authorities' commitment to maintain a liberal trade and exchange system, and to allow the exchange rate to continue to be determined by market forces. The government's acceptance of the obligations under Article VIll is also an important confirmation of its commitment to openness and liberal external policies.  

48. The staff strongly endorses the government's plans to strengthen public expenditure management, including improving monthly fiscal reporting, as these are critical to enhance efficiency, prevent the emergence of domestic payments arrears, and strengthen the capacity for tracking poverty-reducing expenditures. To achieve a lasting solution for the longer term, it is essential that the work on developing an IFMIS remains on track.  

49. In support of the diversification strategy noted above, it will be important to reinforce and accelerate progress in structural reforms, especially privatization, and improvements in transparency and governance. The staff welcomes the authorities? efforts to privatize the ZNCB and urges them to conclude the sale as rapidly as possible. The staff also commends the BoZ's ongoing efforts to improve the functioning of the financial sector. Regarding the oil sector, the recent liberalization, and the liquidation of ZNOC will improve efficiency, and protect the public finances and the banking system.  

50. The staff welcomes the completion of the PRSP, which has benefited from the inputs of a wide cross section of Zambian society. It is designed to focus on reducing poverty through the pursuit of growth and the more effective targeting and implementation of public sector policies. The staff is of the view that the PRSP provides a basis for continued financial support on concessional terms. The staff welcomes the authorities' commitment to follow through on the policy directions specified in the PRSP, especially regarding macroeconomic and structural reforms, and investment in human capital and infrastructure. The staff also notes the strong emphasis in the PRSP on improved governance, including zero tolerance of corruption. This commitment, if rigorously and effectively implemented, will help promote private sector investment and strengthen donor support.  

51. The quality and timeliness of Zambia's data needs to be improved. The staff urges the authorities to make a concerted effort to ensure effective implementation of past technical assistance recommendations, and notes that further technical assistance from the Fund and other donors will be needed for some time.  

52. As noted above, the developments in the copper sector have resulted in considerable uncertainty regarding Zambia's economic prospects. There are also medium-term domestic and external financing gaps stemming, inter alia, from the anticipated further declines in budget support from Zambia's cooperating partners and recently concluded debt-service agreements. Many of these factors are beyond the government's control, and the program is therefore subject to a larger-than-usual degree of risk. The authorities have adopted an appropriate policy framework to address these difficult economic and financial circumstances. They are also committed to strict financial discipline and to implement structural reform measures, in order to improve welfare and living standards for all Zambians. Strict adherence to program implementation should also facilitate more timely external support. The authorities have, thus far, demonstrated their commitment to achieve the program's major objectives, and are committed to monitor developments closely and to adopt any corrective actions that may be necessary. The staff, therefore, recommends completion of the fourth review under the PRGF arrangement, approval of the requested waivers, an augmentation of 5 percent of quota and the extension of interim HIPC Initiative assistance from the Fund for 2002.

# The Copper Sector: The Baseline Program Scenario, Sensitivity Analysis, and Macroeconomic and Social Impact of Closure of Mines  

1. Copper accounts for about two-thirds of Zambia's exports and 7 percent of GDP, and provides employment for about 40,o00 people. Taking into account the linkages to trade and transport and other sectors, however, the mining sector's contribution to domestic production and employment is substantially largcr. Thcrc are four major mining companies operating in Zambia: Konkola Copper Mines (KCM), with Anglo American Plc (AA) holding a majority of its shares, is the largest mining company and accounts for about two-thirds of Zambia's copper production; Mopani Copper Mines, with majority shares owned by First Quantum and Glencore, accounts for about 30 percent of copper production; and two other mining companies, Chibuluma and Bwana Mkubwa, are owned by South African mining groups and together account for about 7 percent of total copper production. These mines also produce a small amount of cobalt.' Thus, given the dependency of the economy on the copper sector, the closure of KCM would have a serious adverse impact on the Zambian economy in the near and medium term. To illustrate the medium-term impact of closing KCM, the staff has developed an alternative scenario of mine closure, which is compared with the baseline program scenario. In addition, a sensitivity analysis assuming that copper prices are 10 percent lower than in the baseline scenario is also presented.  

2. The baseline scenario (scenario 1 in the attached table) projections are based on discussions with officials of KCM, Mopani and Chibuluma mines, members of the government task force, offcials from the Ministry of Mines, other Ministries, and staff of the World Bank. The scenario assumes that the production of KCM in 20O2 is maintained at the level of 2001 and the Konkola Deep Mining Project (KDMP) is postponed indefinitely. However, overall output in the copper sector is assumed to grow by about 2 percent in 2002, based on about 5 percent growth in mining output from all other mines combined. Thereafter, total output of the copper sector declines by 1.6 percent annually during 2003-04. Production in KCM declines gradually consistent with the assumed remaining economic life of the mine of nine years. Production of other mines is assumed to remain unchanged during the period. These production figures are in sharp contrast to the 15 percent increase in output recorded in 2001. Compared with the medium-term projection envisaged in November 2001, which has assumed a marked improvement in copper output and exports, the current baseline scenario results in a significant deterioration in the medium-term growth and external outlook reflecting mainly declining prospects of the copper sector.  

3． Scenario 2 presents the case in which KCM closes down immediately, implying that KCM's production in 2002 would be about one-third of the level in 2001 and zero thereafter. As in the baseline scenario, the production of other mines is assumed to grow at 5 percent in  

2002 with no growth projected thereafter. As a result, there would be a sharp contraction in overall mining sector output, of about 43 percent in 2002 and 35 percent in 2003; thereafter, the level of output would remain unchanged.  

4. The macroeconomic impact of the baseline scenario compared with the closure scenario is shown in the attached table. The estimation of the impact of mine closure on real GDP growth takes into account the direct and indirect effects and an assumed 50 percent pass through of nominal depreciation to inflation. It is also assumed that about half of the external financing gap is covered by a narrowing of the current account deficit through a real depreciation, while the other half is covered by additional donor support and debt relief over and above that provided under the enhanced HIPC Initiative. It is assumed that the impact of a real depreciation on nonmetal exports would be modest during the 2002-04 period because of the relatively low short-term supply elasticity of agricultural production. Regarding the fiscal impact, the closure of the mines would have only a moderately negative impact on fiscal aggregates. The sharp depreciation of the kwacha that would boost the value of net external inflows (balance of payments support net of debt service) and would increase trade tax collections by more than the loss in current PAYE revenue from the mines, and KCM is exempt from other taxes.² On the expenditure side, domestic expenditures relative to GDP are assumed to increase only modestly.3 However, even though on balance the net fiscal impact of mine closure appears to be moderately negative, closure of the mines would have a significant social impact, that the government would certainly need to address. These extra payments to provide social safety nets for the retrenched workers could be substantially higher than the annual cost of 0.5-1.0 percent of GDP assumed in the mine closure scenario and as a result the fiscal impact of mine closure could be substantially negative.  

5. The results of the sensitivity analysis, assuming that copper prices are 10 percent lower than the baseline scenario, are also summarized in the Annex table. Under the baseline scenario and consistent with WEO price projections, the average world prices of a pound of copper are $\mathrm{USS0.74}$ in 2002, $\mathrm{USS0.82}$ in 2003, and $\mathrm{US}\$80.86$ in 2004. The sensitivity analysis does not assume any change in the volume of copper production relative to the baseline scenario.  

# Scenario 1: The Baseline—KCM remains open  

6. As envisaged in the program, for the period 2002-04, the baseline scenario projects real GDP growth of about 4 percent; a reduction in inflation to 5 percent by 2003; an  

improvement in the overall fiscal balance of 2.2 percent of GDP; and a narrowing of the external current account deficit from 20.4 percent of GDP in 2001 to 16.2 percent in 2004.4  

# Scenario 2: Immediate closure of KCM  

7. This scenario results in a decline of 2 percent in real GDP growth in 2002 (a 6 percentage points swing relative to the baseline case) and with virtually no growth anticipated for 2003. The rate of inflation is estimated to be about 8-13 percent higher during 2002-04 than under the baseline scenario primarily reflecting the larger real exchange rate depreciation, which is estimated to be about l4 percent in 2002, and l2-l3 percent in 2003- 04.  

8. A rapid closure of mines leads to a deterioration in the overall fiscal position during 2002-04. However, with domestic expenditure increasing only modestly, the resulting domestic deficits during 2002-3 are only modestly higher than in the baseline case reflecting the large positive impact of the nominal depreciation of the kwacha on the revenue-GDP ratio.  

9. Because of a sharp decline in mining exports, the external current account deficit deteriorates relative to the baseline scenario by about 3-4 percent of GDP annually during 2002-4. As a result, the external financing gaps are correspondingly larger. The ratio of the NPV of official debt to exports would be 263 percent at end-2003, compared with 213 percent in the baseline scenario, while the debt-service ratio would be about 4 percentage points higher than in the baseline case.  

# Sensitivity analysis: 10 percent lower copper prices  

10. Assuming copper prices were 10 percent lower compared with the baseline scenario would have a substantial adverse impact on Zambia's external economic prospects.5 The kwacha is estimated to depreciate by about 10 percent in real terms in 2002 and 4 percent annually during 2003-04. The current account deficit deteriorates by about 1.5 percent of GDP relative to the baseline in each year during 2002-04. As a consequence, an additional financing gap of about $\mathrm{USS}30$ million would emerge in each year during 2002-04. The ratio of the NPV of official debt to exports would rise to 223 percent at end-2003, while the debtservice ratio would be about one percent higher than in the baseline case. However, the depreciation of the kwacha has no negative fiscal impact given the positive impact of the  

nominal depreciation on revenue under the assumption that copper production and employment remain unchanged leading to no change in PAYE revenues.  

11. Assuming copper prices were 10 percent higher than in the baseline case would likely have symmetrically positive impact on the external economic prospects of Zambia. With a likely real appreciation of the kwacha, the fiscal position would likely deteriorate somewhat.  

# Poverty and social impact analysis of mine closure  

12.The Copper belt has a population of 1.6 million, 77 percent of it urban and living in small towns that have grown up around mining. It is not among the poorest provinces; however, urban poverty and inequality have risen since 1996 possibly reflecting a decline in mining activity since then.6 In the past, the parastatal ZCCM provided secure employment and considerable nonwage benefits, including housing and food subsidies; these nonincome benefits have been reduced with privatization.  

13. Closure of the mines in 2002 would affect both the KCM workforce and other linked enterprises. The fiscal cost of accompanying safety nets could be considerable. The KCM mining towns could become “ghost towns" like Luanshya (see box below). Surveys have shown that when RAMCOZ went into receivership, there was a reduction in household consumption and in the use of health and education services; employment in informal and illegal activities increased; defaulting on debts and utilities payments ensued; and “downhousing" (moving into squatter housing in order to rent out newly acquired homes) increased. All this took place in a context of high HIV/AIDS prevalence.  

# Luanshya: From Thriving Mining Town to Ghost Town.  

Masiye7 has described the situation in Luanshya:  

The mine township which was once a hive of activities became quiet and desolate economically...employment levels drastically dropped...companies that used to be suppliers to the mines closed down. Social amenities such as domestic centers, tailoring centers and training workshops have gone under....Unemployment shot up, particularly amongst men and youths. There was an increase in street vending, charcoal burning and subsistence farming.... most of the stands at the street markets were deserted...the minibuses took long to fill because people preferred to walk to town than spend the money on transport...a lot of youths have taken to beer drinking, prostitution and crime...mine installations such as electric cables were vandalized by unemployed youths who stole them for sale. Large numbers of children dropped out of school because their families could not afford the school fees and there are growing numbers of street children..some men have gone to other towns to find work and the number of female-headed households has increased....water and sanitation supply has become erratic and people have had to dig wells and pit latrines...the roads are not being maintained and are full of potholes.  

14. In the medium to long term, the solution to the crisis is growth and economic diversification which is a major objective of the PRSP. Some of this could be directed toward the Copperbelt specifically, in order to take advantage of existing infrastructure and human resources. At a time when the budget is under considerable pressure, it will also be important to protect pro-poor spending in the budget, and to monitor expenditure effectiveness, especially in the Copperbelt where council services will be under pressure. In the short run, there would be a need for a substantial safety net operation. A potential problem is the lack of an institution capable of delivering such an operation. The district councils might be expected to play a major role, working with resident development committees, although there are severe capacity problems at both levels. The World Bank and NGOs are beginning to work with these institutions, and a study is being prepared to provide better information on likely safety net requirements in the event of a further contraction in the industry.

In this letter, we review progress in implementing the 200l program, and the key economic and financial policies for 2002 including the government's response to a sharp deterioration in prospects for the mining sector. # I. PERFORMANCE UNDER THE 2001 PROGRAM AND RECENT ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENTS

For end-September 2O01 the performance criteria on domestic arrears was missed, although since the target was breached by a wide margin at end-March and end-June, it was not expected that the end-September target would be met. Mainly because of a delay in disbursement of $\mathrm{USS50}$ million from the World Bank, the performance criteria for end-December 2001 were not met for net domestic assets (NDA) of the Bank of Zambia (BoZ),' net claims on government (NCG), and gross international reserves. In response, the BoZ tightened monetary policy and the kwacha remained broadly stable for the rest of the year.

# II. The Medium-Term Strategy  

14. The government's medium-term strategy for growth and poverty reduction, which is outlined in the full PRSP, will hinge critically on developments in the copper sector, which have become subject to considerable uncertainty. In late January 2002, Anglo American Plc, the major shareholder in Konkola Copper Mines (KCM), informed government that it would not proceed with further investments in KCM, citing financial losses linked to declining world copper prices and failure to secure funding for the Konkola Deep Mining Project (KDMP) which was the main basis for its investment in Zambia in March 20o0. Other mining companies have also informed the government that the viability of thcir invcstmcnt plans is threatcncd by low coppcr priccs. Anglo American (AA) informed government that the remainder of committed shareholder funding would cover KCM operations for only 2-3 months, and that this period would be used to explore with government the following four options: (i) sell KCM to a third party; (ii) transfer KCM to government as a going concern; (ii) obtain concessional financing for KDMP; and (iv) initiate orderly closure of KCM operations. In response, government constituted a Task Force, which includes the private sector and mining, financial, and legal experts to look at these options and advise government on a course of action. At the same time, the government, in consultation with the private minority  

stakeholders², is conducting negotiations with AA with a view to pursuing options including attracting a new strategic investor to ensure that KCM remains open and also in the hands of the private sector.  

15. The key policy challenge facing Zambia is to sustain the gains made in the past two years in raising the rate of economic growth, in the face of these considerable uncertainties in the mining sector, and to ensure that the benefits of that growth reach all sections of the public in order to reduce poverty. Accordingly, government intends to maintain macroeconomic stability, strengthen and accelerate structural reforms, improve governance, and invest in productive infrastructure and human capital. Given the dificulties in the copper sector, the government is keenly aware of the urgent need to diversify the Zambian economy. Private sector investment will continue to be encouraged, particularly into export-oriented agriculture, light manufacturing, small-scale mining and tourism. However, even with rapid implementation of reforms, it is unlikely that growth in the nonmining sectors will offset the expected lower performance of the copper sector and, therefore, the medium-term growth prospects now appear less favorable. In light of these developments, the main macroeconomic targets for the period 2002-4 are to achieve: (i) real GDP growth of at least 4 percent a year; (ii) a substantial deceleration of inflation to single digits by 2003; and (ii) a build-up of gross international reserves.  

16. Given these macroeconomic objectives, the medium-term fiscal strategy aims to maintain Zambia's relatively strong revenue effort and to restrain overall expenditures, while reorienting spending towards infrastructure, social services and poverty reducing programs. The overall deficit is projected to narrow from 8.1 percent of GDP in 2001 to 5.9 percent in 2004 while the domestic deficit (cash basis) is expected to decline from 4.7 percent of GDP in 2001 to 2.9 percent in 2003.4 However, despite the narrowing deficit, fiscal gaps averaging around 3 percent of GDP in 2003-04 are projected to emerge, partly reflecting the additional debt-service payments to Russia, and a projected further decline in donor budgetary support. Government hopes that these gaps could be covered by a recovery of such donor support and by debt relief beyond the enhanced HIPC initiative. In this context, the government will intensify efforts to enhance relations with donors. The government is also committed to taking such additional fiscal measures as needed to ensure that the remaining gaps are covered.  

17. With the difficulties in the mining sector, Zambia's medium-term balance of payments outlook has worsened substantially. Under the revised baseline scenario, export receipts are projected to grow by 5 percent annually during 2002-4 compared with 13 percent previously. However, as a result of lower imports on account of reduced borrowing by the copper mines, the current account deficit is projected to narrow from 20.4 percent of GDP in 2001 to 16.2 percent in 2004, and financing gaps are projected to average about $\mathrm{\us{\$246}}$ million during 2oo3-4. These gaps would need to be financed by external grants, highly concessional loans, and external debt relief going beyond the enhanced HIPC initiative.  

# III. THEPROGRAM FOR2002  

18. While the situation remains highly uncertain, we believe that a solution can be found to the immediate financial difficulties of the mining sector and all parties are working toward that objective. Therefore, the key underlying assumption for the 2002 program is that KCM will not be closed, and that its production will be at the same level as in 2001. Output of the other mines is expected to increase modestly. The government is committed not to assume ownership of the mines, as this would entail potentially unlimited financial obligations. Moreover, we anticipate that the financial problems of the sector can be resolved without direct financial support from the government. Therefore, the fiscal program for 2002 does not include any expenditure explicitly related to the mining sector. Should such expenditures become unavoidable, the government will consult with the Fund staff before undertaking any new commitments. It willalso seek external concessional financing for any such outlays. If such financing is not available, the government is committed to implement new fiscal measures including introducing broad-based tax measures to ensure that such expenditures would be fully financed. The 2002 program will be reviewed in mid2002 in order to take into account any new developments related to the copper sector, including any changes to the base line scenario, as well as the overall outlook for external budgetary support.  

# A. Macroeconomic Objectives  

19. The key macroeconomic objectives for 2002 are to achieve real GDP growth of about 4 percent; to limit the 12-month rate of inflation to 13 percent; and to build up gross international reserves by $\bf{U S9116}$ million. In addition, to address poverty reduction, budgetary allocations to social and poverty reducing programs are being increased. Achieving these objectives will require: (i) appropriate fiscal and monetary policies; (ii) a deepening and acceleration of structural reforms; (iii) improved public expenditure management and control; and (iv) increasing transparency and accountability in government.  

# B. Fiscal Policies, HIPC Initiative Resources and Expenditures  

20. The fiscal program for 2002 targets revenues of 18.1 percent of GDP, slightly below the outturn for 2001, because of the introduction of the following pro-growth or pro-poor tax incentives: lowering the excise on diesel from 60 percent to 30 percent, and on electricity from 7 percent to 5 percent; restructuring PAYE in favor of the lower paid by increasing the basic tax allowance while removing the 10 percent and 20 percent bands in such a way as to reduce the tax liability for al taxpayers; making anti-malaria supplies tax exempt; and streamlining the VAT by increasing the registration threshold to K10O million and restricting voluntary registrations. Moreover, once the Indeni refinery becomes fully operational, the import duty on refined petroleum products will be reduced to 5 percent in order to limit monopoly pricing. The program also includes some revenue enhancing measures, such as making income from GRZ bonds taxable, and introducing a 5 percent excise on cars and a 10 percent excise on cosmetics and perfumes.  

21. Program assistance from cooperating partners has been projected at 4.2 percent of GDP in 2002, representing a substantial decline in the past two years on budgeted amounts.External budgetary debt service is expected to rise slightly to 2.3 percent of GDP, after interim debt relief under the Enhanced HIPC Initiative of about $\mathrm{USS114}$ million (excluding the IMF), which will support the government's plans to orient the budget increasingly toward poverty reduction.  

22. Expenditures in 2002 will be restricted to 31.0 percent of GDP. Thus, the overall fiscal deficit is projected to narrow from 8.1 percent to 7.3 percent of GDP, while the domestic deficit willfallfrom 4.7 percent to 3.1 percent. The existing wage agreements with most public sector unions extend through end-December 2002, and the government wage bill for 2002 is estimated at 7.1 percent of GDP. This significant increase from last year's programmed level mainly reflects a large wage increase granted to other public workers in November 2001. The domestic interest bill has increased dramatically from last year, along with the sharp rise in interest rates and the stock of government paper outstanding. Government recognizes the critical importance of reducing deficits so as to preserve fiscal sustainability in the medium term. Spending on recurrent departmental charges will be significantly lower than in 2001, reflecting the completion of elections and the OAU summit. Cash costs related to the privatization of ZNCB will be minimized by selling a majority share to offset the one-off costs of cleaning up the balance sheet. The budget includes provision for recapitalization of the BoZ, but this will not be implemented until after the mid-term review of the program, taking account of the report of the Task Force on Government Accounts (see paragraph 27). Although the targeted overall deficit will largely be covered by external resources, there will be a need for domestic financing equivalent to about 1.2 percent of GDP.  

23. As noted earlier, resources will increasingly be directed toward povertyreducing activities. The government is in the process of identifying line items in the budget, which are poverty-related based on the PRSP. Appropriations for the key social and economic functions have increased from 56.4 percent of the total budget in 2001 to 61.5 percent in 20o2. Moreover, government intends to continue adhering to the operational rules to ensure that cash releases are consistent with budget allocations in priority areas.  

24. Part of the PRSP priority expenditures have been designated “poverty reduction programs" in the budget, and funded by HIPC resources. The MoFNP has developed a list of projects amounting to K 350 billion (2.2 percent of GDP or 7.1 percent of total spending). The government will continue to improve its monitoring and tracking of HIPC resources, and intends for these procedures to serve as a model for improved management and control of all PRSP expenditures in the future. Resources corresponding to debt relief are deposited monthly into a special account ("account 49"). These funds will be disbursed to the pre-identified HIPC activities which have a separate budget code. Line ministries are required to report each month on cash released, commitments incurred, and actual expenditures on HIPC activitics, and the Accountant General will continue to make a quarterly summary of this information available to the public. An annual report on the use of HIPC resources willalso be prepared for public discussion, starting with the report for 2001. A HIPC monitoring committee has been set up with representatives of nongovernmental groups and professional bodies to monitor the use of HIPC resources.  

# C. Monetary and Financial Policies  

25. Monetary policy will be geared primarily toward achieving the program's inflation and international reserves targets. Consistent with an inflation target of 13 percent, broad money is expected to increase by about 15 percent in 2002. Achieving the monetary target will be facilitated by continued use of open market operations. The BoZ will continue to adhere to the revised Treasury bill auction guidelines in order to ensure a more efficient functioning of the market. Credit to public enterprises is expected to increase only slightly, which should enable banks to increase credit to the private sector in support of productive activities by about 17 percent.  

26.The government is concerned about the very high level of interest rates prevailing in Zambia, which adversely affect private sector economic activity, particularly in agriculture. We recognize that these rates result mainly from continued high government borrowing together with persistent inflationary expectations and ineficiencies in the banking system. To facilitate a reduction in interest rates and bank spreads, the BoZ will: (i) evaluate and systematically allow banks to hold their cash reserve requirement for foreign currency deposits in foreign currency while sterilizing the liquidity impact in the transition through regular BoZ open market operation; (ii) review the current volume and price penalties put in place on rediscounting of treasury bills with a view to providing banks with greater flexibility in their cash management; (ii) introduce a pilot scheme to exempt the amount lent to agriculture by commercial banks in the calculation of their statutory reserve requirement; and (iv) continue to improve the effectiveness of tender and off-tender sales of government  

securities. In addition, to strengthen monetary management and financial operations, and thereby improve government's cash flow, the MoFNP in collaboration with the BoZ will develop a domestic debt management strategy.  

27. The diverse range of instruments that government uses to borrow from the BoZ needs to be streamlined in order to simplify management of government finances and to better monitor overall net credit to government. Accordingly, the government and the BoZ will streamline and consolidate the BoZ's existing mechanisms for financing government operations to consist of three elements: an overdraft account at the BoZ, treasury bills (with maturity of less than one year), and treasury bonds (with maturity of more than one year). To facilitate implementation of the new system, the government, has appointed a task force which will report by July 20o2 on possible modalities and timetable for consolidating the existing foreign exchange and kwacha bridging loan facilities; putting in place a new overdraft facility; and closing dormant government accounts. A critical element will be proposing terms and interest rates on the overdraft facility and on bonds. The task force's proposals will be geared toward containing the government's domestic interest costs while ensuring that the BoZ receives an appropriate income stream. The government will decide on the recommendations by September 2002 for implementation by end-December.  

# D. Exchange Rate and External Sector Policies  

28. The exchange rate will continue to be market determined, with foreign exchange intervention limited to meeting the program's international reserves target and to smoothing short-term fluctuations. The external current account deficit is expected to narrow to 17.2 percent of GDP in 2002, mainly because of a compression of imports as private sector financing for the metal sector falls sharply. Despite growth in metal export volumes of 2 percent, export receipts are expected to decline slightly in 2002 as a result of lower copper prices. Foreign direct investment, largely outside the copper sector, is envisaged to grow by 14 percent. Notwithstanding the large overall balance of payments deficit, external assistance including interim HIPC debt relief should permit gross reserves to build-up to $\mathrm{USS229}$ million, equivalent to 1.7 months of imports. The government intends to maintain the current liberal trade and exchange system. In order to further strengthen Zambia's stance as an open economy, we have writtn to the Fund accepting the obligations under Article VIlI, Sections 2, 3, and 4.  

29. Government recognizes the critical importance of improving the quality of external debt management to contain the obligations to the extent feasible by prudent negotiations, consistent with our legal liabilities, and to better forecast debt-service obligations. To this end, we intend to ask for assistance from cooperating partners. Finally, the government will consolidate refunds of debt service related to HIPC within the budget.  

# E. Structural Reforms, Public Expenditure Management, and Governance  

# Structural Reforms  

30. Selling the government's majority interest in ZNCB will be critical to improving the investment climate and management of public finances, while also improving public finances. While ZNCB had been offered for sale, the response was limited by the lack of majority control for the buyer and uncertainty about the status of the balance sheet. Therefore, to complete the process as rapidly as possible and to avoid further losses, the Cabinet has approved the sale of at least 51 percent of government's share in ZNCB; that government will take over the liabilities to ZNCB of ZNOC and Roan Antelope Mining Company of Zambia (RAMCOZ) and the contingent liabilities of a legal action by some exemployees of ZNCB that is in process; and that the buyer of ZNCB will not be required to maintain any non-viable rural branch for more than two years. On this basis, the sale has been readvertised. We anticipate that by end-July the ZPA Board will appoint a negotiating team with approval to finalize terms with the buyer and that the sale of ZNCB will be concluded thereafter. In the meantime, the government will commission a study to identify options for dealing with rural branches and creating a new rural financial institution with the objective of integrating the rural population into the economy. Technical and financial assistance from donors, including the World Bank, will be sought for the creation and operation of the new rural financial institution.  

31. The BoZ will continue to monitor the financial situation of ZNCB. Based on the findings of the recently completed targeted inspection, ZNCB has implemented a number of recommended actions to ensure compliance with BoZ directives,including: stopping interest accrual; making additional loan loss provision in December 2001; and completing reconciliation of interbranch accounts. In addition, the BoZ will take steps to ensure compliance with remaining directives, including: stopping the recognition of unrealized exchange gains, and continuing to limit strictly ZNCB's new loans. Moreover, the government will act to expedite the sale of RAMCOZ, which is under receivership.  

32. Regarding the oil sector, consistent with earlier commitments to the World Bank, we will expeditiously complete the actions necessary to facilitate disbursement of the remaining $\mathbf{US}\$50$ million tranche. To that end, Cabinet has approved the ZPA proposal on the concessioning of Tazama and Ndola storage facilities and for government to reduce its shareholding in the Indeni refinery to below 50 percent. The government put ZNOC under liquidation in April 20o2, thereby eliminating its role as an importer and distributor of petroleum products. The financial liabilities of ZNOC and ZNCB will be treated separately from implementation of the reform program, and we willnot meet any of ZNOC's liabilities for which government is not legally liable. Once Indeni becomes operational, government has agreed that the import tariff on petroleum products will be lowered from 25 percent to 5 percent. With regard to the electricity company, ZESCO, the ZPA has advertised for international consultants to be appointed to assist in preparing the company for concessioning and will soon invite international bids for concessioning.  

# Public Expenditure Management  

33. Strengthened public expenditure management is critical to enhance efficiency and to deal with the accumulation of domestic payments arrears, which undermines economic management. The Controller, Internal Audit will be supplied with equipment in order to compile a database of individual bills by end-December 2OO2, and a plan will be drawn up for clearing these old arrears as soon as feasible. Line ministries will be required to document which particular bils in the database were paid with any funds released by MoFNP for arrears clearance. At the same time, government will endeavor to prevent the accumulation of new arrears by clarifying the financial regulations, adopting a Commitment Control System in line ministries, establishing an Expenditure Monitoring Unit in MoFNP, and introducing quarterly expenditure ceilings, as described in Annex II. Government is continuing its efforts to improve monthly reporting,7 which will be used as the basis for arrears monitoring, and regular quarterly audits by the Controller, Internal Audit will also continue. In addition, the government will be piloting the new computerized reporting system. Any cases of overcommitment or unauthorized virement (transfer) will be investigated thoroughly by MoFNP. The personal responsibility and accountability of permanent secretaries, chief accountants, and internal auditors in the line ministries will be further enhanced.  

34. Government will also take steps to improve the cash release process. Any requests for virement between subheads will need to be submitted to the Secretary to the Treasury (ST) in writing before commitments are incurred, and requests for special funding above budget appropriations will no longer be permitted (except in cases of national emergencies, consistent with financial regulations). In the event that ex-ante Treasury authority is not sought, expenditures will be regarded as unauthorized and will be dealt with accordingly. Cash releases will be published in the newspapers and in Macroeconomic Indicators. A mid-term budget review will also be performed by July 20o2. Government will also seek to improve cash management. Line ministries willbe required to submit cashflow projections to MoFNP, which will use these to rephase expenditures in light of revenue forecasts so as to minimize costly borrowing. With these reforms, and tightened approval procedures at the Zambia National Tender Board (whose tender committee will now be chaired by the ST), we believe that public expenditure management will improve substantially.  

35.In the longer term, introduction of an IFMIS will be key to improving government financial control. A three-member project management team was constituted in March 2O02 to work full time on the IFMIS, and a short-term consultant was engaged to help with the functional design. Government intends to hire the main consulting firm by  

September 2002 to help design and pilot the IFMIS project, including hardware and software requirements, which should be initiated by December 2002. It is expected that procurement will take place soon thereafter, thus enabling a pilot program to be launched in at least three ministries during the 2003 budget year.  

36. Government intends to develop a Medium-Term Expenditure Framework (MTEF) that will guide annual budgets from 2003 onward. Such a document will be of critical importance, serving as a bridge between the broad policy priorities outlined in the PRSP and the annual detailed budgeting exercise. The government has taken steps to improve its capacity to plan, coordinate and monitor its programs through the newly established Planning and Economic Management Department. This Department will be responsible for macro-economic planning, the development of the MTEF, monitoring the PRSP and use of HIPC resources, and donor coordination.  

# Governance  

37. The PRSP articulates a broad agenda to improve governance. In order to fight corruption, the government is strengthening the Anti-Corruption Commission, the Office of the Auditor General (OAG), and other relevant government institutions. Furthermore, government has enacted a law against money laundering and related vices. Steps are also being taken to enhance transparency and accountability in the management of public resources.First, the government intends to sustain efforts to comply strictly with Zambian law that the Accountant General (AG) submit government accounts of the preceding year to the OAG by end-September and that the annual audited government accounts be submitted to Parliament by end-year. Second, government intends to publish mid-year budget reports in the mid-year economic review. As already indicated, once the legal liability issues are resolved and the consultants provide the final report on ZCCM cobalt sales, the government will publish the report and refer it to the appropriate authorities for possible further action.  

# IV. Technical Assistance and Data Issues

In recent years, Zambia has received substantial technical assistance (TA) in public expenditure management, tax policy, bank supervision, monetary policy and operations, financial sector issues, payment system and balance of payments statistics. # Controlling New Arrears  

# 1. Clarifying the Financial Regulations  

We intend to issue, by end-May 20o2, a Treasury circular emphasizing that: (1) commitments are to be kept within the quarterly ceiling allocated for each budget line, even if this is smaler than the pro rata budget appropriation; (2) virement bctwccn subheads requires ex ante, written approval from the ST; (3) requests for“special funding" above appropriations willnot be entertained; and (4) strict sanctions, including refusal to disburse future cash, will be imposed on controlling officers (COs) who fail to comply with the monthly reporting requirements, or with their financial responsibilities.  

# 2. Introducing a Commitment Control System (CCS) at the Line Ministries  

Even with an entirely clear legal framework, controlling officers will still benefit from a set of operational procedures to help them in their daily efforts to avoid overcommitments. As such, the government intends to establish a Commitment Control System for the line ministries by end-June 2O02 . First, each ministry will have a Commitment Control Officer (CCO), who will usually be the controlling officer or a designated senior official. A commitment requisition will be prepared before a commitment is incurred in any form, and the Chief Accountant must ensure that the amount of the requisition is within the uncommitted balance under the relevant budget line in the expenditure ledger. Only then will the requisition be approved by the CCO, the commitment be recorded in the ledger (with the uncommitted balance reduced correspondingly), and an LPO be issued. The payment voucher will not be prepared unless the commitment has been approved and recorded in the ledger, and a copy of the approved requisition should be attached to the voucher. After a check is issued for the voucher, payment details are recorded in the ledger, and the commitment gets discharged.  

# 3. Establishing an Expenditure Monitoring Unit (EMU) at MoFNP  

The government also plans to reestablish an Expenditure Monitoring Unit at MoFNP, responsible for monitoring the receipt and the quality of the monthly expenditure returns. By the third week of each month, the EMU should present the ST with a report, summarizing information on government appropriations, cash releases, commitments, expenditures, and arrears, and also indicating those ministries which have incurred overcommitments, submitted reports of poor quality, or failed to report at all. The EMU should also prepare letters,for the ST's signature, seeking explanations from the concerned ministries. In the case of repeated noncompliance with the financial regulations, including in submission of expenditure returns, MoFNP will impose sanctions, including refusal to release cash, and disciplinary measures against the responsible controlling officers (COs).  

# 4. Introducing Quarterly Expenditure Ceilings  

To support line ministries' efforts to comply with the CCS, MoFNP willissue expenditure ceilings, guided by projected resource inflows and the Yellow Book, at the beginning of each quarter, and these willbe fully backed by cash releases, which may continue on a monthly basis.' COs are thus given firm funding assurances, three months in advance. This enhanced predictability and flexibility should facilitate better planning at the line ministries.

# Zambia: Technical Memorandum of Understanding  

# I. INTRODUCTION  

1. This memorandum sets out the understandings between the Zambian authorities and the International Monetary Fund regarding the definitions of the quantitative and structural performance criteria and benchmarks for the program supported by the PRGF arrangement, as well as the related reporting requirements. The definitions are valid at the start of the program, but may need to be revisited during the program reviews to ensure that the memorandum continues to reflect best understanding of the Zambian and Fund staff to monitor the program.  

II. QUANTITATIVE PERFORMANCE CRITERIA: DEFINITIONS AND DATA SOURCES  

# A. Net Domestic Assets of the Bank of Zambia (BoZ)  

2. NDA of BoZ is dcfined as the monthly-average (based on daily data), during the month of the test dates, of the reserve money less net foreign assets calculated at endDecember, 2001 BoZ mid-exchange rates (program exchange rates).' Reserve money includes currency in circulation, required reserves on kwacha deposits, required reserves on foreign currency deposits, positive current account balances of banks with the BoZ and deposits of non-central government institutions. Net foreign assets of the BoZ are defined as gross international reserves (defined below) plus any other foreign asset, including the IMF interim assistance and the $\mathrm{USS}25$ million blocked reserves at the former Meridien Bank, minus foreign liabilities (defined below). The kwacha figures are derived from the U.S. dollar values using the program exchange rate of K 3,830 per U.S. dollar.  

3. Foreign liabilities are defined as short-term (one year or less in original maturity) foreign currency-denominated liabilities of the BoZ to nonresidents and outstanding use of Fund credit.2  

4. The ceilings on NDA will be adjusted upward by the amount of the shortfall of balance of payments support net of debt service as indicated in Table 3 of the letter of intent, up to a maximum of $\mathrm{USS45}$ million for the program year. In the event of excess balance of payments support net of debt service, the ceiling on NDA willbe adjusted downward by  

100 percent of the additional excess support. The kwacha value of the cumulative shortfall/excess will be calculated at the program exchange rate.  

5. The ceiling on NDA will be adjusted downward/upward to reflect decreases/increases in the legal reserve requirements. The adjustor will be calculated as the percent change in the reserve requirement multiplied by the actual amount of reserves (kwacha and foreign currency denominated) at the end of the previous calendar month.  

6. The ceiling on NDA will also be adjusted upward by a maximum of K 80 billion to reflect the government's cash cost arising from the privatization of ZNCB.  

# B. Net Bank Claims on Government (NCG)  

7. NCG refers to the net banking system's claims on Central government and is defined as:  

(i) the net position of the government with commercial banks, including: (i) treasury bills; (ii) bonds issued by the Government of the Republic of Zambia (GRZ); (iii) loans and advances; less (iv) support to Meridien Bank (MBZ); and less (v) Central government deposits (defined to include account balances under the authority of controlling officers); plus  

(ii) BoZ holdings of: GRZ statutory bonds; ordinary GRZ bonds; bonds in respect of loans to former parastatals; treasury bills on the trading portfolio of BoZ; treasury bills issued for interest on the BoZ foreign exchange bridging loan to GRZ; other government stock; and the BoZ Kwacha bridging loan to GRZ (including the GRZ's revolving fund under the Fiscal Sustainability Credit), less government deposits at the BoZ; plus  

(iii）The BoZ foreign exchange bridging (forex) loan to the GRZ less donor suspense account balances. This will be calculated by taking the beginning period stock of the forex loan expressed in kwacha and then adding net external balance of payments support valued at current exchange rates.  

8. The ceiling on the increase in NCG will be adjusted upward by the amount of the shortfallin balance of payments support net of debt service as indicated in Table 3 of the letter of intent, up to a maximum of $\mathrm{USS45}$ million for the program year. In the event of excess balance of payments support net of debt service, the ceiling on NCG will be adjusted downward by 10O percent of the additional excess support. The kwacha value of the cumulative shortfall/excess will be converted at the program exchange rate.  

9. The ceiling on NCG will also be adjusted upward by a maximum of K 80 billion to reflect the government's cash cost arising from the privatization of ZNCB.  

10. The data source for the above will be the “Net Claims on Government Table” produced by BoZ Economics Department, submitted on a weekly basis, and reconciled with the monetary survey.  

# C. Domestic Arrears of Government  

11.Domestic arrears are defined as: (i) any bill that has been received by a spending Ministry from a supplier for goods and services delivered (and verified) and for which payment has not been made within 30 days; (ii) wage and salary arrears that were due to be paid in a given month but remained unpaid on the $\bar{15}^{\mathrm{th}}$ of the following month; and (iii) interest or principal obligations which remain unpaid 30 days after the due date of payment.  

12. The information is to be compiled through audits of the accounts of spending Ministries and agencies, conducted by the Internal Audit division of MoFNP. The audits will be completed and data submitted to Fund staff by the Commitments Monitoring Unit within 6 weeks of the end of each quarter.  

# D. Gross International Reserves of the BOZ  

13. Unless otherwise noted here, gross international reserves of the BoZ will be defined as reserve assets of the Bank of Zambia (Appendix II, Table 2). Reserve assets are defined in the IMF BOP manual $5^{\mathrm{th}}$ edition) and elaborated in the reserve template of the Fund's special data dissemination standards (SDDS). They exclude, for example, foreign assets not readily available to or controlled by the monetary authorities, and foreign currency claims on Zambia residents.  

14. Gross international reserves consist of (i) monetary gold; (ii) foreign currency in cash; (ii) Unencumbered foreign currency deposits at non-resident banks; (iv) foreign securities and deposits; (v) SDR holdings and Zambia's reserve position with the Fund; and (vi) balances in the BIS account related to debt service to Paris Club creditors. Gross reserves will exclude non-convertible currencies, pledged, swapped, or any encumbered reserves assets including but not limited to reserve assets used as collateral or guarantees for third party external liabilities, and will exclude the $\mathtt{U S S25}$ million deposit in Meridien Bank which is under liquidation, and the IMF's interim assistance.  

15. The floor on gross international reserves will be adjusted: (i) downward by the amount in U.S. dollars of the shortfallin balance of payments support net of debt service as indicated in Table 3 of the letter of intent, up to a maximum of $\mathrm{USS45}$ million for the program year; (ii) upward by 100 percent of the cumulative excess balance of payments support net of debt service (iii) downward/upward for any shortfall/excess in the U.S. dollar value of disbursements from the IMF under the PRGF arrangement; and (iv) downward for any increase in BoZ short-term foreign currency denominated debt (to resident and nonresidents), using the definition of short-term debt below.  

16. The floor on reserves will be adjusted downward by a maximum of $\mathrm{US}\$21$ million to reflect the government's cash cost arising from the privatization of ZNCB.  

17. For the purpose of this performance criterion, as well as those for external debt and arrears, valuation will be in U.S. dollars using the program exchange rates.  

18. Data on gross international reserves including its components will be reported by the BoZ on a weekly and end-month basis.  

# E. External Payment Arrears  

19. Official external payment arrears are defined as the stock of external arrears on debt repayments by the central government and BoZ, except on debts subject to rescheduling.  

20. Data on arrears are compiled jointly by the MoFNP and BoZ and will be reported by MoFNP on a quarterly basis.  

# F. Official Medium- and Long-Term Concessional Loans  

21. This is defined as all forms of official debt contracted or guaranteed by the central government and BoZ having a grant element of more than 40 percent, but excludes debts subject to rescheduling. This includes loans, suppliers credits and leases, that constitute current, i.e. not contingent liabilities, which are created under a contractual arrangement or guarantee through the provision of value in the form of assets (including currency) or services, and which require the government or BoZ to make one or more payment in the form of assets (including currency) or services, at some future point(s) in time; these payments discharge the principal and/or interest liabilities incurred under the contract. Under this definition, such debt includes arrears, penalties, and judiciary awarded damages arising from the failure to make payments under a contractual obligation that constitutes debt as specified above. The grant element is to be calculated by using currency-specific commercial interest reference rates (CIRRs) reported by the OECD); for maturities of less than 15 years, the grant element will be calculated based on six-month averages of CIRRs, and for maturities longer than 15 years, the grant element will be calculated based on 1O-year averages. Adjustment lending from the World Bank and IMF will be excluded.  

22. The definition of debt, for the purposes of the limit, is set out in Executive Board Decision No. 6230-(79/140), Point 9, as revised on August 24, 2000 (see Annex below). This performance criterion applies not only to debt as defined in Point 9 of the Executive Board decision, but also to commitments contracted or guaranteed for which value has not been received.  

23. Detailed data on all new concessional and non-concessional debt contracted or guaranteed will be provided by MoFNP on a quarterly basis.  

# G. Official External Short-Term Debt  

24. This is defined as the outstanding stock of external debt (as defined above) with a maturity of less than one year contracted or guaranteed by the central government, and BoZ. For this purpose short term debt will include forward commodity sales but will exclude normal trade credit for imports. There will be no new official external short-term debt during the program period.  

25. The data will be reported by the MoFNP and BoZ on a quarterly basis.  

III. STRUCTURAL PERFORMANCE CRITERIA AND BENCHMARKS  

# A. Monthly Revenue and Expenditure Reports  

26. The Central Computer Services Department (CCSD) shall process monthly FMS reports from the line ministries, so as to enable the Accountant General to submit a consolidated summary of monthly government expenditure, along with revenue collections, to the Secretary to the Treasury (ST) as a management tool. This report should show expenditure by subhead for each spending agency, and should also include a summary for government as a whole. The report should be published, along with data on cash releases, with alag of less than two months, either on the government's website or in Macroeconomic Indicators. As the quality of the Monthly Expenditure Returns improve-or, alternatively, as the FMS reports are modified to include more information-additional data on commitments and arrears shall be included in the report.  

# B. Database on Domestic Arrears  

27. The Controller, Internal Audit shall, using the results of quarterly audits of arrears at spending agencies, construct a database containing records for each individual unpaid bill owed by government. Once constructed, this database will allow the Ministry of Finance to hold line ministries accountable for their requests for funds for arrears clearance. Such requests must identify the particular arrears to be cleared, and the corresponding records in the database will be deleted upon verification from the line ministry.  

# C. Submission of Audited Government Accounts  

28. “Accounts for 2001" are defined as the 2001 annual financial statements, as prescribed in the Finance Act and Regulations thereunder.“Timely preparation of final audited accounts as required by law" is taken to mean that the Auditor General will audit the accounts for 20O1 in sufficient time to allow the Minister of Finance to present the Financial Report to Parliament within twelve months of the end of the year, i.e., by December 2002.  

# D. Procurement of Hardware and Software for IFMIS  

29. Once the long-term IFMIS consulting firm is in place, it will help design the hardware and software requirements for the process. Procurement of hardware and software should commence-i.e., tender documents should be issued--by December 2002.  

# E. HIPC Reporting  

30. The benchmark on publishing an annual report on the use of HIPC debt savings during 2001 and allocations for 2002, will be met following the publication of a report detailing: (i) debt relief received during 2001 and the amounts credited to the MoFNP account 49 at the BoZ; (ii) programs to which HIPC resources were allocated in the budget during 2001, together with the amounts allocated from the non-HIPC budget to these programs; (iii) cash releases to line ministries and expenditures for these programs; (iv) progress with public monitoring of HIPC resources, and any other related activities (e.g. training workshops, dissemination); and (v) the list of programs to receive HIPC resources for 2002.  

# F. Privatization of Zambia National Commercial Bank (ZNCB)  

31.The performance criteria on the privatization of the ZNCB will be met on the day the Zambia Privatization Agency (or other designated agency): advertises the sale of at least 51 percent of ZNCB along with the relevant terms and conditions in national and international newspapers/newsweeklies.  

32.The benchmark on the appointment of the negotiating team will be met on the day the ZPA Board appoints a negotiating team with full authority to negotiate a contract for sale and related matters consistent with existing rules and practices.  

# G. Consolidation of BoZ's Existing Mechanisms for Financing Government  

33.The benchmark and performance criteria on streamlining and consolidating the BoZ's existing mechanisms for financing government operations to comprise three elements: an overdraft facility with the BoZ; treasury bills and treasury bonds, will be met once the following three steps are completed:  

(i) The joint task force comprising MoFNP and BoZ staff submits its proposals on the modalities and timetable for streamlining and consolidating government accounts (along the lines indicated above) to the authorities in MoFNP and the BoZ.  

(ii) The MoFNP and the BoZ authorities formally decide on a strategy (mode and timing of streamlining and consolidating the accounts) and a decision is communicated in writing to all concerned officials.  

(iii）The revised system becomes operational and the BoZ issues the first weekly/monthly report indicating the position of all government accounts in the BoZ under the new system.  

# H. Abstention from Tax Reductions and Other Measures  

34. The government shall refrain from introducing any additional tax exemptions beyond those already included in the 2002 budget and all expiring tax exemptions will be allowed to lapse. The zero-rating for VAT for hotel accommodation in the Livingstone area is carried over until end-2002.  

# Guidelines on Performance Criteria with Respect to Foreign Debt  

Excerpt from Executive Board Decision No. 6230-(79/140), as revised on August 24, 2000  

9. (a) For the purpose of this guideline, the term “debt" will be understood to mean a current, i.e., not contingent, liability, created under a contractual arrangement through the provision of value in the form of assets (including currency) or services, and which requires the obligor to make one or more payments in the form of assets (including currency) or services, at some future point(s) in time; these payments will discharge the principal and/or interest liabilities incurred under the contract. Debts can take a number of forms, the primary ones being as follows:  

(i) loans, i.e., advances of money to the obligor by the lender made on the basis of an undertaking that the obligor will repay the funds in the future (including deposits, bonds, debentures, commercial loans and buyers' credits) and temporary exchanges of assets that are equivalent to fully collateralized loans under which the obligor is required to repay the funds, and usually pay interest, by repurchasing the collateral from the buyer in the future (such as repurchase agreements and official swap arrangements);  

(ii) suppliers' credits, i.e., contracts where the supplier permits the obligor to defer payments until some time after the date on which the goods are delivered or services are provided; and  

(iii）leases, i.e., arrangements under which property is provided which the lessee has the right to use for one or more specified period(s) of time that are usually shorter than the total expected service life of the property, while the lessor retains the title to the property. For the purpose of the guideline, the debt is the present value (at the inception of the lease) of all lease payments expected to be made during the period of the agreement excluding those payments that cover the operation, repair or maintenance of the property.  

(b) Under the definition of debt set out in point 9 (a) above, arrears, penalties, and judicially awarded damages arising from the failure to make payment under a contractual obligation that constitutes debt are debt. Failure to make payment on an obligation that is not considered debt under this definition (e.g., payment on delivery) will not give rise to debt.

# VIII. Safeguard Assessment:  

Under the Fund's safeguard assessment policy, the Bank of Zambia is subject to transitional procedures with respect to the PRGF arrangement approved on 3/25/1999, which is scheduled to expire on 3/28/2003. The transitional procedure require a review of only the Bank of Zambia's external audit mechanism. This assessment determines whether the Bank of Zambia publishes annual financial statements that are independently audited in accordance with internationally accepted standards. The External Audit assessment was completed on July 2, 2001. The assessment concluded that Bank of Zambia's audit mechanism is adequate, as reported in EBS/01,178 (October 24, 2001)  

# IX. Exchange Rate Arrangement:  

The currency of Zambia is the kwacha, which is market determined. The official exchange rate quoted by the central bank is the marginal rate determined by auction at the Bank of Zambia's wholesale foreign exchange window. Since end-March 2001, business entities selling foreign exchange amounting to $\mathrm{\bfUS}\$100,00$ or more per week are required to transact through auctions arranged by the Bank of Zambia. On the basis of the bids and offers received, as well as other considerations, the Bank of Zambia determines the amount of foreign exchange to be sold to, or purchased from, the market. On March 29, 2001,the Bank of Zambia mid-rate was K 3,936.2 per U.S. dollar. LEG has confirmed that the continued existence of private external arrears accumulated prior to October 1985 evidence an exchange restriction under Article VIlI, Section 2(a). However, the government has developed a plan to deal with the arrears and on this basis has written to the Fund accepting the obligations under Article VIII, Sections 2, 3 and 4.

# Zambia: Statistical Issues  

# Institutional organization  

The present arrangements for the compilation of macroeconomic statistics involve duplication of effort and insufficient coordination among the Bank of Zambia (BoZ), the Ministry of Finance, and the Central Statistical Office (CSO). To a large extent, this situation has arisen because of the resource constraints and organizational weakness within the CSO that have affected its ability to produce economic statistics on a timely basis.  

# Real sector  

With regard to national accounts, methodological weaknesses relating to the use of outdated weights, as well as poor timeliness and frequent revisions to the estimates, undermine the authorities' ability to monitor economic developments and formulate policies. Recently, national accounts data for the period 1994-98 were revised on the basis of the 1994 survey of industrial production. The CSO also carries out other surveys, including agricultural surveys to estimate crop production. However, low response rates to these surveys have raised doubts about the reliability of the data.  

Consumer, wholesale, and building materials price indices are calculated in conformity with international standards. A new consumer price index using weights derived from a 1993-94 household budget survey was released in 1996.. However, owing to lack of resources, the CSO has not been able to replace the wholesale price index currently based on 1966 weights, with a producer price index or to revise the price index for building materials. As a consequence, the deflators used to estimate value added at constant prices suffer from serious methodological weaknesses.  

Monthly trade statistics compiled from customs records have not been reported for publication in International Financial Statistics $(I F S)$ on a timely and consistent basis, owing to the delays in transmitting customs documents to the CSO. The delays in publication of trade data forced the BoZ to start compiling trade statistics independently. To improve the information flow from customs posts, the CSO has started using the data provided by the Zambian Revenue Authority.  

# Government finance  

Zambia has resumed compilation of government finance statistics (GFS) data, and it appears that the quality of the reported data has improved; Zambia's GFS data have been updated to 1998 for final data and to 1999 for forecast data. However, the coverage of the data for 1989-99 is limited to budgetary central government.  

The authorities report monthly budget data for the Fund's operational use in a timely manner. However, data are often subject to substantial revisions later in the year. No monthly or quarterly fiscal data are reported for publication in IFS. Local government fiscal data are not available.  

# Monetary accounts  

There has been progress made in the compilation of monetary statistics, and the monetary survey is published on a monthly basis. The BoZ has also eliminated discrepancies between its own records of IMF accounts and data from the Treasurer's Department of the IMF.  

Nonetheless, additional work is needed on monetary statistics. In particular, the BoZ needs to reconcile its data on government deposits and credit to government reported by banks with the information received by the Accountant General. Also, further improvements in accounting procedures and computerization are desirable to ensure that transactions and balances in foreign currencies are properly valued. The lag in the compilation of monetary statistics has increased due to technical problems in the BoZ computer system. Although the BoZ authorities have indicated that they were addressing the problem, reporting to STA continues to be very irregular. In April 2002, STA received preliminary data for monetary authorities and other depository corporations for the period October-December 2001.  

# Balance of payments  

In November 1997, a STA technical assistance mission found that the CSO, which is officially responsible for balance of payments compilation, is unable to do so now or in the foreseeable future. The mission made several specific recommendations for improvements in data collection, especially with regard to external debt, and urged the Bank of Zambia to make a more substantial and longer-term commitment to balance of payments compilation. The latter step was considered necessary to permit the introduction of balance of payments surveys and improve reporting of foreign exchange transactions made through the banking system. In May, 2000 a follow-up STA mission found compilation of data imports, exports, and foreign direct investment to be inadequate and made a number of recommendations to improve the coverage of the data. Since then, the authorities have prepared a work plan to implement the recommendations of the mission and some improvements have been made in the coverage of the customs data and methodology of compiling some components of balance of payments.
