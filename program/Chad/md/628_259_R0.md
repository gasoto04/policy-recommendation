# EXECUTIVE SUMMARY  

# Background  

A three-year arrangement under the ESAF, in an amount equivalent to SDR 49.56 million (120 percent of quota), was approved on September 1, 1995. The second annual arrangement expired at end-June 1997. In 1997, Chad completed the process of putting in place its first democratically elected political institutions since independence.  

# Recent economic and financial developments  

Discussions on a third annual ESAF arrangement could not be concluded in August l997 because of lack of progress in structural reform and persistent weaknesses in budgetary management. The authorities used the last four months of 1997 as a period of program consolidation.  

In 1997, real GDP rose by 6.5 percent as a result of a good crop year and a continued recovery in the industrial sector. Inflation averaged 5.6 percent, with the increase in prices in the twelve-month period ended December l997 at less than l percent.  

The fiscal performance improved markedly in 1997, with revenue growing by 15 percent, while current expenditure fell by 5 percent. However, revenue had been programmed to increase by 25 percent, so the current fiscal deficit, at 0.7 percent of GDP, was 0.5 percent of GDP higher than targeted. Sectoral spending was in line with budgetary appropriations, including expenditure on health and education and transfers to the social security insitutions.  

Chad's balance of payments deteriorated in 1997 because of a deterioration of the terms of trade, a widening of the services deficit, and a reduction in exceptional financing.  

Structural reform regained momentum in the second half of the year with the privatization of three enterprises and the beginning of the revision of regulations governing water, electricity and telecommunications.  

New national accounts were adopted by the government early in 1998.  

# The 1998-99 program  

$\bullet$ The third year program envisages economic growth of 6 percent and inflation of 3.5 percent in l998. With strong cotton exports and an improvement in the terms of trade, the external current account is expected to narrow from 20 percent of GDP in l997 to 17 percent in 1998.  

$\bullet$ Fiscal consolidation remains at the core of the program. On the revenue side, the program focuses on the strengthening of customs, particularly through the control of exemptions. On the expenditure side, the program calls for continued restraint in wage  

spending, for an increase in resources for operating expenditures, especially in health and education, and for a higher domestic contribution to public investment. The budgetary current account is projected to run a surplus of 0.7 percent of GDP, while the overall fiscal deficit is to be reduced below 9 percent of GDP. The'first set of recommendations on the reform of the civil service willbe adopted by the government by end-September l998. By the same date, the results and recommendations of the audits of the two social insurance agencies will be available.  

The reform of the parastatal sector will include development of a strategy, in coordination with the World Bank, to reform the cotton sector, centered on lifting the monopolies heid by the cotton company and the privatization of its oil and soap manufacturing unit. In addition, the program call for the privatization of the sugar company, two banks, the telecommunications company, the airline, the.road maintenance firm, and a slaughterhouse. Also, the management of the electricity company willbe taken over by a private operator.  

Chad's medium-term balance of payment outlook will be affected by the investments related to the exploitation of the country's oil reserves, thus keeping the current account deficit (excluding transfers) slightly above 19 percent of GDP. The Executive Board of the World Bank is scheduled to consider sometime this summer financial support for the construction of a pipeline for Chad's oil exports.  

Chad's external debt service obligations over the medium and long term appear to be sustainable.

# Box 1. The New National Accounts and Key Macroeconomic Ratios  

In January l998, the authorities adopted a new set of national accounts. These new accounts, based in 1995, are comprehensive and consistent with the methodology of the United Nations l993 System of National Accounts. For the first time, they include an input-output table.  

In the new accounts, GDP is on average 40 percent higher than previously estimated. This is because of two adjustments that have been introduced: (a) incorporation of nonmarket household production, which represents some 10 to l2 percent of the new GDP; and (b) better coverage of the informal sector, consistent with the survey on consumption and the informal sector undertaken in 1995, which resulted in an upward revision of some 15 percent of the value added of the commerce, transportation, and nongovernment services sectors. These adjustments are important, given the state ofthe Chadian economy, which is stillrecovering from a 3O-year civil war. In particular, it is expected that household subsistence activity will continue to be a major source of income for the poorest segments of the Chadian population.  

As a result of these adjustments, allthe program ratios to GDP have changed. In particular, as indicated below, the revenue-to-GDP ratio is now more than 2 percentage points lower than previously estimated, confirming the existence of systemic weaknesses in revenue collection, While such an abrupt change made the authorities reluctant to adopt the new accounts,the staff explained that the required improvement in revenue performance implied by the new ratios should be achieved progressively, in line with the ongoing strengthening and modernization of the various revenue-collection agencies.

# II. BACKGROUND AND DEVELOPMENTS IN 1997  

4.In the last two years, Chad put in place its first democratically elected political institutions since the country gained independence in 196l. In 1996, a constitutional referendum was held, followed by multiparty presidential elections in which the incumbent was elected. A pluralistic parliament was seated in the first quarter of 1997, and a new  

government, committed to economic reform, was installed shortly thereafter.3 Political support for the adjustment program remains strong, as the government continues to discuss its policies with a wide range of social partners. The government has decided to publish the PFP.  

5.Chad has made significant progress since mid-1995 when it embarked on a comprehensive structural adjustment program,' and the economic and financial situation of the country has improved markedly.5 However, beginning in late 1996, program implementation began to lose momentum, resulting in delays in structural reform and weak budgetary management and expenditure control. In March 1997, the authorities adopted strong corrective measures to address the fiscal problems; at the same time, they reiterated their commitment to press ahead with structural reform of the cotton, sugar, electricity and water, and telecommunications sectors, and to advance the preparations for a comprehensive reform of the civil service. However, litte progress was made in the second and third quarters of 1997, and the discussions on a third annual arrangement could not be concluded in August l997. The authorities used the remaining months of 1997 as a period of program consolidation. Drawing on a set of staff recommendations, they implemented remedial measures that laid a stronger foundation for the success of a third annual arrangement. These measures, outlined in Box 2, centered on strengthening the public finances, putting in place the technical underpinnings of structural reform, and enhancing program monitoring.

7. On the fiscal side, performance in 1997 improved markedly over l996, but fell short of target (Table 3). The current fiscal deficit was reduced to 0.7.percent of GDP from 2.l percent in l996, with revenue growing by 15 percent to 7.3 percent of GDP. Current expenditure was reduced by some 3 percent, bringing it to 8 percent of GDP for the year. However, the targeted current fiscal deficit of 0.2 percent of GDP could not be attained, because of a revenue shortfall of some 0.6 percent of GDP, linked mainly to weaknesses in customs and petroleum taxation. One-fourth of the revenue shortfall was offset by lower expenditures. Moreover, for the first time since 1995, the sectoral alocation of current expenditure was in line with the budget: the wage bill and spending on the military were on target, budgetary allocations to education, health, and social affairs were fully utilized, and no off-budget expenditure was executed except in the first quarter, and that was regularized by year-end. Also, in l997, for the first time, the government fully honored its obligations vis $\grave{\mathbf{a}}$ -vis the civil service pension fund (CNRT), thus allowing the latter to pay four quarterly installments to pensioners--three in l997 and one early in 1998. With investment expenditure of 9 percent of GDP and a net reduction of 1 percent of GDP in domestic and external interest arrears, the overall deficit—on a cash basis, excluding grants-reached 1l percent of GDP.7 Total external financing amounted to the equivalent of US\$196 million (including debt relief but excluding use of Fund resources), 70 percent in the form of project aid and the remainder as exceptional financing. This external assistance covered the overall cash deficit, as well as some US\$15 milion of external debt obligations. The residual was used to strengthen the treasury's net position with the banking system in anticipation of a slowdown in donor aid in the first half of 1998 resulting from the postponement of the third annual ESAF arrangement.  

8. Chad's balance of payments deteriorated in 1997, as the current account deficit, excluding official transfers, increased by l percentage point to some 20 percent of GDP (Table 4). Exports increased by 23 percent in volume terms, mainly on account of the strong growth of cotton exports, while the increase in import volumes was close to 16 percent, primarily in intermediate goods for cotton and sugar production. The favorable evolution in trade volumes was more than offset by a deterioration in the terms of trade of some 4 percent, that caused the trade deficit to widen by 0.3 percentage point to some 6 percent of GDP. The deficit on services increased by 1 percentage point to 12 percent of GDP in connection with the increase in imports. Growth in foreign-financed investment was supported by an increase in concessional loans and grants, while exceptional financing was less than in l996. Official reserves are estimated to have declined by some CFAF 16 billion (3.7 percent of imports of goods and services).  

9. Monetary developments in l997 were characterized by a decline of CFAF 1.5 billion in net domestic credit of the banking system, as the increase in credit to the private sector of CFAF 2.6 billion, primarily related to the cotton campaign, was more than offset by the reduction in net claims on the government (Table 5). Broad money is estimated to have declined by some 9 percent; however, demand deposits increased more than 25 percent. The evolution of broad money and net foreign assets should be interpreted with extreme caution, because of the large stock of unsorted notes of the regional central bank (BEAC) held by the national agency of the BEAC." The discount rate, set at a regional level, declined from 7.75 percent at end-1996 to 7.5 percent in June 1997 and remained constant thereafter.

The aim is to revise the civil service's institutional and regulatory framework to provide the basis for streamlining the civil service and upgrading its efficiency, to improve the specification of recruitment requirements, and to establish meritbased wage and advancement policies. The first set of recommendations will be made available by end-June 1998, so that the government can adopt changes by September and begin to implement reforms in 1999.  

20. The program also calis for a reform of the social welfare system to ensure its mediumand long-term financial viability. This reform, which willbe implemented with assistance from the International Labor Office and the World Bank, is expected to be launched in early 1999 after completion and study of the recommendations of the audits of the two social security agencies (Appendix I, Attachment, paragraph 20).

21. The regional monetary authorities intend to maintain a prudent policy stance, with the objective of keeping demand pressures in check while consolidating foreign exchange reserves at the regional level. Chad will contribute to this effort by continuing to strengthen domestic fiscal policy. Broad money is projected to grow by 9.7 percent in 1998, in line with nominal GDP (Table 5). A further decline in net credit to government will provide adequate room for expansion of credit to the private sector of some 25 percent, while the expansion of net domestic assets will be contained below the targeted growth rate of broad money. As a result, Chad's contribution to increasing the net foreign position of the BEAC is projected to be about CFAF 5 billon, or slightly more than 4 percent of beginning-of-period money stock.  

22. The authorities are taking actions to strengthen the banking system and increase banking competition, notably through the privatization of the two remaining state-owned banks. The authorities reiterated their commitment to continue to support the recommendations of the COBAC regarding restoration of commercial bank eficiency and profitability. In light of the failure of the two previous requests for bids for the privatization of the Banque Tchadienne de Commerce et de Depot (BTCD), the largest commercial bank in Chad, the authorities have decided to take a more careful approach in close collaboration with the COBAC. While the new request for bids is expected to be launched before end-September 1998, two potential partners of international repute have already expressed their interest in the bank. Preparations for privatization of the Banque de Developpement du Tchad (BDT) have already begun; the COBAC has started to sort out the BDT's complex ownership structure, which includes, inter alia, the BTCD. 23. # B. Structural Policies  

24. The reform of the parastatal sector constitutes the structural core of the third annual ESAF program and will involve the restructuring of the sectors of cotton, sugar, electricity and water, and telecommunications. This restructuring will enhance efficiency and should ultimately result in an improvement in government revenues and in the overall economic situation in the medium term. In the cotton sector, the government, in close consultation with the World Bank, wil, during I998, prepare for the privatization of the oil- and soapmanufacturing unit of the cotton company, COTONTCHAD, and elaborate a reform plan  

centered on abolishing the monopolies held by COTONTCHAD.1° The adoption by the government of the reform plan is expected before end-December 1998. Preparations for the privatization of the oil and soap unit are expected to be completed by end-1998, with divestiture coming in the course of l999. In the sugar sector, the key reform action is the privatization of the SONASUT, bids for which are scheduled to be solicited before endSeptember. In addition, the government will incorporate in the solicitation for privatization bids a timetable for decreasing the import reference price, indicating a predetermined point at which it would be removed.20 The exact mechanism will be determined in consultation with the staffs of the World Bank and the Fund. In the electricity sector, allthe modalities for disengaging the state from the management of STEE, including the adoption by the government of the new draft electricity code, are expected to be determined before end-July 1998; bids will be solicited before end-September. In addition, progress was made in identifying the sectoral investment requirements, including the extension and modernization of the electricity network, and providing for increased access to drinking water. The reform actions in the telecommunications sector will include the adoption of a new telecommunications code, the separation of the public postal agency from the telecommunications activity, and the privatization of the latter. Bids for granting a license to an independent cellular phone company will be solicited before end-June l998. All these reforms will be supplemented by the divestiture of the airline, the road maintenance firm, and a slaughterhouse, so by 2Ooo almost all public enterprises will be privatized or liquidated.  

# C. External Policies and the Medium-Term Outlook  

25. As indicated above, the external current account deficit, excluding official grants, is expected to be about 17 percent of GDP in 1998. Exports volumes are projected to increase by some 30 percent, reflecting mainly the rise in cotton exports. Given an expected 10 percent rise in the average export price, exports shipments in f.o.b.-value terms should increase to some 17 percent of GDP. Import volume growth is projected at l1 percent owing to a recovery of investment. With a very modest increase in average import prices, import values, including insurance and freight, will equal some 28 percent of GDP. The capital and financial account of the balance of payments is projected to register a net surplus of CFAF 140 billion, equivalent to 15 percent of GDP, two-thirds of which wil be for project financing.  

26.Chad's exceptional financing requirements for l998 are estimated at some CFAF 49 billion (5 percent of GDP), including debt relief, of which only CFAF 3.5 billion has been already disbursed. Of the remaining gap, CFAF 3.9 billion is expected to be covered by additional debt relief.2 Multilateral creditors are expected to provide CFAF 37.6 billion, comprising CFAF 11.9 billion from the World Bank under the third structural adjustment credit (SAC II), CFAF 6.4 billion from the African Development Bank as the second tranche of its structural adjustment loan, CFAF 6 billion from the European Union, and CFAF 13.3 billion from the Fund under the proposed third-year ESAF arrangement. The remaining CFAF 8 billion will be covered by existing commitments from bilateral creditors .  

27. Chad's medium-term balance of payments outlook will be affected by the developments in the oil sector, as all the gains in terms of increased export volumes are expected to be more than offset by the sharp rise in imports connected with the investment related to the exploitation of the country's oil reserves. In 1999-2000, exports of goods and services are projected to increase by l2 percent per year, or some 3 percentage points less than imports, which would keep the current account deficit, excluding transfers, slightly above 19 percent of GDP. During the same period, overall financing requirements will average CFAF ll2 billion per year and are expected to be mostly covered by official transfers and concessional loans. The financing gap is estimated at CFAF 30 billon for 1999 and CFAF 14 billion for 2000.22

# VI. STAFF APPRAISAL  

30. Chad has made considerable progress since 1995 in advancing its economic reform program. Problems in program implementation began to emerge in the second half of 1996, but the authorities took strong corrective actions early in 1997 to bring the program back on track. However, the follow-up to these measures was weak, and it was clear by August 1997 that the program again needed strengthening before a third-year ESAF arrangement could be approved. The authorities effectively used the last several months of l1997 to consolidate the public finances and to establish a technical and institutional base for advancing structural reform, and they are now in a better position to move the program forward.  

31. The good economic performance in 1997, although partly the result of an exceptionaly good crop year, indicates that government policies to encourage broad-based growth are paying off. Strong growth with low inflation can be anticipated over the medium term if there is a resolute promotion of an environment that allows the private sector to prosper, including completion of the privatization program.  

32. In 1997, considerable strides were made in strengthening public finances: revenue grew substantially, current expenditure was restrained, and the current fiscal deficit was less than l percent of GDP. For the first time in the ESAF program, the ceilings for the wage bill and military were observed, while budgetary allocations to the priority sectors of health, education, and social affairs were fully utilized. The major risk to continuing fiscal consolidation is low revenue mobilization, particularly in light of the need for continuing increases in spending on priority sectors and for financing the government's investment program. The 1998 revenue targets are ambitious but well within the authorities' reach and constitute an essential step toward fiscal sustainability. Tax rates in Chad are high, thus the emphasis will have to be on tax collection. Efforts to expand the tax base through a unified tax on small enterprises and regularization of collections from large enterprises are welcome. The reform of customs, particularly the control of exemptions, is essential to the success of the 1998 program and to the long-term sustainability of the government fiscal program.  

33. The authorities are aware that the current policies to control the wage bill by freezing wages, promotions, and hiring are neither appropriate nor sustainable over the medium term. Thus, the effort to reform the civil service needs to be advanced. The process of internal consultation begun late in 1997 is a good start, but concrete recommendations for short-term actions in the framework of a longer-term strategy should be developed quickly. The current schedule envisages that these recommendations will be ready for government consideration by end-June 1998. It would be important to develop a workable action plan that could be put into effect with the 1999 budget.  

34. The importance of the efforts to reform the social security agencies should be underscored. This effort began with the government's fully honoring its financial obligations to these agencies for the first time in l997 and with the government retirement agencies' making three quarterly payments to retirees in the calendar year, with the fourth installment being paid early in 1998. Once the financial audits of the two agencies are completed and their financial situation is known, an action plan to establish a sound financial base should be put in place expeditiously, so as to ensure a working social safety net, particularly for retired workers.  

35. This program year will mark an important turning point for the privatization program in Chad. With the completion of the privatization of small enterprises, including the remaining state-owned banks, the government will turn its efforts to privatization in three of the largest and most strategically important sectors-electricity and water, telecommunications, and sugar. Concrete actions in these sectors, following two years of preparatory work, will provide a much stronger commercial infrastructure for economic growth. Although reform of the cotton sector is stillin the planning stage, sufficient preparation has been made so that the splitting off and privatization of some nonessential parts of the cotton parastatal (oil and soap manufacturing) can begin in 1999. But fulliberalization of the cotton sector will be critical both to sustained growth, by increasing eficiency in a major productive sector, and to increasing equity in the distribution of income. Thus, the staff would strongly urge that the authorities, in consultation with the World Bank, develop a time-limited strategy to effect substantial cotton sector reform. In addition, the staff would urge the authorities to remove the reference price on sugar imports as soon as possible.  

36. The management of external debt continues to be good in Chad. Chad met its external obligations in a timely fashion in 1997, except for some small arrears at year's end that resulted from the authorities' attempt to negotiate a rescheduling of some short-term debt. These arrears were cleared early in l998. Chad has begun discussions with Russia on rescheduling its bilateral debt and has requested an extension of the Paris Club agreement until the end of the program period.  

37. The external payments position is expected to improve in 1998 with the continued growth in cotton exports, but imports related to investment in the oil exploitation project will likely result in a deterioration for several years thereafter. Chad's debt burden is not large, and debt-service obligations appear to be sustainable, even with continued concessional borrowing to support public investment. Commitments from donors are suffcient to cover the external financing gap in 1998.  

38.The adoption of new national accounts is an important step in reforming the Chadian statistical system. The staff would urge the authorities to move quickly to develop an overall strategy for developing the statistical system, taking into account the scarcity of resources and trained personnel. The adoption of a statistical law will be an important first step in this direction.  

39. Overall, the authorities' program willend renewed impetus to reform. Continued fiscal consolidation will be combined with concrete actions to privatize the large parastatals. The major risk to the program remains with regard to revenue mobilization, and thus the authorities need to show renewed commitment in this area while firmly controlling the level and composition of expenditure. Timely availability of external financial support and technical assistance will also be required. Given the authorities' strong actions in the last quarter of 1997 to bring the program back on track, the good performance through end-March 1998, and the projected capacity to repay the Fund in a timely manner, the staff recommends approval of the request for the third annual arrangement under the ESAF, and the extension of the commitment period of the three-year ESAF arrangement until March 3l, 1999.

# MEMORANDUM OF ECONOMIC AND FINANCIAL POLICIES  

N'Djamena, April 13, 1998  

# 1. INTRODUCTION AND RECENT DEVELOPMENTS  

1. Since mid-1995, Chad has implemented a wide-ranging structural adjustment program, supported by the International Monetary Fund under a three-year Enhanced Structural Adjustment Facility arrangement covering 1995-98. The program also received financial assistance from the World Bank and other multilateral and bilateral donors in the form of . balance of payments loans, exceptional financing, contributions to the financing of public investment projects, and external debt relief.  

2. Chad's economic progress in the first two years of the program is indisputable. By concentrating on an overhaul of indirect taxation and customs tariffs, liberalization of the economy and foreign trade, and the stimulation of private initiative, the reforms of 1995 and 1996 brought about a sustained upturn in economic activity, reduction of inflation, and the reduction of domestic and external disequilibria.  

3. In the second and third quarters of 1997, however, this momentum of economic reform and rehabilitation slowed somewhat, in the midst of persistent weaknesses in fiscal management and delays in the implementation of structural reforms. Therefore, to ensure the success of the third annual program under the ESAF, the government decided late in 1997 to refocus its efforts on strengthening government finances and accelerating the preparatory work for reform of the large public enterprises and the civil service. The government is of the view that these efforts, aimed at consolidating its economic and financial program, have yieided satisfactory results. Nonetheless, it is aware that further strengthening of policies is needed as some of the anticipated objectives were not fully achieved.  

4. Economic and financial performance was generally satisfactory in 1997, although fisca performance fell short of target. Real GDP growth is estimated at 6.5 percent. This was due, in part, to a good crop, which resulted in agricultural output increasing by 1l.4 percent, and to the recovery of activity in the industrial sector (17.6 percent). The transportation and nongovernment trade and services sectors also grew by 4.6 percent.' However, growth in construction and public works was less than 2 percent because of a slowdown in the public investment program. The rise in consumer prices during the year is estimated at less than 1 percent, confirming that inflation is under control.  

5. Compared with 1996, considerable progress was made in the area of government finance, with a 14.8 percent increase in fiscal revenue and a contraction of nearly 5 percent in current primary expenditure. However, the government's targeted current fiscal deficit of 0.2 percent of GDP could not be achieved, mainly because of a revenue shortfall of CFAF 6 billion (0.6 percent of GDP), of which only CFAF 1.5 billion (0.2 percent of GDP) was offset by expenditure compression. Altogether, the current budget deficit amounted to CFAF 6.4 billion, or 0.7 percent of GDP.2  

6. Revenue amounted to CFAF 68.4 billion (7.3 percent of GDP), or a monthly average of CFAF 5.7 billion, compared with CFAF 5 billion in 1996. While this performance is satisfactory, it is stillbelow potential, particularly in the areas of customs and petroleum import taxation. Current expenditure rose to CFAF 74.7 billion (8 percent of GDP), almost CFAF 1.5 billion less than projected, essentially because allocations to the reintegration of former soldiers were not spent. Actual expenditure in l997 was in line with budgetary priorities. In particular, the wage bill was kept within the ceiling established in the October 1997 revised budget law, while spending in the priority sectors increased by 55 percent, mostly in the education and health sectors. Military expenditure was also on target. With investment expenditure of CFAF 85.4 billion, the overall deficit (commitment basis, excluding grants) was 9.8 percent of GDP, which compares with a projected 9.0 percent. Exceptional financing amounted to CFAF 29.5 billion, which made it possible to clear some CFAF 10 billion of domestic arrears.  

7. The balance of payments' current account deficit, exciuding grants, is estimated at 20.2 percent of GDP in 1997, a deterioration of l percentage point from 1996, reflecting a sharp increase in imports and a deterioration in the terms of trade.  

8. Net domestic assets of the banking system declined by 4 percent of beginning-ofperiod money, with credit to the government dropping sharply, while credit to the economy increased. There was a weakening in the external position of the central bank, which resulted in a decline in net foreign assets of the banking system of 5 percent of the beginning-of-period money stock. Broad money declined by 9 percent.  

9. The privatization program advanced markedly in l997, with the privatization of the Banque Meridien BIAO Tchad (BMBT-BIAT) and the Ofice National Hydraulique Pastorale et Villageoise (ONGPV), the government's decision to privatize the sugar company (SONASUT), as well as progress in the elaboration of new telecommunications, electricity, and water codes. Also, the national commission on civil service reform began its work with a national seminar, study trips, and preliminary notes by various specialized subcommissions. Finally, some l,5o0 employees were removed from the government payroll and the demobilization program of 7,300 soldiers was concluded successfully, with technical assistance from the World Bank.  

# II. MEDIUM-TERM STRATEGY  

10. Chad's medium-term economic growth potential is still constrained by the chronic lack of infrastructure and skilled human resources. To overcome these structural weaknesses, the government's strategy for 1998-200l, as described in the policy framework paper, centers on the implementation of economic and financial policies aimed at (a) improving the overall productivity of the economy, (b) strengthening the country's export and production base, and (c) encouraging and promoting private sector development. Based on increased investment and a more efficient mobilization of domestic savings, this strategy should result in an average annual real GDP growth rate of 6 percent, while stabilizing inflation below 3.5 percent and limiting the external current account deficit to 19 percent of GDP in 2000. Structural reforms will be the pivotal element of this strategy, with particular emphasis on increased liberalization of the economy and improvements in the implementation of the public investment program.  

11. The aim of government financial policies is to generate increasing current budget surpluses throughout the period, reaching close to 2 percent of GDP by 20o0, in order to allow the national budget to make a significant contribution to finance investment. Accordingly, tax policy will continue to focus on improving tax yield, with a view to raising revenues to 9.8 percent of GDP in 2000. Another policy focus will be on rationalizing public expenditure and increasing budgetary allocations to priority sectors. Spending willbe made more effective by improving budget preparation and execution control procedures. It is projected that current expenditure will stabilize at around 8 percent of GDP beginning in 1999. The civil service reform and the restructuring of the social security system will also be essential in the fiscal consolidation process.  

# II. THE 1998-99 PR0GRAM  

12. The third annual program will cover the period April 1, 1998-March 31, 1999. It envisages (a) real GDP growth rate of some 6 percent; (b) an inflation rate of 3.5 percent; and (c) an external current account deficit, excluding grants, of 17 percent of GDP. Within this framework, government policies will continue to focus on fiscal consolidation through measures aimed at improving the performance of tax and customs units, and strengthening fiscal discipline. Furthermore, the government will pay particular attention to the restructuring of large semipublic enterprises and the civil service, as wel as accelerating the ongoing privatization program. It will also encourage private initiatives, particularly those that are export oriented.  

13.Fiscal consolidation will continue to receive close government attention during the third annual ESAF program. Revenues are projected to reach 9 percent of GDP in 1998, which, with a ceiling on current expenditure of 8.3 percent of GDP, would result in a current budgetary surplus of 0.7 percent of GDP. With investment equivalent to 9.4 percent of GDP, the overall deficit, excluding grants, is projected at 8.6 percent of GDP.  

14. Fiscal revenue is projected at CFAF 92.9 billion—CFAF 1 billion less than budgeted, owing to an expected shortfall of taxes on petroleum products. To attain this objective, the government will continue its efforts to improve tax administration and combat tax evasion and fraud. The government introduced a singie business tax for small enterprises (IGL), effective January 1, 1998, in replacement of the business license tax, the business profits tax (BIC, BA, and BNC), the flat tax, the apprenticeship tax, and the turmover tax (TCA), so as to better capture the informal sector and improve small-taxpayer administration. It has also decided  

To increase to 4 percent the rate of the presumptive tax levied at the customs. Effective January 1, 1998, to centralize at the treasury all revenue from earmarked taxes, which will henceforth be distributed through special accounts opened on behalf of the recipient agencies, pursuant to the current regulations; To improve the regulations governing the system of taxation of externally financed government projects, in order to simplify the system, ensure its fiscal neutrality, and increase its effectiveness by redressing the weaknesses that have emerged since its implementation in September 1997; and   
To expedite the collection of back taxes.  

15. The government has set a customs revenue target, excluding earmarked taxes, of CFAF 27.5 billion in 1998. To achieve this objective, the government has decided to reinforce the customs directorate's exclusive responsibility for combating smuggling, and to strengthen its surveillance and control activities, including through the assignment of a national guard unit (GNNT) to these duties. Accordingly, the government has issued a decree in which it identified the GNNT unit to be assigned, specified its role in combating customs fraud, and placed it under the direct supervision of the customs director. Within the Central African Customs and Economic Union (CACEU), the government willalso work toward an update of the list of imports exempted from the turnover tax, so as to eliminate exemptions on goods that are not basic necessities. It will send to the CACEU executive committee a request for revision of the exemption list that would eliminate transportation vehicles and their parts, professional equipment and nonessential food products. A corresponding proposed amendment to Annexes I and II of law Nr. 6/PR/95 dated March 4, 1995 will be referred to the National Assembly for its fall session at the latest. In the second half of 1998, these measures will be supplemented by actions aimed at strengthening customs administration, in particular, by simplifying customs declarations and clearance procedures, updating the valuation database, and strengthening the system for the authorization of customs agents.  

16. The government's expenditure policy will continue to focus on improving public expenditure management and effectiveness, with a view to (a) containing nonpriority current expenditure, particularly the wage bill and subsidies to public agencies; (b) strengthening the priority sectors (education, health, social affairs, and public works); (c) accelerating the repayment of domestic arrears; and (d) increasing the budgetary contribution to investment and improving the selection and programming of public investment projects.  

17. Current expenditure is projected at CFAF 85.3 billion. This projection is CFAF 1 billion less than the budgeted amount, and takes into consideration an expected shortfall of taxes on petroleum products. Within these limits, the budget for the priority sectors has been raised to CFAF 7 billion, which is an increase of 48 percent over the 1997 amount. In contrast, the budget allocations for other sectors have been limited to CFAF 12.3 billion, an increase of 23 percent over l997. With the government decision to maintain the freeze on wages, recruitment (outside priority sectors) and the financial impact of promotions until end-1998, the nonmilitary wage bill is projected at CFAF 32 billion. Public investment expenditure wil total CFAF 96.3 billion, including CFAF 8.9 billion financed with budgetary resources. Priority will be given to education/training (9.5 percent), health (9.4 percent), and infrastructure (25.5 percent).  

18. The government will continue to monitor closely the work of the national commission responsible for reform of the civil service, particularly with respect to revising the laws governing the civil service, evaluating the staffing needs of the various ministries, improving personnel management, and streamlining civil service recruitment and advancement procedures. The commission is expected to submit its initial recommendations by endJune 1998 for implementation in the second half of the year.  

19.The government is determined to clear the stock of domestic arrears by end-1999, which were estimated at a maximum of CFAF 40 billion at end-1997, including CFAF 15 billion in cross debts with public enterprises and agencies. The government plans to repay CFAF 25.6 billion in 1998. It is also committed to budgeting all offsetting operations it undertakes with enterprises holding claims on the government; such operations willbe settled solely through an exchange of checks.  

20. To improve Chad's social welfare system and ensure its medium- to long-term viability, the government has launched audits of the CNRT (Caisse Nationale de Retraite du Tchad) and the CNPS (Caisse Nationale de Prevoyance Sociale) and has decided to revise the Social Security Code. The results and recommendations of these audits are expected in September 1998 and wil be discussed, along with the restructuring measures to be taken, with World Bank and IMF staff during the midterm review of the program. In the meantime, the government will continue to honor its obligations vis-a-vis the CNRT and the CNPS on a regular basis.  

21.In the area of monetary policy, the primary objective of the BEAC is to maintain price stability and consolidate the foreign exchange reserves of the zone as a whole. Chad's contribution to the attainment of this objective will be to keep government's reliance on credit, excluding use of IMF resources, unchanged at its end-1997 level. In terms of the beginning-period money stock, net domestic assets of the banking system are slated to  

increase by only l0 percent. Thus, with a money supply growth projected at l2 percent in 1998, the country's external assets should increase by the equivalent of 2 percent of beginning-period money. The national monetary authorities will encourage the Chadian banks to participate in the regional interbank market and, at the regional level, willadvocate the liberalization of money market interest rates.  

22. Strengthening the banking sector remains a government priority. To that effect, the monetary authorities will continue to support the activities of the regional banking commission (COBAC) by ensuring that the commercial banks comply strictly with prudential ratios and regulations governing external transfers. The government notes the rejection of BICIG's capital participation in the BTCD by COBAC's decision Nr. D-98-11, dated February 2, 1998. Following this decision, it will take all necessary steps to ensure the success of BTCD's privatization. The government intends, with the accord of the COBAC, to work to complete the privatization of the BDT.  

23. In 1998, the external current account deficit, excluding grants, should come down to 17 percent of GDP, 3 percentage points less than in 1997, owing mainly to the sustained growth of export volumes and a 7 percent improvement in the terms of trade. Chad will continue to benefit from substantial external assistance in the form of grants and highly concessional loans in support of the investment program, which will total close to CFAF 87.5 billion. Exceptional financing requirements, excluding debt relief and Fund disbursements, are estimated at CFAF 30.3 billion and are expected to be covered by assistance from multilateral and bilateral donors.  

24. The government will continue to pay particular attention to improving external debt management, with a view to avoiding any delays in the payment of obligations faling due. It has initiated efforts to reach an agreement on debt rescheduling with Russia, a new Paris Club member. It will endeavor to conclude debt relief agreements with non-Paris Club creditors as quickly as possible.  

25.During the program period, the government does not intend to introduce exchange restrictions or multiple currency practices or to conclude bilateral agreements that are contrary to Article VIII of the Articles of Agreement of the International Monetary Fund. Moreover, it is not planning to introduce or intensify import restrictions for balance of payments reasons. Lastly, the government will continue to work toward the rapid conclusion of the agreement on the harmonization of exchange regulations in the BEAC zone, ensuring that its provisions are compatible with those of Article VIII of the Fund's Articles of Agreement.  

26. The government is determined to successfully complete the restructuring of the large parastatal enterprises:  

In the cotton sector, the government has informed the IMF and the World Bank of its intention to privatize the oil and soap manufacturing unit of  

COTONTCHAD. Moreover, to honor its commitments regarding the abolition of the monopoly, the government will adopt before end-December 1998 an action plan for the cotton sector, designed to abolish all monopolies that COTONTCHAD currently enjoys in this sector. The action plan will define the regulatory, institutional, fiscal, and technical components of the sector's reform program. Based on this plan, the government will elaborate a schedule of key reform steps.  

In the sugar sector, the government will finalize its plan to privatize SONASUT before end-March 1998. The two-stage action plan envisages the creation of a new company and investment by a private strategic partner in this enterprise, so as to increase its capital. This will be followed by the sale of government-held shares so as to reduce government participation to 15 percent. Also, the government has reduced the reference value for imported sugar by CFAF 20 to CFAF 350 per kilogram. Within the privatization framework, the government, in consultation with the World Bank and the IMF, will adopt a mechanism of continuing reduction in the reference price according to a preestablished schedule, with a fixed date for the elimination of the reference price.  

In the electricity sector, the government intends to adopt the new electricity code by end-July l998, and to begin to seek bids for privatization of the management of STEE by end-September l998. Furthermore, a comprehensive agreement on the amount, schedule, and the terms and conditions for payment of the government's debt to the STEE was signed by both parties.  

In the telecommunications sector, the government intends to adopt a new telecommunications code before end-April 1998, with technical advice from the World Bank, and begin the restructuring of the sector before end-June 1998. This restructuring will result in the creation of a National Postal Office (ONP), which will be an autonomous public agency, and the establishment of a new telecommunications enterprise, which will then be privatized. Also, the government will solicit competitive bids for a license for a cellular telephone operator before end-June 1998.  

Recently the government has given the management of the Hotel du Chari over to a private operator. It intends to accelerate the privatization operations of three small and medium-sized businesses already in progress: Air Tchad, road maintenance (SNER), and the slaughterhouse (Abattoirs Frigorifiques de Farcha). The government intends to solicit bids for these three enterprises before end-September 1998.  

In the area of business law, the government will endeavor to complete the work of adapting Chadian business legislation to the general framework defined in the treaty on the harmonization of business law in Africa (OHADA). Moreover, in consultation with its CEMAC zone partners, the government will adopt an investment code by end-June 1998.  

27. The government is determined to remedy the current weakness of the national statistics system and plans to submit a draft statistics law to Parliament by end-1998. In cooperation with its development partners, it has prepared a multiyear action program to modernize the statistics system, based in particular on (a) strengthening human resources; (b) upgrading equipment and offices; and (c) increasing financial resources. The government has adopted new national accounts, effective January 1, l998, with technical assistance from the UNDP.  

28. The program will be monitored on a quarterly basis with the help of quarterly quantitative benchmarks, structural benchmarks, and reference indicators, as wellas a midterm review. The quantitative benchmarks for end-June and end-December 1998 and endMarch l999 constitute indicators, and those for end-September 1998 constitute performance criteria. The quantitative benchmarks include (a) a ceiling on net bank credit to the government; (b) a ceiling on the primary current balance; (c) a floor for customs revenue; (d) a floor for the reduction of domestic arrears; (e) no accumulation of external payments arrears; and (f) no government or government-guaranteed nonconcessional foreign borrowing, with the exception of borrowing for petroleum projects, and no short-term foreign borrowing except for normal import financing. The amount of exceptional financial assistance and expenditure on priority sectors will serve as program indicators.  

29. The following will serve as structural performance criteria for end-September 1998: (a) adoption by the government of the recommendations on the civil service recruitment, wage, and promotion policies; (b) solicitation of bids for the privatization of SONASUT; and (c) solicitation of competitive bids for the privatization of the management of STEE, as well as elaboration by the government, and transmission to the CACEU, of a new list of products exempted from the turnover tax and an updating of the customs valuation database before end-July l998. The structural benchmarks for the program are (a) solicitation of competitive bids for an independent cellular telephone operator before end-June 1998; (b) solicitation of bids for Air Tchad, SNER, the BTCD, and Abattoirs Frigorifiques de Farcha before endSeptember 1998; (c) adoption of a privatization plan for the oil and soap operations of COTONTCHAD before end-December 1998; and (d) adoption of a restructuring program for the CNRT and CNPS before end-December 1998.  

30. The government of Chad will conduct a midterm review of the program with Fund staff by end-December 1998. The govermment considers that the measures adopted are sufficient to attain the program objectives. In the event that program benchmarks are not met, the government stands ready to initiate consultations with Fund staff to identify the necessary corrective measures.

At the beginning of the Bank's support of structural adjustment in Chad, a financial rehabilitation credit (FRC) was approved in July 1988. The objectives of the program were to re-establish the financial profitability of the cotton sector and to strengthen policy reforms in public finances. However, owing to the deteriorating fiscal performance, the FRC was suspended in July 1991. The residual balance was canceled in December l992, and the credit was closed.  

The Bank's medium-term goal is to work with Chad toward an approach of managing development in which external assistance is pooled in support of an integrated national program that is adequately focused on poverty. The foundation for this approach is being laid in an interim strategy for 1996-98, that seeks to help Chad restore minimum critical public sector capacity, focus public expenditures on poverty reduction, and promote sustainable use of natural resources.  

The Bank's strategy for Chad supports investments in the productive sectors (agriculture, livestock, and energy), as well as transport infrastructure and the social sectors (health and education). In l994, particular emphasis was given to the support of the devaluation, with a US\$20 milion economic recovery credit (ERC) and the development of human resources and the strengthening of economic management. A structural adjustment credit (SAC) of US\$30 million was approved in early l996 in support of the Chadian structural adjustment program. A second SAC was approved on June 30, 1997.

# C. Activities Under Preparation  

In 1997, the World Bank is continuing its technical assistance program (Capacity-Building Project for Economic Management). A household energy project in the amount of US\$6 million will go to the Board this calendar year. Work on the public expenditure review, focusing on education, will continue throughout the year. Finally, support to the Chad/Cameroon Petroleum Pipeline Project is under consideration.
