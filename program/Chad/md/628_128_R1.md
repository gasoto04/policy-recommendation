# I. Introduction  

The discussions for the l996 Article IV consultation and the midterm review under the first annual arrangement under the enhanced structural adjustment facility (ESAF) for Chad were held in N'Djamena during the period February 2l-March 6, 1996. 1/ A three-year arrangement under the ESAF, in an amount equivalent to SDR 49.56 million, or l20 percent of quota, was approved on September 1, l995 and the first semiannual loan under the first annual arrangement was disbursed on September 15, 1995 (EBS/95/l39); the second disbursement is subject to observance of the performance criteria for end-December l995 and completion of the midterm review. The attached letter from the Minister of Finance and the Minister of Planning and Cooperation, dated May 8, 1996 (Appendix II), reviews the progress made under the program during l995, sets out the policies and measures to be implemented for the remainder of the program year, and requests waivers for the nonobservance of certain performance criteria.  

As of the end of March 1996, Chad's outstanding use of Fund resources amounted to SDR 33 million, equivalent to 79.9 percent of quota. Assuming full disbursement of the loans under the three-year ESAF arrangement, and taking into account repayments and repurchases due during the three-year arrangement period on previous SAF loans and purchases under the 1994 stand-by arrangement, Chad's financial obligations to the Fund would amount to SDR 57.8 million, or l40 percent of quota, at end-June l998 (Appendix I, Table 6). Chad continues to avail itself of transitional arrangements under Article XIV, Section 2, but maintains an exchange system that is free of restrictions on the making of payments and transfers for current international transactions.  

Chad is on the standard 12-month consultation cycle. In concluding the last Article IV consultation on February 17, l995, Directors urged the authorities to improve tax and customs duty assessment and to streamline and strictly enforce tax collection, notably with respect to back taxes. Directors considered that the tight financial situation required restraint on government expenditure and they welcomed efforts to reduce subsidies through appropriate price and efficiency measures in government enterprises.  

Chad's program of structural reform continues to receive support from the World Bank and other donors. On February 15, l996, the World Bank Executive Board approved a one-tranche Structural Adjustment Credit (SAC) of $\mathtt{U S S30}$ million and a Development Credit of ${\tt U S S9.5}$ million aimed at building economic management capacity. Summaries of Chad's relations with the Fund and the World Bank Group are provided in Appendices III and IV, respectively. A debt sustainability analysis is presented in Appendix V.  

The quality, coverage, and timeliness of statistical information in Chad remain poor (Appendix VI) and will be the subject of technical assistance over the period of the ESAF arrangement. Some progress has been made in improving fiscal data but substantial work is required on the national accounts and balance of payments data.  

# II. Background and Performance Under the l995/96 Program  

Early in 1994, in the aftermath of the devaluation of the CFA franc, Chad adopted an adjustment program to ensure that the price and incentive effects of the exchange rate action would be accompanied by appropriate measures to achieve a lasting improvement in competitiveness and growth. Implementation of the l994 program, which was supported by a stand-by arrangement, encountered problems shortly after its inception, and the program went off track. A staff-monitored program was developed for the period October l994-June l995, which sought to strengthen revenue performance and broaden structural reform. Its successful implementation led to the present program, which has as its chief objectives macroeconomic stabilization and the establishment of an enabling envirorment for sustained economic growth. In particular, the program is aimed at strengthening government finances, improving public administration, and revitalizing the economy through price and trade liberalization and privatization, and streamlining regulations.  

From the outset, the authorities have emphasized that they view economic reform as an integral part of Chad's move toward a pluralistic form of government, and have made considerable efforts at every stage to discuss the program with the major political groups, the unions, and private sector employers, as well as the general public. l/ Representatives of the various social partners have been included in meetings between the Government and the staff of the Bretton Woods institutions, during which the details of the economic adjustment program have been explained at some length. The program is frequently discussed in the press.  

Program implementation has been broadly successful so far, and although the economic outcome in l995 was somewhat weaker than envisaged in the  

program, the results of adjustment have begun to be seen. l/ Overall real GDP growth did not exceed 3.2 percent, because poor weather conditions hampered the production of foodstuffs, but cotton production continued to grow, as did activity in manufacturing and services (Chart l; and Appendix II, paragraph 4). 2/ Inflation, as measured by the consumer price index, declined from 4l.3 percent in l994 to 9.5 percent in l995, some 3 percentage points higher than envisaged in the program, primarily as a result of higher-than-expected food prices (Appendix II, paragraph 5).  

The authorities made progress toward establishing sounder government finances (Chart l; and Appendix I, Tables 1 and 3). Revenue grew by 40 percent over $1994$ ,to $8.4$ percent of GDP. Revenue exceeded program targets by some CFAF 2 billion but was somewhat weaker in terms of GDP, given the higher-than-programmed nominal GDP growth (Appendix II, paragraph 8). The strong collection of income and international trade taxes resulted from sustained efforts to improve the administration of the tax directorates, but the collection of the newly instituted domestic turnover tax (TCA) and petroleum taxes was weak (Appendix II, paragraph 9). 3/ The gains in revenue mobilization were offset by slippages on the expenditure side of the budget, particularly overruns in commitments for civil service wages, defense expenditures, and transfers, while expenditures on health and education were smaller than budgeted (Appendix II, paragraph l0). The wage overrun was due to a supplementary wage increase given to teachers, an unanticipated expansion in the number of posts at the Presidency, and unforeseen travel costs of government personnel. Interest payments were smaller than the program had assumed, as was the domestic contribution to the public investment. 4/ The overall deficit was 15 percent of GDP (on a commitment basis, excluding grants) in l995, somewhat lower than programmed.  

Despite the satisfactory overall budget performance, financial management was made very difficult by delays in disbursements of foreign aid. The shortfall in external financing in l995 was some CFAF 25 billion (4.7 percent of GDP). l/ As a result, the end-December performance criteria on external and domestic arrears were not met (Appendix II, paragraph 7). 2/  

The external balance improved markedly in 1995 as a result of strong growth in cotton exports (Chart l; and Appendix II, paragraph 13). Although import volumes were also increasing, a favorable evolution in the terms of trade helped bring about a decline in the current account deficit (excluding official transfers), to CFAF 73.5 billion (l3.8 percent of GDP). 3/ The growth in foreign-financed public investment was supported by a large increase in external loans, while balance of payments financing was half the level received in l994. The overall balance of payments registered a surplus of CFAF 2l.4 billion, and Chad's contribution to official reserves of the regional central bank is estimated to have grown by about CFAF 30 billion (Appendix I, Table 4).  

Strong growth in currency holdings (36 percent) as well as increased demand and time deposits resulted in broad money growing by an estimated 36 percent in l995, well ahead of nominal GDP, and in velocity returning to pre-l990 levels (Chart 1; and Appendix II, paragraph l2). Given the moderate growth of private sector credit and the decline in net claims on government, the strong increase in demand for money in Chad helped finance a sharp jump in the net foreign assets of the banking system. 4/  

BEAC's regional monetary policy was tightened somewhat in the course of 1995 given the gradual deterioration of the net foreign position of the Central Bank. This reflected primarily confidence factors related to the health of some banking systems in the BEAC region. Chad's minimum deposit rates were kept unchanged at 5.5 percent throughout the year, while maximum lending rates remained at l6 percent. However, the monetary authorities decided to liberalize deposit rates (for deposits above CFAF 5 million) and to set the maximum lending rate at a number of basis points above the penalty rate. On January l, l996, the ceiling on lending rates was relaxed and the rate increased to 22 percent.  

Chad's commercial banking system remained relatively sound in 1995. The banks' liquidity position at the Central Bank improved, reflecting growth in the deposit base. Because of the withdrawal of its main foreign shareholder, Chad's largest bank, the Banque Tchadienne des Crédits et des Dépots (BTcD), had to be taken over for a transitory period by the Government. The Banque Meridien BIAO-Tchad (BMBT), an affiliate of the Meridien Group, faced heavy losses related to the collapse of its holding company, as well as a significant decline in its share of the deposit market. In view of the financial fragility of the bank, the BMBT's access to the BEAC's auction window was suspended until February l996, so that it had to seek refinancing through the more expensive repurchase agreements.  

Significant progress was made in improving resource allocation in 1995 through structural reform and economic liberalization, particularly with regard to price liberalization, privatization, and the initiation of the restructuring process of the cotton company (Appendix II, paragraph l4). Exchange regulations were modified to conform to regional standards, and import and export licenses were abolished. A small negative list was established, based solely on security and health concerns. Amendment of the labor code, including abolishing minimum wage requirements set by the Government, a structural performance criterion for end-December, was postponed. The authorities scheduled a seminar to discuss these amendments with social partners late in l995. In the event, the ILO had to postpone its participation due to resource constraints, and thus the seminar was put off until end-January l996. After making the required revisions, the amendments were approved by the Government on April l6, l996, and approval by Parliament is expected soon (Appendix II, paragraph 15).  

In summary, the satisfactory budgetary performance ensured that all quantitative performance criteria for end-December were met except for those on domestic and external arrears. l/ As to the structural performance criteria and benchmarks, only the amendment of the labor code was postponed (Table 1).  

The discussions focused on the measures to be implemented in the first half of 1996 to consolidate the progress made in l995 and to attain the objectives of the l995/96 program. The broad policy outlines for the remainder of the calendar year were discussed but will have to be confirmed in the context of the 1996/97 program discussions.  

The authorities were confident that the l996 macroeconomic objectives of the program could be met. Real GDP growth is forecast to climb to 5.8 percent in l996 (Appendix I, Table 1). Production in the primary sector is expected to expand in line with a further increase in cotton production and a rebound in the production of foodstuffs, while manufacturing growth will be spurred by the public investment program. The consumer price index, which declined by 1.8 percent in the first 3 months of the year, is projected to increase by 4.5 percent for the year as a whole.  

# 1. Financial_.policies  

Aware that the key to the success of the program was continued consolidation of government finances, the authorities strengthened their 1996 fiscal program by raising the revenue target from CFAF 50.6 billion (9.7 percent of GDP) to CFAF 59.7 billion (l0.l percent of GDP) and incorporating in the program a ceiling on current primary expenditures. l/ The current primary surplus for l996 is projected at CFAF 0.5 billion (0.l percent of GDP), with the overall fiscal deficit equivalent to 14.1 percent of GDP (Appendix II, paragraph 17).  

Measures taken at the start of l996 to improve the collection of taxes make the new revenue target feasible, albeit ambitious. To address the weak collection of the domestic turnover tax, the authorities have established units to pursue delinquent taxpayers and improve the monitoring of the tax on imports and of the related tax credits (Appendix II, paragraph l8). To improve the collection of taxes on petroleum imports, the Government has terminated its contract with the private firm that had been collecting them, replacing it on January l, l996, with a locally managed bureau (Appendix II, paragraph l9). The authorities have also instituted a duty-drawback system aimed at eliminating exempted petroleum imports from being used for nonexempted purposes. In the first two months of l996, petroleum tax collections exceeded the monthly average for l995 by some 50 percent, and  

for the year are expected to be more than double the l995 levels. l/ Customs reform in l996 will emphasize the control of exemptions, for which about 50 percent of imported goods (in value terms) in l995 were eligible (Appendix II, paragraph 20). Efforts will be concentrated on military imports, government procurement contracts, and externally financed projects. Improvements in the administration of direct taxes include the introduction of advance payments in installments for large taxpayers, an effort to expand the tax base to the informal sector, and a particular emphasis on the collection of overdue taxes (Appendix II, paragraph 2l). The improvement in the terms of trade in 1995 boosted the earnings of COTONTCHAD. This will have a positive impact on the l996 government revenues.  

Current expenditures in 1996 are projected to be within program targets, except for the addition of CFAF 5 billion for the military demobilization program, the funding for which has been included in the World Bank structural adjustment credit (Appendix II, paragraph 22). The budgets for the priority social sector ministries have been increased by 20 percent in real terms, while other ministries' budgets, including defense, have remained constant in nominal terms. The ceiling on primary current expenditures (excluding demobilization and election costs) has been set at CFAF 29.0 billion for the first half of the year, just below one half the annual amount. To ensure better execution of the budget, including sectoral allocation, and the public investment program, the authorities intend to strengthen the budget directorate at the Ministry of Finance and the external aid directorate at the Ministry of Planning. In addition, the public expenditure review now under way will identify weaknesses in expenditure control systems and suggest remedial measures (Appendix II, paragraphs 24-27).  

A major difficulty in formulating the fiscal program for l996 was determining the level of the wage bill. Because the authorities' monitoring focused on cash payments, the substantial overrun in the l995 wage bill commitments was not detected until early l996. 2/ Taking wage developments in l995 fully into account, the program envisages a wage bill of CFAF 30.9 billion, substantially more than the original budget. 3/ However, at the time of the discussions, results of the civil service census, which the authorities had begun in October l995, were not known. Initially, irregularities were found in the dossiers of some 8,000 workers (out of 25,oo0), and the authorities suspended payments for any

irregularities revealed by the census, irrespective of the position of the individual affected. This ensured immediate cash savings in 1995 and early 1996,but left the issue of the ultimate level of wage commitments in 1996 unresolvable until the examination of each dossier had been completed. 1/ This could not be expected before late April, but early results from N'Djamena indicated that there would likely be savings from eliminating ghost workers and workers who should have retired. 2/ Therefore, the program includes a downward adjustor on the current primary expenditure ceiling for any saving resulting from the census, and the l996 wage bill will be revisited in the context of the discussions of the second-year program.  

The civil service census will permit the computerization and integration of personnel and payroll records, thus providing a sound data base for analysis of the structure of the civil service and its eventual reform, the focus of which will be moderating wage growth and eliminating automatic promotions. The Government has also decided to accelerate the process of military demobilization. It has put in place a program whereby the size of the military is to be reduced from 32,000 at end-l995 to 25,000 men by the end of the third quarter of 1996 (Appendix II, paragraph 29). Extension of the program beyond this first phase will be discussed at a donors' round table scheduled for the second half of 1996.  

The National Retirement Fund of Chad (CNRT) has been able to pay only one month of benefits a year over the last three years (Appendix II, paragraph 28). Its financial insolvency stems primarily from the failure of the Government to pay the employer's share of retirement contributions and to forward the employees' share that was withheld. The authorities are aware of the social importance of restoring the financial health of the CNRT, especially given their plans to demobilize more troops and to reform the civil service, but this will require a multiyear financial restructuring plan based on a detailed financial analysis. For l996, funds from the petroleum tax will continue to be earmarked for the CNRT and the l996 budget provides for adequate funds to meet the CNRT's current obligations. The National Social Insurance Fund (CNPS) is in a better cash-flow situation but also needs financial restructuring to ensure its long-term viability. For both institutions, actuarial analyses are expected to be completed by end-June and the authorities' restructuring plans will be taken up in the context of the discussions on the second-year program.  

The financial program for l996 incorporates the full payment of domestic and external arrears identified in the original program by endJune l996. External financing delayed from l995 should be received in the first half of 1996, which, together with debt rescheduling and the second disbursement under the first year ESAF arrangement, should close the financing gap of CFAF 28.4 billion in the first half of the year. For calendar year 1996, there remains a financing gap of CFAF ll.5 billion, which would be partially filled by the final disbursement under the second annual arrangement under the ESAF. Financing for the 1996/97 program would be sought at a donors' round table in the second half of 1996.  

Broad money growth is expected to continue to outpace nominal GDP growth in 1996, reflecting strengthened money demand (Appendix I, Table 5). l/ Bank credit to the economy is expected to peak during the first half of the year, reflecting the seasonal pattern of cotton crop credit, but by end-l996, private sector credit should be lower than at end-l995, as the cotton company's financial position strengthens and its financing needs for the l996/97 crop season decline. 2/ The Chadian authorities will work within the regional monetary arrangements to encourage the use of indirect instruments of monetary policy at interest rates that reflect market conditions. They are aware, however, that meaningful progress on this score hinges importantly on the strengthening of the banking systems in several countries of the subregion and thus on a lessening of current systemic bank risks. For their part, they intend to further banking reform in Chad by seeking to privatize the remaining government-owned commercial bank (BTCD). With regard to the BMBT, investors are expected to recapitalize the bank in the first six months of 1996, enabling the bank to expand lending activities and compete to regain deposits.  

# 2. Structural policies  

Structural reform will focus on the parastatals, which play an important role in the economy. For the cotton sector, the discussions centered on how the producers, the Government, and the cotton company (COTONTCHAD) should share the costs and profits of cotton production, so as to increase production incentives to farmers and ensure efficient processing of the crop (Appendix II, paragraph 33). The authorities confirmed their  

intention to establish a two-step producer pricing mechanism before the start of the 1996/97 cotton harvest (about October 1996): a pre-season announced price based on the evolution of world prices and the exchange rate, and the remainder to be paid to producers after the financial results of the season are known. l/ A study on the specific modalities of this mechanism is to be completed before end-June l996. As part of the action plan developed to improve the performance of COTONTCHAD, the authorities are also conducting studies on ways to strengthen the company's human resources and organizational structure, and on possible procedures for government divestiture. 2/  

In the sugar sector, the Government has confirmed its intention to eliminate the import monopoly held by SONAsUT, the sugar parastatal, by January 1, l997. As originally envisaged, before end-June 1996, the Government will conduct a study on the various alternatives for its disengagement from SONAsUT. It will include elaboration of any measures that might be needed to provide temporary support to the company, after the withdrawal of the monopoly, without running counter to the UDEAC tax and tariff reform. A study of the technical and financial requirements to achieve operating equilibrium in the electricity and water utility will also be completed by end-September. The privatization program has been expanded to include five additional enterprises (Appendix II, paragraph 37), such that the number of state enterprises is now slated to decline from 40 in 1990 to l5. In addition, the authorities have ratified the regional treaty on the harmonization of business law in Africa.  

The public investment program complements the authorities' efforts to reform public enterprises. Given the civil strife that has plagued Chad since the l960s, substantial rebuilding of public infrastructure is required and the expected public investment will amount to some CFAF 70 billion a year over the next three years. 3/ The program focuses on providing an enabling environment for economic growth, particularly through an emphasis on rebuilding transportation infrastructure as well as development of the agricultural sector and of human resources, especially in health, education, and other social services.  

The Government has made efforts on several fronts to mitigate the impact of its adjustment program on the most disadvantaged groups. As noted above, the only ministries whose budgetary allocations have been increased in real terms are those key to development, especially education and health. In the context of the public investment program, projects have been designed to aid disadvantaged groups and favor labor-intensive public works that focus on provision of basic public services. The most pressing  

environmental problem is the extensive deforestation caused by the cutting of fuel wood near major cities. A household energy study is under way to find alternative sources of energy at reasonable costs. Environmental impact studies are also being conducted in the context of the forthcoming exploitation of the country's oil resources.  

# 3. Medium-term balance of payments outlook  

The medium-term balance of payments projections that underlie the 1995/96 program were updated in light of developments during the first eight months of the program period and the most recent price projections of the World Economic Outlook. The external current account deficit, excluding official transfers, is expected to deteriorate in 1996, to l6.2 percent of GDP, from an estimated 13.8 percent in 1995, as increased 1996/97 cotton production and public investment require substantial inputs, while the volume of cotton exports is likely to be constrained by the lower-thanprogrammed cottonseed production in the l995/96 agricultural campaign (Appendix I, Table 4). Also, net service outflows, are expected to pick up, given the higher freight and insurance charges linked to higher import levels and the increased need for financial and other business services.  

Over the medium term, external prospects are expected to improve progressively with the liberalization of the economy and the realization of the fruits of the public investment program now under way. l/ However, the pace of improvement will be slow in light of the need for substantial import-intensive investment, which will be governed in part by the extent of donor support. In addition, although there is currently room for strong growth in cotton exports, medium-term growth prospects remain constrained by COTONTCHAD's limited ginning capacity, which is not expected to expand before l999. 2/ The external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) is projected to reach some ll.5 percent of GDP in 2001 (Appendix V, Tables 3 and 5). A continued reliance on official transfers and on long-term highly concessional loans is assumed, with the investment program relying increasingly on loan financing. 3/ The residual financing requirements are estimated at CFAF ll.6 billion for l997, and CFAF l0.5 billion for l998. Expectations are that those will be covered through (a) concessional financing to be secured from donors; (b) a Paris Club  

rescheduling; and (c) debt relief from other bilateral creditors. Chad should be able to meet is payments obligations to the Fund in a timely manner.  

The authorities completed all bilateral agreements under the March 1995 one-year Paris Club terms-of-reference rescheduling. A three-year terms of reference rescheduling agreement by Paris Club creditors is expected to be concluded in the near future. Negotiations with other non-Paris Club bilateral creditors have commenced. Debt management capacity is very limited in Chad, and the authorities have requested technical assistance to improve it.  

The debt sustainability analysis presented in Appendix V assumes flow reschedulings on Naples terms ahead of a stock-of-debt operation in 1999, at the end of the current ESAF. l/ The stock-of-debt operation will help close the projected financing gaps, but the contribution will be modest given the relatively small amount of eligible debt. Based on current information, there would appear to be no non-previously rescheduled precutoff-date debt. Hence, the stock-of-debt operation is assumed to involve a topping up to a 67 percent NPV reduction on debt rescheduled on Toronto terms in l989. Under this scenario, Chad's external debt service obligations over the medium and long term appear to be sustainable (Appendix V). The debt stock at the end of l995, including arrears, was CFAF 40l billion (75 percent of GDP), of which 84 percent (CFAF 340 billion) was owed to multilaterals. 2/ The debt service due, including the Fund, was 7.9 percent of exports of goods and nonfactor services. Assuming average export volume growth of 5-6 percent over 1996-2005, total debt service due declines to less than 9 percent of exports by 2005 (Appendix V, Table 5). For debt service to multilaterals, the ratio declines to less than 8 percent by 2005. Because of the need for continued substantial public investment in Chad, the decline in the debt stock as a percent of GDP is gradual over the next ten years, reaching 72 percent in 2005 (l56 percent of exports of goods and nonfactor services in present value terms), and the debt service ratio increases beginning in 2008, but remains below l0 percent of exports through 2015.  

The medium- and long-term balance of payments outlook for Chad may be dramatically improved if oil reserves in the south of the country begin to be exported. As negotiations are ongoing in this regard, the authorities were able to provide only a rough idea of the magnitude and timing of such exports, as well as the imports needed to build the required production and transport infrastructure. A scenario is presented in the context of the debt sustainability analysis (Appendix V), but a fuller elaboration of the potential of these reserves might be possible in the context: of the  

discussions on a second-year program. The downside risk to the medium- and long-term debt sustainability comes from the extent of concessionality on external loans and the price of cotton, which, absent oil exports, will remain the primary export commodity.  

With respect to the exchange system, the authorities indicated that the making of payments and transfers for current international transactions would remain free of restrictions and that the limits on travel allowances would continue to be administered liberally. In consultation with other members of the BEAc, Chad intends to work toward the regional harmonization of exchange regulations and to move towards acceptance of the obligations of Article VIII, Sections 2, 3, and 4 of the Fund's Articles of Agreement.  

# 4. Data issues  

Serious data deficiencies continue to hamper analysis and policy formulation in Chad, especially in the areas of national income accounts and balance of payments and some monetary statistics. Although Chad's reporting of data is now regular (Appendix VI), economic management would be facilitated by improvements in the timeliness and quality of key macroeconomic statistics. The authorities expressed the desire to begin a comprehensive examination of the statistical system, starting with elaboration of a statistical law. Meanwhile, several immediate steps will be taken to improve the data base. Thanks to the l percentage point increase in the statistical import tax in l996, resources are expected to become available to improve balance of payments data. Also, efforts within the Ministry of Finance to improve budgetary monitoring and to bring all public entities within the government accounting system will give a more complete picture of the public finances.  

# IV. Staff Appraisal  

The Chadian authorities have consolidated the progress made since mid-1994 in implementing their structural adjustment program. Their effort to engage their social partners in the discussion of policies has engendered support for the program that transcends political boundaries. Thus, the prospect of elections this year does not alter the authorities' expectation of continued strong commitment to policy reforms.  

Although output growth in l995 fell short of expectations because of poor weather conditions, the effects of the devaluation of the CFA franc, the liberalization of domestic markets, and the reactivation of public investment have already become evident. Cotton production and exports have continued to expand, there are no acute shortages of staple commodities, and 1995 saw rapid growth in construction and public works. However, for growth to be sustained, the provision of basic government services must be strengthened and the public sector must begin to contribute to national savings. Thus, the key policy thrust must be to continue bringing order to government finances.  

Impressive strides were made in 1995 in mobilizing government revenue, permitting the Government to function adequately for the first time in many years. However, revenues as a percent of GDP remained low and serious weaknesses persisted in the collection of some taxes. The commitment of the Government to raise its revenue target for l996 above the original program level is to be welcomed. However, given that targeted revenue represents only l0.l percent of GDP, improvements should be accelerated and targets exceeded whenever possible. The authorities have taken strong actions to address these weaknesses, but continued determined efforts to strengthen tax administration will be required for a number of years to come. In particular, it is critical that the authorities put in place appropriate systems to monitor and control import tax exemptions, to ensure regular payment of direct taxes, and to collect arrears on these taxes, while expanding the tax base.  

The provision of government services is constrained by the low level of revenues; proper sectoral allocation of expenditure, therefore, is crucial to long-term growth prospects. It is regrettable that there were overruns in wages, defense spending, and transfers in l995. Inclusion of a ceiling on current primary expenditures in the program and reinforcement of budgetary monitoring in the Ministry of Finance is an appropriate response to enhance expenditure control, as are efforts to pare the size of the military. The lack of control of the civil service wage bill also underscores the importance of the civil service census in providing a sound base for the civil service payroll. The authorities have prudently revised the programmed level of the wage bill to an upper bound figure for l996, while including a downward adjustor for any saving that might result from the census. Nevertheless, it is critical that the census be fully exploited to achieve such savings, and that strict control over the wage bill be exercised in anticipation of the deeper reform of the civil service--including the substitution of a merit-based pay system for the current automatic promotions--slated to be implemented over the last two years of the arrangement.  

A complicating factor in financial management in l995 was the shortfall in external aid resulting from delayed donor disbursements, which in turn led to the breach of the performance criteria on external and domestic arrears at end-December. Given the strong fiscal program in l996, receipt of the delayed external assistance should make it possible to regain lost ground and meet the end-June program benchmarks. It is important, however, for Chad to reinforce debt management capacity and to make efforts to ensure that external payments obligations are met in a timely fashion. Progress should also be made in reaching rescheduling agreements with non-Paris Club creditors.  

The authorities should continue to lend their support to reforms at the regional level to strengthen the effectiveness of monetary policy and move to market-based instruments of monetary control. Continued reform of the banking system, particularly privatization of the state-owned bank, should be pursued. The authorities should also envisage, along with the other BEAC member states, the acceptance of Article VIII obligations at an early date.  

Over the medium term, the balance of payments position is expected to improve, even without taking into account the possibility of large oil exports. Improvements in the external position will initially be moderate as the large public investment program will require substantial imports and export growth will be somewhat capacity bound. Chad's debt burden is not large and its debt service obligations appear to be sustainable even with continued borrowing to support public investment.  

Much progress has been made since mid-l994 toward economic liberalization and structural reform and there is evidence already that these efforts are bearing fruit. In l996, several key decisions regarding public enterprises need to be taken. In the cotton sector, the mechanism to determine the producer price should ensure both adequate production incentives to producers and revenues to the Government, while reflecting the evolution in world prices, the exchange rate, and protecting the financial position of the cotton company. In the sugar sector, the decision to remove the import monopoly of the state-owned sugar company by the end of 1996 requires that the Government consider what measures, if any, consistent with the UDEAC reform, should be taken to ensure the company's financial viability and competitiveness.  

The statistical system in Chad requires substantial rebuilding. A comprehensive strategy is needed, and although a framework statistical law will be of help, it is crucial for improved program monitoring that early and measurable progress be made to remedy the most glaring deficiencies, especially in the area of balance of payments and national income accounts. The authorities are encouraged to seek further financial and technical assistance in these areas.  

Given the broadly satisfactory implementation of the l995/96 program, the actions taken by the authorities to redress program weaknesses, the approval by the Government of the labor code amendments, and the clearance of arrears with the foreign assistance that has thus far been received, the staff recommends that the nonobservance of the performance criteria on domestic and external arrears and the structural performance criterion relating to the amendment of the labor code at end-December l995 be waived and that the present midterm review under the first annual ESAF arrangement be concluded.  

It is proposed that the next Article IV consultation with Chad be held on the standard l2-month cycle.

Dear Mr. Camdessus:  

1. As provided for in the first annual arrangement under the enhanced structural adjustment facility (ESAF), the Government of Chad and Fund staff conducted discussions during February 2l-March 6, 1996 for a midterm review of the July l995-June l996 annual program. The Chadian authorities and Fund staff reviewed economic and financial developments in Chad during the first eight months of the program year, compared results with program objectives, and examined the outlook for the remainder of the program year and the second half of 1996.  

2.  The key objectives of the July l995-June l996 program are an acceleration of economic growth and a moderation of inflation, with the chief policy action being a consolidation of government finances. The program also calls for the implementation of structural reforms in support of the liberalization of the economy and prices, the reorganization of the public enterprise sector, and the development of the private sector.  

3. The authorities view the reform of Chad's economy as essential in the introduction of political pluralism. Because they have made the structural adjustment program a high priority, its implementation has been closely monitored by top government officials. In order to ensure that the importance of the economic and structural adjustment is widely understood, the authorities have also tried to increase awareness of the main aspects of the program by labor unions and private sector operators as well as by the public at large. Nevertheless, the Government is aware that the country's 1995 performance, while satisfactory overall, still falls short of its potential.  

4. According to preliminary estimates, real GDP growth in l995 was 3.2 percent, which is below program projections. This modest performance is chiefly the result of a disappointing crop year, caused by poor weather conditions. Agricultural output stagnated following a 0.9 percent drop in the volume of food production and despite an 8.5 percent rise in cottonseed output. In the manufacturing sector, cotton fiber and oil production expanded substantially ( $64$ percent in the case of cotton fiber). Value  

added in construction and public works grew an estimated 32 percent, because of the sharp upturn in public investment.  

5.  Inflation, as measured by the change in N'Djamena's consumer price index for the l2 months to December l995, is estimated at 10 percent, or 3.5 percentage points more than projected, owing in particular to pressure on the prices of local food products in the fourth quarter because of reduced supply (a period traditionally characterized by seasonally low prices) and the deregulation of certain prices in June l995. Nevertheless, the 1995 average inflation rate of 9.5 percent confirms the slowing of inflation and a gradual return to the price stability that prevailed before the January 1994 devaluation.  

6. On the fiscal side, the progress made in revenue collection, with the program objective being exceeded by 5 percent (CFAF 2.l billion), was largely offset by slippages in current expenditure, which was 2.6 percent (CFAF l.6 billion) higher than the program target. The current primary deficit is estimated at 2.3 percent of GDP, compared with a program objective of 2.2 percent. Spending on public investment expenditure amounted to CFAF 61.8 billion, or CFAF 4.5 billion less than projected. The overall deficit, on a commitment basis and excluding externally financed investments, amounted to 3.8 percent of GDP, or only CFAF l.7 billion less than programmed, even though the interest on domestic and external debt service and the national contribution to financing the public investment program were some CFAF 3.8 billion below program targets.  

7. Financial management in l995 was adversely affected by the delayed disbursement of external financial assistance, with an estimated shortfall of some CFAF 25 billion, excluding the rescheduling of the postal debt. These shortfalls in external assistance delayed implementation of the fiscal consolidation program and resulted in the end-December performance criteria for the reduction of domestic and external arrears not being met. External arrears declined by CFAF 14.l billion, as opposed to the projected clearance of the entire stock of arrears of CFAF 28.7 billion, while domestic arrears increased by CFAF 10.2 billion instead of decreasing by an estimated CFAF 3.7 billion.  

8． Government revenue amounted to CFAF 44.8 billion (8.4 percent of GDP), as compared with a program target of CFAF 42.7 billion. Customs receipts, including the single tax, exceeded the targeted amount by 3.3 percent, reaching nearly CFAF l4.2 billion, an increase of some 63 percent over 1994. Receipts collected by the tax directorate amounted to CFAF 2l.2 billion, exceeding program projections by 13 percent. Overall, the tax burden was some 7.4 percent of GDP, close to the rate envisaged in the program. All in all revenue performance was satisfactory, except for receipts from the turnover tax and duties and taxes on imports of petroleum products.  

9. Efforts to improve the functioning of, and the coordination between. the main directorates within the Ministry of Finance continued during the first eight months of the program year. These efforts allowed for the design of a single taxpayer record, which facilitated data centralization and helped curb tax evasion. Reform of the customs administration (based or an action plan developed with technical assistance from the Fund's Fiscal Affairs Department and supported by financial assistance from the World Bank), substantially improved the collection of customs revenue. This reform included the institution of periodic staff reports, the elimination of the direct removal credits (credits d'enlevement direct), closer monitoring of suspended duty arrangements, and equipment upgrades. Customs inspections and anti-fraud activities were improved through the enhancement of the system of bonuses for customs officers and the weekly monitoring of customs performance by the Government. In addition, the establishment of quotas on tax-free imports by diplomats and nongovernmental organizations facilitated better control of the exemptions granted to this sector. Implementation of the UDEAC customs and tax reform was completed in 1995; the investment code and the conventions d'établissement (special tax and customs agreements with COTONTCHAD, SONASUT, and MCT) were stripped of the tax provisions contrary to the new system; and the single tax was abolished on January l, l996. The simplification of the tax system ard the lower rates provided for in the new UDEAC system boosted the number of customs declarations. Together with an improved processing, this helped improve overall customs performance substantially.  

10. In 1995, current expenditure commitments rose to CFAF 64.7 billion, or nearly CFAF 1.7 billion more than provided for in the program. Current primary expenditure overruns were some CFAF 3.7 billion, with the nonmilitary wage bill accounting for CFAF 2.7 billion. The overrun in the nonmilitary wage bill was traceable to a supplementary l0 percent wage increase given to teachers, an unanticipated expansion of the number of posts in the Presidency and the Primature and unforeseen costs of travel by government officials. Recruitment of personnel in key development sectors provided for in the program was also more costly than anticipated. Wage savings were effected in the Ministry of Foreign Affairs and at Parliament. Actual wage payments totaled CFAF 27.3 billion (including CFAF 4.4 billion for prior years), as opposed to a revised program target of CFAF 27.8 billion. The additional CFAF 1 billion on overruns were in military expenditure. The funds budgeted for the priority sectors of health and education were not entirely committed; this resulted from a delay in the disbursement of targeted external assistance. Lastly, interest due on domestic and external debt service was less than program projections by CFAF 2.6 billion. Investment expenditure in l995, with a financial execution rate of 67 percent compared with 8l percent in l994, consisted essentially of investments in infrastructure of CFAF 19.3 billion (31.6 percent of the total) and in human resources development totaling CFAF 18.2 billion (29.0 percent of the total).  

11. The Government, aware of the heavy weight of wage expenditure in the budget, undertook a general civil service census in October l995, which revealed important irregularities in the civil service records. When the initial results of the census became known, the authorities decided to suspend the wages of some 8,000 employees whose records contained irregularities and to establish a committee to examine each individual's records. As the work progressed, the wages of any employee unjustly  

suspended were restored retroactively. The review for N'Djaména was completed in mid-February and in mid-April for the outlying areas. The definitive results are in the process of being finalized.  

12. Chad's broad money supply grew substantially in l995 (36 percent), which mainly resulted in a sizable increase in net foreign assets (40 percent). Increased cotton fiber sales and external financing in support of the program effectively expanded the flow of foreign currency receipts to the banking system. The increase in external assets also reflected an extraordinary increase in bank notes of other BEAC countries deposited with Chadian banks. Domestic credit, on the other hand, contracted 9.5 percent, reflecting the improvement in the Government's net position with the banking system, only partially offset by a modest increase in credit to the economy (5.3 percent). The performance criteria at end-l995 for the Central Bank's net domestic assets and net banking system credit to the government were therefore satisfied. Two components of the demand for money--bank notes and time deposits--were particularly buoyant, reflecting the public enterprises' improved cash position and increased competition between banks in resource collection. Currency in circulation was stimulated by larger cottonseed purchases and the rise in producer prices. It should be noted that monetary analysis in Chad remains tenuous, because of uncertainties about currency trends and net external assets, resulting from the accumulation over the past year of a large stock of unsorted BEAC bank notes. The authorities are making efforts to reduce this stock over time.  

13. With respect to the balance of payments, cotton exports, estimated at CFAF 56.2 billion (an 80 percent increase over l994), exceeded program projections by approximately 8 percent. At the same time, sustained domestic demand, fueled in part by increased public investment, led to a 16 percent rise in the volume of imports. The current account balance (excluding official transfers), which also benefited from an improvement in the terms of trade, recorded a deficit of l3.8 percent of GDP instead of the 21.1 percent projected in the program. Externally financed public investments continued to expand in l995 (approximately 6 percent), while external balance of payments assistance (CFAF l4.4 billion) was roughly half that raised in l994. Overall, the balance of payments showed a surplus of CFAF 2l billion, equivalent to 4.0 percent of GDP. The growth of Chad's contribution to the official external reserves of the BEAC is estimated at CFAF 29.6 billion.  

14. Significant progress was made in l995 in the area of structural reforms and liberalization of the economy. The liberalization of prices was expanded. In particular, controls were lifted on sugar prices, on margins at all stages of sugar distribution, and on the prices of oils and soap. Currently, only the prices of electricity, water, petroleum products, and some 60 essential drugs are still controlled by the Government. The privatization program is progressing satisfactorily, with invitations to bid having been issued for the privatization of eight enterprises in l995-96, bringing the total to ten, and a partnership agreement having been signed between Air Tchad and Air Afrique. In the banking sector, the BMBT was  

taken over by a new shareholder in early l996. By end-March l996, a total of 13 public and semi-public enterprises had been liquidated, including the International Trade and Industry Bank of Chad (BICIT), the Sugar Fund, and the Petroleum Fund. The cross-debt between the Government (Cotton Price Stabilization Fund debts) and COTONTCHAD (loan from the French Development Fund onlent to COTONTCHAD) was settled. _A program to implement the measures aimed at improving the productivity of the cotton industry and at reorganizing and streamlining the cotton company was completed at end-l995. The exchange regulations were updated to conform to the transitional provisions adopted by the BEAC Council of Ministers in September l993. The decrees of 1989 and 1966, respectively instituting import and export licenses, were repealed. A negative list of products subject to special import authorization was drawn up, solely on the basis of health and public safety considerations.  

15. At end-December l995, all quantitative performance criteria were met, with the exception of the criteria on the repayment of domestic and external arrears. The structural performance criterion for the elimination of export and import licenses was satisfied, as expected. The performance criterion pertaining to amendment of the labor code, including annulment of the provisions concerning the establishment of wage scales (including the minimum wage) by the Government, was not satisfied, because of the International Labor Office's postponement until end-January l996 of a seminar on labor code reform, held in N'Djaména under the auspices of the Ministry of Labor and with the participation of labor unions and private sector managers. The amendments were approved by Government on April 16, 1996 and are expected to be approved by Parliament shortly. All the structural benchmarks of the program were met.  

16. The economic outlook for l996 is favorable, with projected real GDP growth of 5.8 percent and an inflation rate close to the program objective of 4.5 percent. However, to facilitate the attainment of these results, the authorities will continue to pursue strict economic and financial policies during the year. Aware of the weaknesses that became apparent in the first half of the l995/96 annual program, mainly overruns on expenditures and low yields on some taxes, the authorities have taken concrete measures to improve the budgetary situation in 1996.  

17. The l996 budget, submitted to Parliament in November l995 and approved in March of this year, was consistent with program objectives. However, it did not adequately reflect the 1995 outturn, which was not yet known when it was drawn up. Expenditure and revenue estimates, therefore, have had to be revised to reflect the budgetary situation at end-1995 and to take full account of the impact of measures adopted to improve program performance. Consequently, government revenue is now projected at CFAF 59.6 billion in 1996, in contrast with CFAF 50.6 billion in the original program and CFAF 44.8 billion collected in l995. Current expenditure is set at CFAF 73 billion, as opposed to the program's CFAF 69.5 billion. However, this amount includes the cost of the first phase of a new military demobilization program, which is financed by the World Bank (CFAF 5 billion), as well as election-related outlays of CFAF l.5 billion. Overall,  

the current primary balance (excluding demobilization and election outlays) is now estimated to show a small surplus of CFAF 0.5 billion, compared with a deficit of CFAF 6.5 billion envisaged in the original program.  

18. The revised revenue projections are about a third higher than the 1995 figures. The authorities believe that this level, although high, is within reach. However, it will require strenuous efforts to collect current and overdue taxes and to combat fraud and tax evasion. In this context, the yield of the turnover tax (TCA) that was introduced in June l995 is projected to increase by 67 percent to CFAF l0.4 billion. The authorities are aware that introducing a new tax can give rise initially to administrative setbacks, which requires that delinquent taxpayers be closely monitored. For this reason, a special unit has now been established in the Tax Directorate to provide systematic follow-up of taxpayers. In addition, the extension of the TCA to large Chadian enterprises, formerly subject to the single tax, should result in a sharp rise in TCA assessments in 1996. Moreover, a branch office of the Tax Directorate has been opened at the Customs Directorate to process TCA assessments on imports and more closely monitor TCA credits.  

19. In the area of petroleum taxes, the authorities have set a receipt target of CFAF 4.5 billion (net of payments to the Retirement Fund (CNRT) and the Road Fund (CAER)), as against CFAF 2.0 billion in l995. Given the weak l995 performance, the authorities canceled their contract with the private firm COTECNA at the end of the year and created an autonomous Petroleum Taxation Office, attached to the Ministry of Finance. This office, which reports periodically on its performance, exercises full customs authority and, to ensure its immediate effectiveness in the field, is staffed primarily by senior officials from the Customs Directorate. Moreover, to combat fraud more effectively, the authorities, in establishing this office, instituted a duty mechanism for exempted petroleum product imports. The authorities will ensure that this mechanism, administered by the Petroleum Taxation Office, is applied without exception to all imports, including those of the Electricity and Water Company (STEE). The authorities plan to incorporate the operations of the office officially into the budget in the context of the next supplementary budget.  

20. To meet the objective of CFAF 15.7 billion in customs receipts, in 1996 the authorities will strengthen the measures adopted since June l994 to rehabilitate customs control and restructure its procedures. The new measures will emphasize the monitoring and control of exemptions which, in terms of taxable value, represented about 33 percent of total estimated imports in l995. The authorities also intend strictly to apply the UDEAC regime to imports of military equipment and similar goods, and have begun renegotiating agreements on granting customs exemptions to Chad's military partners. They will present the findings of their study on the appropriate mechanisms for taxing government procurement contracts and externally financed projects as soon as possible, with a view to establishing such a mechanism in the second half of l996. In modernizing and strengthening customs control, the Customs Directorate, with the support of external partners, intends to enhance its computer capabilities in order better to  

monitor exemptions, suspended duty arrangements, valuation records, and statistics on official import flows.  

21. Collections of the tax directorate should amount to CFAF 33.5 billion (including collections of back taxes and petroleum taxes), compared with CFAF 23.3 billion in 1995. This increase will result from the substantial prospective rise in income tax, which is predicated on the improved financial position of the large enterprises, particularly COTONTCHAD; the expansion of the tax base to include the informal sector; and a special effort to collect back taxes. In addition, the introduction of a system of installment payments of corporate tax will facilitate the monitoring of enterprises subject to this tax.  

22. A total current expenditure level of CFAF 73 billion is in line with the original program objectives, given the additional expenditure on demobilization and the organization of elections. The budget appropriations for the priority social sectors of education and health were increased, as provided for in the program, by 20 percent in real terms, while the appropriations for the other ministries have remained unchanged in nominal terms. It should be noted, however, that several expenditure items are at variance with the original program objectives. To begin with, primary current expenditure is now estimated at CFAF 59.l billion, or CFAF 2.7 billion more than in the program. This increase reflects a larger wage bill (CFAF 2.3 billion), as well as slightly higher transfers (CFAF O.4 billion) required by various government agencies, including those in charge of the democratization process. Aware of the difficulties encountered in l995 in controlling expenditure, the authorities have imposed a ceiling of CFAF 29.0 billion on current primary spending (excluding demobilization and election costs) in the first half of l996. To the extent that the final results of the civil service census generate wage bill savings, this ceiling will be lowered by the amount of savings realized. In no case will the ceiling be raised. There will be no general wage increase granted in l996. Second, the authorities have earmarked CFAF 1.5 billion for election costs, to ensure that the three elections scheduled for 1996 are conducted properly. Finally, estimated interest expenditure has been lowered by nearly CFAF 5 billion from the amount specified in the program, reflecting a more precise estimation of these obligations.  

23. The staff of the Ministry of Planning will be responsible for the follow-up, on a quarterly basis for the financial side, and on a semi-annual basis for the physical side, of the public investment operations included in the Public Investment Program (PIP), as well as the publication of special reports, possibly including sectoral analyses. The PIP, which has been reviewed by the World Bank, calls for spending of some CFAF ll7 billion in 1996, of which 32 percent is allocated to infrastructure and transport, 25 percent to agricultural, and 25 percent to human resource development.  

24. The Government is aware of the need to contain the civil service wage bill, given the relatively modest level of revenue and the need to strengthen the social sectors and increase the budgetary contribution to the investment program. To that end, the Government will rely on the final  

results of the civil service census to completely clean up the payroll records and initiate a comprehensive civil service reform. The Government plans to significantly improve wage bill management with the introduction (as soon as possible) of a single, computerized employee record, to be used both by the civil service and by payroll officials. The Government intends also to re-examine, in light of the recommendations of the study on the civil service reform, its wage policy with a view to moderating wage growth and eliminating automatic advancement and promotions, which should be determined by performance.  

25. The authorities expect to achieve a current primary surplus at end-June of CFAF l.9 billion, resulting from revenue in the amount of CFAF 30.9 billion and current primary expenditure of CFAF 29.0 billion, in the first six months of the fiscal year. With other current expenditure and local investment commitments estimated at CFAF 7.4 billion and CFAF 0.5 billion, respectively, and a need to reduce arrears by CFAF 37.8 billion in accordance with program objectives, financing requirements for the first half of 1996 are estimated at CFAF 43.4 billion, although CFAF 15 billion of this amount is covered by disbursements of external assistance already obtained. A financing requirement of CFAF 28.4 billion will thus remain at end-June. It will be covered by receipt of aid from bilateral and multilateral donors and by rescheduling of debt. For the second half of 1996, the financing requirement is estimated at CFAF ll.5 billion, and will be discussed at a donors' round table in the second half of the year.  

26. The Government, mindful of the current weaknesses in budgetary programming, execution, and monitoring, has decided to strengthen both the Budget Directorate of the Ministry of Finance and the External Assistance Programming Directorate (DPAE) of the Ministry of Planning, with a view to improving the use of public resources through better preparation of the General Budget and the three-year Public Investment Program (PIP). The main objectives are to strengthen the expenditure execution and monitoring system, examine work methods and the organization of human and physical resources within departments, and train personnel to help them enhance their performance.  

27. Accordingly, the authorities decided that the Budget Directorate would be given the responsibility, effective end-March 1996, of coordinating the preparation of quarterly reports on the execution of the budget, with a particular focus on monitoring expenditure commitments, including sectoral allocation. Efforts will also be made to ensure that the monitoring of receipts and disbursements by the Treasury Directorate is reflected in the budget nomenclature. The Government has assigned the Treasury Directorate and the Budget Directorate the task of conducting a thorough inventory of cross-debts between the Government and public enterprises and agencies, including the development of transparent settlement procedures. The ongoing public expenditure review will also identify weakness in expenditure control systems and suggest remedies.  

28. The Government is aware of the precarious financial position of the National Retirement Fund of Chad (CNRT), which is essentially due to the Government's failure to pay the employer's share and to forward contributions withheld from employee wages. The Government is determined to put an end to this situation and to make the necessary payments in l996 to restore the normal functioning of the CNRT. A statement of arrears vis-a-vis the CNRT will be drawn up and added to the stock of Treasury arrears. Besides, in view of the social security system's financial position and the upward trend in expenditure, the Government intends to initiate as soon as possible an in-depth study of the social security system and of the measures needed to ensure the short- and medium-term financial position viability of the CNRT and the National Social Insurance Fund (CNPS). An audit of the two institutions is due to be completed by end-June.  

29. With a view to reducing the weight of military expenditure in the economy, the Government has decided to speed up implementation of the military demobilization program. The first phase of this program, which will bring back (before end-September) the size of the army to 25,000 (a reduction of 7,000 men from end-December 1995), is being financed by the World Bank in the amount of CFAF 5 billion. The Government also plans to establish a unit for the demobilization and re-entry program, under the supervision of the interministerial committee in charge of adjustment. This unit, which will benefit from the technical expertise of foreign partners, will prepare quarterly progress reports and a brief monthly statement on the outlays and the number demobilized.  

30. In the monetary sector, Chad's net external assets should continue to increase in l996 as a result of satisfactory cotton fiber sales and external financing support of the program. To consolidate Chad's positive contribution to the zone's external assets, the regional monetary authorities will strive to keep bank credit within reasonable limits. To this end, they will make active use of the reverse auction facility (appels d'offres negatifs) introduced in February l996, while ensuring that the interest rates reflect market conditions. On the other hand, the monetary authorities hope that Chadian banks will participate in the regional interbank market and that greater competition between banks will spur time deposits growth. The Government will actively seek a new owner for BTCD, as it is currently the only shareholder. As for the sorting of bank notes, the BEAC will ensure that the unsorted stock continues to be reduced.  

31. In l996, the authorities will refrain from introducing or tightening exchange restrictions, introducing multiple exchange rate practices, concluding bilateral agreements incompatible with Article VIII of the Fund's Articles of Agreement, and introducing new import restrictions for balance of payments purposes. The limits on foreign currency allowances for travel purposes will be applied nonrestrictively, and all bona fide requests in excess of the limits will be approved without undue delay. Moreover, to simplify and unify the exchange regulations, the authorities will work with BEAC members toward early conclusion of the convention under discussion concerning the harmonization of exchange regulations within the BEAC zone, insofar as its provisions are compatible with Article VIII of the Fund's  

Articles of Agreement. They also intend, in concert with the other BEAC members, to move toward the acceptance of the obligations of Article VIII, Sections 2, 3, and 4, as soon as possible. With foreign trade statistics currently being compiled by the Customs Directorate (data collection at the border), the Statistics Directorate, and the BEAC, the authorities will undertake before end-June l996 a study on ways to improve coordination and speed up collection and compilation of these statistics.  

32. The authorities are aware that the total elimination of external arrears must be a key objective of the program and will therefore give it priority in the use of untied external aid. In April 1996, the authorities concluded all the bilateral agreements with Paris Club creditors and made all payments due under the Paris Club terms of reference of February 28, 1995. The authorities intend to clear all payments arrears related to external debt service before the end of the first program year, including through an additional rescheduling by the Paris Club. They will also attempt to conclude debt relief agreements as quickly as possible with nonParis Club creditors, in order to reschedule the corresponding arrears and current obligations due. They intend to pay all nonrescheduled obligations on a timely basis. The authorities wish to establish an accurate and regularly updated repayment schedule and to this end are receiving World Bank assistance to strengthen the external debt department. In the event of a new Paris Club rescheduling agreement, they will ensure that bilateral agreements with the countries concerned are concluded within the allotted period of time.  

33. In the cotton sector, the authorities wish to have cotton industry costs and profits shared among all participants (COTONTCHAD, the Government, producers). Accordingly, COTONTCHAD will, no later than the start of cottonseed collection for the l996/97 crop year, establish a mechanism for determining producer prices, including (a) a price at the start of the crop year reflecting exchange rates and international price trends in the cotton market, and (b) the remainder in the following year, based on CoToNTCHAD's financial performance, taking into account the company's productivity gains. The system considered most appropriate, on the basis of these criteria, will be the focus of a special study to be completed before end-June l996. A producer premium (calculated on the basis of performance in FY 1995/96) will be added to the producer price of the 1996/97 crop year. For crop year 1996/97, COTONTCHAD will include taxes and customs duties in the prices of inputs, in accordance with the UDEAC tariff system. In April l996, producer prices will be set, in concert with the Fund and the World Bank, within a range of CFAF l50-l60 per kilo, which may, if necessary, be revised as a result of implementation of the new producer price mechanism. The authorities will also conduct studies in l996 on the development of human resources of COTONTCHAD, improvement of its organizational structure, and the possibility of gradual government divestiture from the company.  

34. In the sugar sector, the authorities intend to eliminate the sugar import monopoly, effective January 1, l997. Before end-June l996, they will conduct a study on the various alternatives for government withdrawal from SONASUT.  

35. The Government has undertaken a diagnostic study of the electricity sector, the results of which are due in September 1996. The purpose of this study is to assess the technical and financial conditions for the Electricity Company (STEE) to achieve operating equilibrium, and to make recommendations on the appropriate ways of restructuring the company. The Government will also examine with STEE the payment modalities for the clearance of cross-debts.  

36. The Government will continue to attach special importance to the_social dimension of the development process, with particular attention to the components of each public investment project that are intended to support the most disadvantaged groups and to the expansion of the labor-intensive public works included in the PADS and ATETIP projects. The objectives of the latter are to construct waste water drainage systems, improve city lighting and household waste collection, and drill more wells to provide drinking water in rural areas.  

37. The Government has decided to expand its public enterprise privatization program by extending it to five additional national enterprises, namely, the Chadian Quarry Operation Company (SOTEC), the Pharmaceutical Industrial Company of Chad (SIPT), the National Building and Public Works Laboratory (LNBTP), the National Road Maintenance Company (SNER), and the Inter-Ministerial Research and Projects Bureau (BIEP). Invitations to bid for SOTEC, BIEP, and LNBTP have already been issued. SIPT has, de facto, been privatized. The invitation to bid for the remaining company will be issued before end-June l996. In the area of legal reform, the treaty on the Organization for the Harmonization_of Business Law in Africa (OHADA) was approved by Parliament on April l3, 1996.

At the beginning of the Bank's support of structural adjustment in Chad, a Financial Rehabilitation Credit (FRC) was approved in July 1988. The Bank's medium-term goal is to work with Chad toward an approach of managing development in which external assistance is pooled in support of an integrated national program which is adequately poverty-focused. The foundation for this is being laid in an interim strategy for l996-98, which seeks to help Chad restore minimum critical public sector capacity; focus public expenditures on poverty reduction; and promote sustainable use of natural resources.  

The Bank's strategy for Chad supports investments in the productive sectors (agriculture, livestock, and energy) as well as transport infrastructure and the social sectors (health, education). In l994, particular emphasis has been given to the support of the devaluation with a $\mathtt{U S S20}$ million Economic Recovery Credit (ERC) and the development of human resources and the strengthening of economic management (GEF). # C. Activities Under _Preparation  

In l996, the World Bank is expanding its technical assistance program (Capacity Building Project for Economic Management). A second structural adjustment credit (SAC) is scheduled once the first SAC is satisfactorily implemented and the conditions for the second one are met. The SAC operations accompany the ESAF-supported program of the Fund. A public expenditure review is to be concluded before end-l996. Finally, support to the Chad/Cameroon Petroleum Pipeline Project is under consideration.  

# 1. Introduction  

This appendix develops a medium-term balance of payments scenario to assess the debt sustainability of Chad. In the baseline scenario, assuming conservative GDP and export growth projections and fairly ambitious assumptions on the contracting of external loans, Chad's indebtedness remains relatively low through 20l5, and appears sustainable. The alternative scenarios for lower exports earnings and less concessional financing demonstrate, however, the risks in the outlook and the need to rely on highly concessional financing. World Bank staff agree with this debt sustainability analysis.  

# 2. Existing debt burden  

At end-l995, Chad's official public or publicly guaranteed debt was estimated at CFAF 400.9 billion, including some CFAF 2l billion in arrears (Table 1). The debt stock consisted overwhelmingly of multilateral loans C $84$ percent of the outstanding debt), of which those from the World Bank (IDA), the African Development Fund, and the Fund represented, all together, 92 percent. In nominal terms, the debt stock was estimated at 75.l percent of GDP, and was equivalent to about three times the value of exports of goods and nonfactor services. The net present value of Chad's external public debt was slightly higher than one and a half times the value of exports of goods and nonfactor services, reflecting the high average concessionality of the debt stock and, in particular, that a major component was IDA financing.  

The debt service ratio (in percent of exports of goods and nonfactor services) was in recent years in the range of l0-l5 percent, and was estimated at some 1l percent in 1995. Substantial external public arrears, however, were built up, amounting to about half of government revenue in 1995. Most of the arrears at end-l995 were owed to multilateral creditors, in particular, the European Investment Bank (CFAF 4.4 billion) and the BADEA (CFAF 3.1 billion), as well as to Paris Club creditors, including the nonpayment of interest due on the February l995 Paris Club rescheduling (CFAF 1.8 billion). There were no arrears to the Fund or the World Bank. It is expected that the Government will clear its external current arrears by the end of the first year of the ESAF-supported program in June l996. This will be carried out by payments in cash of the  

nonreschedulable debt, l/ by an expected new Paris Club rescheduling agreement for the pre-cutoff previously rescheduled debt, and by the granting of rescheduling terms by the main non-Paris Club creditors.  

In February l995, the Paris Club creditors granted Chad debt relief on arrears and current service due on its pre-cutoff previously rescheduled debt; the agreement (so-called terms of reference) provided for a rescheduling on Naples terms (net present value reduction of 67 percent) for the nonpreviously rescheduled pre-cutoff debt and a standard rescheduling (without topping up) for the pre-cutoff, previously rescheduled debt. 2/ The Paris Club pre-cutoff debt had already been fully rescheduled on Toronto terms in October l989; however, the outstanding stock rescheduled in 1989 represented only 3 percent of the total outstanding debt stock at end-1995.  

# 3. Medium-term projections  

The macroeconomic framework for the baseline scenario (l996-2015) contains relatively conservative assumptions (Table 2). Real GDP growth is projected to fluctuate between 5 percent and 6 percent until 2000, and to stabilize at 5.5 percent during the following 15 years; population growth is projected to be 2.5 percent during the entire period. The ratio of investment to GDP rises in this scenario from l3.8 percent in l995 to l8.5 percent in 2004 and then remains stable in the later years. Initially, the predominant share of investment is public (l3 percent of GDP as against 2.5 percent of GDP in private investment), but this reverses over the course of the scenario to a public investment level of 7.2 percent of GDP and a private level of 1l.3 percent in 20l5. With the government primary balance improving from a deficit of 2.3 percent of GDP in l995 to a surplus of 5.4 percent in 2015, increasing resources will be made available domestically to finance public investment. As a result, net external public financing needs are projected to decrease from 6 percent of GDP to 1.6 percent during the forecast period. Domestic savings within the same period will increase from 1.6 percent of GDP to 14.8 percent.  

Annual export volume growth is projected to fluctuate between 8.9 percent and 5.5 percent during the period l996-200l, and to remain steady at 5 percent in the following years. It will be mainly driven in the earlier period by the assumption of high export cotton growth (7.9 percent on average), and in the latter period by export diversification (notably livestock and by-products, and agricultural foodstuffs), while cotton exports are assumed to grow at a slower pace of 3 percent a year in the outer years. Import volume growth is projected to slow from ll.9 percent in 1996 to 4.5 percent in 2002 and beyond, reflecting the leveling off of  

import-intensive investment both in the cotton sector (input and ginneries capacity) and in public infrastructure.  

Net official transfers are assumed to be driven by the demand for technical assistance in the government sector. External project grants and loans in the initial years are in conformity with current projections of the public investment program. External project loans are forecast to increase from CFAF 29.4 billion in 1996 to CFAF 56.7 billion in 1998, reflecting a gradual shift from project grants to project loans. From l999 onward, project loans are projected to rise at a slower pace to reach CFAF 76.3 billion in 20l5. Debt service on new borrowing is based on the continued inflow of external project loans assumed to be financed on concessional terms.  

# 4. Baseline scenario  

In the baseline scenario, the current account (excluding official transfers) remains in deficit through 20l5, but improves from l6.3 percent of GDP in l996 to 3.9 percent by the end of period (Table 3). The overall balance turns positive in 2003 and allows for net positive official reserves accumulation through 20l5. It is expected that the financing gaps in the previous years will be covered by debt relief and new exceptional financing (no program loans/grants are assumed beyond l998).  

The baseline scenario assumes that Paris Club creditors will grant flow reschedulings on Naples terms of the pre-cutoff debt, which had previously been rescheduled on Toronto terms, for the three-year period of Chad's ESAFsupported arrangement (Table 4). It is then projected that a stylized stock-of-debt operation will take place in l999, in support of a possible successor ESAF arrangement. l/ As the debt stock involved is small (some CFAF l0.6 billion), the debt relief will cover only a minor part of the financing gap in l999 and beyond; additional exceptional financing will be therefore needed and could be arranged within the framework of a possible successor ESAF arrangement.  

Under the above-noted assumptions, Chad's ability to service its external debt appears encouraging (Table 5). Total scheduled debt service (after debt relief) is projected to decrease as a percent of exports of goods and nonfactor services from 8.8 percent in l996 (excluding the repayments of arrears) to 7.2 percent in 2000, and thereafter to vary within the range of 7-9.5 percent through 20l5. The fluctuations are mainly a result of repayments to the Fund and the beginning of payments of the  

amortization of new borrowing at the end of the grace period. Reflecting the continued inflow of new borrowing, as well as the high degree of concessionality on this borrowing, the net present value of debt stock remains stable as a percent of exports of goods and services between 150 percent and l60 percent during the forecast period. Chad has an acute need to increase the stock of human capital and public infrastructure. This baseline analysis shows that, given Chad's initial low level of indebtedness, the country can largely finance its investment needs by relying on external borrowing while remaining able to meet its debt service and maintaining its overall level of indebtedness. l/  

# 5. Sensitivity analysis  

The medium-term outlook is sensitive to the assumptions made about key external variables, including export commodity prices and the concessionality of new external financing. An attempt has been made to evaluate the effect on Chad's debt-servicing capacity of possible changes in these assumptions. For this purpose, the financing gaps that result from lower exports or higher debt service are assumed to be covered with nonconcessional lending. 2/  

A scenario (Table 6) was developed based on a fall of the world price of cotton, Chad's predominant export, to a level of 58 cents per pound--the historically low world price experienced in l992-93--from l997 onward; this would be equivalent to a drop by about 30 percent as compared with the projected prices. 3/ In this scenario, the improvement of the current account deficit is slower; the current account deficit (excluding official transfers) would be equivalent to 7 percent of GDP in 20l5, as compared with 3.9 percent in the baseline scenario, and yearly financing gaps would average CFAF 38.5 billion. Total debt service would be equivalent to 26.3 percent of exports of goods and nonfactor services, 17 points higher than in the baseline scenario. The net present value of the debt stock

# 6. Oil exploitation scenario  

Although Chad's potential oil resources were identified in the late 1970s, exploitation of the country's main oil fields has not yet started. However, it now appears likely that exploitation could begin in the next 5-10 years. Because negotiations are under way and the exact terms of the projects are not yet finalized, a detailed analysis of the macroeconomic impact cannot be done. However, the authorities indicated the broad lines of their intentions, providing enough information to give an overview of the effects of oil exploitation on Chad's external trade and indebtedness (Table 8).  

The authorities intend to exploit Chad's two main oil fields for different purposes. Oil from the Sedigui site (in the area of Lake Chad) will be refined in Chad (some 3,O0o barrels a day on average) and used to cover a large part of domestic demand in petroleum products, thereby substantially reducing the oil import bill. At the Doba site (in the southeast of the country), crude oil production will be used only for export through a pipeline across Cameroon to a sea port. Full export capacity (about 250,000 barrels a day) is expected to be reached within 15 years. The initial investment period is assumed to last three and a half years for the Doba project and 18 months for the Sedigui project. The whole process of oil production and refinement in Chad is expected to be operated by consortiums in which foreign private oil companies are the major shareholders; the financing of initial investments will be covered mainly by foreign direct investment (private equities and loans). It is therefore assumed that new government external borrowing will be limited to the financing of its minority equity stake in the companies exploiting both  

projects. 1/ The authorities indicated that, in current circumstances, the Sedigui project may start operations at the earliest in early l999, and that of Doba in mid-2000. Because negotiations on the terms of the partnership are ongoing, no assumptions can be made on the impact of these projects on factor income and on the current account balance.  

Under the above-noted assumptions, the trade balance is projected to move from substantial deficits in the investment stage of the projects (1997-99) to a surplus equivalent to about 22 percent of GDP in 200l, and to decline thereafter to l4.2 percent of GDP in 20l5. Export volume growth is forecast to increase by 6.5-7 percent from 2002 onward, driven by an average oil export volume growth of 8.5 percent. Import volume growth reflects the assumed oil investment patterns through 2004, and is projected to rise thereafter at a pace of 7 percent, 2.5 points higher than the baseline scenario as a result of a change in consumption patterns. Real GDP growth, incorporating the additional value added stemming from oil production and a higher level of local investment, is assumed to accelerate sharply at the outset of oil production and to remain steady from 2005 onward, at 5.5 percent. At this stage, the level of real GDP would be on average about 22 percent higher than the baseline real GDP. 2/  

Under this scenario, total debt service and the net present value of the debt stock as a percent of goods and nonfactor services decline continuously, to reach respective levels of 4.2 percent and 64.8 percent in 2015. Because the oil projects are overwhelmingly financed by private resources, the flow of oil export proceeds fully offsets the slightly higher public debt service resulting from the small public share of project financing.

Chad's statistical system is weak and suffers from a shortage of financial and human resources. The compilation of statistics is decentralized at various ministries, but there is a coordinating office of about 30 people, of whom l0 are currently working on the dissemination of the results of the most recent population crisis. The others work on the consumer price index, national accounts, and balance of payments.  

# 1. Real sector  

There are substantial problems with the data sources for the national accounts and the CPI. On the production side, all the available indices seem to be obsolete: the output volume indices date back to l977, and the producer price indices are based on l983 weights. Domestic demand estimates appear to suffer from lack of underlying statistical surveys, particularly with regard to private consumption and private investment, and from methodological problems with regard to the evaluation of fixed public investment. The CPI is also obsolete, with its weights dating back to 1972.  

# 2. Public_finance  

The consolidated government operations table does not include the social security agencies and various public funds that can be considered as part of the Government. Efforts were made in $1994-95$ to improve recording procedures; revenue is now recorded primarily on an accrual basis, while expenditure is accounted on a commitment basis. In the context of the second annual program, the authorities intend to broaden the current scope of coverage of government operations and the necessary data for that purpose and put in place appropriate recording procedures. The authorities are making efforts to improve the quality and timeliness of data and intend to resume as soon as possible reporting of data to IFS and GFSY. Data have not been reported for publication in GFsY since 1991.  

# 3. Monetary sector  

Monetary data are hampered by the large number of unsorted notes of the Banque des Etats de l'Afrique Centrale (BEAC) at the national agency of the Central Bank, equivalent to more than 50 percent of official broad money. Monetary data also suffer from methodological problems with regard to the valuation of the other items (net), which induce erratic and sharp movements in this position and affect the net domestic assets aggregate. The staff renewed its recommendation that a sampling system for the unsorted bills be put in place with a view to estimating better the net official reserves and the currency circulation. The staff also recommended that close attention be given to the classification and treatment of the other items net.  

Monetary data published in IFS are based on reports sent to the Fund's Statistics Department by the BEAC and, during the past year, have been provided on a regular basis.  

# 4. Balance of payments  

The balance of payments data system is very weak. Data continue to be compiled with long delays and are not often reliable, especially as far as the imports and noncotton exports are concerned. The existing data on informal trade between Chad and its neighboring countries are sketchy and unreliable. A technical assistance mission from STA to strengthen balance of payments data collection and compilation, which has been on request since the second half of l994, is scheduled for the first half of FY 1997.
