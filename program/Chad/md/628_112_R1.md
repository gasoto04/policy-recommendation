# I. Introduction  

The $1994$ Article IV consultation discussions with Chad were conducted in N'Djamena during the period November 5-20, 1994. l/ The mission met with the Prime Minister, the Minister of Finance and Computer Services, the Minister of Plan and Cooperation and President of the High Interministerial Committee; members of the High Interministerial Committee; and senior officials from various ministries and agencies. The mission also held discussions with the National Director of the Bank of Central African States (BEAC) $\ Y$ and other officials of the BEAC, and with representatives of the trade unions and the employers' association. The head of the mission was received by the President of the Republic. The mission also was to conduct discussions on the first review under the stand-by arrangement approved by the Fund on March 23, 1994, but these could not be concluded.  

Chad is on a l2-month consultation cycle and continues to avail itself of the transitional arrangements of Article XIV. 3/ The last Article IV consultation was concluded on October 3, 1993 (EBM/93/l4l). At that time, Executive Directors observed that recurring civil wars had greatly weakened general administration, revenue collection, and expenditure control. They hoped that the political framework that had been established by the recent national conference could be used to restore the conditions for orderly government. In particular, Directors urged accelerated implementation of the demobilization program for the army and stressed the need for regaining control over the fiscal situation. Directors also encouraged the authorities to pursue vigorous structural reforms in a number of areas, including the public enterprise sector. Finally, noting the continued deterioration in the terms of trade and the loss of competitiveness, Directors emphasized the need to broaden the adjustment strategy through exchange rate action.  

Following the devaluation of the CFA franc, effective January 12, 1994, the authorities adopted an economic adjustment program covering the period January-December l994. In support of this program, the Fund approved a stand-by arrangement in an amount equivalent to SDR l6.52 million (40 percent of quota). The principal objectives of the program were to ensure that the price and incentive effects of the devaluation would be accompanied by appropriate measures to achieve a lasting improvement in competitiveness and growth. Because it was also essential to break the vicious circle of  

structural disintegration of public administration and secular decline of private sector activity, a significant injection of funds was needed to reduce domestic arrears to suppliers. Following Board approval of the stand-by arrangement, Chad made an initial purchase of SDR l0.325 million, or the equivalent of the first credit tranche. A part of the proceeds from this purchase was placed into an account for meeting debt service obligations to the Fund in l994. No further purchases have been made since.  

With the convening of a national conference in early l993, Chad embarked upon a process of democratization, which resulted in the adoption of a charter for the transition Government and High Transition Council (CST - the current legislative body). Initially, presidential, legislative, and municipal elections were to be held within l8 months from the adoption of the charter. However, delays in setting up parties and agreeing on electoral legislation, as well as in drawing up a new constitution, have caused the elections to be postponed to April 1995.  

II. Economic Developments and Performance Under the Stand-By Arrangement  

Economic and financial developments since early l994 have been mixed. On the positive side, the devaluation of the CFA franc and the accompanying measures have given a strong boost to the traded goods sectors. Cotton production is expected to increase by more than 40 percent during the 1994/95 season. Exports of livestock have surged, reaching their level in 1993 as early as June l994; production and exports of arabic gum are expanding rapidly; and there are signs of an increase in demand for domestic import substitutes. In addition, ample rainfalls since mid-l994 have helped improve the supply of domestic foodstuffs and reverse the 1993/94 shortfalls in agricultural production. Together, these factors suggest a recovery of real GDP in l994, to around 4 percent, much higher than the 1.4 percent that had been projected under the program (Appendix III, Table l). 1/  

Strong export growth, in conjunction with the slowing of imports triggered by the income effect of the devaluation on import demand and temporary transit problems in northern Cameroon caused the current account deficit of the balance of payments (excluding official transfers) to drop below 20 percent of GDP in l994, from more than 26 percent in l993. The balance of payments has been helped further by the mobilization of exceptional external financing and the repatriation of flight capital, so that the net foreign assets of the banking system increased by almost CFAF 43 billion during the first nine months of 1994. This improvement  

mirrored a near doubling of bank liabilities to the private sector l/ and stagnant bank credit growth, following a brief seasonal surge in the first quarter of the year. 2/ As a result, banks fully repaid their earlier recourse to central bank rediscounts and built up sizable reserves in the form of special deposits at the Central Bank. The performance criteria and benchmarks for the expansion of net bank domestic assets were therefore easily observed.  

Against these encouraging developments, a number of factors greatly complicated program implementation, especially during the first half of the year. In particular, the transit problems in the north of Cameroon resulted in a complete stoppage of imports of petroleum products, and a significant slowdown of other, mostly low-cost manufactured imports, from Nigeria. Along with the shortfalls in farm output in the l993/94 agricultural season, these disruptions caused consumer prices to rise by more than can be explained by the devaluation (56 percent by August l994), and the social climate to deteriorate significantly. Since July, however, inflationary pressures have subsided in response to the normalization of import flows and improved domestic food supplies. Price inflation (year-on-year) is now estimated to have declined to about 40 percent by year-end.  

These difficulties were exacerbated by weaknesses in public administration and shortcomings in economic policies in the first half of the year, which had a particularly damaging effect on revenue performance. First, the initial failure of the authorities to adequately explain the program to the civil service and the general public severely hampered program implementation. Second, the absence of mechanisms in government to ensure coherent program implementation resulted in incomplete and, at times, contradictory policy measures. Third, owing to the late adoption of the 1994 budget by the High Transition Council, a number of actions were taken with considerable delays. Finally, program implementation was hampered by absenteeism as well as recurrent and lengthy strikes in the civil service, which had been paid very irregularly in l993. (Average salary arrears amounted to about six months of pay at end-l993 and increased by another three months in $1994$ , of which 2 l/2 months in the first half).  

In this setting, a number of performance criteria for end-June 1994, and of benchmarks for end-September l994, were not met (Appendix III, Table 2). The principal slippages occurred in government revenue  

collections, which, at less than CFAF 10 billion $(2~1/4$ percent of GDP), fell short of the June benchmark by CFAF 7 billion. Although the situation improved considerably during the third quarter, when the authorities took a number of corrective measures (see below), the shortfalls experienced during the first semester could not be made up. The performance criteria for the settlement of domestic and external payments arrears thus also were not observed, while the objective of restoring regularity in payments of wages and salaries to civil servants remained elusive. l/ The cash situation was aggravated further by shortfalls in program financing, which by endSeptember $1994$ had reached only 48 percent of the programmed amount, in part because of slippages in program implementation and/or failures to meet program loan conditionalities. 2/  

Overall, the revenue shortfall is estimated at $31/4$ percent of GDP in 1994, as against a prospective shortfall of $43/4$ percent on an annualized first-semester basis. To contain the fiscal deficit, the authorities drastically curtailed spending on goods and services, transfer payments, and investment. They also accumulated additional wage and salary arrears (approximately three months of pay). In the event, the current primary deficit (commitment basis) is estimated to have been held to 4.4 percent of GDP in 1994, compared with 5 percent in l993, and a program target of 3.5 percent. Similarly, the overall fiscal deficit (commitment basis, including grants) was held to 1l.6 percent of GDP, compared with 10.l percent in l993 and an objective of 1l.2 percent. These deviations, together with the shortfall in external program assistance, led to a further accumulation of arrears (CFAF 6.5 billion through September) on external debt service.  

# III. Report on the Discussions  

Given the magnitude of the slippages in the first half of 1994, the discussions focused on the impact of the corrective measures that had been implemented after the June l994 staff visit, and on the supplementary action that would be needed to reach objectives broadly similar to those of the initial program. They also encompassed structural policy measures to improve the functioning of the administration and the economy, and permanently increase revenue and lower government claims on resources. To establish a track record of performance that could lay the basis for later discussions on a program supported by the enhanced structural adjustment facility (ESAF), a staff-monitored program, which calls for a strengthening of fiscal adjustment and a considerable reinforcement of structural policy measures relative to the initial program, was developed for the nine-month period through end-June 1995.  

The authorities' policy framework and objectives remain broadly unchanged for the medium term. Economic growth is expected initially to benefit from a continued strong expansion of exports, to be supported later by a resumption of consumption and investment. Average growth of real GDP during l995-97 is projected to reach 5 percent, thus ensuring a satisfactory increase in per capita income. For inflation, the objective is to return to low single-digit levels as soon as possible, consistent with the maintenance of a fixed peg for the currency and external competitiveness. Regarding the fiscal and external positions, the principal objective is to achieve viability over a realistic period of time, where the latter will be a function of the as yet unknown level of stable external financing in the context of an ESAF-supported program.  

# 1. Program ownership and implementation  

The authorities shared the concerns of the staff about the policy slippages that had occurred at the start of the program. However, they alsc noted that the difficult political environment, as well as a series of adverse external factors, had played a role. The diverse coalition that constituted the transition Government had made consensus building and program implementation difficult, and existing structures of command and responsibility had been ill adapted to a concerted and decisive adjustment policy. To remedy the situation, a number of measures had been taken since the middle of $1994$ to strengthen awareness and ownership of the program, streamline administration, and ensure a timely implementation of policy measures. In particular, a system of high-level committees had been established in June l994 to focus on the "Preparation, Negotiation, and Monitoring of Structural Adjustment Programs." 1/ Since end-June 1994, these committees had held regular meetings, and reports on economic developments and program implementation had been issued to the Fund and World Bank staffs and, in an effort to increase transparency, to Chad's principal donors.  

Changes had also been introduced in the organization and staffing of a number of key ministries, including the Ministry of Finance and its revenue departments. First, to improve the monitoring and servicing of Chad's external debt, the former Autonomous Amortization Agency (CAA) had been integrated into the Ministry of Finance and made into a Department of Debt and Investment (DDI). Technical assistance in debt management had also been requested, for which World Bank financing is available. Second, a number of high-ranking officials, whose performance had been wanting, had been replaced. Finally, all employees had been put on notice that their continued employment would depend on satisfactory job performance.  

Reviewing developments in quantitative performance criteria and benchmarks since June l994 (Appendix III, Table 2) as well as other elements of program implementation, especially on structural reforms and price policy, the authorities felt that they had made important strides in implementing their program (Appendix III, Table 3).  

# 2. Fiscal policy  

The authorities agreed that the rehabilitation of the public finances was crucial to the restoration of confidence and credibility, and to lasting progress toward financial viability and steady economic growth. It was therefore their intention to reduce the current primary deficit to about 1 1/2 percent in l995 (Appendix III, Table 4). 1/ To achieve this objective, and yet restore an adequate level of spending on wages and on goods and services so as to allow the administration to function normally, government revenue (excluding grants) would need to recover from 6 1/3 percent of GDP in l994 to more than 9 percent of GDP. 2/ This would be about l percentage point of GDP above collections in the second half of l994 at an annual rate. An ad hoc committee on revenue collection, chaired by the Head of State, had been set up to monitor and strengthen revenue performance, notably through the continued strengthening of tax administration and a forceful collection of back taxes.  

Moreover, in the context of UDEAC 3/ indirect tax and customs tariff reform, excise taxes and a general turnover tax (TCA) would be introduced, effective January 1, l995. The TCA would replace the single tax (taxe unigue), the domestic production and consumption taxes, the domestic turnover tax, the turnover tax on imports, the transactions tax, and the proportional stamp tax. In recognition of the need to generate additional revenue, the normal rate for the TCA would be set at l5 percent, and the reduced rate at 5 percent. Moreover, expiring bilateral tax preference agreements with private operators (conventions d'établissement) had not beer renewed, and the few remaining ones would be renegotiated before end-1995.  

Particular attention would be given to reinforcing the customs administration. Interference from paramilitary forces in customs operations had been stopped, and all discretionary exemptions from customs duties had been prohibited. In addition, technical assistance had been obtained in two areas: (a) an expert from the Fund's panel of fiscal experts had taken up his duties in October l994, for an initial period of one year; l/ and (b) a private specialized company with international experience (COTECNA) had been contracted to improve the collection of customs duties on petroleum products. Together, all revenue measures taken since July l994 had increased the average monthly tax collection from CFAF 1.6 billion in the first half of the year to CFAF 2.2 billion in the third quarter, and an estimated CFAF 3.4 billion in the last quarter of 1994.  

Even with these measures, the scope for a rapid increase in tax receipts would remain narrow, in part because of physical and financing constraints in the revenue departments. However, an average level of monthly revenue of CFAF 3.8 billion appeared to be feasible in l995. At the same time, uncertainties about external financing availability would continue to loom large. Government spending therefore would remain severely constrained in l995, and the level of current spending (commitment basis) was targeted to decline to about 12 percent of GDP, from l2.5 percent in 1994, and an average of about 15 percent during the period l990-93. This reduction would require the establishment of clear spending priorities.  

The growth in the Government's wage bill would be strictly limited in 1995. At the same time renewed efforts would be made to achieve regularity of wage and salary payments, with a view to providing appropriate incentives to work and fostering social peace. In this context, the authorities pointed to the Social Pact of July l, l994, which includes an understanding that the repayment of salary arrears is to be linked to the availability of budgetary resources. The authorities also indicated that the 10 percent salary increase that had been envisaged for October l994 would be postponed. Savings from this measure would be used instead to reduce l994 salary arrears in those ministries that had the "oldest" such arrears. For 1995, the objective would be to keep the wage bill unchanged in real terms, implying an increase in nominal terms of no more than 6 percent. This objective, however, would be compatible with a l0 percent increase in civil service wages and salaries from January 1, l995. The reason was the continued staffing reduction of approximately 1,000 persons, predicated on strict enforcement of retirement rules, elimination of inefficient and ghost employees, and reduction in the number of contractual workers in low priority areas.

Regarding spending on goods and services, all potential savings would be exploited in nonpriority areas. Similarly, transfer payments and subsidies would need to be strictly justified. In particular, subsidies to the power utility (STEE) would be curtailed through continued cost-cutting measures, improvements in collection rates, and appropriate adjustments in prices (see below). Additional efforts would also be made to reduce the need for subsidizing. essential pharmaceuticals through an expansion of local production of generic drugs, as well as improvements in procurement and distribution. Further reductions in payments arrears to suppliers would be circumscribed by the constraints on resources and by indispensable current and capital spending--the latter being limited to the domestic counterpart funds for externally financed investment projects.  

# 3. Monetary and credit policies  

In 1994, the principal objective of monetary policy pursued by the BEAC at the regional level had been the strengthening of confidence in the new exchange rate and the return of flight capital. Interest rates thus had been raised significantly in the immediate aftermath of the devaluation. From midyear onward, when capital inflows accelerated and it appeared that credit conditions could be relaxed without significant pressures on the balance of payments, interest rates had been progressively reduced. l/ Nevertheless, credit demand in Chad had remained weak and banks' liquidity ample through September (Appendix III, Table 5). In the event of an upturn in private credit and import demand, as confidence and activity firm up, the authorities would act in the framework of the BEAC to raise interest rates as needed to contain the growth of domestic credit within limits compatible with external objectives.  

In addition, in an effort to move toward reliance on market forces and the use of indirect instruments of monetary management, the BEAC had introduced a money market in July l994, which consisted of three segments: an interbank market, an auction mechanism for central bank advances, and a refinancing window for medium-term credits. Given the liquidity of the domestic banking system, none of these mechanisms had been used so far in Chad. Regarding the auction technique, the staff pointed out that the current method that controls both the cost and volume of the amounts auctioned off was working at cross purposes with the intention to rely on market-based instruments, and that adaptations would be necessary once experience had been gained. l/ Moreover, the system would need to be made symmetrical and thereby empower the BEAC to restrict banks' lending capacity through open market operations if circumstances so required. Finally, the authorities said they would lend their full support to the regional banking commission (COBAC) in its efforts to strengthen supervision and enforce prudential ratios.  

# 4. External sector policies  

In the external area, the authorities agreed with the staff on the importance of consolidating the incentive effects of the devaluation. 2/ Accordingly, they decided not to proceed with the introduction of an export tax on cotton that had been envisaged to address the revenue shortfall. 3/ Instead, they would continue to rely on the more flexible profit-sharing mechanism that was an important element of the original program. Moreover, remaining export taxes on agricultural products (there are none on other exports) would be removed in connection with the presentation of the 1995 budget.  

The authorities indicated that they did not intend to introduce new exchange or import restrictions. Together with the other BEAC member countries, they would seek the elimination of the requirement to produce certain documentation for the making of current transfers to' France, the Comoros, and the BCEAO 4/ countries that had been introduced by administrative order of the Governor of the BEAC in December l993. They would also advocate lifting the suspension on the repurchase of BEAC bank notes circulating outside its zone of issue. Moreover, they would take whatever steps might still be needed to prepare Chad's acceptance of the obligations of Article VIII, Sections 2, 3, and $4$ as soon as possible. In accordance with the UDEAC reform, they also intended to eliminate existing surtaxes on edible oils, sugar, beer, cigarettes, and soap as soon as possible.  

The authorities observed that, even with the envisaged adjustment, Chad's external debt-servicing capacity would remain limited in the period immediately ahead without a resumption of external support. Nevertheless, they would try to normalize relations with creditors as the fiscal and external positions improved, seek to service external debt in a timely fashion, and settle nonreschedulable current maturities and arrears as a matter of priority. They would also continue efforts to secure the rescheduling of Chad's debt to Paris Club creditors on appropriate terms.  

# 5. Structural policies  

The authorities confirmed their determination to proceed with the demobilization program, so as to reduce the size of the armed forces to 25,000. By end-September 1994, more than 16,000 soldiers had already been demobilized and l,00o others were to follow before the end of the year. Indeed, the objective of a reduction to a 25,00-strong armed force would have been achieved by end-l994, had a majority in the CST voted for a bill that sought to retire some 6,0O soldiers by reducing the age limit for army personnel to 55 years and setting maximum service at 30 years. In line with the results of the new ongoing army census, a new bill would be submitted to the CST, or other policies developed, to reach the desired objective at the latest by end-June 1995.  

The authorities remained committed to price liberalization. After prices for rice and flour had been freed in February l994, only eight product categories remained subject to price control (gasoline, pharmaceutical products, sugar, water, electricity, cigarettes, soap, and edible oils). In November l994, the price freeze on pharmaceutical products had been replaced by a limited intervention scheme that maintains controls on only 60 essential products. Moreover, control over the margins of distributors of pharmaceutical products would be abandoned before end-June 1995. Regarding sugar, retail prices had been increased by 2l percent on average in November l994, obviating the need for subsidies to the sugar company (SONAsUT). A detailed study of sugar distribution in Chad would be carried out before end-March l995, with a view to liberalizing prices and eliminating SONAsUT's import monopoly. Price controls on cigarettes were scheduled to be removed by end-December l994. In the public utilities, electricity tariffs would be increased in two steps of 10 percent each before end-June l995; however, water rates would remain unchanged for social reasons, and considering that no need for subsidization existed at current rates. In the cash crop sector, the producer price for cotton (including a possible premium) would continue to be set on the basis of world market prices and a prudent evaluation of the current and prospective level of profitability of COTONTCHAD.  

Given the poor income situation of wage earners and high rate of inflation, the authorities had come under pressure to adjust wages and salaries. In particular, the unions had demanded a tripling of the guaranteed interprofessional and agricultural minimum wages (SMIG and SMAG), which had not been adjusted in many years. In the event, the Government, after extensive discussions with the trade unions and employers, decided in November l994 to double the SMIG and SMAG to levels already paid by enterprises for several years. This adjustment was therefore not expected to  

materially affect the competitiveness gains that enterprises had realized since the devaluation. In any event, it was the intention of the Government in 1995 to rescind the minimum wage legislation altogether and instead to encourage the social partners to strengthen wage bargaining mechanisms at the sectoral or enterprise level.  

The program of public enterprise divestiture had progressed well in 1994, with three state companies privatized (SAPROVET, MCT, and SIMAT) 1/ and ten others in the process of liquidation. In keeping with the intention to foster private sector-led growth, this program would be advanced further in 1995, according to a timetable agreed upon with the World Bank. Other measures to stimulate private initiative had consisted of the adoption of statutes for the creation of a Chamber of Commerce; the abolition of prior administrative authorizations for the creation of enterprises; a simplification of business licensing procedures; and the removal of the credit distribution tax on banks. The authorities would continue to review existing legislation and administrative regulations, with a view to removing obstacles to the creation of an open and competitive private sector.  

Strengthening health and education would remain the centerpiece of social policy. The number of staff in these sectors had been increased by 90 and 280, respectively, as envisaged in the original program, and recruitment efforts would be financed and buttressed by substantial increases in budgetary appropriations in real terms. High priority would also be given to job creation in the context of public infrastructure programs.  

# 6. Monitoring of economic and structural policies  

To follow performance under the staff-monitored program, the authorities have established quantitative benchmarks for end-December 1994, and end-March and end-June l995, as well as structural benchmarks (Appendix III, Table 7). They hope that satisfactory implementation of the program will help set the stage for discussions on a medium-term program that could be supported under the ESAF.  

# IV. Staff Appraisal  

The devaluation of the CFA franc in January l994 offered Chad a unique opportunity to break out of the vicious circle of disintegrating public administration and declining private sector activity that had gripped the country in recent years. It also gave the authorities a chance to restore confidence in their economic policies in the context of an arrangement with the Fund.  

Performance under the program has been mixed in terms of both policy implementation and economic developments. Adverse external factors, combined with major slippages in program implementation, produced a very disappointing first half of l994. However, there has been a determined effort since July to strengthen program implementation, and economic performance has improved noticeably.  

Commendable as they have been, the efforts nonetheless have proved insufficient to make up the earlier slippages, and a staff-monitored program for the period through end-June l995 has been designed to allow policies to take hold and give Chad an opportunity to establish a credible record of policy implementation. The staff is encouraged by the authorities' commitment to policy reform, as evidenced by the intention to deepen the structural adjustment effort through additional measures and the establishment of high-level committees to improve program implementation and monitoring. Attention must be given in particular to a further strengthening of public administration; to the generation of sufficient domestic revenue to finance current government spending; and to the consolidation of the competitiveness gains stemming from the devaluation.  

A substantial improvement in government revenue performance is crucial to the success of the adjustment effort because it would strengthen confidence in the authorities' determination to keep the budget deficit under control. It is also needed to restore regularity in salary payments, motivate civil servants, and allow the administration to function properly. Finally, a successful effort to mobilize domestic resources is essential to unleash foreign financial support. The revenue objectives set under the staff-monitored program are a minimum, and more ambitious objectives will have to be set once the financing of measures to strengthen the capacity and functioning of revenue departments has been assured in the context of an ESAF-supported program.  

To improve revenue performance, action is needed on both tax policy and administration. A significant simplification of the tax system and increase in yields can be expected from the replacement of the current and complex indirect tax structure by a single turnover tax with two rates at levels sufficiently high to generate adequate additional resources. The full and efficient implementation of this measure must be given a high priority, and care has to be taken to avoid unwarranted exemptions or classifications in the reduced rate category. Similar considerations should be applied to the introduction of excise taxes and their rates. Every effort should also be made to collect back taxes, where appropriate in connection with repayments of government payments arrears.  

Customs operations require special attention. Priority needs to be given to improved coordination between the customs, tax, and treasury departments; the early adoption of a single taxpayer identification number; and reorganization of customs clearance procedures to achieve standardization in treatment and transparency in operations. Moreover, import value assessments have to be improved to ensure that duties reflect true values more closely. Determined efforts also have to be made to control fraud and monitor the justification for exemptions. In this regard, the initiative of the authorities to agree on duty-free import quotas with embassies, nongovernment agencies, and providers of project financing needs to be implemented swiftly.  

Financial constraints demand that public expenditure priorities be clearly established. While the return to regular wage and salary payments is essential, the tight revenue situation appears to leave no scope for a repayment of salary arrears in l995. Moreover, the wage bill should not be allowed to rise in real terms, and thus any increase in wage rates will need to be absorbed by payroll reductions in nonpriority areas. The achievement of the demobilization objective for the army remains a key element of medium-term fiscal consolidation. A renewed effort should therefore be made to pass early retirement legislation for the army, or implement alternative solutions. Similarly, spending on goods and services must be shifted toward primary education and health, as well as strengthening the means of the financial administration. The decision to increase electricity prices is a step in the right direction to lessen the need for transfers and subsidies, and similar initiatives should be considered for other utility charges.  

In the monetary policy area, the staff is encouraged by the authorities' intention to support the broadening of current money market arrangements to include mechanisms for liquidity absorption and advocate a further liberalization of interest rates. Greater reliance on market mechanisms should help in improving credit allocation and in keeping credit growth consistent with Chad's and the BEAC region's external objectives.  

The decision to rescind the minimum wage legislation would represent a significant structural increase in labor market flexibility and should be implemented as planned. The authorities have made great progress in liberalizing prices, and the staff would want to encourage them to eliminate remaining price controls--other than for state monopolies--as soon as practicable. The reform program for the state enterprises is well on its way and should be pursued further, in line with the agenda agreed with the World Bank, with emphasis on privatization. Together, these measures should give the appropriate signals to the private sector and encourage the resumption of investment.  

Given the absence of restrictions on payments and transfers for current international transactions and the authorities' commitment to maintain a liberal exchange system, the staff urges Chad to accept at an early date the obligations of Article VIII, Sections 2, 3, and 4. It also recommends that the authorities further bolster confidence in the CFA franc, working at the level of the BEAC to restore the repurchase of notes exported outside the zone of issue.  

As requested by the authorities, the staff will closely monitor performance under the staff-monitored program for l994/95. The staff believes that continued satisfactory progress under this program would offer a solid basis for the start of discussions on a medium-term program that could be supported by an ESAF arrangement.  

It is recommended that the next Article IV consultation with Chad be held on the standard l2-month cycle.

# VII. Exchange Rate Arrangement  

Chad is a member of the Central Bank of the Central African States (BEAC). The exchange system, common to all members of the BEAC, has been free of restrictions on payments and transfers for current international transactions. Repurchase of the CFA franc bank notes exported outside the BEAC was suspended on August 2, l993. The BEAC common currency is the CFA franc, which is pegged to the French franc. Effective January 12, 1994, the CFA franc was devalued by 50 percent in foreign currency terms, and the exchange rate was adjusted from F 1 = CFAF 50 to F 1 = CFAF 100. On December 30, 1994, the rate of the CFA franc, in terms of SDR, was CFAF 780.44 $\mathbf{\sigma}=\mathbf{\sigma}$ SDR 1.

X. Resident Representative None.  

At the beginning of the Bank's support of structural adjustment in Chad, a Financial Rehabilitation Credit (FRC) was approved in July 1988. The objectives of the program were to re-establish the financial profitability of the cotton sector and to strengthen policy reforms in public finances. However, owing to the deteriorating fiscal performance, the FRC was suspended in July 199l. The residual balance was canceled in December 1992 and the credit was closed.  

The Bank's strategy for Chad supports investments in the productive sectors (agriculture, livestock, and energy) as well as transport infrastructure. In l994, particular emphasis has been given to the support of the devaluation with a $\mathtt{U S S20}$ million Economic Recovery Credit (ERC) and the development of human resources and the strengthening of economic management (GEF).

# 1. Outstanding statistical issues  

# a. Real sector  

Chad's statistical base is weak. No information on real sector statistics has been reported directly to STA in recent years. A few real sector data have been updated in IFS using the publication of the "Banque des Etats de l'Afrique Centrale" or UN publications. Despite this indirect updating, all real sector data are lacking for recent years.  

b. Government finance  

The STA data base covers data on central government operations for the period 1986 through 1991.  

# C Balance of payments  

Balance of Payments data are issued with lengthy delays. So far, STA has not yet received BoP data for l992. Moreover, some important coverage weaknesses were identified by IMF staff, concerning the current account as well as the financial account. The Area Department proposed a technical assistance mission in balance of payments in the RAP for FY95.
