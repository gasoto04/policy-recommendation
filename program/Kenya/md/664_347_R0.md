# Executive Summary  

Kenya's socioeconomic conditions deteriorated significantly in the 1990s because of stop-go macroeconomic policies, slow structural reform, and pervasive governance problems that resulted in bouts of financial instability, a rapid buildup of short-term debt, and high real interest rates. This together with increases of other costs of doing business--because of corruption, poor infrastructure, stifling government regulation and deteriorating security-reduced the level and quality of investment, and as a consequence economic growth and employment generation. The poor macroeconomic performance combined with inadequate targeting of government outlays have also resulted in an increase in poverty and a deterioration of social indicators.  

Since early 1998, the government of Kenya has been taking steps to address some of the causes of financial instability and low growth. The fiscal deficit (on a commitment basis and before grants) has been gradually reduced and is estimated to be 0.6 percent of GDP in 1999/2oo0. Monetary policy has been generally conservative. The government has been preparing the ground for key structural reforms in the areas of privatization and public service, and it has taken important steps to address governance problems since mid-1999. These measures have helped increase the confidence of the international community in the government's commitment in this area. However, investor confidence has been slow to turn around, and real GDP growth declined from 1.8 percent in 1998 to 1.4 percent in 1999.  

The government's medium-term policy framework (2000/01-2002/03) aims at removing constraints to sustainable economic growth and poverty reduction, as outlined in the Interim-Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper (I-PRSP). The strategy, which is closely linked to the recently initiated medium-term expenditure framework (MTEF), was worked out in consultation with stakeholders and is expected to be fully developed by May 2001, in the context of the fullPRSP. Despite the authorities' efforts, the current MTEF is very preliminary, and major expenditure reallocations toward priority sectors will need to be defined, in close consultation with stakeholders (especially the poor), in the full PRSP and subsequently incorporated in the 2001/02 MTEF. The planned public service reform and the envisaged reductions in the debt service burden are expected to provide room for additional resources to be allocated to poverty reduction programs over the medium term. In the meantime, the authorities agreed with the World Bank staff that the fiscal program for 2000/Ol should focus on a few key poverty reduction measures and some intraministerial reallocations aimed at improving the targeting of existing expenditure.  

Under the reform program, the authorities are committed to consolidating progress in the governance area by implementing a comprehensive set of measures, covering areas such as (i) enhancing accountability; (ii) strengthening control bodies; (ii) improving budget planning and execution; (iv) changing incentive mechanisms; and (v) removing rentseeking opportunities. The authorities have recently implemented a number of measures as required by their plan, including the submission to parliament of a bill containing the code of conduct for public officials, which, inter alia, requires the declaration of income, assets, and liabilities; the provision of adequate support for independent oversight agencies; and the adoption of important steps to strengthen expenditure management. However, the authorities have undertaken to strengthen the above-mentioned bill at the time of its second reading by parliament.  

The authorities are committed to maintaining macroeconomic stability over the medium term. Reductions in the fiscal deficit (after an initial increase) and the domestic debt burden will be aimed at sustainably reducing real interest rates and building confidence in the government's fiscal prudence. These efforts willbe accompanied by tax reforms aimed at reducing the tax burden (particularly to the poor), improving tax administration, as well as at strengthening expenditure management. Monetary policy will be geared to keeping inflation low, and the exchange rate will remain market determined. The authorities plan to strengthen further banking supervision, privatize the Kenya Commercial Bank, and restructure the National Bank of Kenya to prepare it for privatization. They have eliminated suspended duties on imports and are committed to implementing over the program period a tariff reform aimed at reducing domestic protection.  

The structural reform program of the authorities focuses on streamlining considerably the public service, reducing the role of government in commercial activities, and prioritizing public expenditure. The privatization program willfocus on key enterprises that provide essential infrastructure services, including the Kenya TELKOM.  

Drought conditions have raised serious concerns about food and energy supplies. These developments may pose risks to the implementation of the program. The authorities expressed interest in exploring a possible modification of the program and augmentation of access under the PRGF or receiving assistance under the compensatory or emergency facilities. The staff encouraged the authorities to proceed with an international assessment of the situation and the possible remedies and to subsequently approach the Fund for financial assistance, if needed.  

# 1. INTRODUCTION  

1. Kenya's last arrangement under the Enhanced Structural Adjustment Facility (ESAF) was approved on April 26, 1996, but the first annual arrangement expired in July 1997 without completion of the first review because of insufficient progress in addressing governance issues. In mid-1999, the authorities started to implement a systematic program geared at materially improving governance, and discussions on a program that could be supported by an arrangement under the Poverty Reduction and Growth Facility (PRGF) began in early 2000. In the attached letter dated July 12, 2000 (Appendix I), the Minister for Finance and the Governor of the Central Bank of Kenya (CBK) request a three-year arrangement under the PRGF.' The arrangement is to support the policies and measures described in the memorandum of economic and financial policies (MEFP) annexed to the letter and in Kenya's interim poverty reduction strategy paper (I-PRSP) for 2000/01- 2002/03 (forthcoming).  

2. Under the proposed arrangement, access would be for an amount equivalent to SDR 150 million (55 percent of quota), as under the last ESAF arrangement. There would be eight disbursements. A disbursement of SDR 13.6 million will take place upon approval of the arrangement, a second disbursement of SDR 6.4 million would take place upon completion of the first review of the program by December 15, 2000,² and a third disbursement of SDR 10 million would take place upon completion of the second review of the program in March 2001. The remaining five disbursements would take place upon completion of the semiannual reviews envisaged under the program, starting in March 200l, with each disbursement amounting to SDR 24 million. Over the period of the proposed arrangement, total outstanding Fund credit would increase from SDR 77.7 million in June 2000 to SDR 150 million by June 30, 2003.  

3. The World Bank staff has negotiated an economic and public sector reform credit aimed at supporting reforms in the core areas considered to be triggers for the Bank's Country Assistance Strategy. Consideration of the credit is scheduled for early August 2000. IDA financing is SDR 113.l million. The credit will be disbursed in three tranches of SDR 37.7 million each. The second and third tranches are linked to performance on specific indicators for end-September 2000 and end-March 200l, respectively. It is expected that similar financing will be available from the World Bank in the two subsequent years.

The authorities' proposals for quantitative and structural benchmarks and performance criteria are set out in Table l, while a detailed description of the variables subject to quantitative performance criteria and benchmarks is provided in the technical memorandum of understanding, both of which are attached to the MEFP. # I. BACKGROUND  

5. Kenya's socioeconomic conditions deteriorated in the 199Os mainly because of a failure to sustain prudent macroeconomic policies, slow progress in structural reform, and pervasive governance problems. This weak performance was characterized by periodic financial instability, loss of confidence, a rapid buildup of short-term domestic debt, and high real interest rates. This, together with other high costs of doing business-on account of pervasive corruption, poor infrastructure and services provided by public utilities, inefficient allocation of public resources, deteriorating security, and constraining government regulations-led to a reduction in the level and quality of investment and, as a consequence, in economic growth (Table 2). These developments were paralleled by significant declines in domestic saving, reflecting decreases in per capita income and persistent uncertainty. In addition, in the l99Os, AIDS has imposed a rising human, social, and economic cost to the country.  

6. Low employment creation and economic growth, combined with a population growth rate of about 2.5--3 percent annually, translated into increasing poverty and a deterioration of all social indicators, particularly in the more vulnerable regions of the country (Table 3). Poverty also increased directly on account of the distortions to economic activity mentioned above, which disproportionately constrained the capacity of the poor to earn their livelihood (e.g., in agriculture, where most of the poor eke out a living); the lack of access to markets due to inadequate rural infrastructure; and the misuse and misdirection of public resources away from outlays benefiting the poor.3  

7. Since early 1998, the government of Kenya has been taking steps to address some of the causes of financial instability and low growth. Fiscal deficits have been reduced—and, thus, government domestic borrowing—-and monetary policy has been broadly appropriate. These policies helped reduce inflation and real interest rates. Despite the strain of weak fiscal revenue performance and a large increase in external debt amortization, macroeconomic policies remained prudent in 1999/2000;# the government overall deficit (on a commitment basis and before grants) is estimated to be 0.6 percent of GDP, compared with 0.7 percent in 1998/99, and monetary policy has been generally conservative, although it was complicated by liquidity shifts associated with the Y2K problem. The government prepared the ground for key structural reforms in the areas of privatization and public service, and since mid-1999 it has taken important steps to address governance problems. The measures implemented by the authorities in the area of governance in the second half of 1999 have helped increase the confidence of the international community in the government's commitment in this area, and are listed in Appendix V. However, a number of the constraints noted in paragraph 5 above have continued to operate, and investor confidence has been slow to turn around, causing real GDP growth to continue to decline, to 1.4 percent in 1999 from 1.8 percent in 1998 and 2.3 percent in 1997.  

# II. MEDIUM-TERM POLICY FRAMEWORK  

8. The government is committed to formulating and implementing a medium-term strategy to remove the constraints to sustainable economic growth and poverty reduction. The I-PRSP prepared by the authorities is the first, and appropriate, step in this process. The authorities expect to fully develop this strategy by May 2001, in the context of the preparation of the full PRSP, supported by a wide-ranging consultative process. The I-PRSP is grounded in a National Poverty Eradication Plan, which is consistent with International Development Goals and covers the period l999-2015. The plan aims at reducing the number of the poor by one-half by 2015, and stresses the need to provide better coverage of basic services, particularly education, health, and water and sanitation, as well as for broad-based economic growth. The I-PRSP is the product of a consultative process that was launched with a widely attended National Consultative Forum to discuss the initial draft I-PRSP. The staffs of the Fund and World Bank find the I-PRSP generally satisfactory (forthcoming).  

9. The preparation of the PRSP is closely linked to reforms of the budget process in order to ensure that poverty priorities articulated in the paper feed directly into a recently initiated medium-term expenditure framework (MTEF). The authorities have made an effort in recent months to use the MTEF to prioritize the policies contained in the I-PRSP and to render it the basis for the 2000/O1 budget. However, the current MTEF should be seen as a first step in the right direction, as the reallocation of expenditures toward priority areas has to be sharpened and more completely defined in the full PRSP. It is expected that next year's  

MTEF, which will accompany the PRSP, will reflect a deeper understanding of the determinants of poverty and of the necessary corrective policies, and lay out a credible strategy for expenditure reallocation, which will be reflected in the budgets for 2001/02 and beyond. In the meantime, the government will develop input and outcome social indicators and monitoring mechanisms to gauge the effectiveness of these programs in achieving established objectives.  

10.The government's macroeconomic framework for the next three years, as outlined in the I-PRSP (see also Table 4), aims at (i) progressively increasing real GDP growth to 5.5 percent by 2003; (ii) keeping inflation below 5 percent; and (ii) gradually raising the import reserve cover to about four months, to help limit the impact of adverse external shocks, while maintaining the current account deficit at sustainable levels. These objectives will require a considerable rise in the level and efficiency of investment. This is expected to come about on the basis of (i) continued progress in the area of governance; (ii) further consolidation of recent stabilization gains and reduction of the domestic debt burden; and (ii) improvement in the allocation of resources through an acceleration and broadening of the scope of structural reforms, as these efforts should restore a measure of investor confidence and significantly reduce various costs of doing business in Kenya. To support the required increase in investment from 16 percent of GDP in 1999 to about 25 percent in the medium term, national savings would need to increase over the same period from about 13 percent of GDP to about 20 percent. The consolidation of macroeconomic stability, increases in per capita income, and improvements in financial markets are expected to raise private sector savings to 15 percent of GDP over the medium term-consistent with historical levels from the early 1990s--from some 9 percent in 1999. Government savings are also projected to rise by about I percentage point of GDP (to some 5 percent of GDP) over the same period, reflecting the prudent fiscal stance. Foreign savings are also expected to increase to support the rise in investment.5  

11. Macroeconomic policies over the medium term are based on a gradual and credible reduction in the fiscal deficit (after an initial increase to accommodate higher expenditure in priority areas) and in the domestic debt burden, aimed at sustainably lowering real interest rates while building confidence in the government's fiscal prudence. Small and declining fiscal deficits would stillbe consistent with adequate service delivery, as the government will aim at reducing the size of the civil service in line with identified core government functions while improving the remuneration of public servants. Thus, lower interest and wage bills should also create room for priority outlays within a prudent expenditure envelope. Moreover, the envisaged fiscal deficits would be consistent with a gradual reduction in the tax burden, which, in turn, would support the transition to a higher growth path. The fiscal stance over the medium term would also be consistent with the need to keep the external  

current account deficit at sustainable levels. Monetary policy will support these efforts by targeting low inflation rates, while maintaining exchange rate flexibility and gradually increasing international reserves to the targeted level of import coverage.  

12. In respect of structural reforms, the government intends to accelerate and broaden the scope of privatization of key public utilities and other enterprises; reduce the regulatory constraints affecting economic activity; review the regulatory framework in the labor market to remove impediments to the creation of employment opportunities; and rationalize and liberalize the import duty regime to render it more predictable and transparent, as well as conducive to export-led growth.

# A. Governance  

14.Following important steps taken in the area of governance in the second half of 1999, (see Appendix V) the Kenyan authorities have shown determination in maintaining the momentum of governance reforms by adopting an ambitious timetable to implement a comprehensive set of measures (Appendix VI). The approach focuses on (i) enhancing accountability and transparency; (ii) strengthening oversight bodies; (ii) strengthening budget planning and execution; (iv) changing the incentive structures faced by potential participants in corruption; and (v) removing rent-seeking opportunities. Some of the measures constitute performance criteria or benchmarks under the program, as indicated in Table l. To enhance the transparency of the governance reform program and establish broad public support for it, the government added the list of intended governance measures as an Annex to the published I-PRSP, which was circulated to parliament.  

15.The government implemented the following key measures as prior actions: (i) publication in the official gazette (i.e., submission to parliament) of a bill containing a code of ethics for all holders of public offces, requiring that they declare their assets and liabilities; (ii) provision of adequate support to the Kenya Anti-Corruption Authority (KACA) and the Controller and Auditor General (C & AG) under the 2000/O1 budget; (iii) adoption of the broad outlines of a plan (to be further refined with the assistance of FAD) to strengthen expenditure management; and (iv) reissuing accounting officers' letters of appointment to emphasize their responsibility in strengthening expenditure control and the sanctions to be applied for failure to uphold these responsibilities. However, in the case of the submitted bill containing the code of ethics, the staff has discussed with the authorities the need to amend it in order to strengthen the independence of the Public Service Committee examining alleged infractions of the code, to extend the requirement to declare assets and liabilities to the spouces, children, and other dependents of the officials, and to broaden the definition of the types of assets and liabilities to be declared. The introduction of these amendments, in line with the recommendations of the staff, constitutes a structural benchmark and a condition for the completion of the first review of the program by December 15, 2000.  

16. In recent weeks, the Kenyan authorities have also issued updated comprehensive procurement regulations for allpublic institutions, in accordance with international standards and practices; published their governance agenda; submitted to parliament an amendment to remove the possibility of exempting from audit a state corporation; amended regulations to ensure all government and government-guaranteed foreign liabilities are captured by the debt-management system; specified penalties against ministries failing the reporting requirements on expenditure commitments; eliminated virtually all suspended duties; and adopted a privatization strategy that specifies the parastatals to be privatized and the envisaged modalities aimed to ensure transparency of the process.  

17.A number of other governance-related measures are expected to be completed in the course of 20o0/01. One such important measure involves the Anti-Corruption and Economic Crimes Bill, which would reinforce the current anticorruption legal framework in general and KACA in particular. The government committed itself to publishing this in the official gazette (i.e. submit to Parliament) an Anti-Corruption and Economic Crimes legislation essentially similar to the one annexed to the Report of the Select Committee of Parliament on Anti-Corruption not later than thirty days after the parliament concludes discussions on that report. The government expects that the legislation will be enacted by year's end. With a view to further strengthening budget management and control, by mid-fiscal year, the authorities will have clarified and strengthened the role of finance officers, shifted the supervision of district treasuries from the Ofice of the President to the Ministry of Finance, improved the accounting and recording of expenditure commitments and domestic arrears, and clarified the roles and responsibilities (including on financial reporting) of treasury departments involved in budget preparation and execution.' The authorities also intend to  

utilize technical assistance to design by mid-fiscal year a strategy that would permit them to strengthen expenditure commitment control (including at the district level) and to restructure internal audit. The necessary measures would be implemented in the second half of the fiscal year. In addition to these measures, the government has provided sufficient funding to the Controller and Auditor General to eliminate in the near future the backlog of audits of central government accounts through recruitment and retention of additional qualified staff.

# Monetary policy  

24.The monetary program for 2000/01 (Table 6) aims at keeping inflation at about 5 percent, and is consistent with the envisaged increase in GDP growth and a smal decrease in velocity on account of improved confidence. Thus, it is expected that broad monetary aggregates will grow by about 8 percent, which, given the planned reduction in domestic government debt and the targeted increase in net foreign assets, willprovide room for a 12.6 percent increase in credit to the private sector. This should be adequate to support the envisaged increase in real GDP. Monetary policy will continue to be conducted through indirect monetary control instruments, particularly open market operations and repurchase agreements using government paper. The authorities are committed to monitoring developments closely, including in various interest rates, the exchange rate, and a variety of measures of liquidity in the economy. Achieving the monetary targets is expected to be facilitated in the period ahead by the CBK's issuing and strictly enforcing regulations on the amount of risk that banks may incur in foreign exchange open positions.  

25.The central bank will continue to maintain exchange rate flexibility, with its intervention in the exchange markets guided by the need to achieve the program's net foreign assets targets. In the event of unanticipated private capital inflows, the CBK will allow full exchange rate flexibility and focus on achieving its monetary targets. In such a context, the authorities should consider tightening fiscal policy as a response that would help reduce domestic interest rates. Although traditional competitiveness indicators show some worsening of Kenya's external competitiveness since the early 1990s (see SM/99/287; 12/3/99), Kenya has seen a modest depreciation of both its nominal and real effective exchange rate over the past few years (Figure 1). The envisaged acceleration of structural reforms, improvements in infrastructure, and the overallreduction of costs of doing business in Kenya are expected to improve external competitiveness and offset any strengthening of the real exchange rate that might be caused by a return of confidence in the Kenyan economy.  

26.To help strengthen the financial system and reduce the opportunities for governance problems, the authorities intend to remove the government from the banking business. In this context, they will offer for sale the remaining government shares in the Kenya Commercial Bank (KCB) by September 2000 and expect to complete the transaction by March 2001.15 The privatization of the National Bank of Kenya (NBK) will follow that of the KCB; in the meantime, the restructuring of the bank will be accelerated, including by intensifying efforts to collect or restructure nonperforming loans—an area where the restructuring program adopted in early 1999 has underperformed.  

27. The authorities are committed to supporting the overall bank loan recovery effort through a substantial increase in the number of judges during 2000 to handle debt recovery in the courts and general banking litigation, as well as by providing appropriate infrastructure to these courts. Loan recovery has often been hampered by the absence of proper loan documentation and lending procedures. The authorities thus will submit by September 30, 2000 amendments to the Banking Act to clearly define“reckless lending" and related activities, as well as other necessary amendments to existing legislation, with a view to enabling the courts to deal more effectively with fraud and realization of security. These measures are expected not only to aid in the recovery of past loans but also to serve as a future deterrent.  

28.The Banking Supervision Department (BSD) of the CBK has been significantly strengthened since 1998 in terms of staff and supporting legislation/regulations. The following measures—developed with the assistance of MAE-—are intended to further advance this process: (i) in July 20o0, the CBK Board will adopt (and publicize widely) a written policy for prompt, progressive intervention (including through monetary penalties) in response to noncompliance by banks and their managements with statutes and regulations; (ii) in July 2000, the CBK will issue prudential regulations on capital adequacy and foreign exchange open positions; (ii) by September 2000, the CBK will issue prudential regulations and policies, including, inter alia, on the amount of risk that banks may incur in lending operations, with a view to clarifying, and expanding, on prohibited activities; and (iv) by March 2001, the authorities will submit comprehensive amendments to the Banking Act and the Building Society Act regarding the role of CBK in, inter alia, supervising all institutions involved in banking activities. The authorities intend to engage in a dialogue with the microfinance sector, with a view to improving access of the poor to financial markets and to developing an appropriate regulatory environment for the sector.  

# External sector issues and justification of access  

29.The current account deficit (excluding grants) is expected to rise from 3.9 percent of GDP in 1999/2000 to 6.4 percent in 2000/01. The rising deficit reflects the effects of the expected economic recovery, higher investment-related imports by the energy sector'b and the privately owned Kenya Airways, as well as the increase in the government deficit. After taking into account the expected larger private and public capital inflows,'7 and the need to increase reserve coverage to 2.9 months by June 201, there remains a financing gap of about US\$167 million (Table 7). The authorities expect to close this gap by a rescheduling with the Paris Club and other creditors of Kenya's eligible external payments arrears and some of the maturities faling due on eligible debt. During 2000/01, more than US\$650 million in amortization and interest payments are falling due, with the main creditors being France, Japan, and the United Kingdom. About half of the maturities falling due are on precutoff-date debt that should be eligible for rescheduling if an agreement is reached with Kenya's Paris Club creditors. External arrears, mostly to France and the United Kingdom, amounted to US\$ll0 million at end-May 20o0. The authorities are committed to forcefully addressing the arrears problem, and thus the program contains a continuous performance criterion prohibiting the accumulation of new external arrears.  

30. Kenya's external current account deficits will remain quite large during the remainder of the arrangement, largely on account of envisaged energy sector and airline investmentrelated imports. However, with lower amortization of external debt from 20ol onwards and higher expected foreign financing, Kenya's balance of payments is not expected to show further unidentified financing gaps.  

31. Kenya has progressively lowered its import tariffs in recent years, but it has at the same time allowed a proliferation of suspended duties,l° thereby making the regime opaque and unpredictable. To address these problems, the authorities have begun to simplify the trade regime. Specifically, under the 2000/01 budget, the government replaced existing suspended duties with constant tariffs,20 and at the same time reduced the number of resultant tariff bands, excluding sugar, from 13 to 9. Import duties applied to certain raw materials have also been reduced. As a result of these steps, the average unweighted tariff rate was lowered from 18 percent to 17.2 percent, and--excluding basic agricultural commodities-- the top tariff rate (including suspended duties) was lowered from 95 percent to 40 percent.  

32. The government is also embarking on a program to rationalize and reduce import duties in line with Kenya's commitment under the Cross-Border Initiative (CBl) and to business climate, and is also based on planned investments in Kenya Airways-a foreignowned company-—on the expected pickup in the foreign financing of Kenya's power projects, and on the additional foreign direct investment that should follow the privatization of TELKOM.

# C. Structural Reforms  

36.As noted above, an important aspect of the reform program is the public service?3 retrenchment plan, which has been developed jointly by the authorities and the World Bank, along with the UK's Department for International Development (DFID) and the European Union (EU). It envisions a reduction of the public service by about 48,000 employees over the coming two fiscal years and is expected to lower the wage bill (in percent of GDP) and thereby facilitate the reallocation of resources to nonwage poverty reduction programs over the medium term. Under the plan, which reflects the rationalization and redefinition of the core functions of government, the size of the civil service (excluding teachers) will be reduced by 32,348 (15.5 percent of the total) during 2000/01-2001/02.24 Of this total, 25,783 are slated to leave the service by September 2000 and those remaining will be removed in early 200l/O2. The retrenched civil servants will be provided with adequate severance pay, which has been determined in close consultation with the World Bank staff, DFID, and the EU, and is on average equivalent to about 21 months of the average wage. Savings arising from the civil service retrenchment are expected to peak at 0.5 percent of GDP annually. The numbers above do not include the reduction in the number of civil servants on account of attrition, estimated at about 8,800 during 2000/01-2001/02. In addition to the civil servants, about 7,0o employees of the Kenya Revenue Authority, support services in the universities, and other institutions will be retrenched in 2Oo/O1. This public service retrenchment program willallow the government to better prioritize public spending, improve the efficiency of public service, and continue to gradually decompress the civil service pay structure.  

37.The privatization program is expected to play a major role in addressing constraints in the provision of key infrastructure services and thus in supporting the revival of economic activity. In 20o0/0l, the government willaccelerate the second phase of its privatization program on the basis of a new strategy developed with assistance from the World Bank staff and approved by the cabinet on June 23, 2000. The new strategy emphasizes limiting the role of government in commercial activities, putting in place appropriate regulatory frameworks, and using competitive bidding, audits, reporting to parliament and the public, and other  

modalities to ensure transparency and fairness.25 The major enterprises to be privatized in 2000/01 are the telecommunications company (TELKOM) and the KCB. There are plans to carry out the privatization of Kenya Railways and (parts of) the Kenya Ports Authority in 2001/02. The energy-related parastatals are slated for privatization subsequently, following the development of an adequate regulatory framework in the energy sector. Given the importance of the energy sector for longer-term growth prospects, it is critical that the authorities accelerate the process.  

38.The government's ultimate objective is to improve the prioritization of public expenditure, with a focus on key social programs in line with those established under the IPRSP. The authorities explained that, at present, the reallocation of resources toward poverty areas is constrained by large wage and domestic interest bills. In addition, capacity constraints in the preparation of the first MTEF, as well as existing commitments, have added to the rigidity of the composition of expenditure in 2000/0l. As a result, reallocations at the sectoral and interministerial levels are difficult to discern in 20o0/O1. Spending in the human resource development sector, which includes the nonwage components of education, health, and safety net spending, will remain virtually unchanged. Spending by the Ministry of Education (including on wages) will fall from 6.3 percent to 6.1 percent of GDP, and from 31.1 percent to 27.1 percent of total expenditure (excluding interest and net lending). Spending by the Ministry of Health will increase from 1.4 percent to 1.5 percent of GDP, but will fall from 6.8 percent to 6.7 percent of the total budget (excluding interest and net lending). Expenditure by the Ministry of Public Works will decline from 8.3 percent to 8.1 percent of the total (excluding interest and net lending) while outlays of the Ministry of Environment (which include water and sanitation projects) will rise from 2.5 percent to 2.8 percent of the total budget. It is planned, however, that, over time, the reforms in the public service discussed above willprovide room for additional resources to be allocated to priority poverty reduction programs, which will be sharpened and more narrowly defined through the consultative process for the preparation of the PRSP.  

39. In the meantime, the 2000/0l program focuses on a few key poverty reduction measures and some intraministerial reallocations aimed at improving the targeting of existing expenditure—-an approach that the authorities agreed with the World Bank staff. For example, nonwage expenditures on primary education will jump by 24 percent, and expenditures for district hospitals and rural health centers—-aimed at providing more decentralized preventive health care-will rise by l7 percent. Within the allocation to the Ministry of Public Works, there are reallocations toward rural roads projects, with their share rising from 11.4 percent of the ministry's budget to 16.6 percent. Moreover, to increase access by the rural poor to water and sanitation, allocations for such projects are growing from 9.6 percent to 18.6 percent of the environment budget. There are also more resources for combating HIV/AIDS, including for education programs, especially at the local level.  

40.The agriculture sector accounts for 70 percent of total employment in Kenya, and much of this is on subsistence farms. In this context, the government's program includes important measures that would remove identified structural constraints, build efficient technology services (e.g. extension programs), and improve rural infrastructure. Reforms are to focus on streamlining government regulations, especially in the coffee and tea sectors. Steps to reform the tea sector are well advanced, but there is a need to accelerate the elimination of government licensing in coffee production and marketing, and to overhaul the cooperative system, after appropriate consultation with stakeholders.  

41. The government's program aims at strengthening the Central Bureau of Statistics, including through increased budgetary allocations, so as to improve the socioeconomic statistics needed to formulate and monitor the reform program. In the poverty-monitoring area, particular emphasis willbe placed on filling gaps in the understanding of the nature and determinants of poverty, fully developing input and outcome social indicators, and ensuring the monitoring of poverty dynamics. In macroeconomic statistics, there are plans to continue implementing the recommendations of recent STA missions.  

# D. Risks to the Program and Implementation Environment  

42. Serious and repeated drought conditions have resulted in large shortages of staple food items, especially in certain parts of the country. International food relief agencies have been providing some help, and the budget for 2000/0l includes K Sh 1.5 billion (0.2 percent of GDP) for food relief in the form of maize purchases. The drought has also seriously decreased water levels at many of Kenya's hydroelectric power dams. As a result, severe power rationing has now been introduced. The authorities estimate that the shortage of hydroelectric power willast at least six months, and then will depend on the extent of the short rains expected in November and December. Arrangements to import some power from Uganda have been concluded, and a fossil fuel plant is being rehabilitated. The authorities are investigating all options to minimize the adverse impacts of power shortages on production and living conditions and intend to hold discussions with donors to seek both technical advice and emergency financial assistance. In this context, they have expressed an interest in exploring the possibility of augmentation of access under the PRGF-supported arrangement or in receiving assistance under the compensatory or emergency facilities. The staff has urged the government to proceed with an international assessment (involving the World Bank and international food relief agencies) of the problems and the envisaged remedies and to approach the Fund subsequently, if needed.  

43. Given the importance of fostering a consensus on the poverty reduction strategy in the context of the PRSP participatory process, the authorities' plans, as set out in the I-PRSP, are encouraging. In terms of implementation capacity, while the authorities generally have the capacity to implement the major elements of the program, further technical assistance is needed in the areas of statistics and poverty monitoring, public expenditure management, budget preparation and planning, internal audit, and tax administration. The Fund is planning to provide further assistance in many of these areas while several of Kenya's other development partners also stand ready to provide assistance.  

# V. STAFF APPRAISAL  

44. Kenya has pursued generally cautious macroeconomic policies since early 1998. As a result, despite the absence of donor assistance, inflation has been kept in the low single digits, the external accounts have been at sustainable levels, and interest rates have been reduced. Since mid-1999, the authorities have also begun to decisively address weaknesses in governance, following a systematic approach that aims at improving governance. They identified a comprehensive set of measures in these areas and have been putting them in place over the past year, thereby taking critical steps toward increasing policy credibility and investor confidence and thus creating an adequate basis for increasing investment and economic growth on a sustainable basis.  

45. Despite such progress, economic growth and employment has continued to decline because of burdensome constraints on activity which require time to be removed, and because of the slow turnaround in investor confidence. The constraints include high real interest rates, lingering corruption, lack of security, dilapidated infrastructure, inadequate services from public utilities, and market distortions—-all of which increase the cost of doing business in Kenya. These constraints and the rebuilding of credibility can be addressed only over the medium term, and the authorities expect to do this in the context of a three-year PRGF-supported program.  

46.Low employment and economic growth over the past decade have led to increased poverty and deteriorating social indicators. Inadequate targeting of budgetary spending toward poverty alleviation, and the misdirection and misuse of public resources away from outlays benefiting the poor, have compounded the effects on the poor of low economic growth. In this context, there is a need to decisively reallocate budgetary resources toward areas whose improvement would help reverse recent poverty trends, such as primary (rural) health care, early schooling and primary education, and water and sanitation infrastructure. Intensified consultations with civil society, in particular the poor, as well as with development partners, will be essential in devising such a reallocation, as well as in monitoring and refining it over time. The PRGF-supported program and parallel World Bank-supported operations should help to gradually reprioritize budgetary expenditure toward poverty reduction. The authorities' three-year program should also provide the basis for much-needed increases in resource availability through external aid and debt rescheduling, without which important reforms (for example, in the public service) could no be implemented.  

47. The medium-term macroeconomic framework of the program, which covers fiscal years 2000/01-2002/03, is consistent with the goals for poverty reduction, in particular by promoting more rapid growth and the maintenance of macroeconomic stability. The framework builds on the achievements in stabilization since l998 and aims to effectively mobilize and use an increased flow of donor funds and privatization receipts to reduce domestic debt. The resulting lower real interest rates should help support a private-sector-led economic recovery. The framework seeks appropriately to avoid excessive increases in domestic demand, lest the economic program be put at risk by giving rise to renewed inflationary pressures, unsustainable external current account deficits, and losses of international reserves. Nonetheless, given the severity of the poverty problem, the program allows for some—albeit circumscribed-room for donor funded increases in social outlays above the baseline scenario.  

48. The program recognizes that the shift to a higher growth path will also require the implementation of far-reaching supply-side measures that address existing constraints on economic activity. These include, in addition to measures to improve governance, actions to correct regulatory distortions in various markets, especially in agriculture, redirect public resources toward the rehabilitation of infrastructure, and improve the efficiency and governance of state-owned banks and utilities through privatization. The measures to improve governance are well articulated, but the remaining supply-side measures, while including some important first steps, will need to be developed and prioritized more fully in the period ahead, if sustainable high growth and poverty reduction are to be achieved.  

49. The Kenyan authorities have readily undertaken significant commitments under the program to improve governance in the context of an ambitious timetable. Most prior actions have been adequately implemented and a considerable number of other measures were put in place in recent days, in accordance with the authorities' plan, which together constitute a critical mass needed to improve the momentum of governance reforms in Kenya. It is crucial, however, that the authorities introduce the necessary amendments to the bill submitted to parliament which contains the code of ethics. These amendments should address concerns about safeguarding the independence of the Public Service Committee that will examine alleged infractions of the code, as well as the need to extend the requirement to declare assets and liabilities to the immediate family members of the officials and to broaden the definition of the types of assets and liabilities to be declared. The introduction of these amendments, in line with the recommendations of the staff, is a condition for the completion of the first review under the program. More generally, it is essential, that the governance measures adopted be strictly enforced. The authorities should also continue with the timely and full implementation of all remaining measures included in the government's list, which the staff sees as crucial to the success of the program. In this context, and given its importance for reinforcing the current anticorruption legal framework in general and the KACA in particular, the publication in the official gazette, not later than 30 days after the parliament concludes the discussion on the Report of the Select Committee of Parliament on Anticorruption, of an “Anti-Corruption and Economic Crimes" legislation essentially similar to the one annexed to that report will underscore the government's determination to deal with corruption. It is critical that the legislation be enacted as soon as possible.  

50. The envisaged fiscal deficit in 2000/Ol is consistent with a sustainable external current account deficit and the program's inflation target. It is also appropriate, given the limited reorientation of government spending, if any, toward poverty reduction and the need to improve expenditure efficiency. Although the fiscal program provides for some increase in expenditure on road maintenance (including rural feeder roads) and waterworks, there is no clear reallocation of expenditure toward the education and health sectors. Given that the government's flexibility in reallocating resources toward poverty areas is at present constrained to an extent by the large wage billand heavy debt-service burden, the authorities stressed that such a reallocation will be a gradual process. Thus, the immediate aim is to increase the efficiency of expenditure and improve its targeting to the poor. The government's medium-term strategy, however, focuses on a significant reduction in the size of the civil service. This should free some resources and should allow reallocations of expenditures toward priority areas as these are sharpened and more narrowly defined through the consultative process for the preparation of the full PRSP. Moreover, next year's MTEF, which will accompany the PRSP and will benefit from input from the World Bank staff, will need to lay out a ciear strategy for expenditure reallocations toward the social sectors.  

51.The staff weicomes the government's decision to clarify to parliament before Board consideration of the program that the discrepancy between the fiscal program and the budget is due to presentational practices and that the government is committed to containing the deficit to the level agreed with the Fund.  

52.A clearly positive and commendable aspect of the budget for 2000/01 is the large retrenchment program that is associated with the redefinition of core functions of the government. The separation packages were developed in collaboration with donors and could be deemed adequate compensation for the retrenched, who do not have a social safety net on which to rely.  

53. Even though it follows very small increases in nominal terms in the last two fiscal years, the 1l percent rise in wages and allowances included in the fiscal program is too large and premature—given the lack of significant reallocation of expenditure toward povertyreducing areas-and should have awaited the emergence of greater maneuvering room in the budget on account of staff reductions. The more than 30 percent increase in defense and security (police) spending is justified in the view of the authorities by the need to strengthen the security of Kenya's borders (to guard against the spillover effects of the neighboring countries' internal or external conflicts) and to tackle the growing crime problem in the urban and rural areas. Both are seen as essential to promoting investment and thus revitalizing growth, including in the tourism sector, and with significant repercussions for the lives and livelihood of the poor. Nevertheless, there is a need for greater transparency in the allocations toward security, as well as for developing a more balanced allocation of expenditures over the medium term.  

54. Full implementation of the expenditure control and governance measures should markedly improve the efficiency of expenditures and help minimize the emergence of new pending bills. In addition, the plans to improve internal audit, expenditure control at alllevels of the public sector, and the import duty exemption regime, are welcome. The authorities need to further refine and implement these plans as soon as possible, strictly enforce the resulting rules and regulations, and impose penalties when these are breached.  

55. Initial efforts have been made to rationalize the project roster and deal with extrabudgetary/stalled projects, so as to improve the efficiency of project implementation. However, much remains to be done, as the extrabudgetary/stalled project phenomenon needs to be eliminated in the next budgetary cycle, implying, inter alia, that sufficient budget provisions should be made for the timely completion of any projects that would be maintained.  

56. The authorities are committed to implementing a monetary program consistent with the inflation and growth objectives of the program while maintaining exchange rate flexibility. It will be important to monitor developments closely and act promptly to avoid excessive fluctuations in liquidity, while limiting central bank intervention in the foreign exchange market to achieving the net foreign assets targets of the program. While significant progress in improving bank supervision has been made, the intended further strengthening is welcome.  

57.The decision not to delay any further the sale of the remaining government shares in the KCB is appropriate, as it is generally agreed that the government should move out of the banking business. In this context, the authorities need to accelerate the restructuring of the National Bank of Kenya with a view to privatize it soon after the government's share in the KCB is sold. Privatization appears to be advancing well for TELKOM, but there is a need to expedite the planned privatization of Kenya Railways and (parts of) the Kenya Port Authority to help improve their services and lower the costs of doing business in Kenya. For the same reasons it is necessary to accelerate the privatization of the energy sector parastatals which is currently envisaged to take more time. In addition, there is a need to proceed in a timely manner with privatization to help achieve the envisaged reductions in domestic debt.  

58. In eliminating all suspended duties, the program takes an important step toward more predictable and transparent economic policies, as well as better governance. It is important to supplement this effort with the timely preparation and implementation of the program to rationalize and reduce remaining import duties to improve efficiency and competitiveness, and to meet Kenya's international commitments. The authorities have made some progress toward restricting the scope of import exemptions. Further efforts willbe needed in the next fiscal year, including the discontinuation of all major import exemptions awarded to the public sector.  

59. The intended strengthening of the Central Bureau of Statistics will be an important step in formulating and monitoring the poverty reduction program. Steps have been identified with the help of the Fund, the World Bank, and other donors for developing and updating in a timely manner socioeconomic statistics, including input and outcome social indicators, and for improving macroeconomic statistics. These are essential for the formulation of appropriate policies and, thus, for the success of the poverty reduction program.  

60. The staf shares the authorities' concern about the effects of the drought on food and energy supplies. It urges the government to seek an international assessment of the situation and possible remedies, and stands ready to review the results of this assessment to determine whether a modification of the program should be sought.  

61. In view of the above considerations, the staff recommends that Kenya's request for a three-year PRGF arrangement be approved.

# Memorandum of Economic and Financial Policies of the Government of Kenya, 2000-03  

# I. BACKGROUND  

1. Kenya's socioeconomic conditions have deteriorated since the early 199Os, mainly because of a failure to sustain prudent macroeconomic policies, slow progress in structural reform, and pervasive governance problems. This weak performance has been characterized by periodic financial instability, loss of confidence, a rapid buildup of short-term domestic debt, and very high real interest rates. These factors, together with other high costs of doing business-—on account of poor infrastructure and services provided by public enterprises, inefficient allocation of public resources, deteriorating security, and constraining government regulations (especially in agriculture)-—have reduced the level and quality of investment. As a consequence, economic growth has dwindled and unemployment has risen, which, combined with declines in access to essential services by the poor, has contributed to significant increases in poverty.  

2. The most recent household survey (1997) shows an increase in the population living in poverty to about 52 percent of the total. While most of the poor live in rural areas, this increase is also evident in the urban areas as a consequence of rising unemployment. A decline in the gross primary enrollment ratio from 94 percent in 1993 to 83 percent in 1998 and an erosion in the capacity to provide basic health services are two indicators of the deterioration in living standards. This deterioration is compounded by the heavy toll taken by the HIV/AIDS epidemic, which in part is reflected in the estimated decline of life expectancy from 60 years in 1993 to 57 years in 1998.'  

3. Since 1998, the government of Kenya has been taking steps to address some of the socioeconomic ills confronting the country. Macroeconomic policies have been appropriately tightened, leading to a marked decline in inflation and real interest rates. In 1999/2000 (JulyJune), the government has maintained prudent macroeconomic policies, notwithstanding weak revenue performance and a large increase in external debt amortization. The government deficit (on a commitment basis and before grants) is 0.6 percent of GDP, compared with 0.7 percent in 1998/99, and monetary policy has been conservative; as a result, inflation has remained under control, and net international reserves have been rebuilt. The government has initiated key structural reforms in the areas of privatization and public service (including civil service retrenchment); since mid-1999, it has also taken important steps to address governance problems. However, real GDP growth is estimated to have declined from I.8 percent in 1998 to l.4 percent in I999, reflecting the constraints noted in paragraph I.  

4. The government is committed to intensifying the reforms mentioned above, since considerably more needs to be done over the near and medium term to help increase economic growth and reduce poverty. To this end, it has prepared an interim poverty reduction strategy paper (PRSP) that outlines a preliminary policy framework, which willbe fully developed in the context of the full PRSP, scheduled to be finalized by May 200l. The interim PRSP-- which has been made public and distributed to the IMF, the World Bank, and donors--has benefited from inputs received from representatives of the poor, the private sector, civil society, and development partners in the context of a wide-ranging consultative process that was launched in March 2000. A recently introduced medium-term expenditure framework (MTEF) was used to prioritize the policies contained in the interim PRSP and is the basis for the 2000/0l fiscal program. The implementation of these policies, as well as those to be developed in the subsequent full PRSP, will help achieve the objectives of the National Poverty Eradication Plan (NPEP) for the period 1999-2015. The plan, which was prepared in the context of a participatory poverty assessment, aims at reducing the number of the poor by one-half by 20l5, and stresses the need to provide better coverage of basic services, particularly education, health, and water and sanitation, as well as the need for broad-based economic growth.  

# I1. MEDIUM-TERM POLICY FRAMEWORK  

5. The primary development goal of the government is to achieve sustained poverty reduction through the pursuit of policies and reforms that would increase economic growth to high and sustainable levels, and through key reforms in social areas, including well-targeted poverty reduction measures. The govermment's program for the next three years aims at (i) progressively increasing real GDP growth to 5.5 percent by 2003, which is consistent with modest increases in real per capita GDP; (ii) keeping inflation below 5 percent; and (ii gradually raising the import reserve cover to about four months to help limit the impact of adverse external shocks, while maintaining the current account deficit at sustainable levels.  

6. To achieve these objectives, key elements of the government's policy framework include the consolidation of macroeconomic stability, a necessary condition for achieving the desired economic growth; a comprehensive and systematic set of measures to improve governance, so as to reduce corruption and ensure that public resources are effectively allocated to priority areas such as health, education, infrastructure, and security; and wideranging reforms to remove structural constraints. The proposed program, including the envisaged acceleration of structural reforms, would improve the enabling environment for private sector investment, raising investor confidence and reducing the costs of doing business in Kenya and thus strengthening Kenya's external competitiveness. On macroeconomic policies,the medium-term fiscal objectives in the MTEF during 2000/01-2002/03 are to gradually and credibly reduce fiscal deficits (after an initial modest increase to accommodate higher expenditure in priority areas) and to bring down the domestic debt burden in order to rebuild private sector confidence and lower real interest rates. To improve the quality of service delivery, the government will reduce the size of the civil service in line with identified core government functions. The fiscal strategy is consistent with the need to keep the external current account deficit at sustainable levels and envisages a gradual reduction in the tax burden. Monetary policy will continue to be geared toward maintaining low inflation and gradually raising international reserves.  

7. The government willaccelerate and broaden the scope of privatization of key public enterprises; reduce the regulatory constraints affecting economic activity, particularly in the agriculture sector where most of the poor derive their income; review the regulatory framework in the labor market to remove possible impediments to the creation of employment opportunities; and rationalize the import duty regime to render it more predictable and transparent.  

8. While higher economic growth is a necessary condition for increasing employment and reducing poverty, specific policies and measures are needed to ensure that the benefits of growth will reach all parts of society, especially the poor. Some of these policies and measures, in the areas of education, health, water and sanitation, rural infrastructure, and agriculture sector reform, are already reflected in the government's program, but more needs to be done. During the preparation of the ful PRSP—which will involve close and wideranging consultations between the government and the poor, the private sector, civil society, and development partners—-the government expects to continue to gain a deeper understanding of the nature and determinants of poverty, and of the type of policies and programs that would be more effective in reducing poverty. As a result, the MTEF will be refined to ensure that adequate resources are provided to such programs, including the reallocation of expenditure away from lower-priority areas. At the same time, the government will fully develop input and outcome social indicators and monitoring mechanisms to gauge the effectiveness of these programs in achieving established objectives. The government will ensure that these policies and programs are consistent with and willpromote high real GDP growth and macroeconomic stability.  

# III. ECONOMIC PROGRAM FOR 2000/01  

# A. Governance  

9. Good governance is a fundamental building block of a just and economically prosperous society and, therefore, is an essential component of the action plan to reduce poverty. Since mid-1999, the government of Kenya has made significant progress in addressing governance problems. This progress includes increased transparency of the procurement system, improved budget planning and expenditure management, and the gradual strengthening of the Kenya Anti-Corruption Authority (KACA) and the Office of the Controller and Auditor General (C&AG). The government is determined to maintain the present momentum in governance reforms and has, therefore, adopted a comprehensive set of measures and an implementation timetable (Appendix VI) based on a systematic approach to addressing the problem. The approach has focused on (i) enhancing accountability and transparency; (ii) strengthening oversight bodies; (ii) strengthening budget planning and execution; (iv) changing the incentive structures faced by potential participants in corruption;  

and (v) removing rent-seeking opportunities. Some of the measures will constitute performance criteria or benchmarks under the program, as indicated in Table 1. To enhance the transparency of the governance reform program and establish broad public support for it, the government added the list of these measures as an annex to the interim PRSP, and submitted to parliament its governance program.  

10.The government has by end-June 20oO: (i) published in the official gazette, a bill containing a code of ethics for all holders of public offices that provides for clear sanctions against actions contrary to the code, and that requires holders of public ofice to declare their assets and liabilities upon appointment and every three years thereafter or upon leaving the service;² (ii) provided adequate support to KACA and the C&AG under the 2000/O1 fiscal program; (ii) reissued accounting officers' letters of appointment to emphasize their responsibility in strengthening expenditure control and the sanctions to be applied for failure to uphold these responsibilities; and (iv) adopted a concrete plan to strengthen expenditure management (see paragraph l6). In the case of the submitted bill containing the code of ethics, the government intends to amend it inter alia to strengthen the independence of the Public Service Committee examining alleged infractions of the code, to broaden the categories of family members required to declare assets and liabilities, and to broaden the definition of the types of assets and liabilities to be declared. The government understands that the introduction of these amendments constitutes a condition for the completion of the first review of the program by December 15, 2000. Furthermore, the government is committed to publish in the official gazette, not later than 30 days after the determination (i.e., completion of the discussion) by parliament on the Report of the Select Committee of Parliament on AntiCorruption, an Anti-Corruption and Economic Crimes legislation that is essentially similar to the bill annexed to that report. The government understands that the relevant review of the first-year program is contingent on the fulfillment of this commitment. The bill aims, inter alia, at increasing the accountability of public offcials and strengthening the ability of KACA to investigate and prosecute cases of misuse of public resources. The government expects that the legislation will be enacted by year's end.  

# B. Macroeconomic Policies  

11.  In line with the medium-term objectives outlined in paragraphs 5-8, the program for 2000/0l (July-June) aims at (i) accelerating economic growth from 1.5 percent in 2000 to 3 percent in 2001; (ii) maintaining inflation at 5 percent; and (iii increasing gross reserves by June 200l to the equivalent of 2.9 months of the following year's imports of goods and nonfactor services.  

Fiscal policy  

12.Kenya's fiscal framework for 2000/0l reflects the policy reprioritization described above, and aims at reducing the government's domestic debt (which will help to sustainably lower treasury bill rates, the benchmark for lending rates in Kenya) and help keeping inflation and the external current account deficit under control. To this end, the fiscal program for 2000/0l envisages an overall deficit (on a commitment basis and before grants) of K Sh 12,266 billion, or 1.5 percent of GDP, compared with 0.6 percent in 1999/2000. At the same time, given the need to intensify the efforts to reduce poverty, the program allows for a maximum of K Sh 4 billion (0.5 percent of GDP) in additional outlays in the priority areas of education, health, water and sanitation, and rural infrastructure over and above those included in the fiscal program, provided that additional concessional foreign financing is forthcoming on a sustainable basis. Before committing any additional expenditure in these areas and correspondingly raising the overall government deficit, the government will consult with the staffs of the Fund and the World Bank. Appropriate performance criteria and benchmarks on fiscal performance during 2000/Ol are contained in Table 1. The technical memorandum of understanding (Annex II) defines those variables and actions that willbe subject to these and other performance criteria and benchmarks.  

13. The fiscal program envisages a modest rise in the ratio of revenue to GDP from 23.9 percent in 1999/2000 to 24.8 percent in 2000/01. An increase in the value-added tax (VAT) rate from 15 percent to 18 percent4 will offset the lossof revenue arising from (i) cuts in import duties (see paragraph 22) and the excise on beer; (ii) the full-fiscal-year impact of a cut in income tax rates and bracket adjustment made on January l, 20o0; and (ii) further adjustments in brackets and the personal tax allowance (exemption) that are to take place on January l, 200l. The adjustments in income taxes are mainly aimed at relieving the tax burden on the lower-income groups. In support of its revenue targets, the government will  

continue to strengthen tax administration in 2000/0l by, inter alia, transferring stamp duties and civil aviation revenues to the Kenya Revenue Authority (KRA) for collection, and reducing fraud associated with traffic licenses. These measures have been in place since July l, 20o0. The government also intends to transfer the collection of the catering and training levy to the KRA during 2000/0l. To help improve tax and customs administration, the government will seek technical assistance from its development partners.  

14.As a result of the MTEF exercise and the progress made in the governance area, the effciency and effectiveness of government spending is expected to improve considerably. But there is also a need for a modest increase in such spending. Accordingly, total expenditure is targeted to rise by 1.8 percent of GDP to 26.3 percent in 2000/01, mainly reflecting the impact of the costs of the civil service reform (see paragraph 28), as well as increases in other recurrent expenditure. Personnel costs (excluding retrenchment costs) will increase by about 11 percent--after adjusting the base by nearly K Sh 3 billion savings because of the retrenchment-owing to a wage drift of 2 percent and an increase in the housing allowance for civil servants and teachers; a wage increase to the lowest-paid civil servants to bring their wages to the legal minimum wage; and salary increases for the security forces that had been promised four years ago but postponed for lack of funds. These increases could not be delayed further without undermining the motivation and discipline in the security forces and desired improvements in service delivery in the public sector. Other recurrent expenditure (excluding retrenchment costs) would increase from 10.7 percent of GDP in 1999/2000 to 11.7 percent in 2000/0l on account of higher nonwage expenditure in road maintenance, water and sanitation, and security (for the latter, see paragraph 24), as well as larger transfers to local authorities. Development expenditure and net-lending is expected to rise marginally from 3.5 percent GDP to 3.6 percent, reflecting government efforts to improve the quality of projects, which has deteriorated significantly in recent years.  

15. Consistent with the objective of reducing the domestic debt burden, the fiscal program envisages the repayment of K Sh 6.7 billion of government securities on a net basis and the elimination of pending bills (see paragraph 17). This would be facilitated by the considerable increases expected in privatization proceeds and in external financing, including US\$167 million expected from the rescheduling of outstanding arrears and of part of the extermal debt service falling due in 2000/0l that Kenya intends to request soon from its creditors.  

16.The government willstrengthen its expenditure management systems to help prevent a new accumulation of pending bills. In this context, it reissued on July 11, 20O0 accounting officers' letters of appointment highlighting their financial responsibilities and the sanctions to be applied for failure to uphold these responsibilities. Furthermore, it will shift the supervision of district treasury offices from the Office of the President to the Ministry of Finance by December 2000. Moreover, during 2000/0l, the government will (i) place financial officers at the apex of line ministry financial management, clarify their reporting channels and responsibilities, and provide them with sufficient independence by requiring that their annual performance review be finalized by the Ministry of Finance; (ii) revise the format of the vote book and expenditure returns to incorporate information on expenditure arrears; (ii) develop, with technical assistance from the IMF, a system to strengthen expenditure commitment  

controls, including in the districts; and (iv) clarify the roles and responsibilities (including those regarding financial reporting) of the various treasury departments involved in the budget preparation and execution process. In addition to these measures, the government has provided sufficient funding to the C&AG to enable it to complete the auditing of the 1998/99 and 1999/2000 central government accounts by the end of June 2001. To aid in the implementation of this reform plan, the government has requested technical assistance in expenditure management and budgeting from the IMF. The government is also requesting technical assistance from the IMF to review the internal audit system, with the aim of developing concrete reforms to strengthen it.  

17. In an effort to gradually eliminate outstanding pending bills (domestic arrears), the govermment issued by June 2000 bonds amounting to K Sh 3.4 billion to cancel most of those pending bills that have been properly verified. This leaves the stock of outstanding pending bills at K Sh 8.5 billion, of which K Sh 5.5 billion is being disputed by the government; the remaining K Sh 3 billion will be securitized by December 2000, of which K Sh 1.7 billion is still to be properly verified. The government will develop by December 20o0 a strategy to prevent the further accumulation of pending bills by other public sector institutions, and to eliminate the outstanding pending bills after appropriate verification. In the interim, the accounting officers supervising these institutions will exercise expenditure control and take disciplinary measures on managers incurring commitments outside the approved fiscal program.  

18. The government has terminated all nonpriority extrabudgetary projects and has brought the highest-priority extrabudgetary projects into the 2000/01 fiscal program, in line with the MTEF exercise, and has made provisions in the fiscal program for the termination costs of others that were clearly deemed not to warrant completion. The government has also made provisions in the fiscal program for interest and penalties caused by some stalled projects. All the remaining stalled projects will be reassessed by December 2000 as to whether they will be sold or completed by 2001/02 and beyond. The proposed strengthening of expenditure management will help prevent the reemergence of the extrabudgetary transactions in the future. To further bolster progress in this area and improve the selection, appraisal, and overall management of the development expenditure, the government intends to seek technical assistance from the World Bank.  

# Monetary policy  

19.The monetary program for 2000/0l aims at achieving the inflation and net foreign assets targets of the program, while providing room for an adequate increase in credit to the private sector so as to help achieve the real GDP growth objective. This policy is consistent with a rate of growth of about 8 percent for broad monetary aggregates, taking into account, inter alia, the planned reduction in domestic government debt. The conduct of monetary policy will continue to be based on indirect monetary control instruments. Appropriate performance criteria and benchmarks on monetary performance for 20o0/01, including on international reserves of the Central Bank of Kenya (CBK), are contained in Table l. Regarding the external value of the shilling, the central bank will continue to maintain  

exchange rate flexibility. CBK intervention in the exchange markets will be guided by the need to achieve the program's net foreign assets targets.  

20.Consistent with its privatization strategy (see below), and to help strengthen the financial system, the government is committed to selling the government's shares in the Kenya Commercial Bank (KCB). The offer for sale is scheduled for September 2000, and the transaction is expected to be completed by March 2001. The restructuring of the National Bank of Kenya (NBK) will be accelerated, including by intensifying efforts to collect or restructure nonperforming loans, and the bank will be privatized as soon as possible. The loan recovery effort will be supported by a substantial increase in the number of judges during 2000 to handle debt recovery in the courts and general banking litigation. To strengthen further the role of the Banking Supervision Department (BSD) of the CBK, the following measures have been or will be adopted: (i) in July 2000, the CBK will issue regulations establishing minimum capital adequacy ratios, and the amount of risk banks may incur in foreign exchange open positions, and it willclarify the reporting requirements of banks to the CBK; (ii) in July 2000, the CBK Board will adopt (and publicize widely) a written policy for prompt, progressive intervention (including through monetary penalties) for noncompliance by banks and their managements with statutes and regulations; (ii) by September 2000, the CBK willissue prudential regulations and policies, including, inter alia, on the amount of risk that banks may incur in lending operations, with a view to clarifying and expanding on prohibited activities; and (iv) by March 31, 200l, comprehensive amendments will be submitted to the Banking Act and the Building Society Act regarding the role of CBK in, inter alia, supervising all institutions involved in banking activities.  

# External sector policies  

21. The current account deficit is expected to reach 6.4 percent of GDP in 2000/01, mainly on account of an expected pickup of imports in the energy and airline sectors. However, the projected improvement in the financial account-mainly on account of higher expected capital inflows—should allow reserve coverage of the following year's imports to increase to 2.9 months by June 2001. After tentatively identified program financing has been taken into account, a residual gap of about US\$167 million remains; in order to close this gap, it is expected that Kenya will seek a rescheduling with the Paris Club of its eligible external arrears and some of the maturities falling due on eligible debt.  

22. The government is committed to simplifying the trade regime so as to make it more transparent and predictable. Under the 2o00/0l fiscal program, the government replaced existing suspended duties with constant tariffss and at the same time reduced the number of resultant tariff bands, excluding sugar, from 13 to 9. Import duties applied to raw materials, such as rubber, vegetable oils, pulp, and crude minerals, have been reduced. The government intends to prepare a program to rationalize and lower import duties in line with Kenya's commitment under the Cross-Border Initiative and to facilitate achievement of the common external tariff structure under the Common Market for Eastern and Southern Africa (COMESA) and the East African Cooperation (EAC).6 The government, in consultation with the staffs of the IMF and World Bank, will develop this program by March 200l, with a view to initiating its implementation in July 200l. It is also committed to further restricting the scope of import duty exemptions. In 2000/O1, donated passenger vehicles will no longer qualify for duty exemptions, and in 2001/02 major import exemptions awarded to the public sector will be discontinued.  

23. The government has reviewed its external debt records and has captured all outstanding commitments. To help prevent the reemergence of debt-management problems, the government has simplified debt-management procedures—all debt approval and monitoring now pass through the debt-management division of the Ministry of Finance—and these have been codified in a ministerial circular. Furthermore, the debt-management division and the external resources department has started in June 200O preparing quarterly reconciliations with the Accountant General's records.  

# C. Pressing Issues  

24. Physical security in various parts of the country has deteriorated considerably, reflecting partly the continued instability in bordering countries and the worsening poverty situation in Kenya. Crime, especially in urban areas, is also becoming increasingly serious. As part of its effort to address these problems, the government is taking steps to strengthen security and police forces. These include (i) taking stock of existing training and the state of equipment and facilities in the police force in order to come up with improvements; (ii) increasing pay and housing provisions for security forces to reduce corruption and improve their conditions of living; (ii) providing training, including on human rights practices; and (iv) developing a code of ethics for law enforcement officials.  

25.Serious and repeated deterioration in weather conditions has resulted in large shortages of staple food items, especially in certain parts of the country. International food relief has been helpful, but much remains to be done. In the fiscal program for 20o0/01, the government has provided K Sh 1.5 billion for food relief in the form of maize purchases; the situation will be kept under review.  

26. As in the case of domestic food supply, hydroelectric power supply is also being seriously undermined by recurrent drought conditions. As a result, severe power rationing has now been introduced. The shortage of hydroelectric power will last at least six months, and then will depend on the extent of the rains expected in November and December. Arrangements for limited off-peak power imports from Uganda have been concluded, and a fossil fuel plant is being rehabilitated. The government is investigating all options to minimize the adverse impact of power shortages on production and living conditions. In the short run, these options include increases in domestic prices to ration power, the use of emergency generators, and tax breaks to encourage private sector production and to reduce the costs of incremental fossil fuel usage. Discussions with donors are being held to seek both technical advice and financial assistance. For the medium term, least-cost strategies for stabilizing the power supply are being reviewed, including linking Kenya into the southerm Africa grid.  

# D. Structural Reforms  

27.In the period 2000/01-2002/03,the government of Kenya will accelerate and broaden the scope of structural reforms, which are essential for enhancing the economy's growth potential and reducing poverty. Priority areas include public service reform, reprioritization of government expenditure, the acceleration of the privatization of key state-owned utilities and transportation enterprises, and the removal of other structural constraints, especially in agriculture.  

28. The government completed its preliminary functional review of ministries and departments and has defined core government functions, which are consistent with the objective of establishing an efficient and affordable public service. Accordingly, the size of the civil service (excluding teachers) will be reduced by 32,348 (15.5 percent of the total) during 2000/0l-2001/02; of that total, 25,783 civil servants wil be removed from the payroll by September 2000. In addition, the civil service will be reduced by about 8,800 employees over the two years through national atrition. The retrenched civil servants willbe provided with adequate severance pay, in accordance with a plan developed with the assistance of the World Bank staff and donors. In addition to the expected improvement in the efficiency of the public service, this reform willallow the government to increase the allocation of resources to priority areas, and to gradually improve the civil service pay structure. Furthermore, under the same retrenchment program, about 7,000 employees of the public universities, the Kenya Revenue Authority, an agricultural research institute, and the Catering and Training Levy Trust will also be separated.  

29. Under the first phase of the privatization program, which was initiated in 1992, the government privatized a large number of small and medium-sized enterprises, but progress in privatizing key utilities and transportation enterprises has been slow. In 2000/0l, the government will accelerate the second phase of its privatization program on the basis of a new privatization strategy that emphasizes limiting the role of government in commercial activities, putting in place an appropriate regulatory framework, and using competitive bidding and other modalities to ensure transparency and fairness. This is being done with  

assistance from the World Bank. The Kenya Posts and Telecommunications Corporation has been split into TELKOM and the Postal Authority, and a regulatory body has also been created. Moreover, a license was provided to a second cellular telephone operator to increase competition in the sector. In this context, the govermment has offered for sale 49 percent of the government's share in TELKOM. The separation of Kenya Power and Lighting Company into power generation (KenGen) and power distribution (KPLC) is well advanced and will pave the way for the restructuring and privatization of both companies. Regarding Kenya Railways, the government decided to offer unitary concessions to private sector operators. The government's short-term strategy for the Kenya Port Authority is to improve the availability and reliability of container terminal handling equipment by refurbishment and replacement, while partly privatizing certain services of the port, including the container terminal, through a strategic partner.  

30. Over the medium term, in close collaboration with the World Bank, the government plans to improve the provision to the poor of basic social services, such as preventive health care, primary and secondary education, and water and sanitation. This will be achieved by shifting resources from wage to nonwage expenditure, improving the targeting of subsidies, as well as by improving the efficiency of service delivery. The development of appropriate monitoring and evaluation mechanisms will however, only emerge from the full PRSP process. The 2000/0l program thus focuses on a few key poverty reduction measures. In line with the National Health Strategic Plan, the program envisages a reallocation of resources spent in curative services to primary care in rural areas. In this context, starting in 2000/01, fiscal expenditure allocations will be shifted from centralized hospital care to decentralized, preventive health care. Combating the HIV/AIDS epidemic is an essential element of the government's strategy in the health sector, and the government willprovide more resources for this endeavor starting in 2000/0l, including for education programs, with particular focus on local levels. The government will provide adequate resources to the National AIDS Coordination Council to combat the HIV/AIDS epidemic and assist its victims, especially orphans.  

31. In the education sector, the emphasis is on increasing enrollment by lowering parents' high cost of primary and secondary schools, reflecting too many subjects, and a low studentteacher ratio. In this regard, the 2000/0l fiscal program provides for an increase of 22 percent for nonwage expenditure in primary schools, which will be mainly for well-targeted subsidies, such as scholarships and books, to the poor communities. These resources will be supplemented by financial assistance from development partners. Other steps in this area will include a reduction in the number of subjects taught in primary and secondary schools and other curriculum reforms, a more equitable distribution of teachers across districts, and other measures to improve the eficiency of the education system, including strengthening schoollevel managers and parent participation in education policy.  

32.On water and sanitation, the government has significantly increased fiscal resources to this important sector in 2000/01 in order to increase access by the poor to these facilities. The bulk of the resources will be allocated to rural areas, where most of the poor are concentrated.  

Moreover, the government's program in water and sanitation focuses on strengthening community management in the delivery of these services.  

33. The agriculture sector accounts for 70 percent of total employment in Kenya, and much of this is on subsistence farms. Accordingly, rural sector development is key to increasing growth and reducing poverty. In this context, the government is committed to removing identified structural constraints, building efficient technology delivery services, and improving rural infrastructure. In the tea sector, the government will have fully liberalized the marketing of tea and abolished the Kenya Tea Development Authority by June 2O00. In the coffee sector, recent steps to improve the management of the Coffee Marketing Board will be followed by the enactment of legislation separating the regulatory and marketing arms of the board by December 2000 and establishing a new institution for the marketing of coffee by June 2001. The government will also liberalize the production, processing, and marketing of coffee by December 2000, and at the same time overhaul the cooperative system. To improve rural infrastructure, the government will provide increased fiscal expenditure allocations toward the building and maintenance of feeder roads and the provision of water, and will subcontract maintenance to local communities in areas where labor-intensive techniques are appropriate. The Roads Board Act has been amended to provide an appropriate framework for improving the management of road construction and maintenance, particularly feeder roads.  

34. Reflecting the importance of land ownership in Kenya as an issue related to both equity and poverty (particularly in the rural areas), the government established a Land Commission in November 1999 to develop a new land policy and revised land legislation. This step is expected to increase land ownership by women, facilitate the settlement of squatters, and provide little deeds that enable communities' access to credit from the banking system.  

35. The government will gradually strengthen the Central Bureau of Statistics, so as improve the socioeconomic statistics needed to formulate and monitor the reform program. In this regard, it will provide adequate support to the bureau to finalize the work on the results of the 1999 census. The government will seek financial and technical assistance from development partners to help improve the quality and timeliness of statistics, as well as to help develop a National Sampie Survey and Evaluation Program (NASSEP) based on the 1999 census. The NASSEP is expected to play a key role in filing gaps in the understanding of the nature and determinants of poverty, fully developing input and outcome social indicators, and ensuring the monitoring of poverty dynamics. To monitor social expenditures, the government is committed to developing a program budget classification in time for the 2001/02 fiscal year. In the interim, from the existing administrative and functional classification, a detailed presentation of the relevant social expenditure programs will be prepared to help their monitoring. The government is committed to improving macroeconomic statistics, in line with the recommendations of recent technical assistance from the Fund.

# Technical Memorandum of Understanding  

(July 12, 2000)  

1． This memorandum provides the definitions of the quantitative performance criteria and benchmarks for the three-year program expected to be supported by the IMF under the Poverty Reduction and Growth Facility (PRGF). It also sets out the data-reporting requirements for monitoring the program.  

# I. QUANTITATIVE PERFORMANCE CRITERIA AND BENCHMARKS (TABLE 1 OF THE MEMORANDUM OF ECONOMIC AND FINANCIAL POLICIES (MEFP))  

A. Net Foreign Assets of the Central Bank of Kenya  

2. Net foreign assets are defined as the difference between the Central Bank of Kenya's (CBK) holdings of gross official reserves (excluding pledged, swapped, or any encumbered reserve assets including but not limited to reserve assets used as collateral or guarantees for third-party external liabilities) and its foreign liabilities (including IMF loans and other shortand long-term liabilities). Exchange rates prevailing on March 31, 2000 will be used to convert the net foreign assets into Kenya shillings. Table 1 shows the floors on net foreign assets, specified in Kenya shillings, for September 30 and December 31, 2000 and for March 3l and June 30, 2001.  

3． The net foreign asset floors will be adjusted upward by the difference between any cumulative (from July 1, 2000) excess external budgetary support (EEBS) and the allowable increase in the central government deficit, as defined in paragraph 14. The EEBS is defined as the difference between actual and programmed external budgetary support. Conversions to Kenya shillings will be based on the exchange rates prevailing on March 31, 2000.  

4. The net foreign asset floors will be adjusted downward by one-half of any shortfall in non-project-related external budgetary support relative to the programmed amount.  

5． The net foreign asset floors will be adjusted upward by any cumulative excess in privatization proceeds 2000/01. Excess proceeds are defined as the difference between actual and programmed proceeds.  

Reporting requirement. Data on gross international reserves, encumbered reserves, and foreign liabilities of the CBK will be transmitted to the African Department of the IMF on a weekly basis within five working days of the end of each week. Detailed data on external budgetary support (by donor/creditor, and by currency of denomination) willbe transmitted on a weekly basis within five working days of the end of each week.  

# B. Net Domestic Assets of the Central Bank of Kenya  

6. Net domestic assets are defined as the average over the calendar month of the test dates of reserve money, defined as total currency in circulation and reserves of the banking system with the CBK minus the average over the calendar month of the net foreign assets (as defined above) plus encumbered foreign assets. Table 1 of the MEFP shows the ceilings on the net domestic assets for September 30 and December 31, 2000 and for March 31 and June 30, 2O01. The authorities will consult with the Managing Director of the Fund on any changes in the ratios of reserve requirements.  

7. The ceilings on the net domestic assets will be adjusted upward by one half of any shortfall in non-project-related budgetary support.  

8. The ceilings on the net domestic assets will be adjusted downward by any cumulative excess in privatization proceeds during 2000/0l. The excess proceeds are defined as the difference between actual and programmed proceeds.  

9. The ceilings on the net domestic assets will be adjusted downward (upward) by 12 percent of the excess (shortfall) in the holdings of government debt instruments by the nonbank public over the programmed amounts of K Sh 71,902 million on September 30, 2000; K Sh 75,651 million on December 31, 2000; K Sh 81,188 million on March 31, 2001; and K Sh 74,786 million on June 30, 2001.  

10.Consistent with paragraph 3,the ceilings on the net domestic assets will be adjusted downward by the difference of EEBS over the allowable increase in the central government deficit, as defined in paragraph 14.  

Reporting requirement. The daily balance sheets of the CBK will be transmitted on a weekly basis within five working days of the end of each week.  

# C. Nonconcessional External Borrowing Contracted or Guaranteed by the Central Government, Local Governments, or the Central Bank of Kenya (Excluding Borrowing from the Fund)  

11. Nonconcessional external borrowing (including lease-purchase agreements) is defined as loans with a grant element of less than 35 percent, calculated using currencyspecific commercial interest reference rates. Debt rescheduling and debt reorganization are excluded from the limits on nonconcessional external borrowing. Nonconcessional external borrowing will be zero throughout 2000/01.  

Reporting requirement. Details of all new external borrowing, including government guarantees indicating terms of loans and creditors, will be provided on a monthly basis within three weeks of the end of each month.  

# D. Short-Term External Debt of Central Government, Local Governments, and the CBK  

12. Short-term external debt is defined as debt contracted or guaranteed by central government, local governments, and the CBK with contractual maturity of one year or less, excluding normal trade-related credits. There will be no new short-term external debt throughout 2000/01.  

Reporting requirement. Data on all new borrowing and guarantees (including terms of loans and creditors) by central government, local governments, and CBK will be transmitted, with detailed description, on a monthly basis within three weeks of the end of each month.  

# E. Overall Fiscal Deficit of the Central Government  

13. The overall fiscal deficit is defined as the sum of the change in total credit to government by the banking system plus net changes in the stock of government debt instruments held by the nonbank public, plus external financing and grants (i.e., disbursements of loans and grants plus rescheduling of external debt service, minus scheduled amortization of external debt falling due in 2000/0l, minus outstanding arrears at end-June 20o0), plus privatization proceeds, plus any pending bills and external arrears accumulated in 20o0/O1, minus the change in total deposits of the central government held in the banking system. Table 1 of the MEFP shows the ceilings on the overallfiscal deficit for the periods starting from July 1, 2000 to September 30, 2000, to December 31, 2000, to March 31, 2001, and June 30, 2001.  

14. The ceilings on the overall fiscal deficit will be adjusted upward by the excess in programmed social expenditure (EPSE), to the extent that it is fully financed by EEBS. The EPSE is defined as the difference between actual and programmed social expenditure. The adjustment will not exceed K Sh 1 billion by September 30, 2000, K Sh 2 billion by December 31, 2000, K Sh 3 billion by March 31, 2001, and K Sh 4 billion by June 30, 2001.  

15. The ceilings on the overall fiscal deficit will be adjusted downward by one-half of any shortfallin non-project-related budgetary support relative to the programmed amount and by the full amount of any shortfall in project-related budgetary support relative to the programmed amount.  

# F. Net Repayment of Pending Bills (Domestic Arrears)  

16. The net repayment of domestic pending bills is defined as the repayment of outstanding pending bills as of end-June 2000 minus the subsequent accumulation of new pending bills. Pending bills are payments outstanding for longer than the normal commercial grace period. Pending bills wil be measured by ministerial reports on pending bills reports and, where necessary, supplemented by special audits by treasury and internal audit staff.  

Reporting requirement. Detailed data on repayment of domestic arrears and the remaining previous-year stock of arrears willbe transmitted on a monthly basis within four weeks of the end of each month.  

# G. External Debt-Service Arrears  

17.The stock of pre-cutoff-date of external debt-service arrears at end-June 2000 will remain outstanding until their rescheduling is agreed with creditors; the stock of post-cutoffdate arrears at end-June 200O willbe eliminated by December 2000; and no arrears will be incurred during 2000/01.

# Agriculture  

2. The Bank's activities in the sector support the government's development strategy, which aims at realigning the policy framework and the incentive structure, redefining the role and core functions of the government in the sector, and encouraging a more competitive marketing system with increased participation by the private sector. The government's development strategy also envisages support for the core programs of research and extension. The Bank currently has three ongoing projects in the agriculture and environmental areas, including a Second National Agricultural Research Project, which would set the stage for developing a sustainable agricultural research program.  

3.On environmental/natural resource management, the Bank financed the preparation of the National Environmental Action Plan. The Bank also approved in December 1995 an Arid Lands Resource Management Project, which seeks to ameliorate economic and environmental conditions in the more fragile arid areas. In addition, the Bank approved in July 1996 a Lake Victoria Environmental Management Project, which will support efforts by the Kenyan, Ugandan, and Tanzanian governments to improve management of the lake's ecosystem.  

# Infrastructure development  

4. A major impediment to private sector development is inadequately maintained and inefficiently operated physical infrastructure. Public enterprise inefficiency is at the heart of many of these problems. Addressing these deficiencies requires an appropriate role for the private sector. Bank activity in this sector has aimed at financing critical infrastructure needs while putting in place policy and budgetary reforms to increase long-term sustainability through greater private sector participation.  

5.The Bank has four ongoing infrastructure projects. In January 1996, the Bank approved two infrastructure projects: an Urban Transport Project, which will finance new and expanded road links, intersections, and public transport facilities in urban areas, and a Nairobi-Mombasa Road Rehabilitation Project, which will support rehabilitation of Kenya's chief transport corridor. The Bank also approved an Energy Sector Reform and Power Development Project in June 1997, which is expected to increase generation capacity, reform energy pricing, and encourage private sector participation in the sector. An Emergency Infrastructure Rehabilitation Project to assist the country in coping with the damage done by the “El Nino" floods was approved by the Board in July 1998, providing US\$40 million of new support and redirecting US\$37 million of existing commitments toward rehabilitation. In addition, the Bank is preparing, with the government, a Roads 200o Project aimed at improving access to rural areas.  

# Education and health  

6. In these two sectors, the Bank's emphasis has been on enhancing financing and management capacity to increase the effectiveness and sustainability of public sector expenditures. In education, the emphasis is on (i) improving the quality of primary and seconary education through curriculum reform; (ii) restructuring key sectoral institutions and policies to enhance the efficiency of service delivery; and (iii) shifting public expenditure to improve access to quality basic education, especially in rural areas. In health, the emphasis is on (i) the development of prevention programs for sexually transmitted diseases, including AIDS; (ii) a shift in resources from curative to efficient primary care, including reproductive services; and (ii) a clarification of the respective role of the private and public sectors.  

7. The Bank has two ongoing human resource operations. The Sexually Transmitted Infections Project, which was approved in March 1995, has an objective of reducing both the incidence and impact of infectious diseases, including HIV/AIDS, and of reducing the direct costs of AIDS and the workloads of health facilities. In addition, the project will strengthen institutional capacity at the national and provincial/district levels. The Bank approved in April l997 an Early Child Development Project, which willaim at reinforcing existing institutions and improving the quality of services offered to children of preschool age. Moreover, the Bank is discussing with the government (i) a project aimed at strengthening primary and secondary education, and (ii) a project aimed at reversing the current trends of increasing HIV prevalence rates and deteriorating mother and child health by promoting decentralized delivery of HIV/AIDS and reproductive health services.  

# Public sector management  

8． The Bank has provided two credits to improve public sector management in Kenya. A Parastatal Reform and Privatization Technical Assistance Project provides overalltechnical support for the government's privatization program, as well as for the preparation of specific restructuring plans for those enterprises remaining in the public portfolio. A Civil Service Reform Project provides support for the government's efforts to reduce the size of the civil service and rationalize specific ministries. Both projects are scheduled to close in June 2000. A public sector management technical assistance credit is under preparation to support the reform effort.  

# Private sector development  

9. In addition to the objectives of macroeconomic stability, infrastructure development, and privatization, as well as institutional and public sector reforms conducive to private sector development, the Bank is supporting the development of the informal sector. The  

Micro and Small Enterprise Training and Technology Project aims to enhance entrepreneurial development by reducing administrative constraints and increasing human capital in the informal sector.  

# Adjustment lending  

10. In June 1996, IDA approved a structural adjustment credit (SAC) of US\$126.8 million, including $\mathrm{US}\$36.8$ million in IDA reflows. The first tranche of US\$79.8 million was released in June 1996. The second tranche of about US\$47 million, together with about $\mathbf{US}\$26.6$ million in fiscal year (FY) 97 IDA reflows and US\$17.5 million additional reflows approved in December 1997, was not released because of the government's failure to meet fully all second tranche release conditions. The SAC was closed in June 1998, and US\$87 million was cancelled.  

11.  In September 1998, the Bank's Board endorsed a new Country Assistance Strategy (CAS) for Kenya. According to the CAS, the Bank's lending program for Kenya is currently in the “low case," with a projected lending program of up to $\mathrm{\Us\$1s0}$ million in the next three years. Substantive progress in restructuring public sector institutions and improving public expenditure management could lead to an expansion of the lending program to the “base case," with a potential lending of up to US\$50 million over the next three years. Most of the incremental lending over the "low case" is expected to be budgetary support.  

With a major initiative of public sector reform under way in Kenya, the Bank is preparing a budget support operation of about US\$150 million, which would be disbursed in three tranches: an effectiveness tranche, and two floating tranches. The operation is scheduled for Board consideration in June 2000.

# Governance Measures Adopted During July-December 1999  

With respect to enhancing accountability and transparency, the authorities have taken the following measures:  

increased membership of the Central Tender Board (CTB) from eight to ten, with the additional two members nominated by civil society organizations, and appointed a private sector representative as chairman of the CTB;   
published tender advertisements in at least two newspapers with wide circulation, mad public the summaries of minutes of tender boards meetings, and made available the ful minutes on demand to interested parties;   
prepared quarterly reports detailing the activities of the CTB, including all bids received, names of bidders, and successful bids;   
established an appeals board for tender decisions, with three (including the chairman) of its seven members nominated by civil society organizations;   
required bidders in tenders to declare any contributions made in connection with the tendering process to any politician/official;   
required all local authorities and public institutions to establish tender committees and follow the strengthened government procurement regulations adopted by the central government;   
required that all contract variations follow the strengthened tendering procedures; initiated a procedure to identify and include all extrabudgetary transactions on stalled (but not legally terminated) projects into the budget; and  

In order to strengthen control bodies-including their independence and resources-- the authorities have done the following:  

reviewed budgetary allocations for the Office of the Controller and Auditor General (C&AG) the Kenya Anti-Corruption Authority (KACA), the Attorney General's Office, and the Kenya Revenue Authority (KRA);   
strengthened the capacity of the Office of the C&AG, including the recruitment of 142 additional staff and improvement of remuneration for the staff;   
established a code of conduct for the KACA's staff and board that specifies sanctions against those who deviate from their responsibilities/established regulations; prepared and approved the terms of reference for KACA staff, interviewed candidates, and made the necessary hirings, including the appointment of 32 investigators and 15 prosecutors;   
created a “reception" unit (with a well-advertised “hot line") within the KACA, to which the public can report cases of corruption; and   
transferred the management of the Auditor General's staff from the Office of Personnel Management (civil service) to the office of the Auditor General, in order to bolster the latter's independence, as well as preparing a draft gazette notice whereby the Civil Service Commission would transfer authority for staffing decisions to the office of the C&AG.  

# With a view to strengthening budget planning and execution, the authorities have enacted the following measures:  

prepared quarterly (monthly) revenue and expenditure projections, and established quarterly (monthly) commitment ceilings for line ministries based on these projections; required ministries to report their expenditure commitments to the treasury two weeks after the end of each month;   
engaged four leading accounting firms to verify the validity of outstanding domestic arrears;   
assigned to line ministries finance officers who would have to countersign all   
expenditure commitments and be accountable to the treasury;   
established a budget-monitoring unit to improve budget planning and execution; and initiated a process to engage an external consultant to review the internal audit system of the central government and make recommendations to strengthen it.  

# With respect to changing incentive mechanisms, the government has done the following:  

issued a circular banning any contractors found to have acted corruptly in their dealings with the government from continuing doing business with the government and issued a statement to inform potential participants in tenders of these regulations; and  

strengthened enforcement of existing rules and regulations on sanctions over governance issues, as well as dismissing in the period July-October 1999 at least 15 senior public sector officials (including at the Kenya Port Authority, the Kenya Pipeline, the Coffee Board, the Kenya Tea Development Authority Board, the National Hospital Insurance Fund, the Kenya Veterinary Vaccine Production Institute, the forest conservation office, and various regional administration areas), over 100 judicial system employees; and about 40 central government employees of various levels, including from the Office of the President.  

As for removing rent-seeking opportunities, the authorities have taken the following steps:
