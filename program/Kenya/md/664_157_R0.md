Kenya achieved a major economic transformation during l993-95. Direct Government controls on prices, internal marketing, external trade, and the exchange system were removed, while the government budget deficit was drastically reduced. Real GDP growth rose to 5 percent in 1995-- allowing for the first significant increase in real per capita income in a decade--and inflation was brought under control.  

Following a slowdown in 1995, the pace of economic reform picked up ir the first quarter of l996. Fiscal and monetary policies were tightened, and gross international reserves rose beyond expectations. Substantive actions were taken to accelerate the restructuring and privatization of five key parastatals. Furthermore, a number of measures were adopted to help prevent the recurrence of misuse of public funds. These policies and actions are in line with those that Executive Directors, at the conclusion of the l995 Article IV consultation, had considered necessary for the elaboration of a comprehensive program that could be supported by an ESAF arrangement.  

The Kenyan authorities have now requested a new three-year ESAF arrangement in an amount equivalent to SDR l49.55 million, or 75 percent of quota. The proposed arrangement is in support of Kenya's adjustment and reform program for l996-98.  

The Government's program aims at accelerating real GDP growth and maintaining price stability, while strengthening the external payments position. The program aims for further fiscal consolidation, and the monetary framework features a large decline in net domestic assets of the Central Bank of Kenya (CBK). Structural reforms encompass a major drive to restructure or privatize parastatal enterprises; retrench the civil service; and amend the CBK Act to make the Central Bank more independent. In addition, the program focuses on strengthening budgetary discipline and establishing transparency and accountability in public finances.  

The Kenyan authorities have demonstrated their strong intention to fully implement the proposed ESAF-supported program. The Government has published the policy framework paper; established a Presidential Economic Commission to monitor implementation of the program; and adopted a number of prior actions. The proposed arrangement envisages close monitoring of program implementation by the staff.  

The staff considers the Government's economic program appropriate, and recommends approval of Kenya's request for the three-year ESAF arrangement.  

# I. Introduction  

In a letter to the Managing Director dated April l0, 1996, the Government of Kenya requests a new three-year arrangement under the enhanced structural adjustment facility (ESAF), in an amount equivalent to SDR l49.55 million (75 percent of quota), as well as the first annual arrangement thereunder, in an amount equivalent to SDR 49.85 million (25 percent of quota). The proposed ESAF arrangement is in support of Kenya's adjustment and reform program for l996-98, as described in the Memorandum of Economic and Financial Policies (MEFP) attached to the letter to the Managing Director, and in the accompanying policy framework paper (PFP) (EBD/96/24, 2/20/96). 1/ It is proposed that drawings under the arrangement be phased in six equal semiannual installments. The text of the proposed ESAF arrangement is presented in the annex, with the letter to the Managing Director attached.  

The first three-year ESAF arrangement for Kenya was approved on May l5, 1989, in an amount equivalent to SDR 26l.4 million, or 184 percent of the then existing quota. After the disbursement of five semiannual installments as scheduled, the arrangement became inoperative and was allowed to expire on March 31, 1993 with one semiannual installment left undisbursed. A one-year ESAF arrangement that utilized the undrawn balance (SDR 45.23 million) was approved on December 22, 1993. The midterm review under the one-year ESAF arrangement was completed on November 2l, 1994, and the final disbursement was made on November 30, 1994.  

As of February 29, 1996, Kenya's outstanding use of Fund resources amounted to SDR 248.6 million (l24.7 percent of quota), all involving SAF/ESAF resources (Table 1 and Appendix I). If the amount of the proposed new arrangement is fully disbursed, the outstanding use of Fund resources would rise to SDR 260.6 million (l30.7 percent of quota) at the end of the arrangement in April 1999.  

On January 30, 1996, the World Bank approved $\yen123,456$ million of IDA credits for Kenya to finance two new infrastructure rehabilitation projects. In addition, the Bank's Executive Board is expected to consider a structural adjustment credit (SAC) of $\yen5235$ million (including IDA reflows) in June 1996. Kenya's relations with the World Bank Group are summarized in Appendix II.  

A Consultative Group meeting for Kenya was held on March 2l-22, 1996. Donors welcomed Kenya's recent economic progress, and indicated that they would release funds that had been committed earlier. However, bilateral donors expressed their continued concerns about a number of non-economic governance issues, including the democratization of the political process, the rule of law, and the administration of justice in Kenya; for these reasons, most of them were reluctant to make new commitments of balance of payments support. Nevertheless, disbursements in l996 are expected to total US\$730 million, sufficient to meet Kenya's external financing requirements for this year.  

The quality and coverage of economic data in Kenya are generally satisfactory, in particular with respect to monetary data. Reporting of the core financial data to the Fund is timely. However, the recording of external current account transactions, and the accuracy and coverage of capital account data, need to be improved (Appendix III). Social and demographic indicators are presented in Appendix IV.  

# II. Background  

Kenya has achieved a major economic transformation over the last three years. Direct controls on domestic prices, internal marketing, external trade, and the exchange system have been completely eliminated. The exchange rate and interest rates are now fully market determined. The government budget deficit (on a commitment basis and excluding grants) was reduced from ll.4 percent of GDP in 1992/93 (July-June) to 2.5 percent in 1994/95 (Tables 2 and 3)., Money supply growth was brought under control, and confidence in the banking system restored.  

Real GDP, which had stagnated in 1992-93, began to recover, expanding by 3 percent in 1994, and 5 percent in l995 (Chart 1). Moreover, inflation declined from 46 percent in l993 (period average) to 1.7 percent in 1995. The external current account (excluding official transfers) was in near balance in 1993 and 1994, but a sizable deficit re-emerged in l995 as imports continued to grow strongly from the previously depressed levels.  

The progress in economic reform was slower in l995, however, and some setbacks occurred. A ban was imposed on the importation of maize, wheat, and sugar in April, only to be reversed in June. The budgetary targets for the first half of 1995/96 (i.e., July-December 1995) were not met, mainly because of large off-budget outlays. Restructuring of key parastatals was delayed, and their financial performance in 1994/95 was worse than expected.  

Furthermore, there was little progress in establishing transparency and accountability in public finances. 1/  

Thus, at the conclusion of the l995 Article IV consultation on November l0, 1995, Executive Directors indicated that a rigorous implementation of the authorities' policies for l995/96, as described in the staff report (SM/95/279), would be an important step toward the elaboration of a comprehensive program that could be supported by a new ESAF arrangement. Directors stressed, in particular, the following:  

The overall fiscal deficit and the other major objectives of the 1995/96 budget should be achieved, and expenditure control and budgetary management must be strengthened; fiscal tightening woulc be necessary to avoid a further increase in interest rates. Public enterprise reform and privatization should be accelerated, including substantive progress in the commercialization of the National Cereals and Produce Board (NCPB), privatization of the telecommunications sector, and the restructuring of the railways Civil service reform should be pursued vigorously. Greater assurances are needed that the budget and the macroeconomic balances will not be undermined by corruption; this will require strict enforcement of the civil service code of conduct, full transparency of public finances, and additional recovery of misused public funds achieved.  

Since the last Article IV consultation, economic policy has been implemented in line with the views of the Executive Board. The Government's revised 1995/96 budget, which was adopted in late January 1996, calls for limiting the overall deficit (on a commitment basis and excluding grants) to K Sh l.0 billion (0.2 percent of GDP) in the second half of this fiscal year (January-June 1996), compared with K Sh 7.3 billion (1.7 percent of GDP) in the first half of the year (July-December 1995). 2/ Duties on petroleum products were increased in early February to raise K Sh 1 billion in 1995/96; tax collection efforts were intensified, including the collection of tax arrears of K Sh 1.7 billion of the National Social Security Fund (NSSF) in February; and total revenue was in line with the target by the end of March 1996. Thus, the overall deficit in the third quarter of this fiscal year (i.e., January-March l996) was limited to K Sh 0.9 billion, broadly in line with the indicative target set under the proposed program.  

Moreover, monetary policy was tightened during January-March 1996, in line with the objectives of the proposed program (Chart 2). 1/ Net domestic assets (NDA) of the Central Bank of Kenya (CBK) were reduced by K Sh 9.6 billion, or 13 percent of reserve money stock, between end-December 1995 and end-March l996, driven principally by the decline in net CBK credit to the Government (Table 5). As of end-March, NDA stood at K Sh 57.2 billion, or K Sh 5.5 billion below the indicative target. 2/ This was achieved through a substantial increase in treasury bill sales--with a consequent rise in interest rates to 26 percent in the primary market--as well as the avoidance of an increase in the Government's overdraft with the CBK. Reserve money declined by K Sh 2.8 billion, or at a seasonally adjusted annual rate of 13.8 percent in the first quarter of 1996, more than expected.  

Net international reserves (NIR) of the CBK rose sharply in March, in response to the tightening of monetary conditions. At end-March, gross international reserves amounted to $\cos\$563$ million, exceeding the indicative target by almost Us\$l00 million. 3/

Third, to ensure full transparency of public finances, the authorities have decided that: (a) all public contracts in excess of K Sh 10 million will be publicly tendered, and the minutes of the Central Tender Board published; (b) contract negotiations will be undertaken by a team of officials representing the Treasury, the relevant ministry, the Attorney General's Chambers, the Audit Inspectorate, and a ministry not related to the project; (c) the Government will not enter into any expenditure commitment that has not been provided for in the budget and approved by Parliament; (d) all new external loan commitments will be reported to Parliament at the beginning of each session; and (e) the budget outturn will be published on a quarterly basis. While the above-mentioned actions are important, policy implementation in the first quarter of l996 was not without problems. Nevertheless, the adoption of a large number of corrective measures as discussed above, together with the generally successful implementation of macroeconomic and structural policies over the past three years, provides a favorable track record of policy performance. In addition, the PFP was adopted by the Government after extensive consultations involving all the relevant ministers, and was made available to the Kenyan public with a foreword by the Head of State. Moreover, a Presidential Economic Commission, to be chaired by the Head of State, was established to monitor and ensure full implementation of the PFP. These actions are indicative of the Government's commitment to economic reforms in general, and the proposed program in particular.  

# III. Policy Issues for the Medium Term  

The most pressing challenge facing Kenya is to enhance investor confidence. This is the key requirement for sustaining a high rate of economic growth, maintaining low inflation, and thereby achieving a lasting reduction in unemployment and poverty over the medium term. Nearly 40 percent of Kenya's population now lives in absolute poverty, and 25 percent of the work force is unemployed. As the labor force is projected to increase by more than $4$ percent a year, real GDP growth of at least 6 percent is needed if the unemployment rate is to be reduced over the medium term.  

Accordingly, the proposed program envisages an increase in fixed capital formation from 21.7 percent of GDP in 1995 to 23.2 percent in 1998, with most of the increase taking place in the private sector. The overall rate of return on investment is expected to improve as a result of the ongoing structural reforms, notably the enhanced efficiency of parastatal operations. The projected increase in investment, together with the desired reduction in the external current account deficit, will require a rise in  

national savings from 19.8 percent of GDP in 1995 to 24.5 percent in 1998.1/  

To ensure that this macroeconomic scenario is realized, the authorities intend to pursue the following policy objectives:  

Consolidation of fiscal adjustment. The aim is: (a) to reduce domestic debt 2/ from 28.1 percent of GDP at the end of 1994/95 to 18.4 percent at the end of 1997/98 through a further reduction in the overall budget deficit (Chart 3); (b) to lower the overall expenditure levels by 2.O percent of GDP over the three-year period through savings in wages (0.9 percent of GDP) and domestic interest payments (2.8 percent of GDP); (c) to improve the quality of public expenditure by increasing outlays for operations and maintenance and for social programs, and by ensuring that core projects account for at least 75 percent of total development expenditure; (d) to lessen the heavy tax burden by lowering the maximum rates on income tax, value-added tax (VAT), and import duties, while broadening the tax base (total revenue would decline by 0.7 percent of GDP; and (e) to boost government savings from 5.2 percent of GDP in 1994/95 to 6.6 percent by 1997/98 (PFP, paragraphs 19-23).  

Privatization and restructuring of the parastatal sector. The Government's strategy is to privatize (e.g., Kenya Airways, Kenya Refinery, and National Bank of Kenya), or to contract out the management of essential functions to the private sector (e.g., KPA and KR). All of the remaining 96 nonstrategic parastatals will be divested by the end of 1997 (PFP, paragraphs 37-38). These measures should be sufficient to reverse the recent serious deterioration in key infrastructure, in addition to offering ample investment opportunities for the private sector.  

1/ The staff projects that nongovernment savings would increase from an estimated 12.5 percent of GDP in 1995 to l6.2 percent by 1998. Although this increase is substantial, it represents a recovery from the unusually low level in l995. In that year, nongovernment savings declined by 5.7 percent of GDP in response to the sharp increase in government savings and the recovery of private consumption from the previously compressed levels. Indeed, the projected l998 level is significantly below the average in 199l-94. The expected substantial improvement in parastatal financial performance should also help achieve the required increase in national savings.  

2/ Domestic interest payments accounted for 5.7 percent of GDP in 1994/95, or about 20 percent of ordinary revenue. The debt is predominantly short-term, which makes its management difficult and highly vulnerable to conditions in the financial markets.  

Avoidance of the recurrence of misuse of public funds. Strict adherence to the principle of transparency and accountability should not only ensure a level playing field for all investors, but also enhance confidence in the Government's economic management.  

Promotion of regional economic integration. Recently, the Heads of State of Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda announced their intention to promote the economic integration of their countries (the former East Africa Economic Community), and inaugurated an administrative secretariat. This is to be followed by establishing full convertibility of the currencies of these countries, and by harmonizing customs and other tax structures.  

Further development of outward-looking competitive markets. To this end, a liberal trade regime is to be maintained, while establishing appropriate safeguards against dumping, and the banking system is to be made more competitive. Furthermore, judiciary and other institutions are to be strengthened, so as to adequately protect the enforcement of property rights and contractual obligations, and to provide adequate economic security to investors (MEFP, paragraphs 36 and 39).  

# IV. Economic Policies for 1996  

The proposed ESAF-supported program, as supplemented by the PFP, sets out a medium-term framework that addresses the key requirements for growth outlined above. In this context, the economic program for l996 aims at maintaining real GDP growth at 5 percent; limiting the annual rate of inflation to no more than 5 percent; reducing the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) from 4.2 percent of GDP in 1995 to 1.3 percent of GDP in l996; and increasing gross official reserves from the equivalent of 2.1 months of imports at end-1995 to 2.9 months of imports at end-1996. Additional fiscal adjustment, a tight monetary policy, and further structural reforms will be implemented in order to achieve these objectives.  

# 1. Fiscal policy  

The fiscal program for l995/96 incorporates the following main elements:  

A reduction in the overall deficit (on a commitment basis and excluding grants) by 0.7 percent of GDP to 1.9 percent of GDP. The deficit will be more than covered by external grants and loan disbursements, and the stock of pending bills and domestic debt will be reduced by 0.5 and 1.3 percentage points of GDP, respectively. 1/  

An increase in total revenue by 2.3 percent of GDP, to 34.0 percent of GDP. This reflects collection of tax arrears from the NSSF and other parastatals (equivalent to 1.l percent of GDP); increases in taxes on petroleum products; and higher excise taxes on various other products (MEFP, paragraphs 16 and 17).  

Cuts in maximum tax rates. The standard VAT rate was reduced from 18 percent to l5 percent; 2/ the. three highest rates were cut by 5 percentage points each to 40, 25, and 15 percent; the highest marginal income tax rate was reduced by 5 percentage points to 35 percent; and the drought levy was discontinued.  

A broadening of the tax base. The presumptive income tax was reintroduced on agricultural income; $31$ the coverage of the VAT was extended to all products, except food items and certain unprocessed products; and some customs exemptions were eliminated. Moreover, the newly created Kenya Revenue Authority (KRA) has been strengthened, with appointments in February l996 of a new Chairman of the Board, Commissioner General, and Commissioner of Customs.  

An increase in total expenditure by l.7 percent of GDP, to 36.0 percent of GDP. Allocations for operations and maintenance were raised in order to improve the delivery of essential services (nonwage/noninterest recurrent expenditures were increased by 1.7 percent of GDP). Outlays for capital expenditures were also raised by 0.4 percent of GDP. There were also large unanticipated claims (l.2 percent of GDP) for: the transfer of budgetary resources to help Kenya Cooperative Creameries (Kcc) pay most of its debt to farmers; 4/ the completion of the down-scaled  

Eldoret airport project; the implementation of the national identity cards project; and the purchase of a presidential airplane. 1/  

The l996/97 budget will envisage a further reduction in the overall deficit to l.6 percent of GDP. It will also entail a decline in the revenue/GDP ratio (2.3 percent of GDP) as well as a large expenditure cut (2.7 percent of GDP), accompanied by an improvement in the quality of expenditure. Specific expenditure decisions will be taken on the basis of a public expenditure review to be initiated during the first half of 1996, with assistance of the World Bank. The details of the l996/97 budget policy will be presented at the time of the midterm review under the proposed first annual ESAF arrangement.  

Another important undertaking in the proposed program is to rectify the persistent weaknesses in expenditure control and budgetary management:  

The established budgetary process will be respected, and all expenditure commitments will be provided for and approved by Parliament.   
The development budget will include only the projects that are contained in the public investment program (PIP); and the Government will improve project selection criteria for including   
projects into the PIP (PFP, paragraphs 21-23).   
Control and monitoring of spending by individual ministries will be strengthened. 2/ Close monitoring of expenditures on a   
ministry-by-ministry basis, and of their consistency with the commitment ceilings, has been initiated. The Treasury will fully cover the expenditures that it has authorized.   
Disciplinary actions will be taken against permanent secretaries who have exceeded their spending authorizations.  

The staff will closely monitor the implementation of the above expenditure control measures. A long-term advisor will be assigned shortly under the Fund's technical assistance program. Moreover, expenditure and other budgetary developments will continue to be evaluated on a monthly  

basis, both at headquarters and by the Fund's Resident Representative in Nairobi.  

As regards the social dimension of adjustment, targeted poverty interventions and increased access of the poor to social services are planned. The quality and availability of health services are expected to improve as budgetary resources are reallocated to promote a shift from hospital care to preventive and primary health care (PFP, paragraph 55). Public resources will also be reallocated from university education towards primary and secondary education, particularly for disadvantaged students (PFP, paragraph 58). At the same time, the private provision of these services is encouraged, and decision-making and funding arrangements are decentralized.  

# 2. Monetary and exchange rate policies  

The overriding objective of monetary policy will be the pursuit of price stability (MEFP, paragraphs 24-26). Under the program, NDA of the CBK are targeted to decline by about K Sh 7 billion (about 10 percent of reserve money stock) in 1996, principally through net repayments by the Government. Reserve money is slated to remain broadly unchanged, as the programmed decline in NDA is expected to be fully offset by the targeted buildup in international reserves. Consistent with the reserve money target, and an unchanged required reserve ratio, broad money growth of 2.4 percent is projected for l996, substantially less than the growth in nominal GDP. The low monetary expansion is intended to correct for the higher-than-envisaged growth in 1995.  

The program assumes that foreign exchange intervention will be limited so that net foreign assets (NFA) of the CBK remain within a fixed band of plus or minus K Sh 1.5 billion around the targeted path. This will ensure that reserve money, too, will remain within a band of K Sh l.5 billion around the programmed path, provided of course that NDA is kept on target. This strategy aims to preserve the stability of monetary aggregates, while accepting consequent fluctuations in exchange and interest rates.  

A key issue is how to cope with the volatility of capital flows, a problem that has made monetary management particularly difficult over the last three years. l/ Under the program, foreign exchange market pressures will initially be absorbed in part by foreign exchange intervention and also by exchange rate adjustments. In the event that strong foreign exchange market pressures persist, the authorities will consult with the Fund staff to identify the sources of pressure, and adopt an appropriate fiscal and monetary policy response. The staff will continue to closely monitor developments in the foreign exchange market, with a view to detecting any emerging problems at an early stage.  

The authorities are under strong pressure from the public to take measures to lower interest rates, which have generally been high over the last l2 months (Chart 5). The program envisages a gradual reduction in rates, but only as a result of a decline in the Government's domestic debt and an enhanced credibility in its policy stance. If the envisaged reduction in the interest rates does not take place or in the event of unanticipated adverse developments in the financial market, the authorities will consult with the staff, and modify fiscal and/or monetary policies as appropriate.  

The CBK intends to adopt a number of measures to improve the efficiency of the existing monetary policy instruments and to develop new ones, as recommended by a recent Fund technical assistance mission. These measures include the following: putting in place book entry and settlement facilities to strengthen the security of treasury bills; introducing two-way open market operations through repurchase transactions; establishing a Lombardtype window; relying more on primary auctions through a gradual termination of intra-week tap sales; and applying the cash reserve requirement on a weekly average basis to provide banks greater flexibility in their treasury management. These measures should also make treasury bills a more liquid asset, and encourage the development of an active secondary market in these bills.  

# 3. Financial system reform  

Strengthening the CBK is a key in improving the conduct of monetary policy, developing new policy instruments, and deepening the financial system in Kenya. Accordingly, the CBK Act is to be amended in June 1996 to make the Bank more independent, e.g., by strictly limiting, by law, the ability of the CBK to extend credit to the Government, and by establishing security of tenure for the Governor. In addition, CBK's technical capacity will be strengthened, through technical assistance and other training programs.  

The financial system is to be made more competitive and sounder through the conversion of nonbank financial institutions (NBFI's) to commercial banks and through reforms of government-owned financial institutions (MEFP, paragraphs 36-38, as well as PFP, paragraphs 15-l8). All NBFI's have now met the cash reserve requirement, and the conversion of NBFIs to banks is continuing. Combined with the freeze on the issuance of new banking licenses in l996, the conversion is expected to strengthen the banking sector by creating fewer, yet stronger banks. l/ Furthermore, NSSF will  

be converted into an autonomous pension fund, which should contribute to a more efficient use of the large pool of savings presently held at this institution; necessary legislative changes, as well as an actuarial study, are being prepared with the assistance of the World Bank.  

4. Reform of the public enterprise sector  

The program features a major privatization/restructuring drive for Kenya's parastatal sector, as follows:  

The privatization of KA is to be completed in April l996, with flotation of government shares equivalent to 5l percent of KA's capital on the NSE. 1/ Together with the sales of 26 percent of the capital to KLM in December l995, this will leave the Government with a share of 23 percent.  

KPTC will be financially restructured and separated into three entities (telecommunications, posts, and a regulatory authority) by the end of 1996, a structural benchmark of the program. Subsequently, up to 30 percent of government shares in the new telecommunications company will be offered to the private sector through the participation of a strategic partner and through public flotation by March l997. KPTC's monopoly on telecommunications services will be removed within the next five years.  

The commercialization of NCPB by the end of 1996 is another structural benchmark. Experts will be appointed by end-June (a performance criterion) to prepare and implement a program to enable NcPB to become commercially viable and free of Government intervention by end-December.  

KR will pursue its financial restructuring; contract out locomotive maintenance (a structural benchmark under the program); rationalize and close, if appropriate, loss-making activities; and privatize its marine service operations on Lake Victoria.  

The container terminal at the Mombasa port will be contracted out to the Port of Singapore Authority by end-June (a performance criterion of the program). This, together with the recent managerial changes at KPA, is expected to bring about a needed turnaround in its operations.  

The Government will reduce, in 1996, its shareholdings in the National Bank of Kenya and Kenya Commercial Bank to less than 25 percent and 50 percent of the capital of these two major banks, respectively, through flotation on the NSE. It will also initiate work on the divestiture of three large public enterprises (Kenya Pipeline Co. Ltd., Kenya Petroleum Company, and Nyayo Buses Corporation).  

As for the nonstrategic parastatals, the Government will divest itself of roughly one-half of the remaining 96 enterprises in 1996 and the rest in l997. The privatization process will be kept transparent through reliance on competitive bidding, publication of details of sales of companies, quarterly reports on the overall implementation of the privatization program, and semiannual audits.  

The program also targets, as a performance criterion, an improvement in the combined operating profits of the five key parastatals (KPA, KPLc, KPTC, KR, and NCPB) by 0.5 percent of GDP to 1.4 percent in l995/96 (Table 6); targets for the operating profit and other performance indicators have been established with each of these enterprises. The targeted improvement in the combined operating profits is based on the stronger profitability of KPLC and KPA. The former is benefiting from the full impact of the March 1995 tariff increase, l/ while the latter is recovering from unsound financial management in l995. On the negative side, NCPB is expected to record losses of about 0.4 percent of GDP as a result of its intervention in the maize market in l994/95; the financial performance of KR continues to be impaired by technical problems in equipment availability; and KPT's performance remains weak, in part because of strong competition from international operators.  

# 5. Civil service reform  

The civil service reform seeks the establishment of a leaner and more efficient public employment structure. The number of civil servants is to be reduced in 1996 by 13,000, or 6 percent of the total civil service, through natural attrition, the Voluntary Early Retirement Scheme, and position rationalization. The structure of the Ministry of Health and the Ministry of Agriculture will be rationalized by end-l996; four additional ministries are expected to complete their rationalization plans by mid-1997, and another six ministries by end-l998. Remuneration of the civil service is to be upgraded to narrow the existing salary differential with the private sector, with a view to maintaining and attracting competent professionals.  

The program includes, as a performance criterion, an undertaking that direct controls on prices and external trade will not be reimposed. The repeated imposition of import bans on certain agricultural commodities reflects in part the fact that the legislative framework has not been amended to take account of the change from a regime of widespread direct controls to one free of government intervention. For example, the NCPB Act still contains provisions that authorize the Minister for Agriculture to prohibit exports or imports of maize and wheat, and restrict movements of these commodities within the country. Provisions for similar controls on sugar and dairy products exist in the Sugar Act and the Dairy Board Act, respectively. The midterm review under the first annual ESAF arrangement will focus on the legislative changes needed to bring this legislation into conformity with the current trade regime, and thus help consolidate the liberal economic and trade environment.  

The program also envisages the establishment of an appropriate antidumping mechanism. Since May l993, there have essentially been no quantitative restrictions on imports, while the average import duty was reduced from 23.2 percent in 1992/93 to 19.9 percent in 1994/95, with a further decline slated for 1995/96. 2/ Consequently, imports of heavily subsidized products (e.g., maize, sugar, and certain textile products) have increased substantially. As operationally effective anti-dumping mechanisms do not exist at present, farmers and certain manufacturers have incurred losses because of the subsidized imports. The Government has requested technical assistance from the World Trade Organization (WTo) to prepare anti-dumping legislation consistent with WTo regulations, to be presented to Parliament by end-1996.  

The Government will continue to pursue a prudent debt policy and to improve the administrative capacity for effecting debt service payments so as to prevent a recurrence of external payments arrears. The debt ceiling covers all nonconcessional loans, with a subceiling for those with a maturity of less than 30 years. The overall ceiling under the program has been set at a level sufficient to accommodate nonconcessional loans with a 30-year maturity at an interest rate of 2.5 percent, which are expected to be signed in l996 mainly with a bilateral agency; these loans will be cofinanced or coordinated with the World Bank. The subceiling is set at a low level to strictly limit the contraction of debt on commercial terms.  

In response to interest expressed by some banks, the Government has approached commercial banks with a menu of options for restructuring the existing debt. The options include a debt buy-back, concessional rescheduling, and debt-to-debt swaps. The authorities intend to complete the negotiation with commercial banks quickly, so that arrears that had accumulated by end-1993 will be settled in 1996/97. 1/  

Kenya maintains a multiple currency practice arising from certain obligations that existed under the Exchange Risk Assumption Fund (ERAF) prior to its abolition in June 1994 (EBs/94/l4l). The inventory of existing commitments under the ERAF was completed in l995 and the authorities intend, by end-l996, to verify the validity of the claims and reach understandings with the claimants on the settlement of the outstanding obligations.  

# 7. Discipline in public finances  

The program attaches considerable importance to avoiding the recurrence of misuse of public funds. It envisages: (a) strict application of the civil service code of conduct; and (b) full implementation of the measures to ensure transparency of public finances, as described in MEFP, paragraph 47. It also calls for further recovery of the misused public funds of K Sh 2.6 billion in l996, to bring the cumulative recovery to K Sh 17.5 billion by the end of the year; this would leave an additional K Sh 4 billion to complete the full recovery of the funds that were misused in 1992- 93. 2/ The implementation of these measures will form an integral part of the midterm review.

V. Balance of Payments Outlook, Sustainability of Debt, and Capacity to Repay the Fund  

# 1. Balance of payments outlook  

The medium-term balance of payments outlook is based on the assumption that restrained financial policies will continue and that external competitiveness will remain broadly at the current level. The current account deficit (excluding official transfers) is projected to decline 4.2 percent of GDP in l995 to 1.3 percent of GDP in 1996, and further to 0.8 percent of GDP in l998 (Tables 8 and 9). This improvement is expected to come mainly from a slowdown in import demand and a recovery of tourism earnings, following the depreciation of the Kenya shilling in 1995.  

Import demand (in volume terms) is projected to decline in l996 by 5.2 percent, and then to gradually pick up and grow in line with aggregate demand. The 1996 decline is explained by the fact that government imports are expected to fall back to the trend, following an exceptional jump in 1995 related to the purchase of a presidential airplane and the construction of the Eldoret airport. l/ At the same time, export growth is projected to slow from 15.9 percent in 1995 to 4.l percent in 1996; thereafter, export volume is projected to expand in line with the demand in partner countries. Tourism earnings are assumed to recover from a decline in 1995 and to increase by about $3-4$ percent annually in U.s. dollar terms. Kenya's terms of trade are estimated to worsen modestly in 1996, as coffee prices are expected to stabilize in the middle of the year, and remain broadly stable thereafter.  

The capital account projections assume an increase in project lending by the World Bank (to some $\yen5200$ million annually), and broadly unchanged lending by bilateral creditors. Program lending is expected to be phased out gradually and to be zero after 1998. The capital account also includes a conservative assumption on foreign direct investment inflows at around $\yen50$ million a year.  

On this basis, modest financing gaps are projected for l997-98, totaling about Us\$l30 million. These gaps are expected to be closed on concessional terms, mainly by official bilateral creditors. The estimates of the financing gaps incorporate funding requirements for the targeted increase in gross international reserves by $\tt t s s s540$ million, to nearly $\mathtt{U S S1}$ billion or 4.3 months of imports, by end of 1998.  

# 2. Sustainability of external debt 1/  

Kenya's external debt burden is projected to decline significantly over the next 5-l0 years. The present value of external debt service is projected to drop from about 157 percent of exports to 109 percent in 2000 and further to 77 percent in 2005; the debt/export ratio of 200-250 percent is generally considered as the threshold for heavy indebtedness. The projected reduction in the debt service burden, which is fairly robust with respect to less favorable assumptions for exports and the terms of new financing, will come mainly from a significant improvement in the composition of Kenya's external loan portfolio. Over the next 5-l0 years, Kenya is expected to replace most of the nonconcessional debt with loans on concessional terms. By 200l, Kenya will have repaid most of its nonconcessional debt to the IBRD, all of its arrears to official bilateral creditors that were rescheduled on nonconcessional terms, $21$ and a significant part of its commercial debt.  

The projected decline in the debt service burden is critically dependent on continued tight financial policies to limit the Government's borrowing requirement, and on a prudent debt policy to ensure that new borrowing is undertaken on appropriate terms and used for productive purposes. Assuming strict adherence to such policies, Kenya should be able to meet its current and future debt service obligations without recourse to exceptional financing.  

# 3. Capacity to repay the Fund  

If the full amount of the proposed ESAF arrangement is disbursed, Kenya's obligations to the Fund would rise to SDR 264 million (l32 percent of quota) by the end of l998, and its annual obligations to the Fund would increase from 1.5 percent of exports of goods and services in l995 to a peak of 2.5 percent in l997 before declining markedly (Table l0). In view of the projected strengthening of the external payments position, the relatively cautious assumptions underlying the medium-term scenario, and the authorities' track record of meeting their obligations to the Fund on a timely  

basis, there should be little doubt about Kenya's capacity to repay the Fund.  

# VI. Staff Appraisal  

Kenya has requested a new three-year ESAF arrangement in support of a macroeconomic and structural adjustment program for 1996-98. Kenya's request is buttressed by the implementation of bold liberalization measures, considerable fiscal adjustment, and the pursuit of generally tight monetary policies over the last three years, as well as the adoption of a number of corrective measures in the first quarter of 1996.  

In the staff's view, the cornerstone of the authorities' new program is further fiscal consolidation, complemented by significant improvements in the tax structure and in the quality of budgetary outlays. Thus, as called for in the program, maximum tax rates must be reduced further, the Kenya Revenue Authority made fully operational, less productive outlays avoided, and appropriations for essential services augmented, especially for priority infrastructure, health, and basic education. Furthermore, the established budgetary processes must be strictly observed, expenditure control and management strengthened, and sanctions applied promptly in cases of unauthorized spending or other irregular budgetary practices.  

The conduct of monetary policy will continue to be difficult in the period ahead. Although interest rates are currently high in real terms, the staff believes that they cannot be lowered substantially unless there is a sizable reduction in the Government's domestic debt and a lessening of the risk premium that investors are apparently demanding. As called for in the program, net domestic assets (NDA) of the Central Bank of Kenya (CBK) should be reduced substantially to stem the source of monetary expansion and pressures in the foreign exchange market, while foreign exchange intervention should be strictly limited. If necessary, fiscal adjustment should be increased to facilitate lowering interest rates and to avoid undue appreciation of the exchange rate.  

The staff welcomes the planned amendment of the CBK Act, designed to give the Central Bank greater autonomy. It also recommends that the CBK strengthen its monetary policy instruments, through the development of open market operations based on repurchase agreements and the establishment of a Lombard-type window.  

The program rightly accords high priority to the privatization and restructuring of the parastatal sector, which has been a major obstacle to sustaining a high rate of economic growth in Kenya. In particular, it envisages further major steps for the privatization/restructuring of Kenya Airways, the Kenya Posts and Telecommunications Corporation, the National Cereals and Produce Board, the Kenya Ports Authority, and Kenya Railways; the sale of the Government's shares of the National Bank of Kenya and Kenya Commercial Bank; the initiation of privatization of other \*strategic" enterprises; and the completion of divestiture of \*nonstrategic" enterprises by the end of 1997. The staff considers this parastatal reform program appropriately ambitious, and urges the authorities to complete it on schedule.  

The maintenance of a liberal and open trade regime as well as an exchange system free of controls is another cornerstone of the program. The staff attaches great importance to this policy stance, not only because it is critical for the development of a competitive outward-looking economy, but also because it underscores the Government's commitment to economic reform. In this connection, the staff welcomes the recent announcements by the Heads of State of Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda, affirming their intention to move swiftly toward closer economic integration. The staff also urges the authorities to adhere to the timetable for eliminating the extant multiple currency practice by the end of 1996.  

In the staff's view, the key to translating Kenya's recent economic gains to high and sustained economic growth is to enhance the confidence of private investors. Full transparency and strict discipline in public finances must therefore be assured. It is crucial to demonstrate--by further strong actions--that the Government will not tolerate the misuse of public funds. It is equally crucial that questionable financial operations not be allowed to occur. In addition, the efficiency and professionalism of the civil service will have to be enhanced. Moreover, it is necessary to strengthen the judiciary, and other institutional structures, in order to ensure more adequate enforcement of economic rights and contractual obligations, and to assure economic security to investors. Lastly, and most importantly, confidence in the Government's economic management can be reinforced only if the proposed program is implemented in full.  

The program does, of course, entail some risks of policy slippages. The next presidential and parliamentary elections, scheduled to be held by late l997, could generate pressures for increased budgetary spending, easing monetary policy, and deviating from other programmed policies. The staff urges the authorities to solidify their resolve to resist such pressures, further advance economic reforms, and implement the program in its entirety.  

Kenya's relations with the bilateral donor community have at times been delicate, as donor concerns have encompassed not only economic matters, but also a broad range of governance issues. In view of the importance of donor assistance for Kenya's economic development, the staff urges the authorities to address donor concerns without delay. The staff welcomes the indications of donor support at the recent Consultative Group meeting, and hopes that new aid commitments will be sufficient to meet Kenya's external financing requirements over the next three years.  

The staff believes that the authorities' economic program for 1996-98 is comprehensive and appropriate. The publication of the PFP and the establishment of the Presidential Economic Commission to monitor its implementation are indications of the authorities' commitment to the program. The prior actions already taken are a further indication of their resolve.  

Close monitoring of the program through regular consultations with the staff, comprehensive performance criteria, frequent reports to the Executive Board, and formal reviews provide greater assurances that the program will be successfully implemented. Accordingly, the staff recommends approval of Kenya's request for the three-year arrangement under the enhanced structural adjustment facility, and the first annual arrangement thereunder.

As of December 31, 1995, the World Bank had committed to Kenya $\$3.2$ billion, of which $\$2.8$ billion had been fully disbursed (Statement A). Investments (loans and equity) by IFC totaled \$l83.95 million, of which $\$59$ million remains undisbursed (Statement B).  

# 1. Agriculture  

The Bank's dialogue on agriculture focuses on supporting the broad thrust of the Government's sector development strategy and sector priorities. This strategy is based on realigning the policy framework and the incentive structure, and encouraging a more competitive marketing system with increased participation of the private sector in marketing and distribution of agricultural commodities. The Government's development strategy also recognizes the need to provide support for the core programs of research and extension. The Bank recognizes the need to significantly accelerate implementation, particularly with regard to liberalization of processing and marketing of agricultural commodities and for restructuring the role of Government in the provision of strategic services.  

The Bank's current involvement in supporting agriculture and environmental initiatives in Kenya comprises seven projects. These project: support the development of agricultural research and extension, enhance diversification and export orientation, and encourage natural resources management and conservation. On the institutional development side, the Bank is supporting the reorganization of the Ministry of Agriculture, Livestock Development and Marketing, to develop its management and policy analysis capacity. On environmental/natural resources management, the Bank financed the preparation of the National Environmental Action Plan, and is assisting in wildlife conservation and management. The Emergency Drought Recovery Project, approved in February 1993 in response to the inadequate and irregular rainfall in late l99l and early 1992, also has a component that focuses on mitigating the adverse environmental consequences of drought. In the area of natural resources management, the Bank is supporting a Forestry Development Project aimed at improving management of Kenya's forest resources. In addition, the Bank Board approved in December 1995 an Arid Lands Resource Management Project that aims at: (a) reducing chronic poverty in the arid lands; (b) halting the deterioration of the natural resource base; (c) linking the largely marginalized communities of the arid lands with the rest of the economy; and (d) initiating the diversification from a pastoral economy. Under preparation is a Second National Agricultural Research Project that would set the stage for developing a sustainable research program that is consistent with the needs of a rapidly diversifying sector.  

# 2. Infrastructure  

A major impediment to private sector development is inadequately maintained and inefficiently operated physical infrastructure. Public sector, including parastatal, inefficiency is at the heart of many of these problems. Therefore, the careful restructuring of public expenditures and enhanced financial accountability of strategic state enterprises are  

crucial. Sustained progress in rehabilitation, maintenance and expansion of capacity in the respective infrastructure subsectors requires an appropriate framework of coherent sector development plans and policies, and the requisite institutional and organizational reforms to implement them, including an appropriate role for the private sector, and sound corporate governance and financial policies on management.  

The Bank has five ongoing infrastructure projects. The Geothermal Development Project's principle objective is to assist the country in preparing the necessary expansion of its generating capacity at least cost through utilization of indigenous energy sources. The Third Nairobi Water Supply Project includes major production, treatment and distribution works for Nairobi, and intends for the Nairobi Water and Sewerage Department to operate as a financially autonomous entity. The Second Mombasa Water Project provides for some rehabilitation of the.coastal water system and the preparation of a large production project for the region. In January 1996, the Bank Board approved two additional infrastructure projects: an Urban Transport Project, that will involve investments in new and expanded road links, intersections, and public transport facilities, and a Nairobi-Mombasa Road Rehabilitation Project which will support rehabilitation of Kenya's chief transport corridor. In an advanced stage of preparation is an Energy Sector Investment Project that is expected to increase generating capacity, reform energy pricing and encourage private sector participation in the sector.  

# 3. Education and Health  

The Bank's attention is currently directed at developing viable financing and management capacity in these sectors. In education, this involves: (a) improving the quality of primary, secondary and university education to include technical training; (b) restructuring key sectoral institutions and policies to create incentives for more efficient service delivery in general; and (c) shifting public expenditure subsidies away from higher education and the expansion of the teaching force, to improving access to quality basic education, especially in rural areas. In health, this involves: (a) the development of prevention programs for sexually transmitted diseases, including AIDs; (b) a shift in resources from curative to efficient primary care; and (c) a clarification of the respective roles of the private and public sectors. In both of these sectors, the shift of emphasis from rapid expansion to quality and equity improvements needs to be accompanied by a commensurate effort to build institutional capacity.  

The Bank's ongoing human resource operations number six. The Universities Investment Project focuses specifically on consolidating and developing the university system, with particular emphasis on limiting the growth of government budgetary resources devoted to the public universities by promoting cost sharing and improved investment planning. The Micro- and Small Enterprise Training and Technology Project aims to enhance entrepreneurial development in the private sector to reduce constraints to employment promotion and enhance incomes in the informal sector. The Third and Fourth Population Projects, in addition to providing support for the  

Government's family planning services, provide financial assistance for drugs and information/education activities for addressing the rising incidence of sexually transmitted infections. The Health Rehabilitation Project is intended to support the Government's reform of the health sector by rehabilitating Kenyatta National Hospital, thus reducing its burden on the overall budget and permitting an increase in expenditures on preventive and primary health care by improving the delivery of health service in the Nairobi area and by preparing for future policy and managerial reform. The Sexually Transmitted Infections Project, approved in March 1995, has as objective reducing both the incidence and impact of such infections, including HIV/AIDS, in a cost-effective manner and to reduce the direct costs of AIDS and the workload of health facilities. A major objective will be to strengthen the institutional capacity at the national and provincial/district levels to design, implement and evaluate appropriate interventions. The Bank is currently at an advanced stage of preparation of an Early Child Development Project which will aim at reinforcing existing institutions and improving the quality of services offered to pre-school age children.  

# 4. Public Sector Management  

The Bank has provided two credits to improve public sector management in Kenya. A Parastatal Reform and Privatization Technical Assistance Project provides overall technical support for the Government's privatization program as well as for the preparation of specific restructuring plans for those enterprises remaining in the public portfolio. A Civil Service Reform Project provides support for the Government's efforts to reduce the size of the civil service and rationalize specific ministries.  

# 5. Adjustment Lending  

After two structural adjustment loans in the early l980s, a sectoral approach to adjustment was adopted on the premise that sector ministries would exercise greater ownership and accountability if they were directly involved in the design and implementation of reforms. Since l986, Kenya has received six sectoral adjustment credits. The final tranches of the Agricultural Sector Adjustment Operation, the Industrial Sector Adjustment Credit and the Financial Sector Adjustment Credit were disbursed in 1988, 1990 and 199l, respectively. However, implementation progress under the three adjustment operations that followed, i.e., the Second Agricultural Sector Adjustment Operation (ASAo II) and the Export Development Project (EDP), both approved in l99l, and the EDSAC, approved in l992, was more mixed, with cancellation or delays in tranche releases.  

In light of this experience, the Bank concluded that new adjustment lending would require that the Government make progress on agreed sectoral reforms across-the-board, with a particular focus on improved public sector management and private sector development. Based on commitments made in the Policy Framework Paper for l996-98 the Bank is at an advanced stage of preparing, and hopes to negotiate shortly, a Structural Adjustment Credit that would support agreed policy reforms in the areas of budgetary control, public expenditure allocations, and civil service and parastatal reform.

The quality of the economic data base in Kenya is generally satisfactory (Table l). A detailed account of various sectors and the corresponding statistical data are provided in a comprehensive annual publication (Economic Survey) by the Central Bureau of Statistics (CBS). The financial resources available to the CBs do not permit more comprehensive and timely data. Monetary, exchange rate, and some external data are published on a monthly and quarterly basis by the Central Bank in its Monthly Economic Reviews and Quarterly Statistical Bulletins. Core financial data are available to the Fund on a regular basis. The last technical assistance mission in statistics to Kenya was in 1992.  

# I. National accounts  

National accounts (annual only) and the supporting sectoral data are prepared by the CBs, and are generally of good quality. National accounts data at constant prices use an outdated base year (l982); the authorities intend to rebase their constant price national accounts.  

# 2. Prices and production  

A consumer price index (Nairobi) is compiled by the CBS and is reported on a monthly basis without significant lags. There is no producer price index but the authorities intend to compile one. The index on manufacturing production needs to be rebased, as it uses an outdated base year (l976) and is available only on an annual basis.  

# 3. Government finance statistics  

The staff is provided with current information on central government fiscal accounts, with appropriate disaggregation, on a monthly basis with a short lag. The recording of external financing and expenditure directly financed from abroad remains, however, an area for improvement.  

# 4. Monetary statistics  

Comprehensive financial data are compiled by the Central Bank of Kenya (CBK) and reported to the staff and IFS on a timely basis. Data on the central bank balance sheet and international reserves are made available to the staff, usually within a week from the date they are compiled. However, data on deposit money banks and data on average deposit and lending rates are not current in IFs. The staff encouraged the authorities to provide regular monthly reporting of interest rate data for publication in IFS.  

# 5. Balance of payments statistics  

Kenya reports balance of payments data on a regular basis to the IFS, although with a considerable lag. The overall quality of data is reasonably good for trade transactions; data for other current account and capital account transactions, most notably private sector capital flows, are poor.  

External trade data from the customs are reported on a monthly basis, although with significant lags. Annual trade volume and price indices are compiled using l982 as the base year. The timely availability of customs data as well as the recording of external public financing in the balance of payments, especially imports financed directly by foreign aid, need to be improved.

After having borrowed extensively on nonconcessional terms in the second half of the l980s and in the early 1990s, most of the external borrowing in the last few years has been on concessional terms. This is reflected in the decline in the average interest rate on new commitments, from close to 5 percent in l986 to about 2 percent in l994, and the increase in the average maturity from below 20 years in l986 to about 30 years in 1994. Consequently, the grant element in new borrowing has increased from about 33 percent in l986 to about 62 percent in 1994.

1. Over the last three years, Kenya has achieved remarkable progress in macroeconomic and structural reforms. The government budget deficit was reduced from 11l.4 percent of GDP in 1992/93 to 2.6 percent in 1994/95, money supply was brought under control, and all controls on prices, foreign exchange, imports, and internal marketing were removed. Moreover, important headway was made in reducing the size of the civil service and in privatising the parastatal sector. Thus, the economy recovered from a period of stagnation to achieve real GDP growth of 5 percent in 1995, and inflation declined from the peak of over l00 percent in early 1993 to 6.9 percent in December 1995. Kenya has benefitted from the Fund's support, initially under the staff-monitored programme, and, subsequently, under the one-year ESAF arrangement.  

2. These achievements mark the beginning of an even stronger economic reform effort that targets the remaining weaknesses in our economic management. Over the last two decades, per capita income has not risen appreciably in real terms, and nearly 40 percent of the population continues to live in absolute poverty. This is unacceptable. The fundamental objective of the Kenya Government is to sustain a high level of economic growth, keep inflation in single digits, and thus achieve a lasting reduction in poverty and unemployment. Accordingly, the Government will intensify and accelerate economic reform. The focal points of the reform over the next three years will be to: (a) further enforce fiscal discipline; (b) ensure transparency and accountability in public finances; (c) build an efficient and professional civil service; (d) privatise and restructure key parastatals, in particular the Kenya Posts and Telecommunications Corporation, Kenya Ports Authority, Kenya Power and Lighting Company, and the National Cereals and Produce Board; and (e) rebuild roads, power, water, and other infrastructure.

# I. Economic Performance in 1995  

2.  Macroeconomic developments in l995 were broadly favorable. The economic recovery continued and preliminary estimates indicate that real GDP growth accelerated to 5 percent in 1995. Inflation continued to decline and stood at 6.9 percent in the 12-month period ended December l995, despite some acceleration in recent months.  

3. However, reflecting the continued economic recovery and the substantial appreciation of the Kenya shilling in 1994, the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) widened from 0.4 percent of GDP in 1994 to an estimated 4.2 percent in l995. Exports grew at a brisk pace, but an exceptionally strong import demand and a decline in tourism earnings led to the widening of the current account deficit. Gross official reserves of the Central Bank of Kenya (CBK) declined from $\yen5625$ million (3.7 months of imports) at end-1994 to US\$458 million (2.1 months of imports) at end-1995, while the Kenya shilling depreciated by 20 percent during the year.  

4. The weakening of the external position, as well as some acceleration ir inflation in late l995, partly reflected a fiscal policy during JulyDecember l995, i.e., the first half of 1995/96, which was not as tight as envisaged. Following a reduction in the budget deficit (commitment basis, excluding grants) by 5.4 percentage points of GDP to 2.6 percent of GDP in 1994/95, the deficit widened to K Sh 7.3 billion in the first half of 1995/96 (3.4 percent of GDP on an annual basis), larger than expected by K Sh 3.6 billion. This reflected a shortfall in revenue of K Sh 2.3 billion, and extraordinary expenditures amounting to K Sh 3.5 billion in connection with the construction of the Eldoret airport, the purchase of an airplane, and the printing of new national identity cards. Development expenditures were postponed, and some recurrent expenditures were cut in order to minimise the overrun in the overall deficit.  

5. The conduct of monetary and exchange rate policies was also difficult in l995, largely owing to uncertainties in the financial markets and continued redemptions of treasury bills. At end-l995, treasury bill sales fell short of the target despite relatively high interest rates (20-25 percent on treasury bills), and net domestic assets (NDA) of the CBK exceeded the target by K Sh 8 billion, or ll percent of reserve money stock. Net foreign assets (NFA) of the CBK fell short of the target, by about Us\$30 million, and reserve money expanded more rapidly than envisaged. Under the circumstances, the cash ratio was kept at l8 percent, rather than gradually reduced as planned earlier. Nevertheless, growth in broad money (M3) in 1995, at 15 percent, was somewhat higher than targeted.  

6. Concerted efforts were made in l995 to bring back on track the public enterprise (PE) reform programme. First, the management of a few key PEs, whose performance and commitment to reform had been weak, was strengthened. The Chairmen of the Kenya Ports Authority (KPA) and the Kenya Posts and Telecommunications Corporation (KPTc) were replaced; and a new Chairman and Board were appointed at the Kenya Power and Lighting Company (KPLC). Second, an independent audit of KPA was conducted with a view to promptly taking actions to address the deteriorating performance of the Port and its continued liquidity problems. Third, all direct and indirect subsidies were eliminated, except that KPTC continued to incur tax arrears. Budgetary transfers $\vdots$ to PEs were limited to the compensation for the social services that they provide at the request of the Government, and to debt service payments on behalf of parastatals that were deemed unable to make such payments by the Auditor General. Fourth, a cross-debt study was completed, allowing for the conclusion of agreements with each parastatal on the reduction of its arrears to the Treasury in the near future. Fifth, management of the reform process and the privatisation programme were strengthened by bringing the functions of the Executive Secretariat and Technical Unit (ESTU) under the Treasury. Finally, the Government announced publicly its decision to: (i) open up the telecommunications business to the private sector and privatise the telecommunications functions of KPTC; (ii) commercialise the National Cereals and Produce Board (NCPB) by end1996; and (iii) contract an external operator to manage the Mombasa container terminal of KPA.

# II. The Programme_ for 1996  

12. In the context of the medium-term programme covering 1996-98, the Government of Kenya has set out the following specific objectives for 1996: (a) to maintain real GDP growth at 5 percent in 1996; (b) to limit the annual rate of inflation (end of period) to no more than 5 percent in 1996; (c) to strengthen the external current account position (excluding grants) from a deficit of about 4.2 percent of GDP in l995 to a deficit of about 1.3 percent of GDP in 1996; and (d) to increase gross official reserves to US\$595 million, or the equivalent of 2.9 months of imports at end-l996, from US\$458 million, or 2.1 months at end-l995. Further fiscal adjustment and a tight monetary policy will be implemented in order to achieve these objectives.

Of the K Sh 0.8 billion cuts in recurrent expenditures, about one-half will come out of the public administration sector (including defence), where the reduction is to come primarily from purchases of new equipment. In ministries belonging to the economic sector, which will account for roughly 20 percent of the cuts, reductions are to be concentrated in equipment and transfers. The original 1995/96 budget, approved by Parliament in August 1995, incorporated the following measures to further rationalise the tax structure and reduce the tax burden: (a) a reduction in the standard rate of the Value-Added Tax (VAT) from l8 percent to 15 percent; 1/ (b) a cut of 5 percentage points in the three top customs duty rates, as well as in the duty rates on capital goods and basic raw materials; (c) a lowering of the top marginal income tax rate to 35 percent from lst January, 1996, while total relief granted to individuals under the personal income tax was increased by 20 percent; and (d) the discontinuation of the drought levy. To compensate for the revenue loss in connection with these measures, the VAT coverage was broadened to all products except food items and certain unprocessed products; a 2 percent presumptive income tax on agricultural income was reintroduced; the road maintenance levy on petroleum products, as well as excise taxes on cigarettes and alcoholic drinks, were increased; some customs exemptions were eliminated; and additional employer-provided benefits were made subject to full income taxation. To ensure that KRA brings major expected improvements in tax administration, a new Chairman of the Board and Commissioner General were appointed in February 1996; the replacement of a number of senior officials at the Customs and the KPA should also help reduce tax evasion. In addition, a resident advisor under Fund technical assistance has been requested to advise on the organizational and operational strengthening of KRA. Other measures that have been adopted to improve tax administration include: significantly increased fines and penalties to enforce the maximum compliance with import duties and with the VAT at the retail level; streamlining of import duty exemptions enjoyed by diplomatic missions and agencies, as well as by importers of aid-funded and donated goods, and of inputs used in export production; cooperative procedures between Kenya and Uganda to control transit and export fraud involving the two countries; effective use of the powers of the Commissioner to recover the VAT arrears; a complete phase-in of the installment system of the corporate income tax; and a better reporting of income by branches of foreign banks in Kenya for tax purposes. Moreover, no discretionary import duty exemptions will be granted, effective February l, 1996.

The Government will cease guaranteeing external loans other than those for "core" projects under the PIP.

# Monetary and exchange rate policies  

23. In response to strong expansion in credit, and to avert inflationary pressures, the CBK tightened monetary policy in mid-January. As a result, net domestic assets (NDA) of the CBK declined to K Sh 57.2 billion at endMarch, from about K Sh 67 billion at end-December, substantially lower than the indicative target. Net credit to the Government also declined by about K Sh l0 billion during the same period, owing to increased sales of treasury bills. Net foreign assets (NFA) of the CBK reached K Sh 1l.l billion at the end of March, about K Sh 6.2 billion higher than its level at end-1995. Following these actions, reserve money declined to K Sh 68.4 billion at the end of March, about K Sh 2.8 billion lower than its level at end-December. These actions are expected to have slowed down the growth in broad money (M3) to a seasonally adjusted, annualized rate of 2 percent in the threemonth period ended in March, from 25 percent at end-1995.  

24. For the remainder of l996, the overriding objective of monetary policy will continue to be price stability. In this regard, monetary policy will be formulated on the basis of targeting monetary aggregates, with the NDA of the CBK serving as the nominal anchor. Open market operations will be conducted to ensure that the NDA remain on the targeted path, with interest rates in such operations allowed to adjust as necessary. Credit from the CBK to the financial institutions will be kept low. Foreign exchange intervention by the CBK will be limited to building up international reserves and smoothing short-term fluctuations in the exchange rate, leaving the exchange rate to be determined by market forces. More concretely, intervention will be limited so that NFA remain within a band of K Sh 1.5 billion around the targeted path. These policies will, in effect, ensure that reserve money will be kept within a band of K Sh l.5 billion around the targeted path. 1/  

25. NDA of the CBK are programmed to decline by about K Sh 7 billion or 10 percent of reserve money stock during 1996, mainly reflecting a reduction in net CBK credit to the Government. NFA are programmed to increase by K Sh 7 billion $(\tt U S9125\$ million) by end-l996. 2/ On this basis, reserve money is programmed to remain broadly unchanged at its level in December 1995 (K Sh 7l billion).  

26. Consistent with the programmed path of reserve money and an unchanged required reserve ratio (at l8 percent), broad money growth in 1996 is projected at 2.4 percent, significantly lower than the growth in nominal GDP. This reflects the need to correct for the higher-than-envisaged broad money growth in l995. A reduction in the required reserve ratio could be envisaged, if money demand is stronger than expected and inflation remains on the programmed path.  

27. Under the fully liberalised exchange system, and given the observed volatility in capital flows, the exchange rate could come under severe pressure from time to time. If strong foreign exchange market pressures persist even after exhausting the permissible interventions, the exchange rate will be allowed to adjust freely. In that event, the Government will consult with the Fund staff to identify the sources of the pressures and modify monetary and fiscal policies appropriately.  

28. With a view to facilitating the conduct of monetary policy, an amendment of the CBK Act will be presented to Parliament by June 30, l996 to make the Central Bank more independent. The Bill will amend, inter alia, the provisions pertaining to the appointment and removal of the Governor and Directors; the ability of the Board to adopt decisions without outside interference; and the financing of its fiscal deficit. The Bill will define clearly the purposes of the CBK and grant it sufficient powers to discharge its duties effectively. In addition, the CBK will take steps to improve its capability to forecast reserve money movements, as well as its intervention techniques in the money and foreign exchange markets. The CBK will also take measures to develop secondary markets, as described in paragraph 17 of the PFP.

The entity will also engage in joint ventures with private investors in activities such as cellular phones.  

34. During 1996, the privatisation programme of Kenya Airways will be completed, leaving the Government with less than 30 percent of the capital of the company at end-l996. In addition, the Government will initiate work on the divestiture of three large public enterprises (Kenya Pipeline Co. Ltd., Kenya Petroleum Company, and Nyayo Buses Corporation). The Government's shareholding position in NBK will be brought to less than 25 percent by end-June l996, and its position in Kenya Commercial Bank (KCB) reduced to less than 50 percent by end-December 1996. Moreover, 40 of the remaining nonstrategic enterprises will be divested in 1996, with 20 divestitures realised by end-June 1996.  

35. A number of measures have been implemented to increase the transparency of the privatisation process and the generation of budget revenue. More emphasis will be placed on competitive bidding; the list of enterprises to be sold will continue to be announced in advance, bids opened publicly, and, on conclusion of a sale agreement, a press statement will be issued, giving complete particulars of each transaction (including the terms of sale, the number of offers received, the price at which the sale was executed, and the valuation of assets). In addition, regular quarterly reports on the implementation of the privatisation process and on the privatisation proceeds and their uses, as well as semi-annual audits of the privatisation proceeds and their uses, will be prepared and published. The audit of the 1995 privatisation programme will be completed by June 1996.  

# Financial sector reform  

36. While the banking system is at present considerably sounder than a few years ago, competition in the system remains inadequate, as evidenced by large interest rate spreads at commercial banks and seemingly excessive collateral requirements. The programme for 1996 will contain a number of measures aimed at consolidating the soundness of the financial system and increasing competition in the banking system. First, in order to avoid proliferation of small, inefficient banks (which would tend to reinforce dominance of the four existing large banks), no new banking licenses will be issued before end-l996, except for large new entrants (i.e., banks with capital larger than K Sh 1 billion) that are judged to offer credible  

competition to the existing large banks. Second, the judiciary system will be strengthened so that contractual obligations are strictly enforced. This should help commercial banks to reduce collateral and accept new borrowers. Finally, all prudential regulations, including the recently introduced foreign exposure limit, will be strictly enforced, and a statutory manager will be appointed promptly as soon as serious financial problems are detected.  

37. Given that the CBK intends to rely mainly on open market operations to conduct its monetary policy, appropriate supporting infrastructure will be put in place. This will include: (i) designing and installing a book-entry system for government securities; (ii) improving the interbank settlement system for large payment transactions; (iii) moving toward a delivery versus payments basis for the settlement of transactions; and (iv) ensuring appropriate market organisation and trading arrangements. A programme of additional actions will be formulated to enhance the functioning of the domestic money and foreign exchange markets on the basis of the recommendations of the recent Fund technical assistance mission.  

38. To strengthen the broader financial system, the National Social Security Fund (NssF) will be converted into an autonomous pension fund, and necessary legislation will be presented to Parliament by end-December 1996. The legislation will stipulate, inter alia, that members of the Board be elected by pensioners, and that the Board members elect the chairman. The preparatory work has begun with the assistance of the World Bank, which includes, inter alia, strengthening of information technology as well as an actuarial study by an external auditor. Meanwhile, the NsSF has been instructed to invest its revenue in government securities only, except for deposits in sound and stable financial institutions, which will be limited to 15 percent of NSSF's total assets. An external audit of the financially troubled Kenya National Assurance (KNA) has been completed and specific recommendations have been made for restructuring. Appropriate allocations for the restructuring of KNA will be included in the l996/97 budget.  

# External sector policies  

39. The Government recognises the importance of a transparent trade system that is enforced without discretion. To this end, the import tax structure will be rationalised further, with the objective of achieving a maximum tariff rate of 30 percent, and no more than four rates (including zero as one of the rates) by July 1997. Moreover, the suspended duty on petroleum imports that was introduced in November $1994$ to provide temporary protection to the refinery will be eliminated by October l996, contingent on the completion of the LPG import unloading pipeline. Also, the discriminatory elements of the supplementary levy on sugar will be eliminated by December 1996. Furthermore, specific duties on cereal imports will be abolished by end-l996. By that time, an anti-dumping legislation consistent with WTO rules will be presented to Parliament, and anti dumping duties on cereal imports will be imposed in accordance with the law. Allegations of dumping will be handled administratively according to the procedures established by law.  

40. An agreement with commercial banks on the rescheduling of arrears on external debt service totalling ${\tt U S S72}$ million (including accrued penalty interest and refinancing costs) was not finalised, because one of the banks involved declined to sign. The Government will approach the commercial banks with a menu of options, including a buy-back, with the intention to clear these arrears in the fiscal year l996/97. The Government will also discuss with the commercial banks options to restructure current maturities. The Government is considering the use of privatisation proceeds to finance a debt buy-back operation, if it can secure sufficiently advantageous terms.  

41. The Government will continue to pursue a prudent external debt strategy. The Government will limit contracting or guaranteeing of external loans with a grant element of less than 35 percent to no more than $\yen523,456$ million in l996, 1/ with a sub-ceiling of no more than $\yen100$ million for loans with a maturity of less than 30 years, and there will be no increase in the short-term external debt of the Government. Moreover, the Government will not incur external debt arrears. To this end, the Payments Authorization Form for the CBK will be prepared by the Accounts Comptroller in liaison with the Public Debt Section in the Ministry of Finance for the signature of the Permanent Secretary one month before the payment is due.  

42. The Exchange Risk Assumption Fund (ERAF) was terminated in June 1994, and the inventory of existing commitments under the ERAF was completed in 1995. A committee is expected to be established to assess the validity of these claims with a view to regularising the outstanding obligations by end1996.  

43. Kenya, Uganda, and Tanzania have announced their joint commitment to regional cooperation, including harmonisation of tariff rates and customs administration as well as creation of a customs union. In consultation with the other countries, Kenya will take action towards the unification of tariff rates and customs administration, and promote the full convertibility of the currencies. The CBK will buy surplus regional currencies on a full cost recovery basis.  

# Discipline in public finance  

44. The Head of State identified the eradication of corruption as a key objective of economic reform in his Independence Day speech on 12th December 1995. Accordingly, on 13th December 1995, the Head of the Public Service issued a circular to all Permanent Secretaries stating that "the following actions should be taken against all officers involved in financial malpractices:  

(a) Offenders should be summarily dismissed in accordance with current service regulations.  

(b) Offenders should be taken to court promptly and a plea entered that the court makes a "restitution order"; and   
(c) It should be ensured that offending officers are surcharged and that the Government is fully compensated."  

45. Consistent with this policy, in mid-January 1996, immediately after certain anomalies were ascertained, the Government suspended the following officials: (a) the Commissioner General of the Kenya Revenue Authority; (b) the Commissioner, the Deputy Commissioner, and the senior Assistant Commissioner of Customs; (c) the Managing Director, the Deputy Managing Director, and the General Manager of Operations of the Kenya Port Authority; (d) the Permanent Secretary of the Ministry of Transport, and (e) a number of other senior officials. These officials are currently under investigation. Uneven tax administration, particularly at customs, as well as mismanagement at the Port of Mombasa, have been major sources of concerns that are being $:$ dealt with vigorously. In addition, the Government is at an advanced stage of establishing whether certain ministries have substantially exceeded their budgetary authorisations. If such excess spending is established, Permanent Secretaries of the Ministries concerned will be disciplined promptly, pursuant to Treasury Circular No. l4 of 30th October 1995. Henceforth, the Code of Conduct will be strictly enforced.  

46. Furthermore, disciplinary measures will be applied in relation to the irregularities identified in the Reports of the Controller and Auditor General regarding 1992/93 and 1993/94 accounts, as soon as deliberations by Parliament on these reports are concluded. These irregularities include the budgetary payments for compensation of gold exports (K Sh 5.8 billion) and the central bank credit (K Sh 15.7 billion) that took place during 1992 and 1993. Of these misuses of public funds totalling K Sh 2l.5 billion, K Sh l4.9 billion has been recovered in the form of cash and assets seized, including those that are currently under litigation in court. Specifically, these included CBK recoveries in the form of cash (K Sh 7.9 billion) and asset-seizures (K Sh 2.7 billion), as well as recoveries by the Deposit Protection Fund, in the form of cash (K Sh 1.2 billion), asset sales of institutions (K Sh 0.5 billion), seizure of securities (K Sh 1.7 billion), and court-cases (K Sh 0.9 billion). Additional recoveries of K Sh 1.3 billion is envisaged at end-June l996 and of K Sh 2.6 billion (cumulative) by end-1996 through collection of loans from parastatals, cash transfers from Post Bank Credit Ltd., profits of Grand Regency Hotel, and seizure of personal property of some debtors.  

47. Moreover, in order to prevent irregular budgetary payments in the future, the Government will ensure full transparency of public finances. First, the Government will not enter into any expenditure commitments which are not provided in the Budget. Second, the Government will inform Parliament of any new external borrowing at the start of each Parliamentary sessions: Third, any procurement contracts in excess of K Sh 10 million will be publicly tendered, and the minutes of the Central Tender Board published. Fourth, all contracts in excess of K Sh 2 million will be negotiated by a team consisting of representatives of the Treasury, the  

relevant Ministry, the Attorney General's Chambers, the Audit Inspectorate, and a Ministry not related to the project. Finally, the Government will publish budgetary outturns on a quarterly basis. These measures have been made public, and their implementation will form an integral element of the mid-term review. The government will also provide the Fund with comprehensive monthly statements on budgetary operations, including details of foreign payments.  

# III. Performance Criteria, Benchmarks, and Review  

48. To help monitor progress under the programme, quantitative benchmarks have been set for the following variables: (a) the Central Government's budget deficit (excluding grants and on a commitment basis); (b) net domestic assets of the CBK, with a subceiling on net credit to the Central Government by the CBK; (c) the combined operating surplus of the five monitored public enterprises; (d) net international reserves of the CBK; (e) new nonconcessional external borrowing contracted or guaranteed by the Government, with a subceiling on loans with less than 30 years' maturity; (f) changes in the stock of short-term external debt contracted or guaranteed by the Goverrment; and (g) nonaccumulation of external payments arrears (Table 2). Targets for these variables for end-June 1996 will constitute performance criteria under the ESAF arrangement, while targets for end-September 1996, and end-December 1996 will constitute benchmarks.  

49. Structural performance criteria will include the Government's intention: (a) not to reintroduce direct controls on prices, external trade, and internal marketing; (b) to appoint experts for the commercialisation of NCPB; (c) to present to Parliament an Amendment to the Central Bank of Kenya Act to make the CBK more independent; (d) to reduce the size of the civil service; and (e) to privatise public enterprises. Structural performance criteria and benchmarks are shown in Table 2. Moreover, the Government does not intend to impose new or modify existing restrictions on payments and transfers for current international transactions, introduce new multiple currency practices, impose new or intensify existing import restrictions for balance of payments reasons, or to enter into bilateral payments agreements with member countries of the Fund inconsistent with Article VIII.  

50. The Government understands that implementation of the policies in this memorandum will be subject to a review by the Fund, to be completed by endOctober 1996. The completion of this review, together with the observance of the end-June l996 quantitative and structural performance criteria, will be a condition for the disbursement of the second loan under the first annual ESAF arrangement. The review will focus, inter alia, on the areas covered by performance criteria and benchmarks; public sector reform; the 1996/97 budget; further steps to consolidate the liberal economic and trade environment; and the implementation of policies relating to the transparency of public finances as described in paragraph 47 of this memorandum.
