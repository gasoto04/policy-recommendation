# I. Introduction  

The l993 Article IV consultation with Kenya, and the discussions on a policy framework paper (PFP) and a one-year arrangement under the enhanced structural adjustment facility (ESAF), were held in Nairobi in July, September, and October l993. l/ The Kenyan representatives included Mr. W.M. Mudavadi, Minister for Finance; Dr. W. Koinange, Permanent Secretary for Finance; Mr. M. Cheserem, Governor of the Central Bank of Kenya (CBK); and other ministers and senior officials of economic ministries and public enterprises. The team also met representatives of the international donor community and the private sector.  

Kenya is on the standard l2-month Article IV consultation cycle, and the last Article IV consultation was concluded on July 17, 1992. At that time, Executive Directors stressed that Kenya would need to re-establish a solid track record of sustained policy implementation to restore the confidence of the international donor community, through reduction of the budget deficit; improvements in the monitoring of budgetary developments; tightening of monetary policy and stricter bank supervision; and implementation of action plans for large state corporations. Directors expressed concern that Kenya had reverted to administrative restrictions on access to foreign exchange.  

The Executive Board has been kept informed of developments in Kenya since then (e.g. EBD/92/233,9/30/92 and EBD/93/42,3/16/93). In particular, the Board was apprised of the adoption of a staff-monitored macroeconomic framework by the Kenyan Government in May l993 (EBD/93/90, 5/21/93).  

In a letter to the Managing Director, dated November l6, l993, 2/ the Government of Kenya requested a one-year arrangement under the ESAF, in support of a program for October l, 1993-September 30, l994, in an amount equivalent to SDR 45.23 million (or 22.7 percent of quota). This amount would be disbursed in two equal installments--the first upon Executive Board approval of the arrangement and the second upon completion of a midterm review in July l994. # II. Background  

# 1. Causes of the economic crisis  

Following a generally favorable economic performance in most of the period since independence, Kenya experienced sharply worsened economic conditions from late l99l onward. Economic growth decelerated from 4.4 percent in l990 to 0.4 percent in l992, inflation (measured by the CPI) accelerated from 20 percent (end period) in l990 to 34 percent in l992, and external.: payments arrears emerged for the first time in Kenya's history (Chart l and Table 2). In early l993, the domestic and external imbalances intensified. Economic activity remained depressed, inflation accelerated to 58 percent in the first quarter of the year (annualized increase in the quarter), and “ external payments arrears increased to $\mathtt{U S S652}$ million at end-March. By March l993, the economy was in a crisis, with severe shortages of foreign

Several factors contributed to this crisis: (i) the economy was buffeted by a series of exogenous shocks, including irregular rainfalls,a large influx of refugees from neighboring countries, and lower growth and unfavorable prices in export markets; (ii) unsettled social and political conditions during the democratization process impacted on economic policymaking (the first multiparty presidential and parliamentary elections were held in December l992); (iii) balance of payments assistance from bilateral donors was suspended in late l99l, reflecting donors' concerns over economic policy and political reform; and (iv) several major economic policy reversals occurred, notably in liberalization of the external sector and the maize market, undermining the public's confidence in the Government's economic management.  

In addition, widespread mismanagement of the financial system contributed critically to the economic crisis. The mismanagement involved access to central bank credit by several commercial banks through irregular means, including: (i) large overdrafts with the CBK; (ii) access to the export pre-shipment finance facility for financing of gold exports which do not appear in the customs statistics; and (iii) persistent deficiency in meeting the statutory cash ratio. Moreover, as the overdraft and export preshipment finance facilities were closed in March, these banks obtained CBK credit for an extended period by having the CBK encash checks ahead of settlement and repeating this process. In addition, the banks "sold" foreign exchange to the CBK, and received payment in local currency, but delayed the delivery of foreign exchange or did not deliver it at all. Credit extended by the CBK through these channels amounted to K Sh l4 billion (54 percent of reserve money) at the end of 1992, increasing to K Sh l8 billion (7l percent of reserve money) at the end of March 1993 (Chart 2).  

These problems compounded the perennial difficulties relating to the central government budget and public enterprise performance. The overall budget deficit (excluding grants, and on a commitment basis) averaged 7 percent of GDP over the last five fiscal years. l/ The budget problem could be attributed mainly to relatively large expenditures (30 percent of GDP),resulting from an excessive wage bill, unauthorized expenditures, and large subsidies (Chart 3). Public enterprises have typically had an unnecessarily large staff contingent and have lacked adequate financial discipline. In early l993, public enterprises were in arrears to the Treasury, on debt service and tax payments, in an amount of K Sh l5 billion. Of this amount, K Sh 4 billion was attributable to the Kenya Posts and Telecommunications Corporation (KPTC), which was also in arrears to the National Social Security Fund (NSSF). At the same time, the KPTC had lent a large amount to a relatively small bank on a long-term basis.  

2. Developments under the macroeconomic framework and prior actions  

Faced with the escalating economic crisis, the Government adopted, in April 1993, a macroeconomic framework for April-December l993, to restore macroeconomic stability and resume progress in economic reforms (EBD/93/90, 5/21/93). The implementation of economic policies under the framework was slower-than-envisaged in certain areas, but overall, important strides were made toward achieving the objectives of this framework. The end-September quantitative benchmarks--relating to the central government budget deficit, net domestic assets of the CBK, net credit to Central Government from the CBK, finances of four major public enterprises, net international reserves of the CBK, external debt contracted or guaranteed by the public sector, and external payments arrears--were all met (Table 3). As regards the structural benchmarks, positive real interest rates were attained; the objective of price decontrol was partially achieved (prices of petroleum products and maize remained under control); and, while the date for the increase of retention of foreign exchange by exporters to l0O percent was pushed back from the framework target of September l993 to April 1994, the exchange rates were unified ahead of schedule.  

The main achievements under the macroeconomic framework are as follows:  

First, monetary policy was tightened. The CBK (i) issued an average of K Sh l2 billion in treasury bills per month (or about 46 percent of reserve money) over the March-June period, which brought interest rates on these bills to 85 percent (i.e.,positive real levels) in July (Chart 4); (ii) raised the statutory cash ratio from 6 percent to l4 percent, in four steps; (iii) strictly enforced compliance with the statutory cash ratio; (iv) stopped advance clearing of checks; and (v) tightened the access to the CBK's rediscount and overnight advances by setting the interest rates at 2.5 percentage points above the highest accepted bid in the latest treasury bill auction and by limiting the eligibility of rediscountable securities.  

In April-May, the effort to tighten monetary conditions was severely hampered by CBK credit extended through the irregular means discussed above. To achieve the intended absorption of liquidity, sales of treasury bills to the public had to be considerably larger than had been envisaged, at correspondingly higher interest rates. Consequently, interest payments by the Government were much larger than expected, which required substantially higher-than-programmed CBK credit to the Government. This, in turn, meant that reserve money was higher than originally programmed, and to offset its impact on domestic liquidity, the statutory cash ratio had to be increased further. Reflecting the tighter monetary conditions, currency in circulation was virtually unchanged through September l993 (Chart 4).

Third, the exchange and trade system was liberalized. In May, import licensing was eliminated, except for a short negative list based on health, security, and environmental considerations, and import procedures were streamlined through simplified import documents. Also, payments and transfers for current international transactions were liberalized (see Section III.7). Moreover, export retention was reintroduced at 50 percent of export earnings for all exporters, and a market-based exchange system was reinstated. The official exchange rate was kept, but was applied only to servicing of government and government-guaranteed debt, budgeted government imports, and foreign exchange surrendered to the CBK by exporters. On October l7, the exchange rates were unified; subsequently, the official rate has been set at the average market rate for the preceding day.  

Fourth, maize marketing was substantially liberalized. The monopoly of the National Cereals and Produce Board (NcPB) for importation and transportation of maize has been abolished. Private traders may now import maize without restrictions, and imported maize may be freely transported within the country. In addition, up to 8 tons of domestic maize may be transported freely at one time. As this weight limit applies to one truck-load, control on the movement of local maize in effect has been substantively removed. The consumer price of maize, which will remain under direct control until July l994, was increased by 3l percent in September, and is now higher than prices in the neighboring countries. If necessary, the Government will adjust the consumer price of maize, so that price control would not be an impediment to free private sector trading of maize.  

Fifth, civil service reform was initiated. After some initial delay, the Civil Service Reform Programme and Action Plan was approved by Cabinet in July l993, and a secretariat to coordinate the reform was established in August. Posts that became vacant have not been filled, and hiring has been strictly limited to key positions at higher grades. As a result, during April-September l993 the number of civil servants was reduced by about 6,600. In September, 13,954 civil service positions were eliminated, representing about 5 percent of total positions, compared with the target of 9,750 positions (Chart 5).  

Sixth, the execution of central government budget was broadly in line with the macroeconomic framework, apart from the effect of the larger-thanexpected interest payments and outlays to cover losses incurred by the CBK. Although the l992/93 budget deficit (excluding grants, and on a commitment basis) was an extremely large l0.4 percent of GDP (Table 4), if one excludes the effect of higher interest payments and the transfer to the CBK, the deficit was 6.9 percent of GDP, compared with a revised target of 6.8 percent of GDP under the framework. The l993/94 budget approved by Parliament in July featured revenue measures, expenditure savings, and a deficit target in line with the macroeconomic framework. The budget was revised in early September to take account of the higher interest payments. At that time, some revenue measures were adopted to meet most of the additional expenses (see Section III. 2). The deficit in the first quarter of l993/94 was in line with the revised target.  

While the overall budget performance was broadly in line with the objectives, unauthorized expenditures and spending in excess of the approved ministerial votes continued. To avoid future overruns, the Government has now strengthened expenditure control. Computerized monthly expenditure monitoring by individual ministries has been introduced; CBK and Exchequer Accounts are now simultaneously reconciled; quarterly expenditure allocation has been introduced; and sanctions have been reinforced against accounting officers in noncompliant ministries. Letters of appointment have been sent to accounting officers in all ministries, clarifying their personal responsibility for any expenditure overruns.  

Performance in regard to public enterprises was mixed. The operating performance of four key public enterprises l/ in April-September l993 was somewhat stronger than expected, and the Kenya Power and Lighting Corporation and Kenya Railways undertook cost-cutting measures--including staff reductions--as envisaged. Overall, however, the higher-than-expected combined profit of the four enterprises reflected larger-than-planned tariff adjustments, foreign exchange gains, and sales of fixed assets, rather than improved efficiency or lower costs. The lack of progress in improving efficiency and reducing costs was most pronounced in regard to the KPTC and the NCPB--very little progress was made toward reducing the staffs of these two enterprises.  

In October-November l993, the Government took important first steps toward reforming KPTC and NCPB. The top management of the KPTC was replaced; the Corporation was able to improve its financial position by becoming current on its payments to the NSSF and by consolidating its bank deposits; and a decision was taken to separate the postal and telecommunications operations into two companies, and to privatize the telecommunications operations as a longer-term goal. # III. Report on Discussions and Proposed Program  

The discussions focused on the near- and medium-term prospects for growth and inflation, policies to restore external payments viability, and the program for which the authorities are requesting support under the ESAF.  

The proposed program aims at achieving economic growth of 3 percent in 1994, increasing to 6 percent by l996; reducing the rate of inflation to 15 percent (annualized rate) in the final quarter of l993, and to less than 10 percent in l994; lowering the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) to 1.5 percent of GDP in l994, and further to 0.8 percent of GDP by l996; and raising gross international reserves from l.8 months at end-l993 to 1.9 months at end- $1994$ , and further to 2.3 months at end-l996 (Table 6). External payments arrears would be settled in 1994 through cash payments and multilateral rescheduling, and external payments viability would be restored by 1999.  

The key elements of the proposed adjustment effort for the October 1993-September l994 program period are: (i) a reduction in the central government budget deficit (excluding grants, commitment basis) from 10.4 percent of GDP in 1992/93 to 6.l percent of GDP in l993/94, and to 2.9 percent of GDP in 1994/95; (ii) a turnaround in the combined operating performance of four key public enterprises from a deficit of K Sh 1.2 billion in 1992/93 to surpluses of K Sh 3.0 billion in 1993/94 and K Sh 8.6 billion in l994/95; (iii） credit policy to limit broad money growth to about 10 percent in the l2-month period ending September l994, and maintenance of positive real interest rates; (iv) complete abolition of direct price controls; (v) complete removal of the remaining restrictions on movement of local maize, and scaling back of public-sector involvement in processing and marketing of other agricultural products; (vi) elimination of all remaining requirements to surrender export earnings to the CBK, and removal of disincentives for private investment and exports; and (vii) provisions to alleviate the transitional hardships of civil service retrenchment and liberalization of the maize market.  

# 1. Near- and medium-term outlook 1/  

The prospects for the Kenyan economy in l994 appear difficult. Rainfall has continued to be irregular since June (the planting season for main maize crop next year), and shortfalls in domestic maize production in 1993/94 of l million metric tons, equal to 60 percent of marketed production, are now projected. Although tea and coffee--traditional cash crops-- continue to do well, spurred by export retention and other recent measures, overall agricultural production is projected to grow only by 5 percent in 1994. Moreover, increased incidence of violent crimes (which is related in part to the conflict in a neighboring country), continued ethnic clashes in certain areas, the presence of refugees from neighboring countries, and the emerging religious/ethnic conflict in Mombasa, all tend to weaken business and investor confidence. The increases in production in the industry and services sectors are expected to be limited to 2.7 percent and 1.5 percent, respectively, in l994, as domestic demand continues to be weak. Under these circumstances, the authorities believed, and the staff concurred, that overall real GDP growth of 3 percent in $1994$ would represent a credible target.  

Given the uncertain near-term outlook, the authorities attach the highest priority to consolidating and extending the stabilization gains. The underlying rate of inflation (i.e., excluding food and petroleum) had declined to 24 percent (annualized rate) in the three-month period ended in October l993 and substantial destocking was taking place as a result in part of the tight monetary conditions. The authorities acknowledged the significant inflationary impact from the large increases in the consumer price of maize and the electricity tariffs in September l993, as well as the unfavorable food supply situation, but thought that this would be largely? offset by a lagged effect of the recent substantial appreciation of the Kenya shilling. # 2. Fiscal policy  

The l993/94 budget aims at reducing the overall deficit by 4.3 percent of GDP to 6.l percent of GDP. The planned deficit is still too large, in view particularly of the large domestic debt the Government has accumulated and the need for fiscal policy to play its part in the adjustment process. Nevertheless, the budget is constrained by the high interest payments (10.5 percent of GDP), and the reduction of 4.3 percent of GDP in one year, if realized, would represent a credible objective.  

A number of revenue measures have been introduced for l993/94 to enhance revenue by widening the tax base and improving the tax administration. As regards the value-added tax (VAT), its coverage was expanded to include construction, automobile parts, and equipment repair and rental, while the rate structure was rationalized. In regard to customs duties, the basis for import duty valuation was changed to reflect in part the market exchange rate, and a new contract for pre-shipment inspection will be signed to ensure proper valuation of imports for customs purposes. (Earlier, the emphasis of inspection was for overvaluation and associated capital flight.) Concerning income taxes, a Personal Identification Number system was reintroduced, while presumptive taxation for agriculture was abolished. Also, collection of tax arrears from parastatals will be strengthened, and excise duties on petroleum products were raised and shifted to an ad valorem basis. In addition, in September l993, all import duties were increased by 25 percent, raising the maximum rate to 62 percent. The authorities were reluctant to increase the tariffs, but this was the only practical option to mobilize additional resources (K Sh 2.8 billion) quickly. They intend to lower the rates as soon as alternative revenue sources are identified. On the whole, revenue is projected to increase by l.l percent of GDP to 23.2 percent of GDP.  

Expenditure cuts amounting to 3.l percent of GDP are envisaged for 1993/94. The expenditure cuts are to be achieved largely from broad-based cuts in recurrent operations and maintenance outlays (0.9 percent of GDP), and a reduction in development expenditure through stricter prioritization of projects (0.8 percent of GDP). The authorities raised the consumer price of maize by 3l percent in September, and are committed to take additional measures as required to contain the maize subsidy within the budget provision (of K Sh 2 billion). In real terms, nonwage, noninterest recurrent expenditure will decline by 28 percent in 1993/94 and development expenditure by 19 percent. Additional savings are expected from the elimination of transfers to the CBK (O.8 percent of GDP) and a restructuring of debt owed to the CBK into longer-term, lower-cost instruments (0.5 percent of GDP). The CBK has cut its spending substantially, including through a scaling back of construction of the new CBK building, to assure a profit of at least K Sh 3 billion in l993/94 (i.e., the dividend expected in the budget).  

The authorities expressed concern that the expenditure restraint might undermine necessary maintenance and development projects. They cited cases where donor-financed projects had already been postponed because of lack of government counterpart funds. They concurred with the staff, however, that curbing the deficit and improving the quality and control of development projects were of key importance at this stage of the adjustment effort. Once improvement occurs in these areas, it may be possible to support a higher level of development and maintenance expenditure.  

The staff emphasized that, in order to meet the budget deficit target, strict enforcement of expenditure controls was needed. The Kenyan authorities fully shared this view, citing the actions already taken to this end (see Section II.2 above), and reaffirming that sanctions would be applied to officials who do not comply with the expenditure guidelines.  

The l994/95 budget will aim at further reducing the deficit to 2.9 percent of GDP, entirely through cuts in expenditure. Expenditure savings will derive from lower domestic interest payments (3.l percent of GDP) and elimination of the maize subsidy (0.5 percent of GDP). These savings will create some room for an increase in operations and maintenance outlays and.: development expenditure.  

On the basis of the above budgetary targets for l993/94 and l994/95,. net repayment of K Sh 4 billion to domestic creditors is targeted for the : program period, October l993-September l994 (Table 7). l/ Only a small repayment to the CBK (K Sh 0.2 billion) is programmed. Repayments to nonCBK creditors will take the form of net redemption of government securities, as primary issues in weekly auctions and open market sales will fall short of redemptions. It is assumed that net repayments to commercial banks and to the nonbank public would amount to K Sh 1.3 billion and K Sh 2.5 billion, respectively, roughly in proportion to their share in outstanding treasury bill holdings.  

# 3. Civil service reform  

Civil service reform is expected to contribute to fiscal adjustment in the longer term, and at the same time allow for some reversal of the erosion in real wages of civil servants. The program includes a reduction in the number of civil service positions by l6,000 during October l993-September 1994, and a continued reduction in the number of positions by l6,o0o each year through 1996/97. By July l997, the civil service establishment will have been reduced by about 25 percent, bringing the share of civil service in total formal employment to the level of the mid-l97Os. The program excludes teachers because of the need to improve access to education.  

The reduction in the civil service forms part of a broader reform effort to improve the efficiency of government operations. There appears to be considerable scope for reducing duplication of functions and privatizing activities (such as agricultural extension services, where provision of fertilizer and transport could be taken over by private companies, and health and nutrition programs, which could be operated by nongovernmental organizations). The Kenyan authorities stated that a study of government functions, now being prepared with the assistance of the World Bank, would form the basis for the formulation of specific plans to rationalize the government structure; the plans will be prepared by March 1994.  

# 4. Public enterprises  

The Government has classified public enterprises into strategic and nonstrategic ones; the medium-term objective is to privatize all those classified as nonstrategic. Preparations will be expedited to ensure that at least 20 additional nonstrategic public enterprises (about l0 percent of the total) are divested by September l994. In the early stages of the privatization program, the emphasis will be on enterprises that do not require further extensive operational, financial, or legal restructuring prior to sale. The enterprises targeted for privatization include Kenya Airways, Gilgil Telecommunications Manufacturing Facility (currently owned by the KPTC), at least two sugar factories, and all six cotton ginneries. The Government does not expect to incur losses from the sales of these corporations.  

As regards strategic enterprises, targets have been set in the proposed program on the combined operating profit of four key enterprises, l/ and  

on these enterprises' liquid assets with banks (Table 8). These targets are supported by a range of measures to cut costs and enhance revenue. In particular, the Kenya Power and Lighting Corporation is expected to show a major improvement in its operating performance because of large increases in tariffs (to move toward long-term marginal cost pricing for electricity), and Kenya Ports Authority's performance will be strengthened by restraint on personnel and administration costs as well as tariff adjustments. All four public enterprises will undertake staff reductions.  

A fundamental restructuring of the KPTC has been initiated, involving: (i) appointment of new management; (ii) initiation of an external audit; (iii) separation of postal and telecommunications operations of the Corporation, with a view to privatizing the latter; (iv) freeze on new investments until the restructuring plan is completed; (v) consolidation of the financial position (inter alia, by repaying outstanding arrears to the Treasury, and by avoiding any new external borrowing); (vi) reduction in the staff by 2,000 employees (7 percent) by March l994 and 3,000 (l0 percent) by September $1994$ ; and (vii) conclusion of a performance contract with the Government by September 1994.  

The operations of the NCPB are expected to be scaled back substantially. During the program period, the number of depots operated by the Board will be reduced by 24 percent. The number of depots will be reduced further by 38 percent (cumulative) by March l995 and 52 percent by December 1995. This reduction will be achieved through withdrawal from the depots by the? NCPB,which will then offer the depots for sale or lease. The NCPB's permanent staff will be reduced by at least 20 percent during the program period, in line with the scaling back of depots. Starting in l994/95, the only budgetary transfer to NCPB will relate to the costs associated with the maintenance of the strategic maize reserve.  

The Kenya authorities attach importance to a corporate governance code for public enterprises, which will be presented to Parliament by February $1994$ . This code is intended to strengthen the enterprises' autonomy and accountability, and will formalize an arm's length, owner-manager relationship between the Government and.the strategic enterprises.  

# 5. Monetary policy  

The fundamental objective of monetary policy in the coming period is to bring about the desired sharp deceleration in inflation in the fourth quarter of l993, followed by a sustained period of relative price stability, and to maintain the exchange rate at about the present level of K Sh 69-70 per U.S. dollar. The strategy, therefore; is to keep liquidity conditions very tight through the end of l993, followed by some easing. Consistentwith this strategy, no increase in broad money is envisaged in the fourth quarter of this year on a seasonally adjusted basis, followed by an increase of l4 percent (annualized rate) during January-September l994 (Table 9).:  

This expansion in broad money would allow an increase in net domestic assets of the banking system of 6 percent (or K Sh 5.9 billion) over the period September 1993-September l994. As already discussed, the Government is expected to make net repayments of K Sh 1.5 billion in the period. Bank credit to the other sectors is projected to increase by K Sh 9.2 billion, or 14 percent, broadly in line with nominal GDP growth.  

The operational target for monetary policy will be the net domestic assets of the CBK. The increase in net domestic assets will be limited to K Sh 2.4 billion, or 6 percent, over the program period (Table l0). Net credit to the Central Government will be reduced slightly. An increase in advances and discounts to commercial banks of K Sh 3 billion is projected. Overnight advances will be allowed to expand, offset in part by the recovery of overdrafts owed by the closed banks.  

The reserve money path was projected consistent with the net foreign assets targets and net domestic assets paths described above. Reserve money is projected to increase by 43 percent (seasonally adjusted annual rate) in the fourth quarter of l993, reflecting largely CBK purchases of foreign exchange in the market and redemption of treasury bills. To ensure that this level of reserve money was consistent with the target on broad money, the statutory cash ratio was raised by 4 percentage points during the quarter. In effect, liquidity created through redemption of treasury bills is being absorbed through increases in the cash ratio.  

The authorities are aware of the disintermediation effect of a high statutory cash ratio. To address this problem, market-related interest rates are paid on cash reserves held in excess of 10 percent. They also recognize that it is not ideal to use the statutory cash ratio for shortterm monetary management. The authorities are currently developing repurchase agreements, or alternative instruments, that would allow the CBK to sell/buy its holdings of long-term government paper on a short-term basis. Once these instruments become available, they would be used for short-term monetary management, and the statutory cash ratio would be reduced. The repurchase agreements (or similar arrangement) will also be used to sterilize unanticipated liquidity injection arising from the foreign exchange flows of the CBK.  

As discussed above, substantial net redemption of government securities is planned during the program period. Interest rates on treasury bills will be allowed to decline consistent with the programmed net redemption, provided that the rates remain significantly positive in real terms. Under the prevailing market conditions, this policy is expected to lead to interest rates of about 50 percent in December (net redemption from October to mid-November was broadly in line with the programmed amount, as the interest rate was allowed to decline from 76 percent in September to 50 percent in mid-November). The program assumes that interest rates would decline steadily to 30 percent by June l994 and 20 percent by September 1994. The authorities were concerned about the impact of the present relatively high real interest rates on economic activity, but believed that the overriding objective in the current phase of the adjustment process was to consolidate the stabilization gains.  

The program includes measures to improve the structure of interest rates. In particular, commercial banks' interest rates have not adjusted to the substantial increases in treasury bill rates in the recent past. The authorities have made arrangements with one commercial bank to retail treasury bills through its extensive branch network, at a commission. This way, interest rates on treasury bills are expected to come down gradually while retail savings and lending rates will rise, resulting in a more efficient structure of interest rates in the economy.  

# 6. Financial sector issues  

The authorities indicated that the proposed program will include further measures to ensure a sound banking system, as follows: (i) restructuring of the National Bank of Kenya (NBK) through collection of bad debts and rebuilding of its deposit base, and sales of government shares of the bank to the public; (ii) rehabilitation of another commercial bank through capital injection, freeze on new loans, and collection of nonperforming loans; (iii) amendments of the CBK and Banking Acts to strengthen the role and independence of the CBK; and (iv) improvements in bank supervision, with technical assistance from the Fund. The staff noted that, above all, effective bank supervision would require a commitment at the political level to enforce existing regulations.  

A major constraint to ensuring a sound financial sector stems from the present administration of justice, including ineffective prosecution of economic crimes as well as slow litigation of loan defaults. Efforts to reform the judiciary system are already underway through the establishment in August l993 of task forces to review Kenya's criminal procedures code, including laws relating to companies, partnership, and receiverships. In addition, since l992, special anticorruption units have been set up to investigate economic crime. The authorities plan to proceed further with judiciary reform, by improving the capacity of anticorruption units in the police force, the court system, and the timeliness of litigation.  

# 7. External policies  

The authorities consider it crucial to maintain the exchange rate close to the present level (of K Sh 69-70 per U.S. dollar) for the restoration of public confidence. At the present interest rates and the exchange rate, the CBK is purchasing a significant amount of foreign exchange. These market conditions are expected to continue over the next few months. Subsequently, with the turnaround in economic activity, a substantial increase in the demand for foreign exchange is anticipated. As the upturn in economic activity is expected to be accompanied by a reduction of inflationary pressures, the authorities believe that interest rates could decline and the relative stability of the exchange rate could be maintained, if necessary, with only modest sales of foreign exchange in the market.  

Kenya's export competitiveness has been considerably enhanced over the last l2 months, with the large depreciation of the Kenya shilling. Over the 12-month period ending October l993, the real effective exchange rate depreciated by 22 percent (Chart 7). Even with the projected inflation differential between Kenya and its trading partners, in September $1994$ the real effective exchange rate would remain some l0 percent below the level two years earlier.  

The present exchange system based on 50 percent export retention appears to be working well, and the recent unification of the exchange rate has removed a major distortion vis-a-vis exports that existed under the earlier dual exchange rate system. Moreover, all private sector imports will be financed in the market beginning January l, 1994, as the CBK will no longer sell foreign exchange for oil imports. The authorities emphasized the need to ensure that a stable exchange system be firmly established under the current arrangements before proceeding with further liberalization, including a move to l00 percent export retention. They are determined to avoid a resurgence of instability and the consequent pressure for policy reversal, in the face of difficult social and economic conditions. Accordingly, they have decided to raise the retention rate to l00 percent of export earnings in April 1994.  

The authorities intend to further encourage exports by removal of the existing anti-export bias. First, export taxes that currently apply to a limited number of raw materials and unprocessed products such as pulp, hides, and skins, will be eliminated by June l994. These taxes are intended to promote domestic processing, instead of exporting in raw form, and the authorities found it necessary to allow some time for the affected industries to adjust to the new environment. Second, the authorities plan to abolish all export licenses maintained for food security, except maize, by March l994. They will review export licensing for maize in July 1994. Export licensing, intended for national security (e.g., ammunition) and protection of environment (e.g., ivory) will be kept. Third, the operation of the duty/VAT remission scheme has been hampered by severe understaffing in the office of the Treasury that handles the scheme, as well as by complicated procedures for remission. These problems will be addressed through strengthening the staff, and the establishment of input-output coefficients for remission of duties on a product-by-product basis.  

The program includes tariff reform, which aims at reducing the tariff structure and the level and dispersion of effective protection, while safeguarding government revenue. The number of tariff rates (including the zero rate) was reduced from ten to eight in the l993/94 budget, and the objective is to further reduce the number of rates to four by July 1997. The goal is to at least reverse in the l994/95 budget the tariff increase that was effected in September l993, and to reduce the maximum rate to 30 percent by July 1997.  

The staff welcomed the liberalization of restrictions on current payments. In May l993, the CBK raised the indicative limits below which transfers abroad of education fees, business travel, and rent could be made automatically by authorized dealers. Transfers in excess of these limits would be referred to the CBK, which would approve all bona fide payments expeditiously. The indicative limits are intended to help ascertain whether capital transactions are effected through payments reported as current transactions. In addition, the exchange rates were unified in October 1993. Following these actions, Kenya no longer maintains any exchange measures either under Article XIV or subject to Fund approval under Article VIII. The staff encouraged further liberalization for example by allowing resident foreign currency accounts or liberalizing certain capital account transactions.

# 9. Domestic deregulation  

Some simplification of investment procedures had been achieved with the establishment of the one-stop Investment Promotion Centre. However, procedures for trade licensing remain complicated, with licenses being given for only one year at a time. There are a large number of fees and licenses at the municipal level and land for industrial purposes is scarce, constraining industrial development in urban centers. Moreover, access by foreign-owned companies to domestic credit markets is restricted. The program addresses these issues, to promote a strong private investment response.  

The authorities explained that, given the poor crop expected this year, severe shortages of maize could develop if adequate imports were not secured. If shortages developed, and if the prices and marketing of maize had been fully decontrolled at that time, a socially untenable situation could arise and strong pressures for reintroduction of some controls could mount. Thus, the authorities stressed that full maize decontrol should take place in the middle of next year when the new crop comes on the market. In the meantime, consumer prices on maize would be adjusted, if necessary, so as not to interfere with the effective distribution of maize by private traders. Concerning other agricultural products, the Government will withdraw from agricultural processing, such as sugar refineries and cotton ginneries, to allow the private sector to expand into these activities.  

The Kenyan authorities intend to liberalize petroleum prices and create a competitive market for petroleum products as soon as practical. The oil companies stressed that to ensure an orderly transition to a liberalized market, several issues would have to be addressed before the liberalization. It would be necessary to establish an appropriate legal framework, as well as security and safety standards. Also, the Government and the private shareholders would have to decide whether to close the Kenya Petroleum Refinery or to rehabilitate it. A task force has been established to complete the necessary preparatory steps, and the Government has announced that the marketing and pricing of petroleum products will be fully liberalized in July 1994.  

The authorities expressed their commitment to liberalize all prices by July l994. Currently, in addition to petroleum products, the General Price Control Order covers only one product category (certain pharmaceuticals). The Specific Price Control Order covers one category (maize and maize flour).  

The authorities' policies to protect vulnerable population groups in the adjustment phase include: (i) a program to alleviate the adverse impact of the maize liberalization on the poor; (ii) the maintenance of a minimum level of strategic maize stock and a hard currency reserve by the CBK; and (iii) programs to assist laid-off civil servants. Those laid off as part of civil service reform will be assisted through pensions, terminal benefits, and direct support for retraining. The purpose of the terminal benefits and retraining schemes is to assist the laid-off civil servants in starting their own small-scale businesses or finding alternative employment in the private sector. The staff supported these initiatives, stressing the need to ensure that they do not compromise budgetary adjustment.  

As part of its development of a comprehensive approach to managing Kenya's environment, the Government started the preparation of a National Environment Action Plan (NEAP) in June l993. The NEAP will provide the basis for identifying priority actions and public investments to address environmental issues. The staff supported this effort, which should be directed toward fostering sustainable growth in the Kenyan economy.

# IV. Medium- and Long-Term Balance of Payments Outlook and Capacity to Repay the Fund  

Medium- and long-term balance of payments prospects were assessed on the basis of the following assumptions: (i) export volume growth would average about 7 percent in l994-96, and stabilize at about 5 percent annually thereafter; (ii) import volume would grow by 9 percent per year in 1994-96, following the severe import compression in l990-93, and stabilize at about 6 percent annually thereafter, in line with real GDP growth; (iii) the terms of trade would improve somewhat in 1995 and l996; and remain unchanged in the subsequent period; and (iv) tourism receipts would grow by about 5 percent in volume terms annually (Chart 8). On the basis of these assumptions, the current account deficit (excluding official transfers) is projected to decline from $\boldsymbol{\mathrm{~\tt~{~U~S~S~1~0~0~}~}}$ million (l.5 percent of GDP) in l994 to ${\tt U S}\$64$ million (0.8 percent of GDP) in l996, and to fluctuate in the range of 0.8-1.0 percent of GDP from l996 onward (Table 15).  

The capital account surplus is projected to gradually increase from $\mathtt{U S S50}$ million in l995, except that in 1996-98 there will be substantial deficits relating to the repayment of rescheduled external payments arrears. The projected disbursements of official loans and grants take into account new project assistance that might be expected under normal conditions, but not balance of payments assistance that could be pledged in future Consultative Group meetings. On this basis, the projected disbursements broadly match the amortization payments on official debt, except payments relating to rescheduled arrears. Private net inflows are projected at about $\mathtt{U S S50}$ million in l995 and to grow steadily, reflecting in part higher import financing.  

Taking into account the targeted increase in gross international reserves of the CBK (to 3.0 months of imports by end-2004), a financing gap of about $\mathtt{U S S150}$ million is projected for l995,and $\mathtt{U S S300}$ million for 1996-98. While the pledges of financial support obtained at the last Consultative Group meeting will contribute significantly to covering these gaps, Kenya will need to sustain a strong adjustment effort over the medium term.  

Outstanding medium- and long-term external debt (including Fund credit) is projected to decline steadily from 97 percent of GDP in 1994 to 40 percent of GDP in 2004. The debt service ratio would decline from 25.8 percent of exports of goods and nonfactor services in 1994 to 12.6 percent in 2004.  

In order to assess the sensitivity of the above analysis, an alternative scenario was developed. The alternative scenario assumes significantly lower export volume growth (on average by l.2 percentage points annually), somewhat lower import volume growth, and less favorable terms of trade (no improvement from l995 onward). Under this scenario, the current account deficit would remain at about l.4 percent of GDP over the medium and long term,or about O.5 percent of GDP higher than under the baseline scenario. A small financing gap of $0.5520-50\$ million would remain in 1999-2003. The  

magnitude of these financing gaps is such that they could be closed by additional external assistance and a strengthening of economic policies without undue difficulty and, thus, the alternative scenario should not alter the assessment of Kenya's medium- and long-term balance of payments outlook.  

Kenya has repaid the Fund promptly despite the foreign exchange shortage over the last two years. In l992, Kenya paid debt service to the Fund equivalent to 53.5 percent of gross reserves, or 4.4 percent of exports of goods and nonfactor services; in l993, the corresponding figures were 27.7 percent and 3.0 percent, respectively. In comparison, during the ESAF repayment period (up to 2004), debt service to the Fund would peak at only 15.2 percent of gross official reserves and at 2.2 percent of exports of goods and nonfactor services in l997 (Table l6). The above balance of payments analysis and Kenya's excellent payment record to the Fund suggest that Kenya should be able to meet its debt service obligations to the Fund in a timely manner.  

# V. Staff Appraisal  

Following an extended period of relatively favorable economic developments, Kenya experienced a sharp deterioration in economic conditions beginning in late l99l, and culminating in an economic crisis in early 1993. The drought, the less buoyant world economy, the unsettled political and social climate during the democratization process, and the suspension of balance of payments assistance by bilateral donors, all contributed to the downturn in the Kenyan economy. Without minimizing the importance of those factors, the staff would nonetheless assign the key role in Kenya's difficulties to the mismanagement of public financial institutions, and the associated misallocation of public funds. Given the extent of these weaknesses, severe macroeconomic instability emerged.  

Since May of this year, the Kenyan authorities have taken decisive measures to help restore financial discipline. Four major commercial banks have been closed; a new management team is now in place at the Central Bank of Kenya; foreign exchange allocation and import controls have been abolished; and reform of two major public institutions--one financial and one nonfinancial--is under way, first through the replacement of their management teams and second through the decision to privatize these organizations. The staff commends the authorities on these actions, and urges them to send, through additional strong actions, an unequivocal message to the public and the international community that financial responsibility is now the guiding principle. Comparable efforts will also be required to resolve the ethnic tensions and foster good governance. These actions are critical prerequisites for returning Kenya to a sustainable economic development path.

The Kenyan authorities have proposed a one-year program, in support of which a one-year ESAF arrangement is requested. Their request is buttressed by the record of economic management over the past several months, including the pursuit of a tight monetary policy, the restoration of stability in the financial system, the introduction of a market-based exchange system, and the steps taken to liberalize the maize marketing system. The proposed program features: (i) cuts in government expenditure and a considerable reduction in the government budget deficit, and strict limitations on domestic borrowing by the Government; (ii) rationalization of central government operations to improve the efficiency of public services, combined with a major reduction in the number of civil servants; (iii) enforcement of strict financial discipline in the operations of several major public enterprises and privatization of a number of others; (iv) tight monetary policy to maintain positive real interest rates throughout the program period; (v) further measures to help ensure the soundness of the banking system; (vi) substantial further deregulation of the economy, covering the petroleum sector, investment regulations, the few remaining price controls, and agricultural marketing; and (vii) a range of measures to enhance the incentives to export.  

The staff considers that the proposed program is appropriate, and that, if fully implemented, the program will contribute substantially to sustained economic growth, external viability, and a stable macroeconomic environment. The staff urges the authorities to be vigilant in ensuring full implementation of the program. Policy reversals observed in the recent past should be avoided. The staff has been encouraged by the authorities' commitment to the adjustment effort, as reflected in the recent actions, and supports the authorities' request for an ESAF arrangement.  

It is proposed that Kenya remain on the standard l2-month Article IV consultation cycle.

# IX. Technical Assistance  

A diagnostic technical assistance mission from MAE visited Nairobi in November l993, to provide assistance in bank supervision, foreign exchange management, and monetary and public debt management.  

The World Bank has a large ongoing program in Kenya. As of March 30, 1993, it had committed ${\tt U S}\$3.0$ billion, of which ${\tt U S}\$2.3$ billion had been fully disbursed (Statement A). Investments (loans and equity) by IFC totaled $\mathtt{U S S149}$ million, of which Us\$l48 million had been fully disbursed (Statement B).  

# 1. Agriculture  

The Bank's dialogue on agriculture is centered around a policy reform program supported by two quick-disbursing sector adjustment credits which were approved in June l986 and January l99l. Among the components of this program are (i) measures to increase the availability and distribution of agricultural inputs (especially fertilizer); (ii) movement toward price flexibility and deregulation; (iii) rationalization of government expenditure in the sector; (iv) the divesting of some agricultural parastatals and the restructuring of others in the context of better definec policies on parastatals; and (v) the preparation of action plans for improving drought preparedness.  

These policy measures are complemented by ongoing investment projects in coffee rehabilitation, forestry, agriculture research, rehabilitation of animal health services, and the provision of countrywide extension services. Investment lending will continue to complement the policy reforms, with greater attention being given to implementing land and environmental management, and arid and semi-arid agricultural techniques. The Bank has developed and tested new methodologies or approaches in these areas in order to ensure replicability before embarking on large-scale national investments. To assist the Government in implementing a tourism and wildlife management and conservation policy and investment program, IDA approved a Wildlife Services program in February l992. An agricultural sector management project was approved in December l992. It includes the preparation of restructuring plans for the Ministry of Agriculture and Livestock Development, and restructuring and privatization options for the coffee and sugar subsectors. A drought recovery project was approved in February l993 in response to the inadequate and irregular rainfall in late 1991 and early 1992.  

# 2. Energy  

The Bank's efforts in this sector are concentrating on lessening Kenya's dependence on imported oil through hydroelectric development, geothermal development, and petroleum exploration promotion. While future lending will be consistent with the least cost recommendations of the recently completed power study, greater attention will be focused on alleviating policy constraints (e.g., pricing and distribution) to attain a more efficient and integrated energy sector, and encourage private sector participation in the power sub-sector.  

# 3. Industry/finance  

Until recently, Kenya's industry has obtained World Bank lending through development finance intermediaries. In support of an industrial reform program, IDA approved an industrial sector adjustment credit for $\mathrm{US}\$12$ million in June l988. An additional $\mathsf{U S S53}$ .7 million was subsequently provided for this operation. In order to stimulate investment, promote export production, and make industries more efficient, the reform program covered areas such as trade liberalization, tariffs, price controls, export promotion, corporate taxation, financial sector policies, and industrial public enterprises. A complementary financial sector adjustment operation was completed in l99l. It addressed, inter alia, interest rate reforms, rationalization of the banking regulatory framework, restructuring of depository institutions, and the development of capital and money markets. A sharply focused export development project was approved by IDA in December l990. In May l993, IDA released the second tranche of EDP following the delayed implementation of the sector-specific conditions and improvements in macroeconomic management, especially in the areas of external and monetary policies.  

# 4. Infrastructure  

Highway projects and the promotion of railways have accounted for most of the lending to date. A major water supply project for Nairobi was approved by the Board in l989. The Bank also approved a credit for the water supply in Mombasa and the coastal region, including source works extension with minor adverse environmental impact, and improved coverage of piped water supply to the urban poor. The Bank is currently preparing an urban transport project which will involve investments in new and expanded road links, intersections, and public transport facilities, and the development and implementation of a comprehensive plan for road maintenance, covering issues such as financing and private sector involvement. The transport corridor project, which is also under preparation, will support commercialization and organizational reform of strategic parastatals operating along the corridor from the Mombasa Port to Nairobi, namely, the Kenya Port Authority and Kenya Railways. It will also provide assistance to remedy specific infrastructure deficiencies, remove operational bottlenecks and strengthen the Customs function.  

# 5. Population  

The fourth IDA-financed project to help the Government promote fertility control was approved in l990 and is now being implemented. The project will help meet the growing demand for contraceptives and maintain the momentum of recent progress.  

# 6. Education and health  

The Bank's attention is currently directed at developing viable financing and management capacity in both sectors.. In l99l, lending operations designed to promote more efficient utilization of resources in education and health, and to implement appropriate mechanisms to increase cost sharing were prepared. In September l99l, the Board approved an Education Sector Adjustment credit. In addition, to assist the Government with the medium-term development of university education, the Universities project was approved by IDA in November l99l. In November l99l, a Health Rehabilitation project was also approved.  

# 7. Adjustment lending  

The World Bank negotiated and fully disbursed two structural adjustment loans (SAL) to Kenya. The first, negotiated in March l980, was for $\mathtt{U S S55}$ million and was fully disbursed by September l980. The second, negotiated in July l982 for $\mathtt{U S S131}$ million, was disbursed in two tranches: in September l982 and January l984. The SAL programs were implemented under adverse external circumstances which negatively affected Kenya's performance. The protracted balance of payments crises of l98l and l982, as well as the coup attempt in August l982, forced adjustments in the Government's policies and led to delays and reversals in policy initiatives included in the SAL programs. The speed of implementation was also affected by a shortage of technical personnel and the preoccupation of the authorities with short-term stabilization efforts.  

In 1986, it was agreed between IDA and the Government that, for the foreseeable future, Bank financial support for the Government's structural adjustment efforts will take the form of sector adjustment credits rather than comprehensive SALs. IDA has approved six sector adjustment operations, two for agriculture, one for industry, one for the financial sector, an export development program, and an adjustment credit for education. IDA is currently reviewing this lending strategy because Kenya's failure to deal with cross-sectoral issues is undermining the effectiveness of previous sectoral reforms and is forcing the economy onto a low long-term growth path. IDA is also concerned that the slow pace of adjustment under the sectoral approach has been insufficient for the desired rate of economic growth, job creation, and poverty alleviation.
