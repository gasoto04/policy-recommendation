# EXECUTIVE SUMMARY  

# Recent developments  

Senegal has persevered with the sound macroeconomic policy stance adopted during the previous three-year program supported by a three-year ESAF arrangement, and implementation of the medium-term reform strategy has been broadly satisfactory. Recent economic and financial performance has been better than expected, but there were some delays in structural reforms.  

The quantitative performance criteria and benchmarks for end-June l998 were observed, with the exception of technical deviations in three areas relating to debt management and domestic payment delays, for which corrective actions have been taken. Waivers are being requested. The structural performance criteria and benchmarks for end-July have been met. However, the call for bids for the privatization of the electricity company (SENELEC), a structural benchmark for August, was delayed until October.  

Despite a further reduction in agricultural output, economic growth is projected to increase to 5.7 percent in 1998, about $\nu_{2}$ of l percentage point above the program assumption. Inflation was negligible during the first half of the year; prices increased in the third quarter when food prices went up because of supply shortages, but inflation is expected to remain beneath the program objective (2.4 percent) for the year as a whole. The external current account deficit (excluding official transfers and before debt relief) is expected to be somewhat larger than programmed, mainly because of higher imports of foodstuffs and equipment. The Asian crisis thus far has not significantly affected the Senegalese economy.  

The fiscal targets for l998 are expected to be met. Performance during the first half was better than programmed, as revenue collection was larger than forecast (despite significant reductions in external tariffs and in petroleum taxation) and continued restraint kept current government spending within budget limits.  

The first step in the comprehensive reform of the external tariff (undertaken in the context of the introduction of the common external tariff (CET) adopted by the members of the West African Economic and Monetary Union, WAEMU), and a major liberalization in the trading and pricing of petroleum products, were successfully implemented in April. Five public enterprises were privatized in the first nine months of 1998, but some delays were encountered in preparing for the privatization of other enterprises, including SENELEC. Steps have been taken to speed up the pace of the privatization program, and the list of enterprises to be privatized in 1999-2000 has been finalized.

# Issues emphasized in the staff appraisal  

The importance of preserving a sound fiscal position while continuing to implement the comprehensive external tariff reform in the context of the CET of the WAEMU. Balancing these objectives will require measures directed at improving government revenue and continuing prudent expenditure management, while shifting resources from low-priority areas in favor of human resources development and the social sectors. The urgency of deepening and accelerating the implementation of the broad-based structural reform program---in particular in the areas of privatization, the energy, transport, agriculture, and banking sectors, and the judiciary-—-in order to bolster private sector confidence and investment.   
The possibility of reviewing the medium-term macroeconomic framework next year to take into account more favorable external support for social and other priority   
spending.  

# 1. INTRODUCTION  

1. Discussions for the midterm review of the program supported by the first annual arrangement under the current three-year Enhanced Structural Adjustment Facility (ESAF) arrangement and for the 1998 Article IV consultation were carried out in Dakar during September 14-28, 1998.1 In the attached letter and memorandum dated October 20, 1998 (Appendix I), the Minister of Economy, Finance, and Planning reviews progress made in the first half of 1998, surveys the outlook for the remainder of the year, and sets out the measures to be implemented during the remainder of 1998 and under the budget for 1999 to achieve the objectives of the program.  

2. The Fund has continued to support Senegal's medium-term adjustment and reform strategy through a new three-year arrangement under the ESAF in an amount equivalent to SDR 107.01 million (90 percent of quota), approved on April 20, 1998.² The first annual arrangement thereunder was approved at the same time in an amount equivalent to SDR 35.67 million (30 percent of quota), and the first semiannual loan (SDR 17.8 million) was disbursed shortly thereafter. Availability of the second semiannual installment is contingent upon observance of the quantitative and structural performance criteria for end-June and end-July, respectively, and completion of the midterm review. If the planned disbursements under the ESAF arrangement and allrepurchases and loan repayments are made as scheduled, Senegal's use of Fund resources would increase from SDR 205.3 million (172.7 percent of quota) at the end of September 1998 to SDR 238.2 milion (200.3 percent of quota) by the end of December 20o0. Senegal's relations with the Fund are summarized in Appendix II. Senegal is on the standard 12-month consultation cycle.  

3.In concluding the last Article IV consultation on July 28, 1997, Directors noted the progress achieved in the area of macroeconomic stabilization and structural reforms, but they underscored that Senegal's economic situation remained fragile. In that context, they stressed the need to consolidate the improvement in the fiscal position and to strengthen the financial sector, and urged the authorities to fully implement the structural reform agenda envisaged in the program.  

4.The World Bank has been supporting Senegal's development efforts through structural and sectoral adjustment lending, including four operations approved in 1997 in the sectors of health, urban development and decentralization, agricultural export promotion, and regional power; in addition, an energy sector adjustment credit equivalent to US\$100 million was approved in May 1998. A public expenditure review focusing on the education, health, and judicial sectors willbe completed by the years' end and the recommendations will be implemented in 1999. The International Finance Corporation is involved in seven investments in Senegal in the areas of manufacturing and financial sector development. A Consultative Group meeting for Senegal, held in April 1998, secured donor support for the period 1998- 2000. Senegal's relations with the World Bank Group are summarized in Appendix III.  

5.Senegal's economic database is fairly comprehensive, although weaknesses remain with regard to coverage and internal consistency, in particular in the national accounts, and in data on industrial production, international trade, and the balance of payments. The introduction of a new accounting system for commercial banks continues to cause minor delays in the compilation of monetary statistics, but it has also led to some reclassifications and revisions of monetary data, which have improved their quality. Weaknesses in data collection and compilation, which have not prevented a meaningful assessment of economic policies, are being remedied with technical assistance from the Fund and other partners. The authorities have regularly been providing the core minimum data to the Fund. The statistical issues are summarized in Appendix IV.  

6.The parliamentary elections held in May 1998 were won by the ruling Parti Socialiste, which carried 93 seats in parliament (out of a total of 140), albeit with a fairly low voter turnout. A new government was formed in July, with the former Minister of Finance, Mr. Mamadou Lamine Loum, becoming Prime Minister, and the former Minister delegate in charge of the Budget, Mr. Moustapha Diagne, becoming Minister of Finance. Insecurity continues in the southern province of Casamance, and the Senegalese army has been in the forefront of the efforts to protect the government in neighboring Guinea-Bissau against a military insurrection since June. Diplomatic initiatives toward a peaceful settlement of the crisis are under way, including mediation efforts conducted under the auspices of the Economic Community of West African States'(ECOWAS) and a contact group of Portugesespeaking African countries. The social climate in Senegal was intermittently tense during the summer, with sporadic strikes and demonstrations to protest the continued wage freeze and the arrest on grounds of sabotage of union leaders opposed to the privatization of the electricity company (SENELEC). Tensions have abated somewhat since September 1998.  

# II. BACKGROUND AND RECENT ECONOMIC PERFORMANCE  

7. On the economic front, implementation of the authorities' strategy under the ESAF-supported program has been broadly satisfactory. Most quantitative performance criteria and benchmarks for end-June l998 were observed; however, there has been a technical nonobservance of three performance criteria, and waivers are being correspondingly requested. All quantitative indicators for end-June and structural performance criteria and benchmarks for end-July 1998 have been observed. However, the call for bids for the privatization of SENELEC, a structural benchmark for August, was completed only in early October.  

8. Economic developments so far in 1998 have been encouraging. Despite a further reduction in agricultural output because of drought, continued robust growth in construction, commerce, and other private services is expected to help achieve a real GDP growth rate of 5.7 percent in 1998 (Figure I and Table 1), slightly higher than programmed. Prices were stable in the first half of the year, and, following weather-induced upward pressure on food prices in the third quarter, it is expected that price increases willabate in the latter part of the year, enabling the achievement of the programmed inflation target (2.4 percent) for the year as a whole. Senegal's competitive position has been fairly well maintained: the consumer pricebased real effective exchange rate of the CFA franc so far in 1998 is approximately 30 percent below its pre-l994 devaluation level (Figure 2). Nonetheless, in the first half of 1998, the volume of exports grew by 3.8 percent compared with the same period in 1997, while the volume of imports increased substantially over the level of the previous year, reflecting an increase of both dutiable imports and foreign-financed capital goods (which are largely tax exempt).  

First, a nonconcessional loan from the regional development bank (BOAD) for the financing. together with neighboring countries Mali and Mauritania, of a hydroelectric power plant—which had been agreed in 1997 and anticipated in the 1997 program---was finally contracted only in April 1998, breaching the zero limit on the contracting of nonconcessional lending for this year. Second, an external payments arrear was incurred when, owing to poor coordination within the government, the Economic Promotion Fund did not fully settle an obligation to the African Development Bank on time; this arrear was cleared in June 1998. Third, some domestic payment arrears were incurred because of delays in settling contractual obligations to an international service company; however, the necessary provisions had been deposited with the central bank and discussons had taken place with the company concerning the payment terms, which have now been agreed.

9. The fiscal performance during the first half of 1998 was substantially better than programmed. The overall deficit (excluding grants) was significantly below target, and the basic fiscal surplus4 was twice the programmed level. Government revenue was 9.3 percent above program projections, essentially because of the good performance in corporate income taxes and the earlier-than-expected payment of fishing license fees by the European Union. Revenue from international trade taxes was also higher than forecast, despite the reduction in tariffs introduced in April, because the level of dutiable imports was appreciably higher than expected. Revenue from petroleum taxes was less than expected, reflecting both the decline in international petroleum prices and delays in the payment of duties by the refinery (SAR) because of arrears owed to SAR by SENELEC. As a result of continued stringent expenditure management, spending was in line with program projections. Salary outlays were somewhat lower than anticipated, owing to a delay in implementing the merit-based promotion system in the civil service; meanwhile, capital expenditures were slightly above target, as the externally funded investment program was implemented faster than expected. A deficit reemerged on the special and correspondent accounts because of unscheduled advances granted by the treasury to the National Retirement Fund (FNR) and the Dakar Urban Community. According to preliminary indications, this favorable fiscal performance was sustained through end-September, with overall revenue well above-and salary outlays below——their targeted levels, and an overall basic budgetary balance twice as large as programmed.  

10.  Senegal's monetary policy is conducted at the regional level by the Central Bank of West African States (BCEAO). To thwart some inflationary pressures and to protect the external asset position of the zone, the BCEAO increased the reserve requirement rate from 1.5 percent to 5 percent and lifted its“pension" and discount rates by 0.25 percent in August. In line with the normal seasonal pattern, broad money was on the whole unchanged in the first half of 1998. Net domestic assets of the banking system fell somewhat, as a decline in net credit to the government was less than offset by the continued growth in credit to the private sector. There was a slight positive contribution to the net foreign asset position of the BCEAO. The banking system remained relatively healthy: all banks, except two, were profitable in 1997, and only one small bank needs recapitalization. However, while the rate of nonobservance of prudential ratios established by the regional banking commission is lower than two years ago, a number of ratios are still not being observed by some banks.5 The volume of delinquent loans rose somewhat in early l998, with the ratio of net nonperforming assets to the total credit to the economy increasing from 8.9 percent at end-1997 to 10.4 percent at end-May 1998, though this remains a low level by regional standards. The five nonbank financial institutions met the required prudential ratios at end-1997.

11. Progress on structural reforms has been made in several areas (Box 1), but there were also some delays. As a first step toward implementing the CET adopted by all countries of the West African Economic and Monetary Union (WAEMU), the authorities simplified the import taxation system (merging the customs and fiscal duty rates into a single tariff rate), and the unweighted rate of import taxation (including the 5 percent customs stamp duty) fell from an average of 29 percent in 1994-97 to 18 percent in April 1998. The rationalization of the surtaxes and the complete elimination of the import duty rebates for certain goods contingent on their end use, as prescribed by the code des precisions, has been postponed until early 1999, pending a comprehensive agreement among the WAEMU country members. The distribution, sale, and retail prices of petroleum products were liberalized, and a new petroleum taxation system was introduced in April, as scheduled. Prices are now adjusted monthly, and the lower world market prices have already been reflected in domestic prices. These reforms will also help to reduce the cost of the subsidies on butane gas and the fuel sold to SENELEC for electricity generation. Five public enterprises have been privatized so far in 1998 but some delays were encountered in preparing for the privatization of the other enterprises slated for privatization in 1998, including SENELEC. The reform of the legal and judicial system is proceeding as scheduled, as detailed in paragraph 12 of the attached memorandum.  

# III. POLICY DISCUSSIONS  

12.The discussions were conducted in the context of Senegal's medium-term strategy, which seeks to consolidate the gains recorded under the previous three-year ESAF arrangement and to address the unfinished reform agenda needed to maintain domestic and external financial balances and achieve strong and sustained economic growth and a lasting reduction in poverty. The main medium-term macroeconomic indicators are presented in Box 2. The discussions focused in particular on the implementation of the ESAF-supported program in the first nine months of 1998, on the macroeconomic and structural policies required for the remainder of 1998, and on the draft budget for 1999. Particular attention was given to the areas where some delays had been experienced, in particular the rationalization of external trade taxation and the implementation of the privatization program. The authorities reconfirmed their strong commitment to the reform program and their determination to make up for the delays incurred.

13.The main macroeconomic objectives for 1998 remain largely unchanged from those specified in the memorandum on economic policies and the medium-term policy framework paper of February 1998.% As noted, overall economic growth for 1998 is now expected to reach 5.7 percent, driven by the good performance of the construction, commerce, and other services sectors. This stronger-than-projected growth has boosted private savings slightly above the programmed level. In turn, this outcome, together with stronger-than-targeted public savings and an increased inflow of foreign assistance, has led to a higher-than-projected increase in investment. Total gross domestic investment is expected to be close to 20 percent of GDP, well above the program target and nearer to the goal of 2l percent of GDP set for the year 2000; private investment is expected to reach 13.7 percent of GDP, versus a programmed 13.l percent (Table 2). As noted, average inflation for the year is projected to be in line with program targets.  

14.The external current account deficit (excluding offcial transfers and before debt relief), at 7.7 percent of GDP in 1998, would be about $\%$ of 1 percentage point of GDP higher than programmed, essentially owing to a sharp increase in externally funded imports of capital goods. This increase was made possible by higher-than-assumed leveis of official transfers and net capital inflows (both public and private), as the implementation of structural reforms and gradual improvements in investment programming allowed the absorption of greater inflows of aid and private investments? without jeopardizing fiscal and external sustainability. Once debt relief granted by the Paris Club in June l998 is taken into account (see Box 3), the current account deficit should be close to the targeted level. Senegal's contribution to the foreign assets of the BCEAO is projected to be larger than originally assumed. Following the Paris Club stock-of-debt operation, Senegal has initiated discussions with Paris Club creditors and expects to conclude the agreements before end 1998. Similarly, the authorities have approached non-Paris club creditors to request debt relief on terms comparable to Paris club creditors, but this process could take longer to finalize.  

15.With respect to fiscal policy, the positive results in the first part of the year and the government's intention to continue with appropriately restrained policies augur well for achieving the program's fiscal targets for the year as a whole. The overall deficit (on a commitment basis and excluding grants) is expected to be equivalent to 2 percent of GDP in 1998, as initially programmed (Table 3). As grants are likely to exceed projections, the deficit after grants should be only 0.3 percent of GDP. This willbe achieved with total tax revenue in line with program projections, though with a higher-than-expected level of taxes on imports offset by a lower-than-programmed level of VAT on domestic products, since the strong performance of VAT receipts in the last quarter of 1997 was insufficiently discounted as a catch-up effect resulting from legal changes in the timing of payment for telephone services. Nonetheless, the mission stressed the need to move more decisively in implementing measures to broaden the tax base, curtail exemptions, and improve tax administration to achieve the revenue targets. In particular, the dysfunctional protective tariffs on rice imports will be reviewed and streamlined before end-1998. On the expenditure side, the authorities intend to swiftly implement the merit-based promotion system and to restrict the number of civil servants to below the present level of 67,o0o. The cost of the military intervention in GuineaBissau so far has been limited to 0.1 percent of GDP, as it has covered only food and  

operational premiums. Other current expenditures for this (and next) year will be higher than programmed because subsidies on sales of butane gas and fuel for electricity generation are now shown explicitly rather than netted out from taxes on petroleum products. Adequate allocations will continue to be made to the priority sectors of health and education, and the FNR will be reformed in early 1999. It was agreed that the savings resulting from lower interest payments following the stock-of-debt operation obtained from the Paris Club (0.5 percent of GDP in 1998) will be used mainly to settle a number of unforeseen obligations in the areas of social assistance and restructuring of public enterprises.9

# IV. MEDIUM-TERM OUTLOOK FOR THE BALANCE OF PAYMENTS AND DEBT SUSTAINABILITY  

21.The medium-term outlook for the balance of payments was revised in light of the recent developments in the external sector, including changes in commodity prices; meanwhile the original macroeconomic assumptions are virtually unchanged.14 On the assumption that the value of exports would increase at a slightly more rapid pace than imports, and that the balance of services would improve beginning in 1999, the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers and after debt relief) is projected to decline by a little more than 1 percent of GDP over the program period, from 7.6 percent of GDP in 1997 to 6.3 percent in the year 2000, and then to continue narrowing to 3.3 percent of GDP by 2003 (Table 6). Assuming a gradual reduction in official transfers, the external current account deficit, including official transfers, is projected to increase slightly from 1.6 percent of GDP in 1997 to 2.2 percent in 2000, followed by a sharp reduction to 0.1 percent of GDP in 2003.  

22.An assessment of Senegal's external debt situation at the end of 1997 and its sustainability in a long-term perspective was prepared in February 1998.15 On the basis of this analysis, the country's external debt was judged by the Executive Boards of the Fund and the Bank to be sustainable under the terms of the Initiative for Heavily Indebted Poor Countries (HIPC). The broad conclusions of this analysis remain valid. Senegal is expected to continue to pursue a prudent debt-management policy and to implement sound economic, financial, and structural policies. Taking into account the debt relief obtained from Paris Club and other bilateral creditors, external debt service as a percentage of exports of goods and nonfactor services will remain modest, declining from 13 percent in 1998 to 10 percent in 2003. Debt service to the Fund is projected to be between 1.5 percent and 2 percent of exports of goods and nonfactor services over the l998-2006 period, and to decline thereafter. Given Senegal's excellent record in meeting its debt-service obligations to the Fund and its low debt ratios, its ability to repay future obligations to the Fund is expected to remain very strong.  

# V. STAFF APPRAISAL  

23.The Senegalese authorities have persevered with the sound macroeconomic policy stance adopted during the previous three-year ESAF arrangement. Achievements have been commendable, with a significant reduction in financial imbalances, containment of inflation, and notable progress in a number of structural areas, including trade liberalization and energy sector reforms. The overall economic and financial prospect for l999, including the main elements of the 1999 draft budget, are also favorable. The good macroeconomic management and the gradual lessening of distortions over the past four years have started to elicit a good supply response in the secondary and tertiary sectors, which have led the recent expansion in output. However, overall growth remains below potential, and a significant reduction in poverty has not yet been achieved because of repeated delays in reforming a number of key, deep-rooted structural rigidities. The authorities should forcefully implement the structural reform agenda, while continuing to pursue macroeconomic stability and efficiency, so as to enhance the credibility of the government's adjustment program and bolster private sector confidence and investment. These actions are crucial to put the economy on the path of longterm sustainable growth and to achieve a lasting reduction in poverty.  

24.From this perspective, the authorities should concentrate their efforts in the following two areas:  

Persevere with fiscal consolidation. It is essential to preserve the improved fiscal position while implementing the comprehensive external tariff measures under way in the context of WAEMU's CET reform. To this end, the government needs to rationalize the system of import surcharges and reference values, and to eliminate the remaining import duty rebates for certain industries by January 1, 1999. Aiso, the authorities should carefully prepare measures to compensate for possible government revenue losses following the implementation of the second phase of tariff reductions on January 1, 2000. These measures should include a broadening of the tax base, a sharp reduction in exemptions, a further improvement in tax administration, and, in particular, the introduction of a singie-rate VAT. In parallel, there should be a continued restraint in expenditure management and a shift of resources from the government wage bill and lowpriority expenditures (including military outlays) toward the areas of human resources development, in particular, education, health, basic infrastructure, and the judicial system.  

Strengthen the implementation of a market-driven, private sector-based development strategy. Senegal's growth performance remains hampered by delays in implementing structural reforms and in addressing deep-seated structural rigidities. The authorities are encouraged to move as expeditiously as possble to liberalize the regulatory framework and to implement their structural reform program, in particular in the areas of privatization and in the transport and agricultural sectors. In this regard, it is important that the authorities continue their efforts to build a broad-based constituency for reforms so that the government is better able to address the concerns of labor or special interest groups determined to oppose them.  

25.The government's near- and medium-term macroeconomic objectives are fully achievable in the absence of large unfavorable supply-side shocks. The external prospects appear generally favorable, as exports are increasing and prospects for sustained donors support are better than originaly envisaged. Donors have indicated their readiness to increase funding on very concessional terms, with a larger proportion to be directed to social sectors. This funding would provide the opportunity to accelerate the implementation of urgent social and other priority programs without burdening the policy of fiscal consolidation. If confirmed, such forward-looking aid commitments would provide welcome room for fiscal policy that could be usefully explored for 1999 and beyond. To avoid jeopardizing fiscal and external sustainability, however, it willbe essential that revenue performance continue to improve and that the budgetary control mechanisms be strengthened to ensure the effectiveness of the expenditures.  

26. In view of the technical nature of the nonobservance of the performance criteria, the corrective actions taken, the progress made thus far, and the authorities' strong commitment to the successful completion of the program, the staff supports the authorities' request for waivers and recommends that the present midterm review under the first annual ESAF arrangement be completed.

# Memorandum for the Conclusion of the Midterm Review of the Program for 1998  

October 20, 1998  

1． The medium-term economic program presented in the policy framework paper for 1998-2000 and in the memorandum on economic and financial policies for 1998, dated February 27, l998, aims to consolidate the results of previous programs, and, primarily through second-generation reforms, to achieve strong, sustained growth, to reduce poverty, and to maintain domestic and external financial viability. To achieve these objectives, the government has focused its actions in five key areas: consolidation of the fiscal position; the modernization of the state, primarily through the reform of public administration and privatization; the acceleration and deepening of sectoral reforms, particularly in the energy, transportation, and agriculture sectors; the creation of a favorable environment for private sector development; and an intensification of efforts to develop human resources.  

2. The quantitative medium-term objectives of the program are to increase real GDP growth from an annual average of about 5 percent in recent years to about 6 percent (to be achieved primarily through higher rates of private saving and investment); to maintain a rate of inflation of below 3 percent, in line with Senegal's main trading partners; to maintain an overall budget deficit (on a commitment basis and excluding grants) of not more than 2 percent of GDP; and to lower the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) to less than 7 percent of GDP in 2000, consistent with expected flows of project assistance and private foreign investment. The principal macroeconomic program objectives for 1998 were real GDP growth of at least 5.3 percent, an inflation rate of below 2.4 percent, an overal budget deficit (on a commitment basis, excluding grants) of 2 percent of GDP, and an external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) of 7.3 percent of GDP. The program also envisaged an increase in the investment ratio from 18.7 percent of GDP in 1997 to 19.2 percent, which would require an increase in the domestic saving rate of about $\%$ of l percentage point of GDP to 13.8 percent.  

# I. RECENT ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENTS  

3. In 1997, despite a contraction in agricultural output, real GDP growth reached 5.2 percent and inflation was contained, with the consumer price index for Dakar increasing by l.8 percent. The volumes of exports and imports rose by 1.6 percent and 3 percent, respectively, and the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) remained at 7.6 percent of GDP. Achievement of the programmed level of government revenue and effective control of spending made it possible to reduce the overall fiscal deficit (on a  

commitment basis and excluding grants) from 2.2 percent of GDP in 1996 to 1.5 percent in 1997. The money supply grew by 7.3 percent, in line with the increase in nominal GDP. The net domestic assets of the banking system were reduced by 8 percent, largely through a strong contraction in net credit to the government, owing, in turn, to large privatization receipts and the mobilization of a concessional loan from Taiwan Province of China. These inflows were reflected in a sharp increase in Senegal's contribution to the net foreign assets of the Central Bank of West African States (BCEAO). Credit to the economy increased by nearly 14 percent, mainly because of sustained activity in the construction, commerce, and services sectors.  

4. Overall, economic developments in the first half of 1998 were favorable. Despite a further contraction in agricultural production, caused by a drought during the 1997/98 growing season, preliminary indications of output trends in other sectors suggest that GDP growth is likely to be 5.7 percent for the year. In the first half of 1998, the harmonized consumer price indexl showed an increase of only 0.7 percent over the corresponding period in 1997, as it was influenced by the decline in the prices of petroleum products and some other products, following the petroleum and tariffreforms. However, a late start to the rainy season accentuated the normal seasonal rise in food prices in July and August. The real effective exchange rate for the CFA franc remained stable in the first half of 1998, some 30 percent more depreciated in real terms than just prior to the 1994 devaluation, and Senegal thus maintained its competitive position. Export volumes grew by about 3.8 percent compared with the first half of 1997, primarily because of a slowdown in groundnut exports. In contrast, imports increased substantially over the corresponding period in the previous year, mainly as a result of a strong rise in imports of rice and capital goods.

Tax receipts were CFAF 222 billion---3.5 percent above the program target-—because of strong receipts from income taxes and taxes on foreign trade. 7. Public expenditure was in line with program targets through June of 1998. Current expenditure was lower than programmed, including on the wage bill which was maintained below the benchmark in part because the introduction of the merit-based promotion system in the civil service was delayed. However, capital expenditure was slightly higher than programmed because of faster-than-expected implementation of the externally funded investment program. The deficit on the special accounts and correspondent accounts deteriorated in the first half of the year, largely owing to advances granted to the National Pension Fund (FNR) and the Urban Community of Dakar. The overall budget deficit (on a commitment basis and excluding grants) was CFAF 3.2 billion (and, therefore, wellbelow the CFAF 22.9 billion programmed), and the basic fiscal surplus? of CFAF 44 billion was double the amount programmed.  

8． In line with the normal seasonal pattern, the money supply remained broadly unchanged over the first half of 1998.4 The net domestic assets of the banking system fell, mainly owing to a decline in credit to the government, arising from the government's improved financial position. But credit to the economy continued to rise, particularly in the second quarter, reflecting strong activity in the secondary and tertiary sectors. Senegal's contribution to the net foreign assets of the BCEAO continued to be significant. Because of regional concerns about inflation, the expansion of credit, and transfers abroad by banks, the BCEAO raised reserve requirements for banks as of August l6, 1998 from 1.5 percent to 5 percent, and, as of August 31, 1998, it increased its repurchase and discount rates by $1/4$ of 1 point, to 5.75 percent and 6.25 percent, respectively. Some interest rates have also risen slightly since the beginning of the year, with deposit rates settling at 4.95 percent at end-September l998; however, the bank prime lending rate has continued to average 8.6 percent. The Senegalese banking system is generally healthy, and most banks remained profitable in 1997. However, a number of prudential ratios established by WAMU's Banking Commission are not yet being observed by some banks, and the number of delinquent loans rose during the first quarter of the year.  

9. Structural reforms in the tax area were introduced as scheduled:  

■ A significant tariff reform, linked to the gradual implementation of a common external tariff (CET) by all WAEMU member countries, was adopted into law on April l7, 1998. This reform combined the customs and revenue duties into a single customs duty, simplifying the tariff system and reducing tariff protection through the introduction of four rates (O, 5, 10, and 25 percent), in addition to a customs duty stamp of 5 percent. This led to a reduction in the average effective rate on dutiable imports from 22 percent to 17 percent. Surtaxes on agricultural products and cigarettes were maintained with unchanged rates, pending the adoption of communitywide arrangements for surtaxes. In anticipation of the common product classification expected to enter into force on January 1, l999, Senegal has already adopted a provisional tariff classification consistent with the WAEMU proposals. However, delays were experienced in the introduction of some reform measures associated with the tariff reduction, such as the reduction of exemptions, the follow-up to conditional import arrangements, and the improvement of the GAINDE software used by customs.  

The reform of taxation on petroleum products was adopted, as planned, in April and entered into effect on May 9. The impact on government revenue was fully felt only in July because of the 60-day period for the payment of duties. The drop in world petroleum prices resulted in a fall in petroleum product retail prices and made it possible to reduce to below programmed levels the subsidies on butane, and on the fuel used by SENELEC to generate electricity.  

10.Among the structural measures of the program, the liberalization of the importing, refining, storage, transportation, and distribution of hydrocarbons was adopted in April. Prices are now automatically adjusted each month to reflect world prices (in advance of the timetable in the program, which targeted quarterly adjustments in 1998 and monthly adjustments starting only in 1999).  

11. Five companies were privatized in the first nine months of the year: the Thies phosphates company (SSPT); the Teranga Hotel; HAMO, a housing company; Dakar-Marine, a shipyard; and SONADIS, a distribution company. In addition, in June 1998, the chemicals company, ICS, increased its equity capital in readiness for expansion; since the government did not participate in this operation, its shareholding in the company was reduced from 58 percent to 47 percent. An international invitation to bid for the urban transport concession and the assets of the existing transport company (SOTRAC) was issued on September 1. However, some delays occurred in the preparations for the privatization of other companies, including SENELEC. A report on the economic and financial performance of public enterprises in 1996 was provided to Fund staf, and the report relating to their performance in 1997 will be available by end-1998, after the accounts have been certified by the auditors.  

12. The reforms of the legal and judicial framework governing economic activity are progressing smoothly, both with respect to the regional initiatives to harmonize business law through the OHADA Treaty and the complementary initiatives taken by the Senegalese government. Currently, eight standard OHADA instruments have been adopted, six of which already have force of law in Senegal, and the remaining two will have force of law by January 1, 1999. The initiatives of the legal reform committee, chaired by the Minister of Justice, include a number of reforms relating to, inter alia, international commercial arbitration; to those professions associated with the legal control of enterprises in difficulty; the procedures for foreclosing on collateral; and a banking charter. These reforms are being finalized and willbe submitted for approval by the National Assembly at its forthcoming session. Efforts are continuing to step up the training of magistrates and to modernize the courts. In the area of good governance, it has not been possible to launch the user survey on the quality of public services, scheduled for the second quarter of 1998. However, arrangements have recently been made to complete the preparation of the questionnaires and establish the consultative bodies, and the survey will be finalized before end-1998.  

# II. PROGRAM FOR THE REMAINDER OF 1998  

13. Based on the progress made in the first half of l998, the government is determined to press ahead with the implementation of allthe measures in the program, so as to meet the targets adopted for the year. Growth prospects continue to be favorable, in particular in the construction and public works, commerce, and services sectors, and real GDP growth is expected to reach 5.7 percent in 1998. Inflation is anticipated to be in line with program targets, as the price increases observed in July and August are expected to be reversed before the year's end because of the improvement, albeit belated, in rainfalland the increase in the supply of foodstuffs that was expected to begin in October. The external current account deficit (excluding official transfers and before debt relief) is expected to be somewhat larger than the programmed level of 7.3 percent of GDP, mainly because of a sharp increase in  

externally funded imports of capital goods and in private capital inflows. However, once debt relief is taken into account, the overall deficit should be close to the programmed level.5  

14. In light of the positive results in the first half of the year and sound economic management in the second half, the government believes that budget execution for the whole of 1998 will be in line with program targets. Total fiscal revenue is expected to be CFAF 449.4 billion, which is slightly higher than the CFAF 446.9 billion initially programmed. The overall budget deficit (on a commitment basis, excluding grants, and after debt relief) is estimated to be 2.1 percent of GDP, close to the 2.0 percent in the program. Because of a higher-than-anticipated level of grants, the overall deficit including grants and on a commitment basis, wil be 0.3 percent of GDP, compared with a programmed deficit of 1 percent. The basic fiscal surplus is'expected to be 1.3 percent of GDP, as programmed.  

15. Reflecting prudent fiscal management, expenditure and net lending will remain within program limits, aided in part by a reduction of interest charges (of some CFAF 14 billion) resulting from the debt relief obtained from Paris Club creditors. The merit-based promotion system will be implemented by the end of the year (having been postponed until now), the overall wage bill will be kept within the program limits, and the number of civil servants (excluding volunteer teachers) at less than 67,0o0. Any savings achieved on wages will continue to be directed toward sectors promoting the development of human resources. The share of the social sectors is expected to reach 41.2 percent of the operating budget, as programmed, with 33.6 percent going to education and 7.6 percent going to health. In addition, the government will meet a number of unscheduled obligations: the provision of foodstuffs to the regions affected by drought; military expenditure on food and operational bonuses relating to the intervention in Guinea-Bissau; coverage of the FNR's structural deficit following the delay in introducing deficit-reduction measures; and severance payments to employees of SOTRAC, which will be liquidated before the year's end. The expenditure forecasts for 1998 also include a l997 payment for work done by an international services company. The authorities consider that the delay in paying for part of this work does not constitute a payments arrear because substantial provisions for these payments have already been made in the form of a treasury deposit with the central bank, and agreement has been reached on a schedule for payment by the end of the year. Nonetheless, the authorities request a waiver for the nonobservance of the relevant performance criterion.  

16. Capital expenditure in 1998 as a whole is being implemented as planned, within the framework of the three-year public investment program (PTIP) for 1998--2000. The government has provided Fund staff with the report on the new approach to budget programming and the action plan for improving the preparation of the investment budget (an end-July 1998 structural benchmark). Lastly, the review of public expenditure in the  

education, health, and justice sectors, which is being carried out in collaboration with World Bank staff, is expected to lead to improved policy formulation in these sectors and lay the groundwork for a reform of budgetary procedures. It willbe completed by the end of the year.  

17.The government will continue to pay its bill promptly for the consumption of water, electricity, and telephone services, so as to prevent any payment delays. The remaining government obligations on account of past extrabudgetary arrears continue to be paid and will be fully settled by the year's end, as programmed. In the future, all arrears on legally mandated payments from the government will be integrated in the budget and paid as current expenditure. Regarding the FNR, the action plan to reduce the structural deficit atributable to delays in the introduction of corrective measures willbe adopted by the year's end and launched in 1999. The government will also accelerate the finalization and submission to the Audit Office and the National Assembly of the final finance bills still pending.  

18.Following the Consultative Group meeting for Senegal in April 1998, Senegal benefited in June from a stock-of-debt operation on Naples terms (a 67 percent reduction of eligible debt and rescheduling of the remainder) from the Paris Club. This debt relief, together with previously unanticipated financing from the World Bank and the European Union, largely covers the residual financing gaps for the 1998-20o0 period identified in the program.  

19. Under the program, the monetary policy of the BCEAO will remain prudent, in accordance with the regional objectives of stabilizing prices and consolidating the external position of the union. Broad money in Senegal is expected to increase by about 8 percent in 1998, in line with the growth in nominal GDP. The net domestic assets of the banking system will increase by nearly 4 percent over end-1997, owing to the combined effect of a 4 percent growth in net credit to the government and an 1l percent rise in credit to the economy, sufficient to meet the needs of the productive sectors of the economy. In these circumstances, it is expected that there will be a further increase in Senegal's contribution to the external position of the BCEAO. Given the more favorable outcome in the first half of the year, and taking into account the recent revisions to the monetary accounts, the ceiling on net bank credit to the central government at end-December 1998 has been revised downward.  

20. Senegal, in collaboration with the other WAEMU member states, will support the efforts of the monetary authorities to improve the instruments of indirect management of monetary policy. It is expected that the WAEMU regional stock exchange, which began operations on September 16, will help improve the efficiency of intermediation between private savings and investments. The government will strongly support the efforts made by the WAMU Banking Commission to regularly inspect banks operating in Senegal and to work out a program to achieve full compliance with prudential ratios. It will also pay particular attention to monitoring the development and solvency of mutual savings and loan institutions.  

# II. FISCAL PROSPECTS FOR 1999  

21. In the context of the medium-term framework of the program, the authorities discussed with Fund staff the draft budget for 1999, in which provision is made for a narrowing of the overalldeficit (on a commitment basis and excluding grants) to 0.8 percent of GDP in 1999, and a basic fiscal surplus of the order of 2.3 percent of GDP. Fiscal revenue is projected to be CFAF 492 billion, or 15.8 percent of GDP, which is slightly below the level initially programmed, essentially because of lower income taxes. This projected fall in income taxes results from the decision not to implement an across-the-board salary increase; it also reflects both an increase in the threshold of, and a reduction in, income tax rates to increase the disposable income of all wage earners—-in particular those on low wages. The impact of the lower income taxation willbe limited to a maximum of CFAF 6 billion in order to minimize the reduction in government revenue. The estimates for petroleum taxation are based on an assumption of international prices and an import parity that are 10 percent higher than the CFA franc prices prevailing on September 19, 1998. Based on this assumption, a cap of CFAF 8.2 billon will be placed on the amount of subsidies on fuel products used for the production of electricity and on butane gas. Any additional increase in international prices will be accommodated by promptly raising retail selling prices for these two products, which will continue to be subsidized until 200l and 2002, respectively, as established in the energy sector reform program agreed with the World Bank. Prices of other petroleum products will continue to be automatically adjusted every month on the basis of developments in international prices.  

22. Total expenditure and net lending has been set at CFAF 517 billion (16.6 percent of GDP) in 1999, slightly below the programmed level. The wage bill will be maintained at CFAF 167.5 billion, which is below the original target of CFAF 174.3 billion, following the decision not to provide a general salary increase and to maintain staff below 67,000 employees. The social sectors will continue to be given priority, and their share of current expenditure will be 41.1 percent, with 32.9 percent going to education and 8.2 percent going to health. The correspondent accounts are expected to generate net revenue of CFAF 4.4 bilion, owing to the repayment to the treasury by the defunct rice stabilization authority (CPSP) of past onlending. Capital expenditure executed within the framework of the PTIP for 1999-2001 will total CFAF 178 billion in 1999 and will ensure continued growth in domestically financed expenditure and an increase in the share of spending allocated to social infrastructure. The composition of the PTIP for 1999-200l and the improvement of budgetary procedures were discussed with World Bank staff. The weaknesses in investment appraisal and monitoring highlighted by the public expenditure review will be addressed, in particular through the improved capacity for planning and executing public investments. Among other things, these improvements will establish a more consistent linkage between the PTIP and the budget, as all capital expenditures executed by the government—-including those financed with external resources that were off budget in the past—will be included in the government's table of financial operations.  

23. Effective January l, 1999, Senegal will make some additional adjustments to the tariff classification of imported products to match the common classification adopted by the WAEMU Council of Ministers on July 3, 1998. In this context, Senegal willalso eliminate any of the remaining tariff differentiations under the code des precisions. This step will follow the adoption by the WAEMU of customs procedures more appropriate for the imported inputs used in the local production of some products, such as medicines and schoolbooks, which have been classified in the CET as goods with a zero rate. In addition to the CET, temporary surtaxes and the use of reference values for purposes of protection are the subject of discussions at the regional level; these are expected to be completed by end-1998. Senegal intends to limit such surtaxes to a very small number of products, and in no case will the combined rate of duties and surtaxes exceed 50 percent. Finally, the protective tariffs on rice imports, which do not work efficiently, will be reviewed before the end of 1998.  

24. After completing a study on the unification of value-added tax (VAT) rates, the government has decided to introduce a singie VAT rate, but not until January l, 20O0, when it can be better integrated into the second phase of tariff reform within the framework of the CET and the regional harmonization of indirect taxation. Meanwhile, more detailed studies will be carried out to assess both the fiscal and social implications of this measure. In order to cnsure the achievement of the fiscal revenue targets for l999, the government will take the necessary steps to increase the efficiency of the customs and tax administrations in line with the recommendations of the recent technical assistance mission of the Fund. Pending the adoption of the regional investment code within the WAEMU, the government will not broaden the scope, or the provisions, of the Senegalese investment code. The government's central objective is to achieve a tax system that is applied as widely as possible, and to eliminate special arrangements. With this in mind, a number of measures will be implemented by end-1998.  

At customs, the authorities will  

improve the monitoring of the exemptions granted under the regimes suspensifs; extend and improve the computerized management system of the customs (GAINDE); audit the exemptions granted under investment codes, special duty-free   
admissions, and suspensions of duty;   
limit to a strict minimum the waiting periods and formalities for clearing goods through customs;   
introduce detailed monthly monitoring of customs receipts and exemptions; and consolidate operations relating to the new system of taxation of petroleum products.  

At the tax department the authorities will  

complete the computerization of VAT collection procedures and reinforce spot checks on VAT receipts; install a better system for the centralized tax auditing of large enterprises; and improve the management of small and medium-sized taxpayer accounts.  

# IV. STRUCTURAL REFORMS  

25. The government, together with the staffs of the International Monetary Fund and the World Bank, has reviewed the progress made on the reforms contained in the policy framework paper for 1998-2000. The review covered, in particular, the privatization program, support for private sector development, the national program of good governance, reforms in the key sectors of energy, agriculture, and transportation, the development of human resources through improvements in education and health, poverty reduction, and the improvement of statistics. Generally'speaking, these programs are proceeding as planned. However, there have been a few delays in some areas, partly linked to the parliamentary elections and the formation of a new government. These delays are significant in regard to the privatization of SENELEC and the implementation of the program of good governance. The government firmly intends to regain lost time by the end of the year.  

26. The government expects to make final preparations by end-1998 for the sale of shares in eight public enterprises instead of the three initially planned. This program includes five large enterprises (SENELEC; SONACOS, the groundnut processing company; SOTRAC; the Meridien President Hotel; and SODEFITEX, the textile development company) and three small enterprises,? for all of which the preparatory work and the drawing up of bidding documents are well advanced. As regards the electricity company, SENELEC, the call for bids was made on October 13, 1998. For SONACOS, given the difficulties experienced during previous attempts at privatization, the procedures for implementing the privatization strategy are being reexamined with the assistance of an investment bank and willbe reviewed by the government at end-October.  

27. To complete the restructuring program, the list of enterprises to be privatized in 1999-20o0 has been drawn up. The new list includes ten enterprises, for which the preparatory work has already started; seven of these will be put up for sale in 1999: the real estate company, SICAP; Air Senegal; the film company, SIDEC; the company for  

development of the Petite Cote, SAPCO; the national drilling company, SONAFOR; the reinsurance company, SENRE; and the Dakar-Bamako line of the national railway, SNCS. As regards the national lottery (LONASE) and the international foreign trade center (CICES), consideration is being given to studying the possibility of opening up the capital to the private sector. The research center (CEREEQ) is scheduled for an institutional reform, after which a 50 percent ceiling willbe placed on government ownership. At the end of the restructuring of the parapublic sector, which started in 1994, only a very small number of public enterprises will remain in the government's portfolio, and the government's share of portfolio will have been reduced to less than 30 percent, on average, of its initial value. The remaining enterprises will be those that provide a public service, are engaged in industrial or commercial promotion, or have an essential social function (which is thought to be unlikely to be assumed by the private sector). The government will ensure that Fund staff is provided with the report on the economic and financial performance of public enterprises in 1997 as soon as possible, and in any case by the end of 1998.  

28. In support of private sector development, the government will make every effort to facilitate the carrying out of a survey that can be used to prepare a roadmap for investors, as described in the policy framework paper of February 27, 1998. This project, which is being carried out with the assistance of the World Bank's Foreign Investment Advisory Services (FIAS), is expected to pinpoint the difficulties faced by private investment operations and to identify additional reforms needed to improve the investment climate. The survey on the quality of public services will be conducted during the last quarter of 1998.  

# V. OTHER POLICIES  

29. Senegal's debt-management policy will continue to be prudent, with recourse made only to grants or to concessional long-term external loans. The government will neither contract nor guarantee nonconcessional loans, except for trade credits with a term of less than a year. All loans contracted or guaranteed by the government will be subject to prior authorization by the Minister of Economy, Finance, and Planning. The government will ensure that its external obligations are met in a timely fashion and willnot accumulate any external or domestic payments arrears. It will quickly conclude bilateral agreements with Paris Club member countries, in accordance with the agreement obtained in June, and it will seek to conclude agreements with its non-Paris Club bilateral creditors as soon as possible, on terms comparable to those obtained from Paris Club members.  

30. The government is aware of the need to step up actions required to improve the quality and coverage of economic and social statistics. In addition to the measures already taken and those now being implemented to improve monetary, balance of payments, and production statistics, the government will, in the next few months, implement an action plan to reduce remaining statistical weaknesses in the national accounts, foreign trade, industrial production, and social indicators. To this end, it will increase the resources allocated to its statistics departments and will seek technical assistance from the IMF and other partners.  

31.The Senegalese government believes that the policies and measures described in this letter, which supplements the memorandum of February 27, 1998, will help to meet the objectives of the program for 1998. The government wil take any additional measures that may prove necessary for this purpose. In particular, it will (a) oversee the proper conduct of the privatization of SENELEC, in accordance with the new timetable established; (b) complete the reconciliation of budgetary and extrabudgetary investment operations; and (c) settle residual payments arrears by the end of the year.  

32. To build on the considerable progress made in fiscal consolidation and economic restructuring since l994, the government will follow its program of reforms, so as to strengthen growth prospects and significantly reduce poverty. These reforms are expected to contribute to improving the efficiency of public policy in essential areas, to complete the revision of the tax system, especially within the framework of harmonization within the WAEMU, and to promote Senegal's integration into the regional and global economy. These reforms should also contribute to a substantial improvement in the climate for private enterprises and in the functioning of government departments, including the judiciary. The government counts on the continued support of the International Monetary Fund to assist it in achieving these objectives and will seek a second annual arrangement under the ESAF, with a view to consolidating the gains achieved under the current arrangement.

# Commentary on lending operations  

1. The overarching objective of the Bank's assistance strategy for Senegal is to reduce the incidence of poverty and create gainful employment, especially for youth. The approved lending program for the period fiscal year (FY) 1998-2000 under the Country Assistance Strategy (CAS) is almost double the actual FY 1995-97 level; it aims at deepening the structural reforms under way. Achievement of the poverty-reduction objective willbe supported by a two-pronged strategy: (a) supporting policies and programs for more rapid and sustained growth; and (b) ensuring the social sustainability of programs. This strategy will be implemented through a combination of structural adjustment lending (energy sector reform and trade liberalization), the sector investment program (SIP), specific investment lending, and appropriate nonlending operations. Analytical work on the transitional costs of trade reform, currently under way, is expected to lead to the preparation of the trade reform adjustment credit planned for fiscal year FY 2000. The ongoing public expenditure review is helping the government improve the size and composition of public expenditures for the period 1998--2000.  

2. As of September 30, 1998, the World Bank had approved 112 operations in Senegal for a total amount, less cancellations, of US\$1,808.3 million, consisting of 89 IDA credits (including three Special Fund credits and two Special Facility credits for Africa) for US\$1,679.0 million, and 23 IBRD loans for US\$129.3 million. Past projects have supported agricultural diversification, irrigation, human resources development, institutional development, and expansion of the country's infrastructure, particularly its transport system. In recent years, emphasis has shifted to utilizing and maintaining existing facilities better, and to helping the government resolve some of the key issues related to long-term development prospects. The current portfolio represents a commitment vaiue of about US\$564.8 million, equivalent with undisbursed balance to about US\$395.2 million. The portfolio is composed of 16 operations, involving the agriculture sector, human resources (population/health/nutrition/education), infrastructure/urban development, industry, and private sector development. # Senegal: Statistical Issues  

Overall Senegal's economic database is comprehensive, but there are weaknesses in the data on national accounts, production, international trade, the balance of payments, and social indicators. The authorities are continuing their efforts to improve the quality and availability of data. In addition to ensuring that the legal provisions requiring economic agents to report statistical information on a timely basis are enforced, the government intends to implement an action plan to reduce remaining statistical weaknesses in the national accounts, foreign trade, industrial production, and social indicators data. To this end, it will increase the resources allocated to its statistics department and will seek technical assistance from the IMF and from other partners.  

# Real sector  

The national accounts data suffer from serious deficiencies in their compilation and projections. Finalized historical data are based on returns from more than 1,oo0 firms, a process that typically takes up to three years. Preliminary data are often subject to substantial revisions, partly because they are increasingly based on the use of an input-output simulation model developed with French technical assistance, and less on the analysis of a sample of 300 enterprises. In addition, there are some methodological difficulties in the treatment of indicators of the trend growth in value added in the some sectors. The industrial production index is based on a highly outdated sample, and developments in the tertiary sector, which represents about 60 percent of total value added, are difficult to monitor since the regular preparation of a business survey has been discontinued. A new regional consumer price index, the“harmonized consumer price index" (HCPI) of the West African Economic and Monetary Union, was introduced in January l998. Based on 1996 weights, the HCPI covers only Dakar. Trade data are incomplete, owing to the time taken by the customs directorate-up to three years—to report trade flows. As of March l998, the latest exhaustive trade data, covering the period 1992-96 (with provisional data for 1996) were published in December 1997.  

# Public finances  

Government finance statistics (GFS) data are compiled by the Ministry of Finance from the following three sources: the customs, the tax, and treasury directorates. GFS data have not been reported by the authorities to STA for many years. The latest data reported for Senegal were published in the l990 Government Finance Statistics Yearbook and covered consolidated central government through 1984. However, comprehensive fiscal data are provided regularly without delays to AFR on a quarterly basis.  

# Monetary data  

Monetary data for Senegal are prepared by the national agency of the Central Bank of West African States (BCEAO). Outstanding data problems include the lengthy time period needed to finalize the data and some inconsistencies between the balance sheets of the BCEAO and the commercial banks.  

Monetary data have recently been revised to exclude assets and liabilities of banks that have ceased banking operations. In addition, there have been recent revisions to the classification of foreign assets, and to the classification of government and nongovernment bank deposits. These revisions were based on the returns generated by the accounting system for commercial banks introduced by the BCEAO on January 1, 1996. The delays the new system caused in the compilation of the accounts have been somewhat reduced.  

# Balance of payments data  

Balance of payments data for Senegal are compiled by the BCEAO national agency. There are several shortcomings in the compilation procedures: (a) trade data for goods other than the main export and import categories are incomplete and reported with lengthy delays; (b) bank settlement reports are not processed by the BCEAO; (c) surveys sent to companies are often inaccurately answered or unanswered; and (d) there is little information on private capital flows. Efforts are being undertaken to address these issues. A long-term technical advisor has been posted at BCEAO headquarters to provide technical assistance to all BCEAO member countries. Enhancing the capacity of the computerized system to compile customs data should also help reduce delays in reporting.
