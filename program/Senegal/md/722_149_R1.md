# I. Introduction  

The discussions for the l996 Article IV consultation and the midterm review of the program supported by the second annual arrangement under the enhanced structural adjustment facility (ESAF) for Senegal were held in Dakar during the period May 2-l6, 1996. l/ The attached letter from the Minister of Economy, Finance, and Planning of Senegal, dated May 15, 1996 (Appendix II), reviews the progress made by Senegal in l995 and early 1996, and sets out the policies and measures to be implemented in the remainder of 1996.  

The three-year ESAF arrangement for Senegal, in an amount equivalent to SDR 130.8 million (ll0 percent of quota), was approved on August 29, 1994, together with the first annual arrangement thereunder, in an amount equivalent to SDR 47.6 million (40 percent of quota). On December 1l, 1995, the Executive Board approved the second annual ESAF arrangement, in an amount equivalent to SDR 47.6 million (40 percent of quota). The two semiannual disbursements provided under the first annual arrangement, as well as the first semiannual disbursement (SDR 23.8 million) under the second annual arrangement, were effected. Availability of the second semiannual installment under the latter is contingent upon the observance of the performance criteria for end-March l996 and completion of the midterm review.  

As of end-March 1996, Senegal's outstanding use of Fund resources amounted to SDR 230.3 million (l93.7 percent of quota). If all the disbursements under the ESAF arrangements and all repurchases and loan repayments are made as scheduled, Senegal's use of Fund resources would amount to SDR 24l.2 million (202.9 percent of quota) by end-June 1997 (Appendix I, Table 1).  

Senegal is on the standard l2-month consultation cycle. In concluding the last Article IV consultation on June 9, 1995, Directors urged the authorities to persevere with, and intensify, their adjustment and reform efforts, in view of Senegal's still fragile economic situation.  

The World Bank has been supporting Senegal's development efforts through structural and sectoral adjustment lending, including a private sector adjustment and competitiveness credit (SDR 27.6 million) and an agricultural sector adjustment credit (SDR 29 million) approved in 1995. Senegal's relations with the Fund and the World Bank Group are summarized in Appendices III and IV, respectively.  

Senegal's economic data base is comprehensive, but there are weaknesses, notably in the areas of national income accounting, the balance of payments, and consumer price index (CPI) data. Technical assistance is being provided by INSEE/EUROSTAT to improve the CPI compilation. In addition, STA is implementing a balance of payments technical assistance project for the member countries of the Central Bank of West African States (BCEAO) through the posting of a long-term advisor at BCEAO headquarters in Dakar. The authorities have been regularly providing the core minimum data to the Fund (Appendix V). The weaknesses in the data base do not, however, hamper or impede a meaningful assessment of economic developments and policies.  

# II． Background and Recent Developments  

Building upon the progress made since the January l994 devaluation of the CFA franc, Senegal's program for 1995-96 seeks to strengthen financial policies to ensure attainment of financial viability over the medium term, and to deepen structural reforms to promote private sector development and durable economic growth (Box l).  

# Box 1. Main Objectives of the 1995-96 Program  

The 1995-96 program aims at achieving an annual real GDP growth rate of 4.5 percent; lowering inflation, as measured by changes in the GDP deflator, to about 8 percent in 1995 and to less than 3 percent in 1996; and reducing the external current account deficit, excluding official transfers, to 8 percent of GDP in 1995 and to 7.3 percent in 1996. The program seeks to increase investment from less than 14 percent of GDP in 1994 to nearly 17 percent in 1996, with domestic savings rising by more than 4 percentage points to about 12 percent of GDP in 1996, as the consolidation of the fiscal position continues and private sector savings respond to the ongoing liberalization of the economy and the improved competitiveness.  

Overall, developments through March 1996 point to encouraging progress and broadly satisfactory implementation of the program. The quantitative and structural benchmarks for end-December l995 were observed, except for the benchmark on government revenue, owing largely to delays in privatization (Appendix II, Table I). All the quantitative and structural performance criteria and benchmarks for end-March l996 were met as well. Real GDP growth in l995 is estimated to have reached 4.8 percent, higher than targeted (Appendix I, Table 2; and Chart l), as economic activity continued to pick up in the construction and export-oriented sectors. The increase in the average CPI for Dakar fell from 32 percent in l994 to

On the revenue side, the authorities took effective actions to reinforce the tax and customs administrations, combat fraud, and widen the tax base. However, in the second half of the year, there was a CFAF l0 billion shortfall in nontax receipts, as lengthy negotiations with a prospective buyer delayed the sale of the Meridien President hotel. Total government spending was kept below program, thanks to restraint in nonpriority outlays and lower-than-projected interest obligations, while the wage bill was kept within the program ceiling of CFAF l57.5 billion (6.5 percent of GDP). Measures were also taken to correct a number of spending overruns that had occurred during the first half of l995, in particular higher outlays for the social safety net, unanticipated transfers to the price stabilization fund (CPSP), higher appropriations for embassies, and a larger-than-projected deficit in the national retirement fund (FNR). The measures included the elimination of the subsidy on wheat flour, the liquidation of the CPsP, the closure of six embassies, and the recall of  

diplomats stationed abroad. To reduce the deficit of the FNR and ensure its financial equilibrium, the authorities implemented a number of cost-cutting measures, and legislation was passed to raise, starting in l996, the employer's contribution by 5 percentage points (to 20 percent) and the employee's contribution by 3 percentage points (to 15 percent). The 1995 appropriations for social safety net measures were fully utilized to subsidize rice and wheat flour and finance basic health and education services. Investment spending is estimated to have proceeded about as planned, and both the external and verified domestic payments arrears were eliminated in conformity with the program.  

Broad money growth decelerated more rapidly than expected, from 38 percent in l994 to 8 percent in l995, which reflected a stronger adjustment of cash balances to the decline in inflation than had been foreseen (Appendix I, Tables 5 and 6). l/ At the same time, there was a reduction in net bank credit to the Government, reflecting the Government's improved cash position. Accordingly, the net domestic assets of the Central Bank remained below the applicable ceiling (after adjustment in accordance with the program provisions). Credit to the economy increased only moderately during l995, as the much improved cash flow position of enterprises in the tradable goods sector allowed them to repay crop credits almost entirely and to keep recourse to bank loans well below what could be accommodated under the program. As a result, the contribution of Senegal to the net foreign assets position of the BCEAO continued to improve. As inflationary pressures abated, the BCEAO reduced its discount rate from 8.5 percent to 7.5 percent in December l995, and bank lending rates have started to come down. 2/  

On the structural front, most of the measures planned for l995 and early l996 were implemented. 3/ In the regulatory area, wheat flour imports and prices were liberalized in November l995; the rice sector was also fully liberalized, and the CPSP was liquidated in December, ahead of schedule. The export processing zone and points francs were replaced at end-1995 by a new regime for the Entreprises franches d'exportation, and simplified procedures for temporary imports of inputs used in the production of exports were enacted in December, in line with World Bank recommendations. In the agricultural sector, the groundnut marketing enterprise (SONACOS) was put up for sale to the private sector in November 1995, but the bids received to date have been inadequate. In addition, several savings and loan associations were established in rural areas.

# III. Report on the Discussions and the Program for 1996  

Against this background, the discussions focused on the effective implementation of all the measures called for by the program supported by the second annual ESAF arrangement, and on the adoption of any measures that might be required to keep the program on track and achieve its objectives. Particular attention was given to improving government reverue collection further; ensuring that spending restraint is maintained at the same time that expenditures are being reallocated to priority areas; and accelerating structural reforms, especially in the public enterprise sector. While noting the social pressures they continue to face, the authorities reiterated their strong commitment to the successful implementation of the program.  

The authorities were confident that the initial objectives of the program for 1996 were within reach, given the progress made so far in program implementation, their determination to sustain the reform effort, and their commitment to avoiding policy slippages. The authorities expected that, with a good crop year and the continued expansion of export-oriented activities, real GDP growth would exceed the targeted 4.5 percent in 1996. A reduction in average inflation to 3 percent was also seen as feasible, as all of the post-devaluation corrective inflation had now worked its way through the economy and policies were kept consistent with the exchange rate peg. The external current account deficit, excluding official transfers, is projected to be near the programmed level of 7.3 percent of GDP, reflecting continued strong export growth and a moderate rise in imports.  

# 1. Financial policies  

The main focus of policy in l996 continues to be on consolidating the fiscal position, in order to lower the debt-to-GDP ratio and reduce the need for recourse to exceptional financing (Box 2).  

# Box 2. Fiscal Policy, 1996  

The 1996 government budget, which was approved by the National Assembly in December 1995, aims at reducing the overall fiscal deficit (on a commitment basis and excluding grants) by more than 1 percentage point, to about 2 percent of GDP, in line with the mediumterm program. To this end, government revenues are to increase by 10 percent in 1996, to the equivalent of 15.4 percent of GDP. Spending, especially on wages and nonpriority outlays, is to be strictly limited, with total outlays increasing by only 3 percent.  

The authorities expressed confidence that the l996 government revenue target of CFAF 402.5 billion would be achieved. The benchmark for the first quarter of l996 had already been exceeded, reflecting a strong tax revenue performance. To ensure that the improvement in tax collection is durable, the authorities will persevere with the strengthening of the customs and tax administrations, as well as the fight against fraud, taking into account the recommendations of a recent FAD technical assistance mission. The actions include strict control over statutory tax exemptions, and completion of the ongoing reorganization of the tax and customs administrations. At customs, emphasis continues to be placed on strengthening internal controls; improving and extending the computerized information system (GAINDE); reinforcing the import verification program; and bringing all bonded warehousing under tighter supervision. In addition, the implementation of the single taxpayer registration number for enterprises is expected to improve control of taxpayers and compliance.  

On the spending side, the program calls for continued prudence, particularly as regards the wage bill and nonpriority outlays, but it also makes adequate provisions for basic health and education services. The share of the wage bill in total spending is to decline further, while those of education, health, and capital outlays would continue rising. To keep the wage bill within the programmed amount of CFAF 162.5 billion in 1996, no general pay raise is to be granted and new hiring is to be strictly limited--with the total number of civil servants not exceeding the 1995 level of 67,l00. A one-time premium (totaling CFAF 2 billion) for military personnel on active duty in the Casamance region is to be fully offset by cuts in nonpriority outlays. The authorities intend to implement the recommendations of the recently concluded audit of the civil service, and to replace the system of automatic salary adjustments by one based on merit. 1/ The l996 budget has also allocated CFAF 10 billion for social safety net measures. The staff stressed the importance of integrating these measures into the Government's social programs and of targeting them more effectively. 2/ Capital spending is to rise by about 8.5 percent, with priority given to infrastructure and high-yielding projects, especially in agriculture and human resource development.  

A study on the expenditure system has recently been completed, as envisaged in the memorandum on economic and financial policies for 1995-96. The authorities intend to continue adhering rigorously to the rules for executing and controlling public spending. They reiterated that there were no domestic payments arrears other than those already verified and settled by end-1995. In the last two budgets, instructions were given barring extrabudgetary expenditures, and suppliers were warned that the Government would honor its financial obligations only if budgetary procedures had been followed properly. In any event, the authorities intend to conduct (before end-l996) an audit to determine whether there are any other domestic payments arrears.  

The program calls for a continued prudent credit policy by the BCEAO, consistent with the regional balance of payments objective. The rate of growth of broad money for Senegal is projected at some ll percent in 1996, assuming a moderate decline in velocity as confidence takes hold. At the same time, the improvement in the fiscal position being sought should enable the Government to reduce further its indebtedness vis-a-vis the domestic banking system. This would make room for an adequate provision of credit to the private sector, while allowing for a further improvement in Senegal's contribution to the net foreign assets position of the BCEAO.  

To encourage financial savings and ensure efficient credit allocation, the authorities intend to continue working in the context of the WAEMU to improve the indirect instruments of monetary policy at the disposal of the BCEAO, to encourage the development of broader-based market debt instruments, and to ensure that interest rates are market determined and competitive with those abroad. The BCEAO has recently prepared draft regulations on introducing open market operations, which are being considered by member countries. The Fund staff stressed that central bank bills are the most appropriate monetary instrument for the region, and that open market operations in the secondary market should begin only after a  

well-functioning primary market has been developed. The authorities also intend to remain vigilant in enforcing the prudential ratios set by the regional banking commission, and to improve the legal procedures for the collection of claims. Two banks still experiencing difficulties (CNCAS and BST) are to be audited before end-l996.  

# 2. Structural policies  

Structural reforms continue to focus principally on three areas: (a) further liberalization of the economy and modernization of the regulatory framework; (b) greater efficiency and diversification in agriculture; and (c) an acceleration of public enterprise reform and privatization. 1/  

In the regulatory area, the authorities are proceeding with the establishment of a more transparent and simple tax-incentives regime for investment purposes. In addition, the modernization of the legal environment for business operations, in the context of the ongoing harmonization of business law in Africa, is being given urgent priority. The authorities are also determined not to reintroduce price controls or ad hoc protectionist measures.  

In the agricultural sector, the privatization of SONAcoS and the restructuring of the cotton marketing enterprise (SODEFITEx) are proceeding as planned. The authorities also intend to step up efforts to promote horticulture and livestock production, to further strengthen local savings and loan associations in rural areas, and to raise investment efficiency in the sector.  

The authorities are now speeding up the sale of the Meridien Président hotel and the small hotel entities, as well as the privatization of the Societé senegalaise des phosphates de Thies (SSPT) and the Sociéte propriétaire de l'hotel de l'union Teranga (SPHU-Teranga). An action plan defining the next phase of public enterprise reform has been prepared (March 1996). The plan calls for the privatization of ten more enterprises and the liquidation of two others by end-1997. The authorities are also working with the World Bank on the reform of the energy sector. Finally, the authorities intend to adhere to the original timetable for the preparation and periodic reporting of comprehensive data on the financial operations of key public enterprises, which constitutes a structural benchmark for endJune 1996.  

The authorities are participating actively in all the initiatives aimed at promoting regional integration and trade within the WAEMU, including the harmonization of domestic taxation and customs tariffs, as well as pursuing trade liberalization with other countries. Finally, in concert with its WAEMU partners, Senegal accepted the obligations of Article VIII,  

Sections 2, 3, and 4 of the Fund's Articles of Agreement, effective June 1, 1996.  

3. Medium-term balance of payments outlook_ and debt sustainability  

The medium- and long-term balance of payments projections and debt sustainability analysis presented in EBS/95/l83 (ll/20/95) have been updated until 2015, in light of developments in l995 and the latest estimates of the World Economic Outlook. l/ On the assumption that the measures called for by the program continue to be carried out as planned, the external current account deficit, excluding official transfers, should narrow to about 7.3 percent of GDP in l996, and to 4.6 percent by 2000, in line with the medium-term framework (Appendix I, Tables 7 and 8). With the improved competitiveness of the economy, export shipments are expected to continue diversifying and growing faster than GDP, while a slight increase in export prices appears to be in prospect. Tourism receipts are also projected to increase further. Import growth should remain moderate, as restrained demand policies keep import demand in check, especially for consumer goods.  

The financing requirements for l996 are expected to be fully covered by already secured debt relief and assistance from the World Bank, the European Union, and bilateral donors. 2/ Official bilateral creditors agreed at the recent Paris Club meeting to make effective the second tranche of the April 1995 rescheduling agreement covering maturities due in l996. The Executive Board of the World Bank approved, on March 6, l996, a grant from the IDA Debt Reduction Facility of up to Us\$l3.4 million to support a commercial debt reduction operation, which is expected to be completed in the next few months. 3/ The Fund staff stressed the importance of continued prudent debt management. On this assumption, Senegal's debt service obligations are projected to remain sustainable (Appendix I, Tables 9, 10, and ll), an assessment shared by the World Bank; the detailed debt sustainability analysis provided in EBS/95/l83 thus remains valid. Under the updated scenario, total debt service would fall to about 14.5 percent of exports of goods, nonfactor services, and private transfers, and to 28 percent of government revenue by 2000, and to less than 6 percent and l0 percent, respectively, by 20l5. Similarly, the net present value of the stock of debt would decline to 98 percent of the above-mentioned exports and transfers by 2000, and to less than 40 percent by 20l5. Repayments to the Fund will remain relatively small in relation to projected exports of goods and nonfactor services, and Senegal can be expected to meet its future obligations in a timely manner (Appendix I, Table 12).  

# IV. Staff Appraisal  

Senegal's ESAF-supported program remains broadly on track. Economic developments in 1995 and early l996 were encouraging, and the macroeconomic objectives of the program were largely achieved. In l995 real GDP growth exceeded the target; average inflation dropped sharply once the initial wave of corrective price increases had worked its way through the economy; and the external current account deficit narrowed as projected. The quantitative and structural benchmarks for end-December l995 were observed, except for the benchmark on government revenue, owing largely to delays in privatization. All quantitative and structural performance criteria and benchmarks for end-March 1996 were met.  

Senegal has made important strides over the last two years in reducing financial imbalances and in building a sound basis for sustainable economic growth. Provided that the program continues to be implemented effectively, and pressures from certain interest groups are successfully resisted, the original objectives for l996 are likely to be achieved. Noretheless, the economic situation remains fragile and will require that the authorities guard against slippages during the remainder of the year by standing ready to take all measures necessary for the success of the program. The authorities should concentrate their efforts especially on the following three key areas:  

First, fiscal consolidation must be pursued vigorously and the overall fiscal deficit has to be curbed further to preserve the competitiveness gains already achieved, increase public savings to raise the domestic contribution to investment, and at the same time reduce the need for exceptional financing. To this end, government revenue collection must be enhanced, through the strengthening of the customs and tax administrations, and the fight against fraud. Continued prudence on the spending side, particularly as regards the wage bill and other nonpriority outlays, is also essential.  

? Second, fiscal restraint should be accompanied by a prudent credit policy in the context of the regional monetary program of the common central bank. To encourage financial savings and ensure efficient credit allocation, the staff urges the authorities to accelerate, in the context of the WAEMU, the implementation of measures designed to improve the effectiveness of the indirect instruments of monetary policy at the disposal of the BCEAO, including the introduction of efficient open market operations. The authorities should also remain vigilant in enforcing the prudential ratios  

set by the regional banking commission, and in ensuring the soundness of the banking system.  

Third, the authorities should pursue further structural reforms and adhere to the original agenda, especially in the areas of modernization of the legal and regulatory framework, deepening of financial intermediation, and public enterprise restructuring and privatization. Stepped-up efforts in these areas are necessary to restore fully the confidence of the private sector, an important prerequisite for boosting investment and growth.  

Senegal has made considerable progress in its reform efforts since the devaluation of the CFA franc in January l994. However, the challenges ahead, especially to improve living standards, remain substantial. Over the medium term, the projected 4.5 percent real GDP growth rate, while sustainable, would not be sufficient to rapidly reduce poverty and raise per capita living standards. Accordingly, the next phase of reforms, as outlined above, should aim at giving a strong boost to savings and investment, thereby raising the growth prospects of the Senegalese economy, all in a context that would preserve macroeconomic stability. Foreign assistance directed toward high-yielding projects will also still be essential.  

Senegal maintains an exchange system that is free of restrictions on the making of payments and transfers for current international transactions. The staff welcomes the recent decision by Senegal, in concert with its WAEMU partners, to accept the obligations of Article VIII, Sections 2, 3, and 4 of the Fund's Articles of Agreement. The staff also encourages the authorities to continue improving the statistical base, especially in the areas of national income accounts and the balance of payments.  

In view of the encouraging developments so far, and of the authorities' strong commitment to the continued success of the program, the staff recommends that the present review under the second annual ESAF arrangement be completed.  

It is proposed that the next Article IV consultation with Senegal be held on the standard l2-month cycle.

# Mr. Michel Camdessus Managing Director International Monetary Fund Washington, D.C. 20431  

Dear Mr. Camdessus:  

1. The Executive Board of the International Monetary Fund approved on December ll, l995 the second annual arrangement under the enhanced structural adjustment facility (ESAF) for Senegal. This arrangement was in support of Senegal's economic and financial adjustment program covering the period l995-96. In accordance with that arrangement, we held discussions with an IMF mission regarding the midterm review of the program. The discussions focused primarily on the progress made under the program during the second half of 1995 and the first quarter of l996, as well as on the prospects and measures envisaged for the remainder of l996, in accordance with the medium-term objectives set forth in the economic and financial policy framework paper (PFP) and the memorandum on economic and financial policies for 1995-96, both dated October 12, 1995. We reached understandings on the economic and financial policies to be implemented in order to meet the economic objectives for 1996.  

2. Progress made in 1995 and recent developments. The 1995-96 program was formulated within the framework of the comprehensive medium-term adjustment strategy adopted in early 1994, which included the devaluation of the CFA franc. This strategy aims at achieving sustained economic growth and financial viability over the medium-term, based on the pursuit of sound fiscal and monetary policies and the acceleration of structural reforms. The main macroeconomic objectives for l995-96 were to achieve an annual growth rate of real GDP of 4.5 percent; to lower the inflation rate, as measured by changes in the GDP deflator, to about 8 percent in l995 and to less than 3 percent in l996; and to reduce the external current account deficit, excluding official transfers, to 8 percent of GDP in l995 and 7.3 percent in 1996.  

3.The results in l995 were generally encouraging, and the macroeconomic objectives were achieved. Real GDP growth increased from 2 percent in 1994 to 4.8 percent in l995, slightly above the program target, reflecting notably the expansion of export-oriented activities which continued to benefit from the improved competitiveness of the economy. Inflation, as measured by the change in the GDP deflator, was lowered from 33.2 percent in  

1994 to 7.6 percent in l995, while the average increase in the consumer price index (CPI) for Dakar was reduced from 32 percent to 8 percent, in line with program objectives. In April 1996, inflation further declined to 3 percent on an annual basis. In the external sector, exports continued to respond favorably to the devaluation, particularly those of groundnut products, fisheries, and chemicals. Total export volume thus increased by 7.5 percent in 1995. Imports picked up in 1995, but at a modest pace, increasing by only 3 percent in volume terms. As a result, the current account deficit, excluding official transfers, narrowed from 9.3 percent of GDP in l994 to 8 percent in 1995, as targeted. Total external budgetary assistance, together with debt relief, slightly exceeded projections and contributed to a further improvement in Senegal's contribution to the net foreign assets position of the Central Bank of the West African States (BCEAO).  

4. As indicated in the attached table, the structural and quantitative benchmarks for end-December 1995 were observed, except for the benchmark on government revenue, owing to a shortfall in non-tax receipts related to privatizations. All external and domestic payments arrears were eliminated before end-l995 as programmed. The performance criteria and structural and quantitative benchmarks for end-March 1996 were met. Government revenue exceeded the program benchmark for the first quarter of 1996.  

5. In the fiscal area, the Government continued its efforts during 1995 to improve revenue performance durably and contain spending. As a result, the overall fiscal deficit (on a commitment basis and excluding grants) was reduced from 5.7 percent of GDP in $1994$ to 3.2 percent, slightly below the program objective. A restrained expenditure stance continued to prevail. No general wage increase was granted and the wage bill, including the rappels speciaux, was kept within the program benchmark. Measures were also taken to correct a number of current spending overruns that occurred during the first half of l995, in particular those related to the social safety net (higher-than-anticipated wheat flour subsidy), unanticipated transfers to the price stabilization fund (Caisse de peréquation et de stabilisation des prix-CPSP), and to appropriations for diplomatic posts abroad. The subsidy on wheat flour was eliminated and its price liberalized, the CPSP was liquidated, six embassies were closed, and the number of diplomats stationed abroad was reduced. The deficit of the national retirement fund (FNR) slightly exceeded the programmed level. The Government, however, took both cost-saving and revenue-enhancing measures to restore the FNR's financial equilibrium. A law, adopted in December l995, increased the employer's contribution to the FNR by 5 percentage points (to 20 percent) and the employee's contribution by 3 percentage points (to 15 percent). Investment spending was in line with projections. Overall, total expenditure and net lending were slightly lower than programmed for 1995.  

6. Reflecting the continued reinforcement of the tax and customs administrations, as well as the pick-up in economic activity and imports, tax revenue increased by 23 percent and was CFAF 5 billion above the program target for l995. Non-tax revenue, however, fell CFAF 10 biilion short of target, owing notably to delays in the privatization of the Meridien Hotel.  

Overall, government revenues were about CFAF 5 billion lower than programmed. With continued improvements in revenue performance in the first quarter of l996, total receipts exceeded the program benchmark for March, although nontax revenue continued to suffer from delays in the settlement of payments between the power company (SENELEC), the petroleum companies, the Societé africaine de raffinage (SAR), and the State.  

7. In the monetary area, the applicable benchmarks and performance criteria for end-December 1995 and end-March 1996, with respect to changes in net credit to the Government and net domestic assets of the BCEA0, were met. Net credit to the Government declined in l995 and in the first quarter of 1996, as a result of the improvement in the Government's financial position. To strengthen the situation of the agricultural credit bank (CNCAS), the Government assumed some CFAF 8.3 billion in non-performing loans. Taking this operation into account, credit to the economy increased moderately but remained well below projections, reflecting the ample liquidity position of exporting enterprises, the almost full repayment of crop credit, and the settlement of domestic payments arrears by the Government. Broad money growth decelerated to 7.6 percent in 1995, reflecting a stronger adjustment of cash balances to declining inflation than projected. Senegal's contribution to the net foreign assets of the BCEAO improved further by CFAF 42 billion. As inflation abated, the BCEAO further reduced the discount rate from 8.5 percent to 7.5 percent in December 1995. Commercial banks lending rates have started to decline, with the maximum rate brought down to 15 percent and that for prime customers to about 10 percent.  

8. Most structural measures included in the program for l995 and the first quarter of 1996 were implemented. Concerning the regulatory framework, imports and prices of wheat flour were liberalized on November 7, l995, as scheduled. To encourage export activities and foreign investment, the Government replaced in December l995 the regulations governing export processing zones and points francs by a new regime for the entreprises franches d'exportation. Simplified procedures for the temporary imports of inputs entering the production of goods to be exported (régimes douaniers suspensifs) were also enacted before end-l995. Civil service reform efforts continued, and the audit of the civil service was completed in July 1995. In the agricultural sector, the groundnut marketing company (SoNACoS) was offered for sale in November l995, but the bids received so far have been unsatisfactory and far from reflecting the real value of the company. The rice sector was entirely liberalized and the CPSP was liquidated in December 1995, ahead of schedule.  

9. In the area of public enterprise reform, the privatization of the national water company (SONEEs) was carried out in December l995; the company was replaced by the Sénégalaise des Eaux (SDE), a joint private consortium in charge of the production and distribution of water, and the Sociéte Nationale des Eaux du Senegal (SONES), an entity in charge of asset management. The Société industrielle d'assainissement du Sénegal (SIAS) and the Sociéte hoteliere-Saly have both been liquidated. All small hotel entities (l6) belonging to the State were put up for sale, and six of them were sold. In preparation for the partial privatization of the telecommunications company (SONATEL), a new regulatory framework for the sector was adopted by the Government in December 1995. In the energy sector, SENELEC has been allowed to purchase its fuel on the international market. There were delays, however, in the completion of the privatization of the Meridien Hotel and the Société sénegalaise des phosphates de Thies (SSPT), as the bids received were not adequate. Finally, most agreements were signed between the Government and public enterprises to eliminate the cross-debts of the public sector.  

10. Program for 1996. The Government is determined to consolidate the progress achieved in 1995 and continue rigorous implementation of the macroeconomic and structural reforms described in the memorandum of economic and financial policies and the PFP of October l995, while taking all necessary measures to achieve the medium-term objectives set out in these documents. The macroeconomic objectives for l996 remain the same as those stipulated in the memorandum of October 12, l995, namely to achieve a growth rate of real GDP of at least 4.5 percent, taking into account in particular the rebound in agricultural production, to lower average inflation (as measured by the change in the GDP deflator) to less than 3 percent, and to reduce the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) to 7.3 percent of GDP. The expansion of exports and tourism should continue in l996, reflecting the improvement of competitiveness. Imports would increase only moderately, with a stronger rise for imports of capital goods than for consumption goods, which continue to be substituted by domestically-produced goods. On the basis of commitments of external assistance and debt relief already obtained, Senegal's contribution to the net foreign assets position of the BcEAO should improve further.  

11. The strengthening of the fiscal position will continue to be a key element of the adjustment program. The 1996 budget, approved by the National Assembly in December l995, aims at reducing the overall fiscal deficit (on a commitment basis and excluding grants) to about 2 percent of GDP in 1996. The efforts made in 1995 to improve tax collection will be pursued in l996 in order to consolidate this improvement on a durable basis. Total government revenues should increase by l0 percent in l996, to CFAF 402.5 billion. In early 1996, the Government adopted a number of measures to strengthen the tax yield. The value-added tax (VAT) was extended to all importers and manufacturers' clients and will be applied to a base that includes excise taxes. A 5 percent withholding tax replaced the equalization tax, thereby securing a minimum tax collection from the informal sector. In addition, the authorities will accelerate the implementation of the single taxpayer registration system for enterprises, which should result in more accurate taxpayers files and a systematic control of tax returns. Efforts will also continue at customs, with the strengthening of the unit in charge of customs investigations, the improvement of the computerized information system (GAINDE), the reinforcement of the import verification program, and the enforcement of tighter customs control of all bonded warehousing. In addition, exemptions will continue to be strictly controlled. These efforts will take into  

account the specific measures recommended by the recent Fund technical assistance mission aimed at strengthening tax and customs administrations.  

12. Concerning expenditures, the objective is to maintain their level within the limit initially set for l996. Total government expenditure, therefore, will increase by only 2.5 percent, to CFAF 456 billion, in 1996. The wage bill will not exceed CFAF l62.5 billion, as envisaged by the program; any temporary supplementary indemnity (not to exceed CFAF 2 billion) to be granted to soldiers on active duty will be fully compensated for by savings on other non-priority outlays. No general wage increase will be granted and recruitment will be limited essentially to the priority areas of health and education; in this context, the number of civil servants will not exceed its level of end-1995 (67,l00). The measures recommended by the 1995 audit of the civil service are being put in place. These measures include in particular: ensuring consistency between the payroll files of the Direction de la solde, the civil services registry, and the staffing plans of the different ministries; updating the files concerning the payments of allowances and social benefits; and preparing and implementing a system to improve management tools. In addition, the replacement of the system of automatic salary adjustments by one based on merit, which has not yet been approved by the National Assembly, is to be submitted for its consideration at the October l996 legislative session. Spending planned for the social safety net (CFAF l0 billion), allocated primarily to the education and health sectors, will be integrated in the Government's social programs and targeted more effectively. The increase in the contribution rates for the national retirement fund, effective January l996, is expected to help eliminate the financial imbalances of the fund. The program foresees that capital expenditures will increase by 8.5 percent (to CFAF 127.6 billion) in 1996, giving priority to infrastructure and high-yielding projects, particularly in the agriculture sector. l/ Before end-December 1996, a three-year public investment program for 1997-99 will be adopted, in consultation with the World Bank.

# 1. Real sector  

The national accounts data suffer from some deficiencies in their compilation. Finalized data are based on returns from more than 1,000 firms, a process that typically takes up to three years. For preliminary data, the accounts are based on a sample of 300 enterprises, and use an input-output model that was developed with French technical assistance. The CPI coverage is limited to Dakar, and is based on l967 weights. Currently, the CPI compilation is being improved with technical assistance from INSEE/EURoSTAT. The results of a household budgetary survey undertaken in 1991/92 are currently being processed, and they will be used to update the CPI weights. Trade data are incomplete owing to the time taken by the customs directorate to report trade flows to the BCEAo, which may take up to three years.  

# 2. Public finances  

GFS data are compiled by the Ministry of Finance from the following three sources: the Customs, the Tax, and the Treasury directorates. GFS data have not been reported by the authorities to the Statistics Department for many years. The latest data reported for Senegal were published in the 1990 GFsY and covered consolidated central government through 1984. However, comprehensive fiscal data are provided regularly without delays to the African Department on a quarterly basis.  

# 3. Monetary data  

Monetary data for Senegal are prepared by the National Agency of the BCEAO. Outstanding data problems include the lengthy time period needed to finalize the data, and some inconsistencies between the balance sheets of the BcEAo and the commercial banks.  

Monetary data still include assets and liabilities of banks that have long ceased banking operations, but whose liquidation has never been formally completed. Other statistical issues relate to the proper revaluation of the Compte d'Opération that the BCEAO maintains with the French Treasury, and other foreign currency denominated accounts, including Fund accounts. The reporting of monthly monetary data to the Fund is current.  

A money and banking statistics course was held in Senegal in April 1995. BCEAO member countries have subsequently requested the Fund to organize additional training courses in money and banking statistics and in other statistical areas.  

# 4. Balance of payments data  

A technical assistance mission by the Statistics Department visited Dakar in 1992 and identified three shortcomings in balance of payments compilation procedures: (i) trade data were incomplete and reported with lengthy delays; (ii) bank settlements reports are not processed by the BCEAO; and (iii) surveys sent to companies were often inaccurately answered or unanswered. Efforts are being undertaken to address these issues. Enhancing the capacity of the computerized data compiling system of the customs should also help reduce delays in reporting.
