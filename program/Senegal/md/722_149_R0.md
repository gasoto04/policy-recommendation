# I. Introduction  

The discussions with the Senegalese authorities on an adjustment program for 1995-96 that could be supported by a second annual arrangement under the enhanced structural adjustment facility (ESAF) were held in Dakar during the period September 19-October 3, 1995. 1/  

The three-year arrangement under the ESAF for Senegal, in an amount equivalent to SDR 130.8 million (1l0 percent of quota), was approved on August 29, 1994, together with the first annual arrangement thereunder, in an amount equivalent to SDR 47.6 million (40 percent of quota). The ESAF arrangement replaced a one-year stand-by arrangement, which was approved in March 1994, in support of the adjustment program adopted by Senegal soon after the devaluation of the CFA franc. The midterm review under the first annual arrangement of the ESAF and the l995 Article IV consultation were completed on June 9, 1995, and the two semiannual disbursements provided under the arrangement were effected.  

In the attached letter to the Managing Director dated October l2, 1995, the Minister of Economy, Finance, and Planning of Senegal requests the second annual ESAF arrangement, in an amount equivalent to SDR 47.6 million (40 percent of quota). The requested use of ESAF resources would be in support of Senegal's adjustment policies described in the updated policy framework paper (PFP) for 1995-98 (EBD/95/157, 11/20/95), which was transmitted to the Managing Director of the Fund and to the President of the World Bank on October 12, 1995. The PFP, which was prepared by the authorities in close collaboration with the staffs of the Fund and the World Bank, updates and extends Sanegal's previous policy framework paper for 1994-97 (EBD/94/129，7/28/94).  

As of end-September l995, Senegal's outstanding use of Fund resources amounted to SDR 22l.0 million (l85.9 percent of quota). If all the disbursements under the second and third annual ESAF arrangements, and all repurchases and loan repayments, are made as scheduled, Senegal's use of Fund resources would increase to SDR 24l.2 million (202.9 percent of quota) by end-June l997 (Appendix I, Table I).  

Fund and World Bank staffs continue to collaborate closely on Senegal The World Bank has been supporting Senegal's development efforts through structural and sectoral adjustment lending, including a private sector adjustment and competitiveness credit for SDR 27.6 million approved in February 1995, and an agricultural sector adjustment credit for SDR 29 million approved in June 1995. Senegal's relations with the Fund and the World Bank Group are summarized in Appendices IV and V, respectively.  

Overall, Senegal's economic data base is comprehensive, but there are deficiencies in the methodologies for compiling the national income accounts, the balance of payments, and the consumer price index (CPI) (Appendix VI). Data on public enterprises and the labor market also need to be improved. The authorities are taking steps to enhance the quality and availability of economic data, and technical assistance is being provided to improve the compilation of the CPI. Weaknesses in the data base do not hamper or impede a meaningful assessment of economic policies.  

II. Recent Economic Developments and Performance Under the l994-95 Program  

In early 1994, Senegal adopted a comprehensive adjustment strategy aimed at achieving sustained economic growth and financial viability over the medium term, in the context of increased regional cooperation and economic integration. The strengthened strategy rested in good measure on the zone-wide correction of the parity of the CFA franc; the implementation of strong supporting fiscal and credit policies, designed to keep inflation in check and ensure that the competitiveness of the economy would be restored on a lasting basis; and the acceleration of structural reforms aimed at improving incentives for the private sector and promoting growth.  

Overall, the adjustment program remained broadly on track throughout most of l994. 1/ However, in the last months of the year, difficulties re-emerged in revenue collection, delays were encountered in the implementation of some structural reforms, and there were shortfalls in external budgetary assistance. The performance criteria for end-December $1994$ and the quantitative and structural benchmarks for end-September and end-December i994 under the ESAF arrangement were observed, except for the benchmark on government revenue and the performance criteria related to the elimination and nonaccumulation of external payments arrears (Appendix II, Annex Table l). Waivers for the nonobservance of these performance criteria were granted at the time of the midterm review.  

In spite of the difficulties with fiscal management, the macroeconomic objectives of the program were largely achieved. Real GDP growth was 2 percent in l994, close to target (Appendix I, Table II; and Chart 1). Output in the agriculture sector rebounded strongly, aided by good weather conditions, and activity expanded in the fishing, tourism, and other exportoriented sectors. The average increase in the CPI for Dakar was contained at 32 percent, significantly less than forecast. Accordingly, the CPI-based real effective exchange rate declined by about 35 percent and the

All quantitative and structural benchmarks for end-March and end-June 1995, as revised at the time of the midterm review, were observed. Economic activity continued to recover, particularly in the export-oriented sectors, and it is expected that the growth target of 4h percent for l995 will be reached. Inflation declined further, with the CPI for Dakar rising by only $33$ percent between December l994 and July l995, although a temporary surge in prices of imported rice and wheat flour led to a rise of almost $4$ percent in August. With the expected seasonal fall in prices for the remaining months of the year, the projected average inflation of 8 percent for l995 also appears to be within reach.  

The improved competitiveness of the Senegalese economy has continued to affect exports favorably in l995, notably in the fishing, groundnut, and tourism sectors, and there has also been a significant rebound in phosphates and chemicals. Meanwhile, nontraditional exports, particularly of fruits

These developments, together with levels of external budgetary assistance that exceeded program projections for the. first half of 1995, contributed to a greater-thananticipated improvement in the net foreign assets of the banking system. The real effective exchange rate, based on relative consumer prices, remained stable during the first half of 1995.  

In the fiscal area, the Government continued its efforts to strengthen the public finance position in the first half of l995: To improve revenue performance, a number of measures were put in place to strengthen tax administration, combat fiscal fraud, and widen the tax base. In particular, the reorganization of the customs department was continued; hierarchical controls of customs operations were strengthened; and tax exemptions were reduced. The authorities have also begun to put in place a single registration system for enterprises, and are reinforcing the periodic and systematic cross-checking of companies' tax returns with customs declarations. As a result of these measures, the indicative targets for total government revenue for end-March and end-June l995 were slightly exceeded.  

The authorities maintained a restrained spending policy in the first half of l995. Total government outlays remained in line with program projections. Wage spending and nonpriority outlays in particular were contained, while adequate expenditures were provided for education and health services. No general wage increase is being granted in l995 and recruitment is being strictly limited, with priority given to the health and education sectors. However, spending on· transfers and subsidies exceeded the programmed level, and the deficit of the special accounts was larger than expected, owing to the continued financial difficulties of the national retirement fund (FNR). These expenditure overruns were more than compensated for by lower-than-expected spending on the wage bill, interest, and investment. Domestic and external payments arrears were reduced as programmed.  

Broad money grew only slightly during the first half of 1995, reflecting the adjustment of cash balances to declining inflation. Net bank credit to the Government fell significantly as a result of the strengthened fiscal position and a more rapid-than-envisaged pace of disbursement of external assistance. The net domestic assets of the banking system fell during this period; credit to the private sector remained slightly below levels at end-l994, owing to the repayment of agriculture-related credits following the crop season, and limited recourse to bank financing by enterprises as a result of their improved liquidity. The contribution of Senegal to the net foreign assets position of the BCEAo increased more than projected.

# 2. The program for 1995-96  

Within this medium-term strategy, the program to be supported by the second annual ESAF arrangement covers the period July l995-June 1996, straddling the two fiscal years 1995 and 1996. The program aims at achieving an annual increase in real GDP of $43$ percent, based on a continued strong response of output to the gains in competitiveness and favorable weather conditions; limiting the rate of inflation, as measured by the GDP deflator, to about 8 percent in l995 and then to less than 3 percent in 1996; and containing the external current account deficit, excluding official transfers, at 8 percent of GDP in l995, before reducing it to 7% percent in 1996. Taking into account the projected net capital inflows, exceptional budgetary assistance, and debt relief obtained, Senegal's contribution to the net foreign assets position of the BCEAO should improve further in 1995 and in 1996.  

The key elements of the 1995-96 program are provided in the following sections, while a more detailed description is presented in the authorities' memorandum on economic and financial policies for l995-96 (Appendix II, Annex). The table on the performance criteria and benchmarks under the proposed arrangement is annexed to the memorandum (Table 2).  

# a. Financial policies  

The pursuit of tight fiscal policies remains at the core of Senegal's adjustment strategy (Box 2).  

# Box 2. Fiscal Policy, 1995-96  

In view of the importance of further lowering the debt-to-GDP ratio, promoting domestic savings, and reducing the need for recourse to exceptional external financing, the durable reinforcement of the Government's budgetary position remains a key element of Senegal's adjustment strategy. Therefore, the authorities' objective is to reduce the overall fiscal deficit (on a commitment basis and excluding grants) from 5.7 percent of GDP in 1994 to 3.3 percent in 1995 and to 2 percent in 1996, 1/ through specific measures designed to improve revenue performance and to strictly limit spending. Government revenue is programmed to increase by the equivalent of 1.5 percent of GDP and total expenditure to be cut by 2.2 percent of GDP over the two-year period 1995-96.  

On the revenue side, total government receipts are expected to increase in 1995 by 23 percent, as initially programmed, and would rise further by 8 percent in l996 (Appendix I, Table IV). The achievement of these revenue targets, in addition to the impact of the anticipated continued pickup in economic activity and imports, will depend critically on determined actions by the authorities to strengthen the tax and customs administrations, widen the VAT base and combat fraud. At customs, an action plan is being implemented, involving the establishment of regional directorates; the redeployment of staff to border areas; the strengthening of the unit in charge of customs investigations; the improvement and extension of the computerized information system; the upgrading of the import verification program, with the assistance of the Sociéte génerale de surveillance (SGS); the tightening of customs control with regard to all bonded warehousing; and the strict application of sanctions and acceleration of judicial procedures in case of irregularities. With regard to domestic taxes, the measures being implemented include the completion of the single tax registration system for enterprises by March l996, allowing for the updating of taxpayer files and a systematic cross-checking of tax returns; the extension of the VAT to all importers and clients of manufacturers; and the replacement of the equalization tax by a 5 percent withholding tax to improve the taxation of the informal sector. Finally, the Government intends to continue  

cooperating with other WAEMU member countries with a view to harmonizing domestic tax and customs duty systems.  

On the expenditure side, the Government intends to limit the increase in total outlays (on a commitment basis) to about 6 percent in 1995 and to less than 1 percent in l996. Measures are being taken to correct the spending overruns of l995. With the liberalization of the price and importation of wheat flour by end-November l995, as well as the privatization of rice marketing and the liquidation of the CPSP by February 1996, subsidies on these commodities will be eliminated. To offset the spending increases on embassies following the devaluation, the authorities closed six embassies in early October, and decided to recall nearly l00 diplomats stationed abroad. With regard to the FNR, measures are being taken to ensure its financial equilibrium through the implementation before end-1995 of a number of administrative measures and an 8 percent increase in contributions.  

Wage restraint will also be maintained. The wage bill will be limited to CFAF 157.5 billion in l995, below the initial program level, and to CFAF l62.5 billion in l996. Recruitment will be strictly limited so that the size of the civil service does not increase from its current level of 67,100, and no general pay raise will be granted. Moreover, the system of automatic salary adjustments is to be replaced in early l996 by one based on merit. The measures recommended by the recent audit of the civil service, notably those addressing the rectification of the payroll files and the files concerning the payments of allowances and other benefits, will be put in place before end-l995. Nonessential expenditures will be curtailed so that adequate provisions can be made for education and health services. The expenditures that will be undertaken in the context of the social safety net, amounting to CFAF 10 billion in 1996, are to be integrated within the Government's social programs and targeted more effectively.  

The authorities intend to adopt before end-l995, in consultation with the World Bank, a three-year public investment program for the period 1996-98, which will give priority to infrastructure and high-yielding projects in the productive sectors. In l996, capital expenditures are projected to rise by 10 percent. The program further provides for the elimination by end-l995 of all external and verified domestic payments arrears. To avoid the renewed accumulation of extrabudgetary payments arrears, spending procedures will be strictly adhered to. The recommendations of a study that is now under way to improve the expenditure system are to be implemented with the l996 budget.  

The authorities will continue to monitor fiscal performance very closely. The program includes quarterly targets for total government revenue and the wage bill. Any shortfall in receipts or excess in the wage bill is to be offset by corrective measures. Furthermore, the fiscal implications of any adverse price shock will have to be compensated for by additional revenue or expenditure measures. Finally, any shortfall in external budgetary assistance (beyond CFAF 20 billion in 1995, and CFAF 10 billion in the first half of l996) will be offset by strengthened adjustment measures, and any excess will be saved through appropriate downward adjustments in.credit ceilings.  

In the area of monetary policy, the authorities intend to maintain a prudent stance. With declining inflation and inflationary expectations, the growth of broad money is projected to slow down to less than l8 percent in 1995 and to about l2 percent in 1996 (Appendix I, Tables V and VI). The strengthening of the fiscal position that is being sought should enable the Government to reduce its indebtedness vis-a-vis the banking system and make room for an adequate provision of credit to the rest of the economy, as well as some further improvement in Senegal's contribution to the net foreign assets of the BCEAO.  

Monetary policy will continue to be based on the use of indirect instruments put in place by the BCEA0 since October l993, notably the pursuit of a flexible interest rate policy, consistent with the exchange rate peg; the reliance on reserve requirement ratios in the event of excessive credit expansion; and a strengthened role for the money and interbank markets. The authorities are aware of the shortcomings of the securitization scheme put in place in l994, in particular the fixed interest rate, the exemption of taxes, the use of the securities in the fulfillment of minimum reserve requirements, and the full redeemability of the claims at all times. To enhance the efficiency of monetary policy and encourage the use of nonbank financial instruments in the WAEMU, the Senegalese authorities intend to speed up, in cooperation with other union members, the development of a true regioral securities market.  

To further facilitate the access of private sector borrowers to bank credit, efforts are being made to improve the legal procedures with regard to the collection of claims, while ensuring the observance of prudential ratios. The authorities are also encouraging banks to lower the spread between lending and deposit rates. Lending rates for prime customers have started to come down, as competition has grown among the banks for profitable lending opportunities, and enterprises have begun to tap international markets for financing. l/ The provision of credit in rural areas will also be strengthened through the promotion and upgrading of local savings and loan associations. Finally, the soundness of the banking syster will remain under close scrutiny, and the financial position of the CNCAS is to be further reinforced.  

# b. Structural policies  

The structural measures that constitute the other pillar of the adjustment program are designed to promote the development of a dynamic private sector and to support rapid and sustained economic and per capita income growth. The structural reforms focus on three main areas:  

(a) further price and trade liberalization and modernization of the regulatory framework; (b) strengthening of reforms in the agricultural sector; and (c) acceleration of public enterprise reform (Box 3). Over the long term, Senegal's growth potential is also to be raised by the ongoing reform efforts in education, health, and transportation.

Building upon the significant progress made in price and trade liberalization under the l994-95 program, the next major steps in this area include the liberalization of wheat flour imports and prices by November 1995, and the full withdrawal of the CPsP from the purchasing and marketing of rice and its liquidation by February 1996. Furthermore, to attract foreign investment and encourage export activities, the regulations governing the export processing zone and points francs will be simplified and the benefits aligned with those in other developing countries. A new investment incentives regime also is to be put in place before end-l995, and simplified procedures for temporary imports of inputs for the production of export goods are to be enacted by the same date. Finally, the authorities intend to modernize the legal framework for business activities.  

In agriculture, the authorities are continuing their efforts to increase efficiency and encourage diversification in production and marketing. In the groundnut sector, SONAcoS will be put up for sale by December l995, as a prior action. In the cotton industry, the restructuring of the textile fiber development company (SODEFITEx) is to be accelerated, with the aim of reducing its costs and restoring financial equilibrium on a lasting basis. In addition, steps are being taken to support nontraditional agricultural exports, in particular horticultural products and livestock.  

The authorities intend to speed up public enterprise reform. l/ In this context, the liquidation of SH-Saly, the sale of the Meridien hotel, and the privatization of the management of SONEES are to be completed by end-1995, while the privatization of SSPT and SPHU-Teranga are scheduled for the first half of 1996. The Government will adopt by mid-1996 an action plan for the reform of.the energy sector, entailing the full liberalization of the petroleum subsector by ending the privileges granted to the Sociéte africaine de raffinage (SAR), as well as.the privatization of the electricity company (SENELEC). The authorities are committed to reporting comprehensive data concerning the public enterprise sector on a regular basis starting in June l996; this will constitute a structural benchmark under the program.  

The authorities intend to continue 'playing an active role in promoting regional trade and economic integration among the member countries of the WAEMU. In this context, a regional initiative is under way to harmonize customs duties and indirect taxation. Senegal also intends to accept the obligations of Article VIII, Sections 2, 3, and 4 of the Articles of Agreement of the Fund at an early date.  

3． Medium-term balance of payments outlook and capacity to repay the Fund  

The medium-term balance of payments projections presented in EBS/95/80 have been updated, taking into account the most recent available information and the latest World Economic Outlook projections (Appendix I, Tables VII and VIII). It is expected that, barring major terms of trade or other exogenous shocks, the improved competitiveness of the economy, together with the effective and sustained implementation of the measures described in the preceding paragraphs, would contribute to a further improvement in Senegal's external position and ease the transition toward external viability over the medium term. This improvement would be brought about by a decline in the trade deficit, despite some weakening of the terms of trade during the first years of the projection period, the impact of debt forgiveness and of higher tourism receipts, and increased net inflows of private transfers. The volume of exports is projected to increase at an annual average rate of almost 6 percent over the six-year period l995-2000. This growth is based largely on a continued expansion in the volume of traditional exports (groundnut products, fish, and phosphates), but also on the prospective increase in the volume of nontraditional exports from the agro-industrial and textile sectors as the economy responds to opportunities for diversification. Over the same six-year period, the volume of imports is

projected to grow by about 4 percent a year, with imports of capital and intermediate goods registering the highest rates of increase, and the growth of import demand for consumer goods kept in check by tight incomes and fiscal policies.  

Senegal will continue to rely heavily on concessional external assistance to finance investment projects. After taking into account projected disbursements of project aid, private capital inflows, and Fund assistance under the ESAF arrangement, the remaining financing requirements for the three-year period July l995-June l998 are expected to be covered by multilateral and bilateral aid, debt relief, and sectoral adjustment loans from the World Bank. l/ For the period covered by the second annual ESAF program (July l995-June l996), the needed external financing totals CFAF 110 billion, and is to be fully covered as follows: CFAF 43 billion in assistance from multilateral institutions (World Bank and European Union); CFAF l9 billion in official bilateral assistance; and about CFAF 48 billion in debt relief from Paris Club and other official bilateral and private creditors. The remaining financing gap of CFAF 1O billion for the second half of l996 is expected to be covered by World Bank and bilateral assistance.  

To illustrate the sensitivity of the projections to the underlying assumptions, particularly for the terms of trade, alternative scenarios have been prepared. They show that if the prices of groundnut exports were on average l0 percent lower than projected, the external current account deficit would widen by the equivalent of $34$ percentage point of GDP by 2000. Alternatively, if the prices of fish exports were l0 percent lower than in the baseline scenario, the external current account deficit would widen by percentage point of GDP.  

To assess the sustainability of Senegal's debt service and its longterm capacity to repay the Fund, the baseline scenario has been extended to 2005. 2/ As external financing is expected to remain on concessional terms, with a significant proportion in the form of grants, the debt service ratio is projected to decline from about $19*$ percent in $1994$ to 16 percent in 1998, and then to less than l0 percent in 205. With a continued prudent management of its external debt, Senegal's debt service obligations are thus projected to remain sustainable. l/ Repayments to the Fund would continue to be relatively small in relation to projected exports of goods, nonfactor services, and private transfers (a peak of almost 4 percent in l997). In view of this, the anticipated improvement ir Senegal's balance of payments and fiscal positions, and its good record of payments to the Fund, Senegal can be expected to continue to meet its future obligations to the Fund in a timely manner.  

# 4. Social.and environmental aspects  

The strategy of the Government in the social and environmental sectors aims at increasing the standard of living, reducing poverty, and improving the management of natural resources and the urban environment. The main objectives in these areas are presented in detail in the PFP (Sections V and VI). A key element in the improvement of the living conditions of a large part of the. population is the pickup in economic activity expected as a result of the enhanced competitiveness of the economy and increased investment. To further human resource development, the authorities intend to raise the enrollment ratio in primary education from the current level of 56 percent to 65 percent by l998 (Appendix I, Table IX), and to improve the efficiency of expenditures in the education sector. In the health sector, the Government aims to expand the coverage of primary health care and family planning, reduce maternal mortality by half, and promote the procurement and distribution of generic drugs by the private sector.  

# IV. Staff Appraisal  

Senegal made important strides under its first annual ESAF-supported program. Economic growth picked up; exports and tourism expanded considerably; inflation appears to have been contained; domestic and external disequilibria were reduced; and wide-ranging structural measures were implemented, especially in the area of price and trade liberalization.  

However, some difficulties were encountered. In late l994, there were shortfalls in government revenue, although efforts made in the first half of 1995 to improve revenue performance were largely successful. Spending overruns occurred in recent months, explained in large part by higher outlays for the social safety net and a larger deficit of the retirement fund. There were also some delays in the completion of the privatization of certain public enterprises.  

The gains achieved so far are to be welcomed, but the economic situation remains fragile. The authorities nust therefore persevere with their adjustment efforts, consolidate the progress made, and improve performance in those areas where weaknesses have emerged. The key areas where the authorities should continue to focus their efforts remain the same as those mentioned in the last staff report:  

Ensure a better government revenue performance. At about l4 percent, the revenue-to-GDP ratio is below the average for developing countries at a similar stage of development. It is thus essential that revenue collection be improved and that Senegal succeed in achieving an increase of at least 2-3 percentage points of GDP in its receipts over the next three years. To this end, strong actions are needed to enhance tax compliance (civisme fiscal), reduce fiscal fraud, strengthen customs and tax administrations, and widen the tax base.  

0 Pursue spending restraint..while providing adequately for health and education. The measures that are being taken to correct the expenditure overruns of 1995 are essential to maintain spending within the targeted limits. For the program to be successful, the authorities must avoid any new spending overruns. The authorities' commitment not to grant any general wage increase and to strictly limit recruitment in the remainder of l995 and in l996 is also crucial to achieve the fiscal objective. In this connection, the decision of the authorities to replace the present automatic salary adjustment mechanism with a merit-based system is to be welcomed. Adequate provisions should, however, continue to be made for basic health and education services, as well as for the social safety net, with the important aim of developing Senegal's sizable human resource base and alleviating poverty.  

Pursue a prudent credit policy and deepen financial intermediation. The improvement in the fiscal position should enable the Government to reduce its indebtedness vis-a-vis the banking system and make room for an adequate provision of credit to the private sector. As in other WAEMU countries, the indirect monetary policy instruments of the BCEAO will need to be improved and refined to ensure that interest rates are market determined and deal more effectively with any potential surge in credit growth. Financial intermediation will also need to be deepened, through continued strengthening of the banking system and development of new financial instruments." Moreover, local savings and loan associations should be encouraged to widen access to financial services, especially in rural areas.  

Accelerate the_ next phase of structural reforms and make up for any delays that have occurred. While the progress achieved so far on structural reforms is commendable, the staff urges the authorities to effectively implement the next phase of reforms, especially in the area of trade and price liberalization, as well as agricultural and public enterprise reforms. In particular, the completion of the privatization operations now under way must be pursued actively without incurring further delays.  

Senegal continues to avail itself of the transitional arrangements under Article XIV, Section 2, but intends to keep its exchange system free of restrictions on payments and transfers for current international transactions. The staff urges Senegal to accept the obligations of Article VIII, Sections 2, 3, and $4$ of the Fund's Articles of Agreement at an early date. The staff also encourages the authorities to continue improving the statistical base, especially in the areas of national accounts and balance of payments.  

The authorities have reiterated at the highest level their commitment to a firm and effective implementation of the adjustment program. The program objectives, while attainable, remain subject to some risks, including the evolution of the terms of trade and internal social pressures. The Government should therefore stand ready to take any additional measures that may be required by changing circumstances. Timely availability of external financial assistance is also a key condition for the success of the program.  

In light of the progress made thus far, and the strength of Senegal's proposed adjustment program, the staff recommends approval by the Executive Board of the authorities' request for the second annual arrangement under the ESAF.

# Memorandum on Economic and Financial Policies for l995-96  

October 12, 1995  

# 1. Introduction  

1.In early $1994$ , the Senegalese authorities decided to adopt a comprehensive medium-term adjustment strategy. This strategy, which included the 5O percent devaluation of the CFA franc in January $1994$ ,was aimed at improving the competitiveness of the economy, restoring sustained economic growth and ensuring the return to financial viability over the medium term. Senegal's adjustment efforts were initially supported by a one-year stand-by arrangement from the IMF, which was replaced, on August 29, 1994, by a three-year arrangement under the enhanced structural adjustment facility (ESAF) and the first annual arrangement thereunder.  

2. To consolidate the progress made during the first year of the program ended in June 1995, the Government of Senegal has updated and extended its medium-term economic and financial policy framework paper (PFP) to cover the period July l995-June l998. The general economic policy orientation described in that paper, prepared in cooperation with IMF and World Bank staffs, remains consistent with the orientation set forth in the previous PFP. The revised framework provides for the continued implementation of a restrained fiscal policy aimed at a balanced budget by l998, the pursuit of a prudent credit stance and a flexible interest rate policy at the regional level, as well as the deepening of a wide range of structural reforms geared toward developing the private sector and promoting durable economic growth.  

3. In support of this strategy and the program covering the period July l995-June l996, the Government of Senegal hereby requests assistance from the Fund in the form of a second annual ESAF arrangement. The program straddles the two fiscal years l995 and l996. The Government of Senegal is also seeking support from the World Bank in the form of sectoral projects, as well as financial assistance from other multilateral and bilateral donors and creditors. During the Consultative Group meeting for Senegal held in Paris in July 1995, donor pledges of program and project aid totaled USSl.5 billion for the period 1995-97, covering most of Senegal's prospective external financing requirements for that period.  

# II. Progress Under the Adjustment_Program for l994-95  

4. Economic developments in l994 were encouraging, and the program remained broadly on track for most of the year. Real GDP growth is estimated to have been 2 percent, close to the program objective. This outcome reflected a rebound of agricultural production, helped by good weather conditions, as well as of other export-oriented activities. Inflation in l994 was more moderate than foreseen, as the average increase in the Consumer Price Index (CPI) for Dakar was contained at 32 percent, instead of the projected rate of 39 percent. Accordingly, the real effective exchange rate declined by some 35 percent and the competitiveness of the Senegalese economy improved considerably. There was a significant pick-up in exports and tourism receipts, as well as a sizable reflow of private capital. The contraction of real domestic demand and the beginning of a shift in consumption toward domestically produced goods were reflected in a sizable fall in the volume of imports. As a result, the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) was limited to 9.3 percent of GDP, below the programmed level.  

5. During the last months of l994, however, difficulties re-emerged in budgetary revenue collection and there were shortfalls in disbursements of external budgetary assistance. The applicable performance criteria for endDecember l994 and the quantitative and structural benchmarks for endSeptember and end-December l994 under the ESAF arrangement were observed, except for the benchmark on budgetary revenue and the performance criteria related to the elimination and nonaccumulation of external payments arrears (see attached Table 1). Waivers for the nonobservance of these performance criteria were granted at the time of the midterm review of the program.  

6. Available information for the first half of l995 indicates further encouraging progress and satisfactory implementation of the program. All applicable quantitative and structural benchmarks for end-March and end-June 1995, as revised at the time of the midterm review, were observed. Economic activity continued to expand and it is expected that the growth objective of 4.5 percent for l995 will be achieved. Inflation further declined, with the cumulative increase in the CPI for Dakar amounting to only 7.6 percent between December $1994$ and August l995, in line with the targeted average increase of 8 percent for the year as a whole, given the expected seasonal declines in the last months of the year. The pickup in exports and tourism receipts continued during the first half of l995, while the growth of imports has remained moderate. External budgetary assistance for the first half of 1995 exceeded program projections.  

7. With regard to public finances, the Government implemented in 1994 a set of measures aimed at broadening the tax base and improving revenue collection, while maintaining a restrained spending stance, particularly on wages. An average wage increase of 10 percent became effective in April 1994, as planned, and the wage bill remained slightly below the programmed level for the year as a whole. Other current expenditures were broadly as programmed, with the CFAF 15 billion budget allocation for social safety net measures almost fully utilized. However, mainly because of the financial imbalances of the National Retirement Fund (FNR), the deficit of the special accounts was larger than projected. Investment spending was slightly lower than expected. Budgetary revenue for l994 fell short of the targeted amount by about l percent of GDP, because of organizational weaknesses of the tax and customs administrations, fraud, and delays in the disbursement of  

payments from fishing agreements. As a result, the overall fiscal deficit, on a commitment basis and excluding grants, reached 5.7 percent of GDP, exceeding the program target by about l percentage point. Verified domestic payments arrears were reduced as planned, but external payments arrears could not be eliminated and amounted to CFAF 45.5 billion at end-1994.  

8. Efforts to consolidate the fiscal position have continued during the first half of l995. To improve revenue performance, the authorities have taken measures to strengthen the fiscal and customs administrations, combat fraud, and widen the tax base. Key steps in this area include the reorganization of the customs department, the strengthening of hierarchical controls of customs operations, the launching of a single registration system for enterprises, the periodic and systematic cross-checking of tax returns of companies with customs declarations, and the reduction of tax exemptions. Information for the first half of l995 indicates that the benchmarks on total government revenue for end-March and end-June were exceeded, as positive results in direct taxation have more than offset shortfalls in indirect taxes and receipts from the petroleum sector caused by arrears of the electricity company (SENELEC). The authorities continued to maintain a restrained expenditure stance in the first half of 1995, especially regarding the wage bill and nonpriority outlays, while providing adequately for basic health and education services. No general wage increase has been granted in l995, and recruitment is strictly limited, with priority being given to the health and education sectors. In the first half of l995, total outlays including the wage bill are estimated to have remained in line with program projections. However, the deficit of the special accounts was larger than projected, reflecting in particular the continuing financial difficulties of the FNR, and outlays on subsidies and transfers were greater than planned. These spending overruns were more than offset by the lower-than-expected wage bill, interest payments, and investment spending. Domestic and external payments arrears were reduced as planned.  

9... In 1994, broad money grew by 37.8 percent, more than projected, reflecting a strengthening of money demand and a return of confidence. Money growth decelerated markedly in the first half of 1995, however, reflecting declining inflation. At the same time, net bank credit to the Government and the net domestic assets of the banking system remained well within the program limits. As a consequence, since the devaluation, the contribution of Senegal to the net foreign assets position of the Central Bank of West African States (BCEAO) has improved much stronger than foreseen.  

10. Monetary policy was designed and coordinated within the broader context of the West African Economic and Monetary Union (WAEMU). Following the devaluation, the discount rate of the BCEAO was raised on January 18, 1994, by $4$ percentage points to l4.5 percent; subsequently, the declining inflation rate allowed the gradual reductior of the discount rate in five steps to 8.5 percent on June 5, 1995. Starting in July $1994$ , the monetary authorities also proceeded with the issuance of securities against the consolidated debt assumed by the Government in the context of the  

restructuring of the Senegalese banking system. These l2-year securities, which are redeemable at par and carry a tax-exempt fixed interest rate of 5 percent, amounted to CFAF l38.7 billion at end-June 1995, and were purchased by Senegalese investors as well as investors from other WAEMU countries. Regarding the continued strengthening of the banking system, the Caisse nationale de credit agricole du Senegal (CNCAS) has put in place measures to improve its financial position and reinforced its capital base at end-l994 by retaining all of its profits.  

1l. In the structural area, the authorities implemented a wide range of measures in $1994$ and during the first nine months of 1995. Labor legislation was made more flexible, with the elimination of the monopoly of the Office of Port Personnel in July l994, and the revision of the labor code in November $1994$ to allow enterprises to freely adjust their work force according to their level of economic activity. The lifting of price controls proceeded as planned, with the liberalization in June l994 of the prices of six commodities which had been frozen following the devaluation, and the liberalization in December l994 of the prices of six additional commodities, which had previously been administered or subject to the approval system (homologation). As a result, prices remain controlled for only ll commodities deemed essential. l/ Furthermore, the producer price of rice was liberalized in early June l994 and the wholesale and retail prices of broken rice in March l995. All producer prices are marketdetermined, except those for cotton and groundnuts. The producer price for cotton is determined by a committee representing producers, the marketing agency, and the Government, taking into account world market prices; a similar scheme is being introduced for oilseed groundnuts.  

12. The liberalization of foreign trade has proceeded as planned. In June 1994, the authorities informed all firms whose special agreements and protocols (conventions speciales and protocoles d'accord) had lapsed, that these would not be renewed. The remaining firms that still benefit from such agreements and protocols signed new agreements denouncing the previous ones, thereby abolishing monopolies and bringing tax privileges in line with common law. The prior authorizations for exports and imports have been eliminated, except those required for public health and security reasons, for the import of second-hand clothing and gold and silver bars, as well as for the export of precious metals. The prior authorization required to import wheat flour will be abolished in November l995, after expiration of the one year notice required by the special agreement binding the Government and the millers.  

13. Several reforms aimed at improving the efficiency and encouraging the diversification of the agricultural sector have also been implemented. All the measures planned for l994 relating to the withdrawal of the Caisse de perequation et de stabilisation des prix (CPsP) from the rice sector were taken, and, by June l995, the CPSP closed all its warehouses, including that of Dakar, and the transport subsidy was eliminated. In the groundnut sector, the call for bids for the Societe nationale de commercialisation des oléagineux du Sénégal (SONAcos) was delayed to December l995, as agreed with the concerned donors. The restructuring of the cotton company (SODEFITEX) is in progress. In the area of public enterprise reform, in March 1995 a law was adopted to allow for the privatization of the management of the national water company (SONEES), and in July l995 the Government announced its intention to privatize the national telecommunications company (SONATEL), to help Senegal take advantage of new technologies. In the electricity sector, the signing of a new performance contract with SENELEC was postponed to take into account the conclusions of a study on the reorganization of this company and the reduction of its costs. SENELEC is now allowed to purchase its fuel requirements on the international market. However, delays were experienced in the privatization of two enterprises, the Societe senegalaise des phosphates de Thies (SSPT) and the Societé propriétaire de l'hotel de l'union Teranga (SPHU-Teranga), as well as in the liquidation of the Societé hoteliere-Saly (SH-Saly), originally planned for June l995. Finally, the audit of the civil service was completed in July 1995.  

# III. Medium-Term Strategy_and_the Program for 1995-96  

14. Senegal's medium-term adjustment strategy as outlined in the PFP for 1995-98 continues to be based on the pursuit of a restrained fiscal policy aiming at reestablishing financial viability over the medium term, a prudent credit stance, and a flexible interest rate policy at the regional level. In the area of structural reforms, the focus is on the further liberalization of the economy and other measures to encourage private sector development, the acceleration of public enterprise reform, and the deepening of reforms in the agricultural sector. 1/ The macroeconomic objectives for l995-98 remain broadly in line with the original medium-term objectives of the program, and are as follows: (a) to achieve an average annual economic growth rate of some 4.5 percent, which would allow for an increase in per capita GDP of about l.5 percent a year; (b) to reduce average inflation, as measured by the GDP deflator, by 1996 to its pre-devaluation level of 2-3 percent, .compatible with the exchange rate parity; and (c) to reduce the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) to about 6 percent of GDP by 1998, a level seen as consistent with a viable external position. The balance of payments should benefit from significant inflows of external financing associated with investment projects and structural reforms. After covering the financing gaps, available resources should allow Senegal to improve its contribution to the BCEAo's net foreign reserve position.  

15. Attainment of these objectives requires the continued implementation of a strong package of macroeconomic and structural measures tailored to reduce financial imbalances and improve resource allocation. To this end, the program aims at balancing the budget by l998, and improving the incentives for private sector investment and production. Reflecting the impact of these policies, domestic savings should increase from 8.3 percent of GDP in 1994 to l4.5 percent in 1998, with pubiic savings increasing from 2.2 percent of GDP to 6.7 percent over the same period. For economic growth rates to be achieved as planned, however, the investment ratio will have to increase from l3.6 percent of GDP in l994 to l8 percent of GDP in 1998. Sizable inflows of external resources will therefore continue to be needed to supplement domestic resources. In addition to higher levels of investment, the program also relies on improvements in its overall efficiency brought about by the structural reforms, and the increase in the share of private investment reflecting the improved climate for private sector activity.  

16. Within this general framework, the main macroeconomic objectives for 1995 and l996 are as follows: achieve a real GDP growth rate of 4.5 percent per year; reduce the average inflation rate, as measured by the GDP deflator, to 7.8 percent in l995.and further to 2.7 percent in l996; and reduce the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) to 8 percent of GDP in 1995 and 7.3 percent of GDP in 1996. A further recovery in key sectors of the economy, fostered by the improved competitiveness and the ongoing structural reforms, as well as good rainfall, are expected to account for most of the improvement in growth performance. On the external side, export volume is expected to increase by 7 percent in l995 and 7.6 percent in l996, reflecting significant increases in exports of groundnuts and fish. Tourism is also expected to continue to recover. Following a decrease in l994, import volume should rise moderately, by 5 percent in l995 and 4.2 percent in i996, as restrained incomes and fiscal policies limit the growth of import demand, especially for consumer goods. After rescheduling and coverage of financing requirements, Senegal's contribution to the net foreign assets of the BCEAO is projected to amount to SDR 76.5 million (CFAF 58 billion) in l995 and about SDR 50 million (CFAF 37 billion) in l996.  

17. A central objective of the comprehensive adjustment strategy of the Government of Senegal is to increase the standard of living of the population and to. reduce poverty. The Government's priority social objectives, which are presented in detail in the PFP (Section V), include increasing the literacy and school enrollment rates, improving health care, strengthening women's participation in development, and reducing demographic pressures. A key element in the improvement of living conditions is the revitalization of economic activity, expected as a result of the enhanced competitiveness of the economy and increased investment. The encouragement of private sector activity and the continued liberalization of the economy should also enhance economic growth and employment creation. In rural areas, higher producer prices have resulted in an increase in incomes. In order to protect the poorest groups, the authorities are taking accompanying measures to improve the availability of basic social services in the areas of health and education, and to directly assist targeted vulnerable groups

The stock of verified domestic payments arrears, which stood at CFAF 14.9 billion at end-December l994, and CFAF 4.8 billion at endJune 1995, will also be eliminated by end-l995. In addition, the authorities are determined not to accumulate any new external or domestic payments arrears. The budget deficit on a cash basis and the reduction of net domestic liabilities will be financed entirely by external financial assistance, including the debt relief already obtained. Before endDecember 1995, the Government will adopt a budget for l996 that is consistent with the program's objectives.

Furthermore, quarterly targets have been set for total budgetary revenue and the wage bill. Any shortfall in budgetary revenue or overrun in the wage bill will be offset by corrective measures in order to meet the objectives of the program. Finally, the fines and penalties applicable in case of fraud will continue to be strictly applied, and judicial procedures will be accelerated. Regarding the tax administration, the single registration system for enterprises should be fully operational by March l996, allowing for the updating of taxpayer files and a systematic control of tax returns. In addition, the VAT will be extended to all importers and all manufacturers' clients, and a 5 percent withdrawal tax will replace the equalization tax in early 1996 in order to secure a minimum tax collection from the informal sector. The Government will continue to work with other WAEMU member countries toward the regional harmonization of domestic tax and customs duty systems.  

21. On the expenditure side, a very prudent policy will be pursued, particularly as regards the wage bill and nonpriority spending, while providing adequately for basic health and education services. Total expenditure, on a commitment basis, will increase in l995 by about 6 percent, to reach CFAF 45l.3 billion, while in l996, it will increase only slightly to CFAF 454.5 billion: The wage bill will be contained at CFAF 157.5 billion in 1995 and CFAF 162.5 billion in 1996. To this end, no general wage increase will be granted and recruitment will be strictly limited, giving priority to the health and education sectors; the total number of civil servants will not exceed the current level of 67,100. Savings obtained from a reduction in the size of the civil service will be used to regularize the verified rappels (corresponding to the financial impact of promotions for which payment orders have not yet been issued) while remaining within the above-mentioned wage bill.. .Furthermore, starting in early l996, the system of automatic salary adjustments will be replaced by one based on merit. The measures recommended by the recently concluded audit:of the civil service will be put in place before end-l995, and are expected to improve the efficiency of the civil service. These measures include in particular: (a) ensuring consistency between the payroll files of the Direction de la solde, the civil service registry and the staffing plans of the different ministries; (b) updating the files concerning the payments of allowances and social benefits, particularly by correcting for inconsistencies; and (c) preparing and implementing a system to improve management tools. The expenditures planned in the context of the social safety net (CFAF 14 billion in l995 and CFAF 10 billion in l996) will be integrated within the Government's social programs, and will be targeted more effectively. The closing of six Senegalese embassies abroad and the repatriation of diplomatic staff will further help to contain spending. The deficit of the special accounts should disappear in 1996 thanks to a number of measures required to balance the operations of the FNR, including ir particular a cut in administrative costs and an increase in contributions before end-l995. In the area of capital expenditures, the three-year public investment program (PTIP) covering the period 1996-98, which is to be adopted before end-December l995 in consultation with the World Bank, should allow for an increase in real terms in investment and give priority to infrastructure and high-yielding projects in the productive sectors. Ministries will abide by the instructions given to not incur extrabudgetary spending. A study on the expenditure system is currently being completed and the measures to be retained to improve the system and ensure a strict application of the rules for executing spending will be implemented with the 1996 budget. The Government is convinced that there are no domestic payments arrears other than those already verified. Nevertheless, once the study on the expenditure system is completed, an audit on extrabudgetary payments arrears and all other arrears will be conducted and finalized before end-l996.  

22. Monetary policy. Credit policy will continue to be prudent, in line with the balance of payments objectives. With declining inflation, the growth of broad money is projected to decelerate further to l7.8 percent in 1995 and 12.3 percent in 1996. The improvement in the fiscal position that is being sought should enable the Government to reduce its indebtedness visa-vis the banking system and make room for an adequate provision of credit to the private sector; credit to the economy is expected to increase by 13.4 percent in 1995 and 12.5 percent: in l996. The net domestic assets of the BCEA0 (excluding the stock of securities issued in July l994) will not increase by more than 4.3 percent of beginning-of-period money stock in 1995 and 1.8 percent in 1996. Monetary policy will continue to be based on the use of the indirect instruments introduced by the BCEAO since October 1993, including in particular a flexible interest rate policy, the use of reserve requirements ratios, and the strengthening of the role of the money market. In order to facilitate the provision of credit to the private sector, the Government will attach particular importance to the improvement of legal procedures with the aim of accelerating the collection of claims. To encourage savings and ensure efficient credit allocation, monetary policy will also aim at maintaining interest rates at an adequate level, while encouraging banks to lower the spread between lending and deposit rates. The strengthening of the financial position of the CNCAS will be continued through improving its management and reducing its operating costs. Finally, the monetary authorities have noted some of the shortcomings of the securitization scheme for consolidated public debt launched in 1994, including in particular the fixed interest rate and the tax exemption. They will seek, in collaboration with other WAEMU members, to promote the development of a broader-based financial market.  

23. The full and prompt implementation of the structural measures of the program is essential for the promotion of private sector activities and the attainment of sustained and rapid economic growth. While important progress has already been made so far in modernizing the regulatory framework and in price and trade liberalization, as well as in reforming the agricultural and public enterprise sectors, it is of crucial importance that any delays that have occurred are made up for as soon as possible, and that the next phase of reforms in these areas be effectively implemented.  

24. The regulatory framework. The next phase of reforms in the area of price and trade policy will focus on the liberalization of wheat flour imports and prices by November l995, and on the full withdrawal of the CPSP from the purchasing and marketing of local and imported rice by end-February 1996,at which date the CPSP will be liquidated. To protect domestic producers from significant declines in world market prices for rice, the authorities have implemented a temporary surtax on imported broken rice. Furthermore, the prices of petroleum products will be subject to automatic quarterly adjustments in accordance with international prices. To encourage export activities and foreign investment, the regulations governing the export processing zone and points francs will be revised and the Government will put in place a new incentives regime before end-l995, taking into account recent World Bank recommendations. Also before end-l995, the Government intends to enact simplified procedures for temporary imports of inputs entering the production of goods to be exported (regimes_ douaniers suspensifs). To accelerate judicial reform, the authorities will redouble their efforts to modernize the legal framework in areas that directly affect business operations.  

25. Agricultural policy. The authorities will continue the ongoing reforms aimed at improving the efficiency and diversification of the agricultural sector. The rice sector will be fully liberalized by February l996, and the import of broken rice will be taken over by the private sector. The CPSP will be liquidated and replaced by a management unit whose role will be limited to monitoring the rice markets, importing broken rice in cases of emergency, and managing a security stock in conjunction with the private sector. Regarding the groundnut sector, the revised timetable for putting SONACOS up for sale by December l995 will be adhered to, as a prior action. The restructuring of SODEFITEX, aimed at reducing its costs and assuring financial equilibrium, will be stepped up. In addition, horticulture and livestock production, which have substantial export potential, will be encouraged. Finally, the provision of credit in rural areas will be strengthened through measures promoting local savings and loan associations and improving their management.  

26. Public enterprise reform. The reform of the public enterprises sector constitutes a key element of the strategy adopted by the Government of Senegal to improve competitiveness and resource allocation. The restructuring of the enterprises targeted in the previous PFP will be accelerated with a view to completing the privatization of SSPT by  

March l996 and that of SPHU-Teranga by June l996. Furthermore, the sale of the Meridien hotel and the liquidation of SH-Saly will be completed by end1995. In the energy sector, an action plan for the reform of the sector is being prepared by the Government and will be completed by June l996. The Government intends to fully liberalize the import, transportation and distribution of petroleum products by eliminating the privileges granted to the Sociéte africaine de raffinage (SAR) and modify"the institutional framework to encourage private investment in energy production. In addition, SENELEC will be privatized as part of the overall action plan for the energy sector, to be prepared before end-June l996 in consultation with the World Bank. In the meantime, payments between SENELEC, the petroleum companies, the SAR, and the Government will be regularized, with steps taken to eliminate existing payments arrears and prevent their reappearance. Furthermore, the privatization of the management of SONEES is under way and should be completed by end-l995. The authorities are also committed to updating information on the public enterprise sector, and beginning in June l996, will report comprehensive data on the financial operations of key enterprises on a periodic basis. These data will also cover transfers from the Central Government, bank borrowing, and arrears. Finally, the crossdebts of the public sector will be settled before end-l995, in accordance with the previously established timetable. Restructuring plans are also under way in the road, railway, maritime and air transportation sectors, in line with the transportation sector adjustment program. In this context, the Government has put in place reforms aimed at increasing the efficiency and the competitiveness of the sector, including in particular significant reductions in staff. An action plan defining the next phase of reforms for public enterprises will be prepared with the assistance of the World Bank and finalized by March 1996.  

27. In the context of improving the services provided by government agencies, the authorities are taking steps to improve the quality and availability of economic data. Although Senegal's economic data base is comprehensive, there are some weaknesses, in particular in the national accounts, the balance of payments and the CPI data. At present, the CPI compilation is being improved with external technical assistance. Technical assistance needs regarding other statistical areas will be assessed, and external assistance, in particular from the IMF, will be sought in order to cover these needs.  

28. The Government of Senegal participates actively in all the work aimed at promoting regional integration within the WAEMU. Moreover, during the program period, the Government does not intend to introduce or intensify any exchange restrictions, introduce or modify any multiple currency practices, conclude any bilateral payments agreements that are inconsistent with Article VIII of the IMF's Articles of Agreement, or introduce or intensify import restrictions for balance of payments reasons. Finally, Senegal intends to accept at an early date the obligations of Article VIII, Sections 2, 3, and 4 of the IMF Articles of Agreement, in conjunction with its partners of the WAEMU and the Franc zone.  

29. In line with the budgetary objectives of the program, and after taking account of project aid already committed, the financing requirements for 1995, and the first half of l996 will be covered by financial assistance from multilateral institutions and bilateral creditors, and by debt relief through rescheduling and cancellation operations. l/ For the second half of 1996, a financing gap of some CFAF 10 billion would remain, after account has been taken of project-related external assistance and debt relief already obtained. This amount is expected to be covered by assistance from the World Bank and bilateral donors.  

30. Senegal will continue to seek grants and loans on highly concessional terms to enable it to pursue its development strategy on a sound financial basis. Senegal will also continue to conduct a prudent external debt management policy. In this context, the Government will neither contract nor guarantee any new external nonconcessional loans, including loans with maturities of less than l year, with the exception of normal short-term import-related credits and loans associated with debt rescheduling or refinancing agreements. The Government will make every effort to obtain concessional loans including a grant element of at least 35 percent. Senegal will continue to make all loans contracted or guaranteed by the Government subject to the prior authorization of the Ministry of Economy, Finance and Planning. The Government will service its debt in a timely manner and will not accumulate any new external payments arrears. All outstanding external payments arrears will be eliminated before end-Decembe1 1995.

Senegal's total outstanding external debt, including payments arrears, amounted to CFAF 1,729 billion $({\tt U S S3.2}$ billion) at end-l994, or about 80 percent of GDP (Table l). Multilateral creditors accounted for almost 60 percent of the total stock of outstanding debt at end-l994, with outstanding Fund credit representing about 9 percent, and debt owed to the World Bank about 33 percent. l/ The remainder was owed to official bilateral creditors (36 percent) and private creditors (3 percent); shortterm debt amounted to l percent of GDP.

Debt service obligations in l995 would represent about l8 percent of exports of goods, nonfactor services, and gross private transfers, and 42 percent of total government revenue. A long-term balance of payments scenario was prepared for the period 1995-2005 (Table 3). This scenario assumes that, as a result of the improved competitiveness of the economy, export volume will grow at an average rate of 6 percent a year over the period 1995-2000, largely because of the continued expansion of the volume of traditional exports, but also of a rise in nontraditional exports (agro-industrial products and textiles). From the year 200l onward, export volume is projected to increase by 4 percent a year, in line with real GDP growth. Import volume is projected to grow on average at an annual rate of 4 percent during l995-2000, and then by 3.5 percent in subsequent years. The terms of trade are assumed to remain virtually constant over the projection period as a whole. The external current account deficit, excluding official transfers, is projected to narrow steadily from 8 percent of GDP in l995 to 3 percent by 2005. The debt service projections were made on the. basis of outstanding debt at endSeptember l995, with the annual level of new borrowings declining moderately after 2002. More than two-thirds of new borrowings would be contracted from multilateral creditors. It is assumed that the future contracted debt bears an interest rate of 2 percent, and an average maturity of 40 years for multilateral and 30 years for bilateral debt.  

Under this scenario, the external debt position of Senegal is projected to improve considerably over the next l0 years. The stock of debt as a percentage of GDP would be more than halved, from 72 percent in 1995 to 34 percent in 2005. The debt service ratio in terms of exports of goods, services, and private transfers would decline from l8 percent in 1995, to l4 percent in 2000, and to about l0 percent in 2005. Debt service to multilateral creditors is projected to fall from 9 percent of exports of goods, services, and private transfers in l995 to $53$ percent in 2005. Similarly, the ratio of debt service obligations to government revenue would decline from 42 percent to 18 percent, respectively. Payments to the Fund are expected to reach a peak of almost 4 percent of exports of goods, services, and private transfers in l997, then to decline steadily until 2007, and disappear thereafter (Table 4). This baseline scenario therefore shows that, with continued pursuit of adequate policies and prudent debt management, Senegal's debt service obligations would be sustainable over the next 10 years.  

An alternative scenario was prepared to assess the sensitivity of the above results to lower export growth. With annual export growth 1 percentage point lower than in the baseline scenario, the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) would widen by almost l percentage point of GDP by 2000, and by almost 2 percentage points of GDP by 2005. The debt service ratio in percent: of exports of goods, services, and private transfers would increase by $3$ percentage point by 2005 as compared with the baseline scenario, but would still be sustainable.

# Commentary on lending operations  

1. Bank assistance strategy in Senegal is guided by four basic objectives: (i) promoting competitiveness and establishing the basis for self-sustained growth; (i) supporting human resource development and sustainable management of natural resources; (iii) reducing poverty; and (iv) improving management capacity in the public sector. The vehicles to support these objectives have been adjustment lending buttressed by appropriate technical assistance operations and sector-based rehabilitation/investment projects.  

2.As of September 30, 1995, the World Bank Group had approved 108 operations in Senegal for a total amount committed, less cancellations, of ${\tt U S}\$1$ ,498.55 million, consisting of 76 IDA credits (including 3 special fund credits and two SFA), 20 Bank loans, and 12 IFC operations. Past projects supported agricultural diversification, institutional development, and expansion of the country's infrastructure, particularly its transport system. In recent years, emphasis has shifted to better utilization and maintenance of existing facilities, and on helping the Government resolve some of the key issues related to long-term development prospects. Ongoing or planned operations for agricultural extension and research, health, education, and transport sector reforms relate to both of these concerns.  

3.The World Bank Group has committed $\$218.9$ million in fiscal year 1995. A higher education project of about $\mathtt{U S S2S}$ million is planned for fiscal year 1996. Future operations include investment operations in education, health services, energy, transport and agriculture, and adjustment lending to deepen policy reforms and to support agricultural sector development.

Overall, Senegal's economic data base is comprehensive, but there are weaknesses in the areas of national accounts, the consumer price index (CPI), international trade, and the balance of payments. The authorities are continuing their efforts to improve the quality and availability of data. They are making appropriate arrangements to ensure that the legal provisions requiring economic agents to report statistical information on a timely basis are enforced.  

# 1. Real sector  

The national accounts data suffer from.some deficiencies in their compilation. Finalized data are based on returns from over l,Oo firms, a process that typically takes up to three years. For preliminary data, the accounts are based on a sample of 300 enterprises, and use an input-output model that was developed with French technical assistance. The CPI coverage is limited to Dakar, and is based on l967 weights. Currently, the CPI compilation is being improved with technical assistance from INSEE/EUROSTAT. The results of a household budgetary survey undertaken in l99l/92 are currently being processed, and they will be used to update the CPI weights. Trade data are incomplete due to the time taken by the customs directorate to report trade flows to the BCEAO, which may take up to three years.  

# 2. Public finances  

GFS data are compiled by the Ministry of Finance from the following three sources: the Customs, the Tax, and the Treasury directorates. GFS data have not been reported by the authorities to the Statistics Department for many years. The latest data reported for Senegal were published in the 1990 GFSY and covered consolidated central government through 1984.  

# 3. Monetary data  

Monetary data for Senegal are prepared by the National Agency of the BCEAO. Outstanding data problems include the lengthy time period needed to finalize the data, and some inconsistencies between the balance sheets of the BCEAO and the commercial banks.  

Monetary data still include assets and liabilities of banks that have long ceased banking operations, but whose liquidation has never been formally completed. Other statistical issues relate to the proper revaluation of the Compte d' Operation that the BCEAO maintains with the French Treasury, and other foreign currency denominated accounts, including Fund accounts. Monetary data are prepared by the National Agency of the BCEAO. The reporting of monthly monetary data to the Fund is current. However, the reporting of data on deposits, lending, and money market rates has been interrupted since 1993.  

A money and banking statistics course was held in Senegal in April 1995. BCEAO member countries have subsequently requested the Fund to organize additional training courses in money and banking statistics and in other statistical areas.  

# 4. Balance of payments data  

A technical assistance mission by the Statistics Department visited Dakar in l992 and identified three shortcomings in balance of payments compilation procedures: (i) trade data were incomplete and reported with lengthy delays; (ii) bank settlements reports are not processed by the BCEAO; and (iii) surveys sent to companies were often inaccurately answered or unanswered. Efforts are being undertaken to address these issues. Enhancing the capacity of the computerized data compiling system of the customs should also help reduce delays in reporting.
