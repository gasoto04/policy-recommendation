# I. Introduction  

The discussions for the l995 Article IV consultation and the midterm review under the first annual arrangement under the enhanced structural adjustment facility (ESAF) for Senegal were held in Dakar during the period March l0-24, 1995. 1/ The attached letter from the Minister of Economy, Finance, and Planning of Senegal, dated March 24, 1995 (Appendix II), reviews the progress made by Senegal under the first annual ESAF program in 1994, and sets out the policies and measures to be implemented in 1995.  

On August 29, l994 the Executive Board approved a three-year ESAF arrangement for Senegal in an amount equivalent to SDR l30.8 million (ll0 percent of quota), together with the first annual arrangement thereunder in an amount equivalent to SDR 47.6 million (40 percent of quota)--EBS/94/l50, Supplement 1. The ESAF arrangement replaced a one-year stand-by arrangement, which was approved on March 2, 1994, in support of the adjustment program adopted by Senegal soon after the devaluation of the CFA franc. The first purchase under the stand-by arrangement (SDR 30.9 million) and the first semiannual disbursement under the ESAF arrangement (SDR l6.65 million) were effected upon Executive Board approval. The second semiannual disbursement under the ESAF arrangement is to be made available subject to observance of the performance criteria for end-December 1994 and completion of the midterm review.  

As of end-March 1995, Senegal's outstanding use of Fund resources amounted to SDR 202.5 million (l70.3 percent of quota). .If all the disbursements under the ESAF arrangements and all repurchases and loan repayments are made as scheduled, Senegal's use of Fund resources would amount to SDR 24l.2 million (202.9 percent of quota) by end-June 1997 (Appendix I, Table I).  

Senegal is on the standard l2-month consultation cycle. In concluding the last Article IV consultation on March 2, 1994, Directors welcomed the adoption by Senegal of a broad-based and comprehensive adjustment strategy, involving the devaluation of the CFA franc, and stressed the need for the authorities to implement all the accompanying financial and structural measures to ensure the achievement of sustainable economic growth and the restoration of internal and external financial viability over the medium term.  

The World Bank has been supporting Senegal's development efforts through structural and sectoral adjustment lending, including an economic recovery credit for SDR 18.2 million approved on March 17, 1994, and more recently, a private sector adjustment and competitiveness credit for SDR 27.6 million approved on February 16, 1995. Senegal's relations with the Fund and the World Bank Group are summarized in Appendices IV and V, respectively.  

# II． Background and _Recent Developments  

In early l994 the Senegalese authorities decided to take the necessary steps to tackle the serious difficulties that had beset the economy since the late 1980s, and to achieve sustained economic growth and financial viability over the medium term. The strengthening of the adjustment strategy was predicated in good measure on the devaluation of the CFA franc by 50 percent in foreign currency terms, which had been decided in the context of renewed efforts to increase regional cooperation and integration. In addition to correcting the exchange rate parity, the strategy involved: (a) the implementation of strong supporting fiscal and monetary policies, designed to keep inflation in check and ensure that the competitiveness of the economy would be restored on a lasting basis; and (b) the acceleration of structural reforms aimed at improving incentives for the private sector and promoting growth.  

Immediately following the devaluation, the authorities implemented a comprehensive adjustment program that was supported by a stand-by arrangement from the Fund, which was replaced in August l994 by the current ESAF arrangement. Overall, despite weaknesses in government revenue performance, the program remained broadly on track throughout most of l994. However, in the last months of the year, difficulties re-emerged in revenue collection, delays occurred in the implementation of some structural reforms, and there were also shortfalls in external budgetary assistance. Consequently, although developments in $1994$ were broadly encouraging, the overall results under the program were mixed. The performance criteria for end-December 1994 and the quantitative and structural benchmarks for end-September and end-December $1994$ under the ESAF arrangement were observed, except for the benchmark on government revenue and the performance criteria related to the elimination and nonaccumulation of external payments arrears (Appendix II, Annex). The authorities have requested waivers for the nonobservance of these performance criteria.  

The macroeconomic objectives of the program have been broadly achieved. Real GDP growth in l994 is estimated to have amounted to 2 percent, close to the program objective for the year (Appendix I, Table II, and Chart 1). Agricultural production, which was helped by good weather conditions, rebounded strongly, as did the fishing, tourism, and other export-oriented activities. On the other hand, activity in the commerce and transportation sectors contracted more than foreseen, partly reflecting the fall in the volume of imports. Inflation in l994 was more moderate than projected, as the rise in the GDP deflator was around 33 percent, and the average increase in the consumer price index for Dakar was contained at 32 percent, instead of the projected rate of 39 percent. Government spending restraint, particularly on wages, as well as a cautious monetary policy, helped moderate inflationary pressures after the devaluation.  

In the external sector, the real effective exchange rate--based on relative consumer prices--declined by some 35 percent and the competitiveness of the economy improved considerably (Chart 2). There was a significant pickup in tourism and exports (especially of groundnuts and fish products), which increased in volume by more than expected, and a sizable reflow of flight capital was recorded. Also, the change in relative prices led to a shift of consumption toward domestically produced products, which was reflected in a drop in the volume of imports. The external current account deficit, excluding official transfers, thus amounted in 1994 to 9.2 percent of GDP, below the programmed level (Appendix I, Table VI). However, disbursements of external budgetary assistance fell CFAF 46 billion short of projections, owing largely to delays in reaching agreements with the World Bank on the private sector competitiveness and agricultural sector adjustment credits. These shortfalls contributed to the nonobservance of the performance criteria on the nonaccumulation and elimination by endDecember $1994$ of external payments arrears. l/  

In the fiscal area, the authorities maintained a very restrained spending stance throughout l994, particularly on wages. The average increase in wages, which became effective in April l994, was kept below 10 percent, in line with the program, and the benchmark on the wage bill was observed. Other current spending was broadly as projected, with the  

CFAF l5 billion provision for social safety net measures almost entirely utilized. l/ In contrast, the deficit of the special accounts of the Treasury was larger than projected, primarily because of the difficult financial situation of the National Retirement Fund (FNR). Capital outlays remained somewhat below forecasts, owing to administrative delays encountered in the start-up of certain projects. On the receipt side, government revenue performance was weaker than projected, with collections falling almost CFAF 26 billion (l percent of GDP) short of the program's benchmark for the year as a whole. This shortfall was due primarily to administrative weaknesses and tax fraud, particularly at customs, as well as delays in the receipt of funds under the fishing agreement with the European Union. The overall fiscal deficit, on a commitment basis and excluding grants, thus exceeded the program target of 4.5 percent of GDP by over l percentage point (Appendix I, Table III). 2/ Domestic payments arrears were reduced as planned but, as noted above, external payments arrears could not be eliminated as programmed, largely because of the shortfall in external budgetary assistance.  

In the monetary area, where policy is determined by the BCEAO at the level of the West African Economic and Monetary Union (WAEMU), money supply growth amounted to 39 percent, or more than projected, reflecting a strengthening of money demand and a return of confidence (Appendix I, Table V). At the same time, net bank credit to the Government and the net domestic assets of the banking system remained well within the program ceilings. As a result, the contribution of Senegal to the net foreign assets position of the BCEAo improved much faster than forecast.  

Credit conditions remained generally tight throughout the year. Following the devaluation, the Central Bank's discount rate was raised from 10 percent to 14.5 percent on January l8, 1994; subsequently, with inflation in the zone abating, the discount rate was reduced in four steps, to 9 percent on January 23, 1995. After an initially sluggish expansion of bank credit to the private sector, credit demand rose moderately in the second half of the year in line with the pickup in economic activity, but remained well below what could be accommodated under the program. Starting in August l994, the authorities issued l2-year securities against the consolidated public debt--amounting to CFAF l44 billion--assumed by the Government in the context of the restructuring of the Senegalese banking system. These securities, which are redeemable at par and carry a tax exempt interest rate of 5 percent, were purchased by local investors, as well as by investors from other WAEMU countries.

On the structural front, most of the measures planned for l994 were implemented. In the regulatory area, the Office of Port Personnel lost its monopoly in July l994, and the labor code was amended in November 1994, ahead of schedule, allowing enterprises to freely adjust their work force according to economic needs. Price controls on l2 commodities were eliminated as planned, leaving only a limited number of controlled prices. All remaining prior authorizations for imports and exports have been rescinded, except those required for public health and security reasons, for the import of gold and silver bars and second-hand clothing, as well as for the export of precious metals and mineral products. The special agreements (conventions _speciales) and protocols between the Government and several companies, which granted them a certain degree of protection and tax advantages, were renegotiated as planned. The objective was to put all. these enterprises under the common tax regime. l/  

In the agricultural sector, the authorities implemented several reforms aimed at improving efficiency and encouraging diversification. The marketing and processing of domestically produced rice was completely liberalized and the Caisse de Peréquation et de Stabilization des Prix (CPSP) began permitting the private sector to import broken rice. However, delays were encountered in offering the groundnut marketing enterprise (SONACOs) for sale to the private sector, even though a law was passed for its privatization. After discussions with concerned donors and the World Bank, the date for opening bids was postponed to September 1995. 2/  

In the area of public enterprise reform, a financial audit of the Senegalese electricity company (SENELEC) was carried out and the signing of a new performance contract for the company, planned for January 1995, was postponed to later in the year in order to take into account the conclusions of a study, which began in March l995, on the reorganization and reduction of costs of the company. Reforms in other public enterprises are being carried out as planned. The civil service audit, expected to be completed before end-l994, began only in April 1995, because of delays encountered in the preparation of the terms of reference and the mobilization of external resources to finance the audit.  

Overall, Senegal's macroeconomic data base is comprehensive, but there are deficiencies in the methodologies for compiling the national accounts, balance of payments, and the CPI data (Appendix VI). The authorities are taking steps to improve the quality and availability of economic data.  

# III. Report on the Discussions and the 1995 Program  

In light of developments in l994, the discussions with the Senegalese authorities focused on the effective implementation of all the measures under the first annual ESAF arrangement, as well as the additional measures required to maintain the program on track and achieve its objectives. While noting the social pressures they continued to face, the authorities reiterated their strong commitment to the success of the program. In March 1995, the main opposition party rejoined the Government of President Diouf, which now enjoys broader-based support.  

The authorities believed that the original program objectives for 1995 remain attainable, given their determination to sustain the reform efforts and to avoid any new slippages. The macroeconomic objectives for l995 are to achieve a real GDP growth rate of 4.5 percent; to reduce average inflation, as measured by the GDP deflator, to 7.8 percent; and to lower the external current account deficit, excluding official transfers, to 7.7 percent of GDP. With the restoration of competitiveness, export receipts are expected to benefit in l995 from the continued increase in export volumes of groundnuts, cotton, and fish products, as well as from a pickup in nontraditional exports, including horticultural products. The tourism sector is also expected to expand further. The buoyancy of exports should more than compensate for the projected recovery of imports as output growth gathers steam.  

# 1. Financial policies  

The main focus of the authorities' policies in l995 is on consolidating the fiscal position through a significant improvement in government revenue performance and continued restraint in spending, particularly on wages. The 1995 government budget, which was approved by Parliament last December, aims at reducing the overall fiscal deficit, on a commitment basis and excluding grants, from 5.7 percent of GDP in $1994$ to 2.6 percent, in line with the objectives of the medium-term program. To this end, a number of measures were implemented as prior actions, and others are being carried out.  

On the revenue side, the authorities believed that their efforts in recent months would lead to an increase in government revenue of 23 percent to CFAF 37l.2 billion in l995 (Appendix I, Table IV), following the disappointing performance in l994. Already, during the first quarter of 1995, the program's benchmark related to government revenue was exceeded. To ensure that this improvement is durable, determined actions to reduce fiscal fraud, expand the tax base, and strengthen the customs and tax administrations are being carried out. They include, in particular, a reduction in statutory and ad hoc tax exemptions, as well as an extensive reorganization of the tax and customs administrations designed to improve revenue collections. As prior actions, the reorganization of the Customs Department was completed, and a single registration system for enterprises was initiated before April 15, 1995. The reorganization of the customs administration involved the setting up of regional directorates; the redeployment of staff to border areas where controls are being intensified; the strengthening of internal hierarchical controls; and the improvement of the use of the computerized customs clearance system. Emphasis is also being put on reinforcing the import verification program implemented with the help of the Société générale de surveillance (SGS); the creation of public bonded warehouses and the elimination of fictitious warehousing; the systematic cross-checking of customs declarations against tax returns of companies subject to the value-added tax (VAT); and the strict application of sanctions to combat fraud. The authorities recognized that the key to improving revenue performance remains the political determination to combat and reduce fraud.  

On the expenditure side, the authorities intend to limit total spending, on a commitment basis, to CFAF 434.9 billion in l995, an increase of only 2 percent from the l994 level. Spending on wages is to be contained at CFAF 157.5 billion, or somewhat below the originally programmed amount of CFAF l60 billion. To this end, no general pay raise will be granted in 1995 and new hiring is to be strictly limited. The increase in the wage bill in 1995 will amount to about 6 percent, reflecting the full-year impact of the pay raise granted in l994, normal promotions, and some limited recruitment in health and education. The measures that will be recommended on the basis of the audit of the payroll, which started in April 1995 as part of the ongoing reform of the civil service are to be put in place beginning in July 1995. A very prudent policy will also be pursued with regard to nonwage spending, while providing adequately for basic health and education services. The l995 budget also allocates CFAF 10 billion for social safety net measures. l/ The staff stressed the importance of integrating these measures within the Government's social programs and targeting social spending more effectively, in line with the recommendations of the recent FAD social safety net mission. Furthermore, the authorities intend to take the necessary measures to balance the operations of the FNR from June 1995, through reduction in costs and increases in contributions. Any additional expenditure related to the recent broadening of the Government will be compensated by economies elsewhere, and as a prior action, the necessary instructions have been given to ensure that no extrabudgetary spending takes place. Finally, capital expenditure is projected to rise by l6 percent, with priority given to infrastructure and high-yielding projects, especially in the rural sector.  

At the end of l994, the remaining identified and verified domestic payments arrears amounted to CFAF l4.9 billion, and outstanding external payments arrears to CFAF 45.5 billion. l/ The projected budget deficit, as well as the elimination of all these arrears by end-l995, will involve a total external financing requirement (excluding project-related assistance) of about CFAF l30 billion for the year as a whole. “Out of this amount, financing for about CFAF l05 billion has been identified, 2/ leaving an unfinanced gap of some CFAF 25 billion. For the first six months of 1995 (second half of the first annual ESAF program), exceptional budgetary external assistance of CFAF 58 billion has been identified. This will fully cover the financing requirement for this period, allowing for the originally programmed reduction in domestic payments arrears of CFAF 10 billion by endJune l995, and a reduction of CFAF l8 billion in outstanding external payments arrears. The staff urged the authorities to seek additional external financing for the second half of 1995 to ensure that the gap for the entire year is fully covered. The staff also indicated that the completion of the negotiations on the second annual ESAF arrangement, to be carried out later in the year, will hinge upon the continuation of strong policies, as well as assurances that the financing gaps for l995 and 1996 are fully covered.  

The monetary authorities intend to pursue prudent credit policies consistent with the balance of payments objective. With confidence strengthening, and with lower inflationary expectations, the rate of growth of broad money is projected to decelerate to less than 20 percent in 1995. The expected improvement in the fiscal position should enable the Government to avoid net recourse to the domestic banking system, thereby making room for an adequate provision of credit to the private sector; the latter is projected to increase by l3 percent, in line with the nominal GDP growth. The increase in the net domestic assets of the banking system is thus projected to be limited in l995 to 8 percent of the beginning-of-period money stock, while the net foreign assets position is expected to improve further.  

Monetary policy will continue to rely on the use of the indirect instruments introduced by the BCEAO in October 1993, including the application of a flexible interest rate policy, a reliance on reserve requirements, and the expansion of the role of the money market. Also, the agricultural bank will be recapitalized in l995, and the authorities intend to encourage the establishment of local savings and loan institutions. The authorities recognized some of the shortcomings of the securitization scheme put in place during l994--notably the fixed interest rate, the exemption from taxes, and the ability of banks to use the securities to meet reserve  

requirements. They are now studying in collaboration with other WAEMU members the possibility of developing a broader-based market for debt instruments.  

# 2. Structural measures  

The authorities are continuing to implement the structural measures envisaged in the memorandum of economic and financial policies for 1994-95 (EBS/94/l50, Appendix II) and the PFP, as well as in the recently negotiated private sector competitiveness and agricultural sector adjustment credits with the World Bank. The structural reforms focus on three main areas: (a) further liberalization of the labor market, prices, and external trade; (b) increases in the efficiency and diversification of the agricultural sector; and (c) rationalization of public sector operations and acceleration of public enterprise reform. In addition, the authorities are continuing their reform efforts in the education, health, and transport sectors.  

With regard to the regulatory environment and price and trade policy, the authorities noted that they had already implemented a number of farreaching reforms, including liberalization of most prices, elimination of virtually all prior authorizations for imports and exports, and the renegotiation of special agreements with several companies. They intend to continue implementing the second phase of the price and trade liberalization program, including in particular the elimination of administered wholesale prices for rice by June l995, and the liberalization of wheat flour imports and prices by November 1995. 1/ To foster export activities and foreign investment, the regulatory framework governing the free trade zone and the investment regime will also be revised. Moreover, as prior actions, in midApril l995 the transport subsidy of the CPSP was abolished in all regions, and the CPSP abandoned its activities in the distribution of imported rice and closed its regional stores, with the exception of those in Dakar, which are to be closed by June l995.  

In the agricultural sector, the staff pressed for adherence to the original agenda of reforms and for making up for the delays that had been encountered to date, such as in the privatization of SONAcoS. The authorities explained that these delays had been recommended by key donors to ensure the success of the operation. Other reforms are being carried out as planned, including the restructuring of the cotton marketing enterprise (SODEFITEX), which aimed at reducing its costs. Both the groundnut and cotton sectors have benefited from the devaluation and registered surpluses in l994. Producer prices for these two crops are determined by a committee representing the farmers, the marketing agencies, and the Government, taking into account world market prices. All other producer prices are freely determined in the market. In the context of its withdrawal from rice import and marketing activities, the CPSP will be restructured and its role limited to monitoring the rice sector and managing a security stock. The rice sector is to be entirely restructured by February l996. Moreover, several studies are being carried out to further develop the agricultural sector, including studies to promote livestock production as well as exports of horticultural products.  

Progress is also being made in public enterprise reform and privatization. As a prior action, a law was adopted on March 24, 1995 allowing for the privatization of the water company (SONEES), in spite of strong labor opposition. Furthermore, before end-June 1995, the necessary actions will be taken to finalize the privatization of two enterprises (SSPT and SPHU-Teranga) and SH-Saly will be liquidated. l/ Finally, before endDecember 1995, the Government will adopt, in consultation with the World Bank, a three-year public investment program (PIP) for 1996-98. The PIP will continue to give priority to infrastructure and high-yielding projects in the productive sectors.  

The authorities intend to continue playing an active role in promoting regional trade and economic integration among the member countries of the WAEMU. In this context, a regional initiative is under way to harmonize customs duties and indirect taxation. The authorities indicated that Senegal intends to keep its exchange system free of restrictions on payments and transfers for current international transactions. Senegal also intends to accept the obligations of Article VIII, Sections 2, 3, and 4 of the Articles of Agreement of the Fund as soon as possible.  

# 3. Medium-term balance of payments outlook  

The medium-term balance of payments projections presented in EBs/94/150 were updated in light of developments in l994 and the latest estimates of the World Economic Outlook. It is expected that the restoration of the competitiveness of the economy, together with the effective implementation of the measures described in the preceding paragraphs, would contribute to a significant improvement in Senegal's external position and the achievement of external viability over the medium term. According to the updated scenario (Appendix I, Tables VI and VII), the external current account deficit, excluding official transfers, is now projected to narrow from 9.2 percent of GDP in l994 to less than 6 percent in 1999. This improvement is expected to be brought about by a decline in the trade deficit, an increase in tourism receipts, and higher net inflows of private transfers. The increase in export volume is projected to average 6 percent a year over the five-year period 1995-99. Meanwhile, the volume of imports is projected to grow by about 4 percent a year, with imports of capital goods registering the highest rate of increase. The terms of trade are expected to remain virtually unchanged over the projection period.  

Senegal will continue to rely heavily on concessional external assistance to finance investment projects. After taking into account projected disbursements of project aid and private capital reflows, disbursements under the ESAF, as well as the recent Paris Club rescheduling, $\scriptstyle{\frac{1}{2}}/$ the remaining financing requirements for the period 1995-97--averaging about CFAF 35 billion a year--are expected to be covered by multilateral and bilateral aid, additional debt rescheduling, as well as by sectoral adjustment loans from the World Bank. The multilateral debt of Senegal amounted to ${\tt U S}\$1$ .9 billion at end-1994, representing close to 60 percent of total outstanding debt. Service obligations on multilateral debt represent 47 percent of total debt service in l995. As external financing is expected to remain on concessional terms, the debt service ratio, after debt relief, is projected to decline to 13 percent by 1999, of which about half owed to multilateral creditors. Under the updated scenario, repayments to the Fund would continue to be relatively small in relation to projected exports of goods and nonfactor services (l.6 percent in the year 2003), and Senegal can be expected to meet its future obligations in a timely manner.  

# IV. Staff Appraisal  

Economic developments under Senegal's ESAF-supported program were generally encouraging in l994, although there were some setbacks and the results were mixed. The macroeconomic objectives of the program were largely achieved. Real GDP growth turned positive and was close to target, while inflation was lower than projected. With the restoration of competitiveness following the devaluation of the CFA franc, there was a significant pickup in tourism and export activities, as well as sizable reflows of flight capital; the external position thus improved appreciably.  

However, in the last few months of the year, difficulties re-emerged in government revenue collection, delays were registered in the implementation of some structural reforms, and there were shortfalls in external assistance, owing largely to difficulties in reaching understandings with the World Bank on the private sector competitiveness and agricultural sector adjustment credits. The performance criteria and the quantitative and structural benchmarks through end-December l994 were observed, except those related to the nonaccumulation and elimination of external payments arrears and to government revenue.  

In the fiscal area, the authorities pursued a very restrained spending policy, especially on wages, but government revenue performance was weak, owing largely to administrative problems and tax fraud, particularly at customs. The overall fiscal deficit thus exceeded the program target, and was partly financed by higher nonbank resources. Net bank credit to the Government and the net domestic assets of the banking system, however, remained well within the program ceilings. On the structural front, most measures were implemented as envisaged, but there were some delays, notably in carrying out the audit of the government payroll files and in privatizing the groundnut marketing agency.  

Overall, in spite of some setbacks, Senegal has made progress in implementing important structural measures and moving its economy toward financial viability. Nonetheless, the economic situation remains fragile, and further efforts are needed to achieve the medium-term objectives of the program. The authorities are thus urged to persevere in their efforts, notably in four key areas.  

Improving revenue collection. To achieve the program objectives, it is essential that government revenue performance be improved substantially and durably, especially at customs. To this end, determined actions must be pursued to reduce fiscal fraud, strengthen customs and tax administrations, and widen the tax base. Although the results obtained during the first quarter of l995 in improving revenue collection have been encouraging, the progress made needs to be sustained. In this context, the staff welcomes the political commitment at the highest level to combat fraud and to ensure a better revenue performance.  

Maintaining spending restraint. The authorities' successful efforts in containing spending last year should also prevail in 1995. Restraint as regards the wage bill and other current outlays is essential, but adequate provisions should be made for basic health and education services, as well as for the social safety net. The authorities' commitments not to grant any general wage increase and to strictly limit recruitment in l995 are crucial to maintain spending within the targeted level. They will have to remain particularly alert in this area and ensure that no extrabudgetary outlays take place.  

Pursuing_a prudent credit policy. Credit policy needs to remain prudent. The strengthening of monetary management through a more effective use of indirect instruments in the context of the WAEMU should be given high priority. Key interest rates should be fully market-determined to encourage savings and an efficient allocation of resources. Efforts to improve the effectiveness of monetary policy instruments and stimulate money market development, to accelerate economic integration among WAEMU countries, and to strengthen cooperation and monetary policy coordination are all essential. The staff considers the actions of the Senegalese authorities in these areas to be of decisive importance for the region.  

Accelerating structural reforms. A wide range of structural measures were implemented under the ESAF-supported program and the World  

Bank sectoral adjustment credits, focusing in particular on trade and price liberalization, as well as agricultural sector and public enterprise reforms. While welcoming these efforts, the staff urges the authorities to adhere to the original agenda of actions and to effectively implement the next phase of reforms in these areas. These steps are necessary to ensure that full advantage is taken of the window of opportunity opened by the devaluation and to promote private sector development and economic growth.  

Senegal continues to avail itself of the transitional arrangements under Article XIV, Section 2, but intends to keep its exchange system free of restrictions on payments and transfers for current international transactions. The staff will keep under active review developments in Senegal's exchange system that may involve Fund jurisdiction, and urges Senegal to accept the obligations of Article VIII, Sections 2, 3, and $4$ of the IMF's Articles of Agreement at an early date.  

In spite of the progress achieved to date, Senegal's program objectives, while attainable, remain subject to some risks, including the evolution of the terms of trade and internal social pressures. The authorities are keenly aware that they cannot afford any slippages in the period ahead, and they should stand ready to take any additional measures that may be required by changing circumstances. The risks are attenuated somewhat by the prior actions that were implemented. Timely availability of external financial assistance is also a key condition for the success of the program.  

The authorities have requested waivers for the nonobservance of the performance criteria on the elimination by end-December 1994 of external payments arrears and the nonaccumulation of such arrears during the period of the arrangement. As the authorities have already taken adequate corrective measures, including strong prior actions, and have committed themselves to taking additional measures if necessary, and in view of the strength of the proposed adjustment program for l995, the staff recommends that the requested waivers be granted and that the present review under the first annual ESAF arrangement be completed.  

It is proposed that the next Article IV consultation with Senegal be held on the standard 12-month cycle.

Dear Mr. Camdessus:  

1.  The Executive Board of the International Monetary Fund approved on August 29, 1994 a three-year arrangement for Senegal under the enhanced structural adjustment facility (ESAF) and the first annual arrangement thereunder. In accordance with that arrangement, we held discussions with an IMF mission regarding the midterm review of Senegal's first annual economic and financial adjustment program covering the period July 1994-June 1995. The discussions focused primarily on the progress made under the program during the second half of 1994 and on the prospects and measures envisaged for the first half of 1995, in accordance with the medium-term objectives set forth in the medium-term economic and financial policy framework paper (PFP) of July 1, 1994. We reached understandings on the economic and financial policies to be implemented and the economic objectives for 1995. The Government of Senegal remains committed to the implementation of the economic and financial policies described in this memorandum, which supplements the memorandum of July 1, 1994.  

2.  Progress made during the second half of 1994 and results for 1994. 5Faced with the economic and financial difficulties confronting Senegal, the Government decided in early $1994$ to adopt a comprehensive and strengthened medium-term adjustment strategy, aimed at improving the competitiveness of the economy and strengthening the conditions appropriate to re-establish sustained economic growth and to ensure the return to financial viability over the medium term. This strategy, formulated in a context of enhanced regional integration and cooperation, has three essential components: the 50 percent devaluation of the CFA franc in January l994; the restrained accompanying fiscal and monetary policies; and the acceleration of structural reforms aimed at private sector development and promoting growth. Within the framework of this strategy, the Government has implemented an adjustment program for l994 that was supported initially by a stand-by arrangement from the IMF, which was replaced in August $1994$ by an arrangement under the ESAF. The main macroeconomic objectives for l994 were to raise the real GDP growth rate to 2.4 percent; to limit inflation, as measured by the GDP deflator, to 35.4 percent; and to contain the external current account deficit, excluding official transfers, at 9.8 percent of GDP.  

3. The results achieved in l994 were generally encouraging, but some difficulties have arisen, notably concerning government revenue collection and delays in the disbursements of external budgetary assistance. The macroeconomic objectives of the program for l994 were largely achieved. Real GDP growth, estimated at around 2 percent in l994, was close to target. Inflation, as measured by the GDP deflator, was 33 percent, below projections, while the average increase in the consumer price index for Dakar was contained at 32 percent, instead of the projected rate of 39 percent. In the external sector, the real effective exchange rate (calculated on the basis of relative consumer prices) declined by some 35 percent and the competitiveness of the economy improved considerably. There was a significant pickup in tourism and exports of groundnuts and fish products, in particular, and a sizable reflow of private capital was recorded. In addition, a shift of consumption toward domestically produced products was reflected in a drop in the volume of imports. As a result, the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers), estimated at 9.2 percent of GDP in l994, was below the programmed level.  

4.  However, during the last months of the year, the Government again encountered difficulties in revenue collection. Furthermore, disbursements of external budgetary assistance fell CFAF 46 billion short of the projected amount, owing largely to delays in the negotiations with the World Bank on the private sector competitiveness (PAsco) and the agricultural sector adjustment (PASA) credits. As a consequence, the overall results under the program were mixed. As indicated in the attached table, all the performance criteria and the quantitative and structural benchmarks for end-September and end-December l994 under the ESAF arrangement were observed, with the exception of those related to the elimination and nonaccumulation of external payments arrears and to total government revenue.  

5. In the fiscal area, in 1994 the Government implemented a number of measures aimed at broadening the tax base and improving revenue collection, while strictly controlling expenditure, in order to contain the deficit of the central government financial operations, on a commitment basis and excluding grants, at 4.5 percent of GDP. Throughout l994, a very restrained spending stance was maintained, particularly on wages. The average increase in wages, which became effective in April l994, was limited to 10 percent, as programmed, and the benchmark on the wage bill was observed. Other current spending has been broadly as projected; the CFAF 15 billion budget allocation for social safety net measures was almost entirely utilized. In contrast, the deficit of the special accounts was larger than projected, primarily because of the National Retirement Fund (FNR). Investment expenditure was slightly lower than projected. Government receipts for the year as a whole fell short of the programmed amount by close to CFAF 26 billion (l percent of GDP). This shortfall was largely due to organizational weaknesses of the tax and customs administration, tax fraud, particularly at customs, as well as delays in the receipt of funds from fishing agreements. The overall fiscal deficit, on a commitment basis and excluding grants, thus reached 5.7 percent of GDP, exceeding the program target by about l percentage point. Verified domestic payments arrears were reduced in conformity with the performance criterion, but the external  

payments arrears could not be eliminated as programmed and amounted to CFAF 45.5 billion at end-l994.  

6. In the monetary area, credit policy was designed and coordinated within the broader context of the West African Economic and Monetary Union (WAEMU). Following the devaluation, the Central Bank's discount rate was raised on January l8, 1994 by 4 percentage points to 14.5 percent; subsequently, the moderation of inflation allowed a reduction of this rate in four steps, to 9 percent on January 23, l995. Starting in August 1994, the monetary authorities also proceeded with the securitization of the consolidated debt assumed by the Government in the context of the restructuring of the Senegalese banking system; these securities were purchased by local investors as well as by investors from other WAEMU countries. The money stock grew by 39 percent, more than projected, reflecting a strengthening of money demand and a return of confidence. All the benchmarks and performance criteria with respect to net credit to the Government and the net domestic assets of the banking system at end-September and end-December l994 were observed. Credit to the economy rose moderately in the second half of the year, in line with the pickup in economic activity, but remained well below what was envisaged under the program. The contribution of Senegal to the net foreign assets position of the Central Bank of West African States (BCEAO) improved much more strongly than foreseen.  

7.  Several structural measures included in the l994 program were implemented. Labor legislation was made more flexible: the monopoly of the Office of Port Personnel was abolished in July l994 and the labor code was revised in November 1994, ahead of schedule, to allow enterprises to freely adjust their work force according to their level of economic activity. The lifting of price controls proceeded as planned, with the liberalization in June l994 of the prices of six commodities (soap, milk, iron bars, soft drinks, instant coffee, and yeast) which had been frozen since the devaluation. Subsequently, in December l994, the Government also liberalized the prices of six additional commodities (fresh tomatoes, tomato concentrate, cement, sugar, vegetable oil, and groundnut oil), which had previously been administered or subject to the approval system (homologation). For some of these products, the prior authorizations for their import were eliminated following the renegotiation of the special agreements (conventions_speciales). The remaining prior authorizations for exports and imports have been eliminated, except those required for public health and security reasons, for the import of second-hand clothing and gold and silver bars, as well as for the export of precious metals and minerals. The renegotiation of general special agreements and protocols (protocoles d'accord) between the Government and a number of companies was carried out as planned. l/ Various reforms aimed at improving efficiency and encouraging diversification in the agricultural sector, including the rice and cotton sectors, have been implemented. All the measures planned in 1994 relating to the disengagement of the Caisse de péréquation et de stabilisation des prix (CPSP) from the rice sector were implemented. The restructuring of the Societé de developpement des fibres textiles (SODEFITEx) is being carried out in the context of its March l992 plan aimed at reducing its costs. The restructuring of other public enterprises is being carried out as planned. However, there have been some delays in the implementation of other structural measures. In particular, the offering for sale of the groundnut marketing enterprise (SONAcos) was delayed, in consultation with the donors concerned, even though a law authorizing its privatization was passed; the call for bids was postponed to May 1995. Moreover, the signing of a new performance contract for the Senegalese electricity company (SENELEC) planned for January 1995 was postponed to a later date in order to take into account the conclusions of a study on the reorganization and reduction of costs of the company, which began in March 1995. Finally, the civil service audit, planned to be completed before end1994, will only begin in April 1995 in light of delays encountered in the preparation of the terms of reference and the mobilization of external resources to finance the audit.  

8.Program for l995. In light of the developments in l994, the Government is determined to pursue resolutely its macroeconomic and structural reform policies described in the PFP, and to take the necessary measures to achieve the medium-term objectives set out in that document. The l995 objectives for growth and inflation are overall the same as those stipulated in the memorandum of July l, 1994, aiming to raise real GDP growth to close to 4.7 percent, and reduce inflation, as measured by the GDP deflator, to 7.8 percent. As for the external current account deficit, excluding official transfers, the objective is to reduce it from 9.2 percent of GDP in 1994 to 7.7 percent in l995, slightly below the initially programmed level. With the competitiveness of the economy restored, export receipts are expected to benefit in 1995 from the continued increase in export volumes of groundnuts and fish products, as well as from an increase in nontraditional exports such as horticultural products. This buoyancy of exports, as well as of tourism, should more than compensate for the recovery of imports that is expected as economic growth gathers steam. The balance of payments position in 1995 should also improve thanks to significant amounts of external financing associated with the implementation of development projects, support for structural reforms, and budgetary assistance. After covering the financing requirements, the external capital inflows should allow for a further strengthening of Senegal's net foreign assets position.  

9.With regard to the strengthening of the public finance situation, the Government has begun to implement the necessary measures to ensure an improved revenue collection and contain expenditure. The 1995 budget, whicl was approved by Parliament last December, aims at reducing the fiscal deficit, on a commitment basis and excluding grants, to 2.6 percent of GDP, in line with the medium-term program. At the end of l994, the stock of verified domestic payments arrears amounted to CFAF 14.9 billion, and the stock of external payments arrears to CFAF 45.5 billion. The projected budget deficit, as well as the elimination of these arrears by end-1995, will result, after taking into account disbursements of external budgetary assistance already provided during the first quarter (CFAF 2l billion), in an additional financing need of CFAF $104$ billion for the year as a whole. At present, external budgetary assistance of about CFAF 68 billion has already been identified, including debt relief, leaving an uncovered financing gap of some CFAF 36 billion. For the remaining part of the first six months of 1995 (corresponding to the second half of the first annual ESAF program), external budgetary assistance of CFAF 3l billion has been identified. This will fully cover the projected financing gap for that period, allowing for a reduction in domestic payments arrears of CFAF 10 billion, as originally programmed, and a reduction of CFAF l8 billion in outstanding external payments arrears by end-June 1995. The Government of Senegal is actively seeking additional external financing for the second half of the year to ensure that the financing gap for l995 as a whole will be fully covered. Senegal benefits from debt rescheduling with the Paris Club until end-March l995, and has requested from the Paris Club a multiyear rescheduling agreement for the rest of the period covered by the ESAF arrangement. Senegal would also seek comparable terms for debt relief from other non-Paris Club creditors. Progress has also been made in the IDAsupported commercial debt reduction operation, which is expected to be completed in the next few months.  

10. The Government recognizes that considerable efforts will be required to mobilize government receipts and to improve tax collection on a durable basis. After the unfavorable results of the previous year, total revenue in 1995 should increase by 23 percent, to reach CFAF 37l.2 billion. To that end, the Government has already adopted several measures within the context of the l995 budget and has decided to take additional measures, designed to expand the tax base and significantly reduce fiscal fraud. These measures aim in particular at reducing tax exemptions, broadening the value-added tax (VAT) base, and strengthening the customs and tax administrations. For the customs administration, the reorganization of the Direction Generale will be completed before April 15, 1995 on the basis of the new organization chart. In particular, regional directorates will be set up and staff will be redeployed to borders where control will be more strict. In addition,  

hierarchical control of customs operations will be strengthened, sufficient appropriations in personnel and materials for the unit in charge of customs investigations will be effected, and the computerized information system (GAINDE) will be improved and extended. In addition, the import verification program implemented with the help of an import verification agency will be reinforced; the notional bonded warehousing will be eliminated as public bonded warehouses are created. Finally, the fines and penalties in case of customs fraud will continue to be strictly applied and judicial procedures will be accelerated. Regarding the tax administration, a single registration system for enterprises will be initiated in April 1995, in order to allow the updating of taxpayers files. Moreover, a system of periodic and systematic controls of tax returns of companies subject to the VAT, and of customs declarations will be put in place by the tax and customs directorates. An information campaign will also be launched to promote tax compliance.  

1l. On the expenditure side, a very prudent policy will be pursued, particularly as regards the wage bill and nonpriority spending, while providing adequately for basic health and education services. In 1995, total expenditure will only increase by about 2 percent, to reach CFAF 434.9 billion. To keep the wage bill within the originally programmed ceiling of CFAF l6O billion, the adopted budget foresees no general wage increase in 1995. No such increase will be granted and recruitment will be strictly limited, giving priority to the health and education sectors. Moreover, the costs of regularizing the verified rappels (corresponding to the financial impact of promotions for which payment orders have not yet been issued) will be included within the above-mentioned wage bill. The increase in the wage bill in l995 should, therefore, be held to about 6 percent, reflecting the full-year impact of the l0 percent pay raise of last April, normal promotions, and the limited recruitment. The civil service audit will be initiated in April 1995 and the recommended measures will be put in place beginning in July 1995. Expenditure planned in the context of the social safety net (CFAF 10 billion) will be maintained, but will be integrated within the Government's social programs, and social spending will be targeted more effectively, in line with the recommendations of a recent IMF technical assistance mission. Any additional expenditure made necessary by the recent change in the Government will be compensated by reductions in other areas. The deficit of the special accounts will be substantially reduced by taking the measures required to balance the operations of the National Retirement Fund (FNR) from June 1995. The program for 1995 foresees that capital expenditure will amourt to CFAF 1l6 billion, with priority given to infrastructure and high-return projects in the productive sectors. The necessary instructions have been given to ensure that no extrabudgetary expenditures are made, and this will be respected by all ministries. Before end-September l995, a study on the expenditure system, as well as on the procedure for commitment and payment, will be completed, and the necessary measures will be implemented in the context of the 1996 budget.  

12. The authorities will pursue a cautious credit policy in l995, in line with the balance of payments objectives. The rate of growth of broad money should decelerate to less than 20 percent in l995. The expected improvement in the fiscal position should allow net credit to the Government to remain unchanged from its level of December l994, which will make room for adequate provision of credit to the private sector without causing the growth of the net domestic assets of the banking system to exceed 8 percent of the beginning-of-period money stock in l995. Monetary policy will continue to be based on the use of indirect instruments introduced by the BCEAO since October l993, in particular a flexible interest rate policy, reserve requirement ratios, and the expansion of the role of the money market. Monetary policy, within the WAEMU, will aim at maintaining positive real interest rates, to encourage savings and ensure sound credit allocation. Furthermore, the rehabilitation of the banking system will be continued: the agricultural bank (Caisse nationale de credit agricole du Senegal) will be recapitalized in 1995, and the establishment of local savings and loan banks will be encouraged. Finally, the monetary authorities have taken notice of some of the shortcomings of the securitization scheme for consolidated public debt launched in $1994$ (the fixed interest rate, and the tax exemption), and will seek, in collaboration with the other WAEMU members, to promote the expansion and development of a full-fledged securities market.  

13. A cautious external debt management policy will be pursued during 1995. In this regard, the Government will neither contract nor guarantee any external nonconcessional loans with maturities of less than l year, or between l and l5 years, with the exception of debt rescheduling or refinancing agreements and normal short-term import credits. The Government will not accumulate any new external payments arrears; all external payments arrears will be settled before end-December 1995.  

14. As regards structural measures, the relaxation of the regulatory environment will be continued, in order to create an enabling environment for the diversification of industrial production and the creation of small and medium-sized enterprises. The second phase of the price and trade liberalization program will be implemented; this envisages in particular the elimination of administered wholesale prices for rice by June l995, and the liberalization of wheat flour imports and prices by November l995. By April l5, 1995, the transport subsidy of the CPsP will be abolished in all regions, and CPsP will abandon its activities in the distribution of imported rice and close down its regional stores; its stores in Dakar will be closed by June l995 at the latest. CPSP's withdrawal from all rice import activity will become effective by February l996 at the latest, and the importation of broken rice will continue to be gradually taken over by the private sector. Furthermore, the offering for sale of SONAcoS will be launched in May 1995. The reform of the public enterprise sector will also be accelerated. The law allowing for the separation of the water company (SONEES) in a public and a private entity was approved by the National Assembly on March 24. Before end-June l995, the Government will take the necessary actions to finalize the privatization of two enterprises (SSPT and SPHU-Teranga) and SS-Saly will be liquidated. The restructuring of the electricity company (SENELEC) will be pursued. Finally, before end-December 1995 the Government will adopt, in consultation with the World Bank, a  

three-year public investment program for l996-98, giving priority to infrastructure and high-yielding projects in the directly productive sectors.  

15. The Government of Senegal believes that the policies.and measures set forth in this memorandum, which supplements the memorandum of July 1, 1994 are adequate to achieve the program objectives for 1995. It will take any additional measures that may become necessary to achieve these objectives. Finally, given that appropriate measures have been taken to strengthen revenue collection and to make up for delays in the implementation of certain structural measures, and that the external payments arrears are being settled, the Government of Senegal requests that the Fund grant waivers for the nonobservance of the performance criteria related to the nonaccumulation and elimination of external payments arrears set for endDecember 1994.  

Sincerely yours,  

Papa Ousmane Sakho Minister of Economy, Finance, and Planning

The representatives of l2 Paris Club creditors $11$ met on April 19 and 20, 1995 to consider Senegal's request to alleviate its external debt service obligations. They reached an agreement on a flow rescheduling for Senegal under Naples terms, involving a 67 percent reduction in net present value. The rescheduling covers the period through August 31, 1997, in line with the ESAF arrangement.  

The debt relief applies to: (i) commercial credits guaranteed or insured by, and loans from, the governments or appropriate institutions of the participating creditor countries, with an original maturity of more than one year, and which were extended to, or covered by a guarantee of, the Government of Senegal pursuant to an agreement concluded before January l, 1983; (ii) repayments of principal and interest due as a result of the consolidation agreement concluded according to the Agreed Minutes dated November 2l, 1986 and November 17, 1987; and (iii) repayments of principal and interest due as a result of the consolidation agreements concluded according to the Agreed Minutes dated January 24, 1989, February 12, 1990, and June 2l, 1991.  

The debt relief applies to l00 percent of principal and interest (including late interest) due and not paid as at March 3l, 1995 and to 100 percent of principal and interest (excluding late interest) falling due from April 1, 1995 to August 3l, 1997 and not paid on credits and loans specified above. For credits and loans specified in (i) and (ii) above, obligations other than those on official development assistance (ODA) loans will be rescheduled under one of the following two options (see Table 1): (a) Canada, France, and the United States opted to cancel 67 percent of the debt service obligations, with the remaining 33 percent to be consolidated on the basis of the appropriate market rates over a period of 23 years, including a grace period of 6 years; and (b) Brazil, Italy, Norway, Spain, and the United Kingdom opted to reschedule or refinance the amount eligible over 33 years with no grace period, on the basis of concessional interest rates, so as to reduce by 67 percent the net present value of the payments due (see Table 2). Debt service on ODA loans specified in (i) and (ii) above will be rescheduled or refinanced over a 40-year period, including a grace period of 16 years, on conditions and rates of interest at least as favorable as the concessional rates applying to those loans. These terms will also apply to the rescheduled debt owed to Denmark and Japan.  

For credits and loans specified in (iii) above, which have been previously rescheduled on Toronto terms, obligations will be rescheduled on the basis of the appropriate market rates and repaid in 16 graduated semiannual installments, starting on December 15, 1998 and ending on June 15, 2006. Arrears and current maturities rescheduled in $1994$ on London (enhanced concessional) terms were excluded from the rescheduling.  

The details of the rescheduling or refinancing arrangements will be determined in bilateral agreements between the Government of Senegal and the government or appropriate institutions of each of the participating creditor countries. The bilateral agreements are to be concluded with the least delays and, in any case, before November 30, 1995.  

The Government of Senegal also undertook to pay all debt service obligations due and not paid to the governments of the participating or observer countries, or their appropriate institutions, and not covered by the Agreed Minute as follows: (a) for the amount in principal and interest due on March 3l, l995, pursuant to the deferral of payments arrears as at December 3l, 1993 on credits and loans having an original maturity of more than one year pursuant to contracts concluded after January l, 1983, repayments will be made in six graduated installments starting on May 31, 1995 and ending on July l, 1997; and (b) all other amounts as soon as possible and, in any event, not later than September 30, 1995.  

The Government of Senegal will seek to secure from each creditor country not participating in the Agreed Minute rescheduling or refinancing arrangements on terms comparable to those set forth in the Agreed Minute, and will not accord any such creditor country repayment terms more favorable than those accorded to the participating creditor countries. The Government of Senegal also agreed to negotiate promptly rescheduling or refinancing arrangements with all other creditors on debts of a comparable term, and to inform in writing the Chairman of the Paris Club, not later than November 30, 1995, of the progress made in these negotiations.

# Commentary on lending operations  

1. Bank assistance strategy in Senegal is guided by four basic objectives: (i) promoting competitiveness and establishing the basis for self-sustained growth; (ii) supporting human resource development and sustainable management of natural resources; (iii) reducing poverty; and (iv) improving management capacity in the public sector. The vehicles to support these objectives have been adjustment lending buttressed by appropriate technical assistance operations and sector-based rehabilitation/investment projects.  

2. As of March 31, 1995, the World Bank Group had approved l04 operations in Senegal for a total amount committed, less cancellations, of ${\tt U S}\$1$ ,326.58 million, consisting of 72 IDA credits (including 3 special fund credits and two SFA), 20 Bank loans, and l2 IFC operations. Past projects supported agricultural diversification, institutional development, and expansion of the country's infrastructure, particularly its transport system. In recent years, emphasis has shifted to better utilization and maintenance of existing facilities, and on helping the Government resolve some of the key issues related to long-term development prospects. Ongoing or planned operations for agricultural extension and research, health, education, and transport sector reforms relate to both of these concerns.  

3. The World Bank Group has committed $\$43,2$ million thus far in fiscal year 1995. The Bank's lending program is projected to expand over the three-year period 1996-98. Overall, Senegal's economic data base is comprehensive, but there are weaknesses in the areas of national accounts, the consumer price index (CPI), international trade, and the balance of payments. The authorities are continuing their efforts to improve the quality and availability of data. They are making appropriate arrangements to ensure that the legal provisions requiring economic agents to report statistical information on a timely basis are enforced.  

# 1. Real sector  

The national accounts data suffer from some deficiencies in their compilation. Finalized data are based on returns from over l,o0o firms, a process that typically takes up to three years. For preliminary data, the accounts are based on a sample of 300 enterprises, and use an input-output model that was developed with French technical assistance. The CPI coverage is limited to Dakar, and is based on l967 weights. Currently, the CPI compilation is being improved with technical assistance from INSEE/EUROSTAT. The results of a household budgetary survey undertaken in l99l/92 are currently being processed, and they will be used to update the CPI weights. Trade data are incomplete due to the time taken by the customs directorate to report trade flows to the BCEAo, which may take up to three years.  

# 2. Public finances  

Data on public finances are collected by the Ministry of Finance on the basis of inputs from the Customs, the Tax and the Treasury directorates. Government finance statistics have not been reported by the authorities to the Statistics Department for many years. The latest data available for Senegal were published in the l990 GFSY and covered the consolidated central government budget through 1984.  

# 3. Monetary data  

Monetary data for Senegal are prepared by the National Agency of the BCEAO. Outstanding data problems include the lengthy time period needed to finalize the data, and some inconsistencies between the balance sheets of BCEAO and the commercial banks.  

# 4. Balance of payments data  

A technical assistance mission visited Dakar in 1992 and identified three shortcomings in balance of payments compilation procedures: (i) trade data were incomplete and reported with lengthy delays; (ii) bank settlements reports are not processed by the BCEAo; and (iii) surveys sent to companies were often inaccurately answered or unanswered. Efforts are being undertaken to address these issues. The enhanced computerization system of the customs should also help reduce delays in reporting.
