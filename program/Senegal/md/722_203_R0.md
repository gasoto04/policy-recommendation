# EXECUTIVE SUMMARY  

# Recent developments  

Senegal has made important progress in implementing its medium-term adjustment and reform strategy during the past three years. The 1995-96 adjustment program, supported by the second annual ESAF arrangement, remained broadly on track.  

The quantitative and structural benchmarks for end-December 1995 were observed, except for the benchmark on government revenue. All performance criteria and benchmarks for end-March 1996 were met, and the quantitative and structural benchmarks for end-June were observed as well.  

The fiscal targets for l996 are expected to be met. Government spending, especially on wages, has been restrained, and revenue collection during the first nine months of 1996 exceeded the program target.  

Most structural measures planned for 1995 were implemented, and several further measures were taken during the first half of 1996. There were, however, delays in the reform of the energy sector; the pace of implementation of the privatization program slowed during the year, but regained momentum with the successful privatization of a major enterprise late in the year.  

Economic growth in 1995 slightly exceeded the target and the growth objective for 1996 is also likely to be surpassed. Inflation through November 1996 decelerated to less than 1 percent. Senegal's improved competitiveness continued to support a further expansion of exports, while the growth of imports has remained moderate; the external current account deficit in 1995 narrowed as programmed and is expected to remain unchanged in 1996 on account of a number of temporary factors and higher petroleum prices.

# The 1996-97 program  

Within the framework of the medium-term adjustment strategy adopted in early 1994, the focus of the 1996-97 program, in support of which the authorities are requesting the third annual ESAF arrangement, will be on:  

Reinforcing macroeconomic policies to ensure a balanced budget by.1998. Fiscal revenue performance will be further improved through continued actions to strengthen the' customs and tax administrations, reduce fraud, and widen the tax base. Spending policy will remain very prudent; a moderate wage increase in l997, partly on account of a new system of merit-based salary adjustments that is to become effective in early 1997, will be accompanied by a freeze on net recruitment. The program also calls for a reduction in net credit to the government, and the maintenance of interest rates at levels such as to encourage financial savings and efficient credit allocation.  

Deepening and accelerating structural reforms, with emphasis on (a) improving the legal and regulatory environment; (b) accelerating public enterprise reform and privatization; and (c) strengthening reforms in the agricultural, transport, and energy sectors.  

Issues stressed in the staff appraisal  

The need to ensure full implementation of measures envisaged in the program and to take any additional actions that may be required.  

The urgency of improving government revenue performance substantially and durably.  

The necessity of persevering with overall government spending restraint, while continuing to increase the share of government expenditures devoted to basic health and education.  

The importance of ensuring the successful implementation of a market-driven, privatesector-based development strategy, including in particular the acceleration of the privatization program, the comprehensive reform of the energy sector, and the improvement of the judiciary system.  

The need to pursue a prudent credit policy and deepen financial intermediation.  

# 1. INTRODUCTION  

1. The government of Senegal has been implementing a comprehensive medium-term adjustment and reform program since the beginning of 1994. Initially, this program was supported by the Fund through a one-year stand-by arrangement approved in March 1994, soon after the realignment of the parity of the CFA franc. The stand-by arrangement was then replaced by a three-year arrangement under the enhanced structural adjustment facility (ESAF) approved in August 1994 in an amount equivalent to SDR 130.8 million (110 percent of quota). Alloan disbursements provided under the first and second annual ESAF arrangements were effected.  

2. In the attached letter to the Managing Director dated November 7, 1996, the Minister of Economy, Finance, and Planning of Senegal requests the third annual ESAF arrangement in an amount equivalent to SDR 35.7 million (30 percent of quota).1 The requested use of ESAF resources would support Senegal's adjustment policies described in the policy memorandum annexed to the letter and in the updated policy framework paper (PFP) for 1996-99 (EBD/96/164, 12/24/96), which was prepared by the authorities in close collaboration with the staffs of the Fund and the World Bank. The authorities have indicated their intention to publish the PFP.  

3. If the disbursements under the third annual ESAF arrangement, as well as all repurchases and loan repayments, are made as scheduled, Senegal's use of Fund resources would increase from SDR 239.6 million at the end of September 1996 to SDR 24l.2 million (202.9 percent of quota) by the end of June 1997 (Appendix I, Table 1). Senegal's relations with the Fund are summarized in Appendix IV.  

4. The World Bank has been supporting Senegal's development efforts through structural and sectoral adjustment lending, including a private sector adjustment and competitiveness credit of SDR 27.6 million approved in February 1995, and an agricultural sector adjustment credit of SDR 29 million approved in June 1995. Discussions are continuing on medium-term reform programs in the energy, transport, and health sectors. Senegal's relations with the World Bank Group are summarized in Appendix V.  

'Discussions on the program to be supported by the third annual ESAF arrangement were held in Dakar during the period October 21 to November 7, 1996. The Senegalese representatives included Mr. Sakho, the Minister of Economy, Finance, and Planning; Mr. Loum, the Minister Delegate in charge of the Budget; Mr. N'Diaye, the National Director of the Central Bank of West African States (BCEAO); and other senior government officials. President Diouf and Prime Minister Thiam also met with members of the mission. The staff representatives were Mr. Francois (head), Mr. Coe, Mr. De Vrijer, Ms. Fouad, Mr. Lewis, Mr. Lazar, and Mrs. Culp, administrative assistant (all AFR). Mr. Thiam, the Fund's resident representative in Senegal, assisted the mission. A World Bank staff team participated in the discussions on the PFP.  

5. The social and political situation in Senegal has generally remained stable, although progress has been slow in resolving the conflict in the southern Casamance region. The ruling party won the majority of races in the local, municipal, and regional elections on November 24, 1996. If there is a change of government following the elections, the new government's commitment to the economic adjustment and reform program is expected, if anything, to be strengthened.  

# II. ADJUSTMENT AND STRUCTURAL REFORM, 1994-96  

6. The objectives of the government's medium-term adjustment strategy are to restore domestic and external financial viability, establish the conditions for strong and sustainable economic growth, and reduce poverty. Fiscal and monetary policies have focused on the achievement of macroeconomic stability through rapid control of inflation and budget deficits. Structural policies have aimed at raising Senegal's medium-term growth potential by reducing the size and scope of the public sector, liberalizing the economy, and fostering the development of a dynamic private sector. From the beginning, the strategy has emphasized improved competitiveness and enhanced regional cooperation and integration through the zone-wide correction of the parity of the CFA franc in January 1994, and the simultaneous signing of the treaty establishing the West African Economic and Monetary Union (WAEMU).  

7. The improvement in Senegal's economic and financial situation since 1994 indicates that the authorities' medium-term strategy has been appropriate; and their implementation of the strategy thus far has been encouraging. The macroeconomic objectives of the first two annual ESAF-supported programs were largely achieved, and in some cases surpassed (Appendix I, Table 2 and Chart 1).² Growth has recovered sharply from the stagnation of the early 1990s, although weaknesses in data collection and in the compilation of the national accounts make it difficult to be precise about the extent of the recovery. Despite a decline in agricultural production, real GDP is estimated to have expanded by almost 5 percent in 1995, with strong increases in private investment and industrial output (Appendix I, Table 9). Output is expected to rise further, by slightly more than 5 percent in l996, as private construction remained strong and agricultural production increased substantially. Inflation, as measured by the 12-month change in the consumer price index for Dakar, was reduced from a high of 37 percent at the end of 1994 to $5\%$ percent in 1995, and fell further to less than 1 percent in November 1996. These encouraging developments were accompanied by an appreciable upturn in domestic saving in 1994 and l995, which, together with the improved competitiveness of the Senegalese economy, was reflected in a reduction in the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) from $10\%$ percent of GDP in 1993 to

8. A central element of the government's adjustment program has been a substantially improved fiscal performance, although there were some revenue shortfall and expenditure overruns in the first two years of the program. The overallbudget-deficit (on a commitment basis and excluding grants) was reduced from almost 6 percent of GDP in 1994 to 31/4 percent in 1995 (Appendix I, Table 3). Measures were implemented during 1994-95 to broaden the tax base, strengthen tax administration, reorganize the customs department, and combat fiscal fraud. These reforms alowed the government to increase budgetary revenues from 14 percent of GDP in 1994 to more than 15 percent in l995, without recourse to higher tax rates (Appendix I, Table 4). Government spending was kept in check, while increased allocations were provided for education and health services. The government's wage bill was contained by limiting recruitment and by not granting a general wage increase in 1995. Verified domestic and external payments arrears were eliminated by the end of 1995.  

9. Fiscal performance improved further in 1996, and all program targets are expected to be met. The government's overall budget deficit (on a commitment basis and excluding grants) is projected to be reduced to slightly less than 2 percent of GDP, lowering substantially the need for exceptional external resources compared with previous years. Income tax and nonpetroleum VAT revenues have been higher than expected, reflecting a further strengthening of tax administration. Most of this improvement, however, was offset by a significant shortfall in petroleum revenues because domestic prices were not adjusted to reflect the rise in world market prices. Assuming the collection of privatization revenues of CFAF 10 bilion in December 1996, which now appears likely (cf. paragraph 16 below), the government is expected to achieve its revenue objectives for the year. At the same time, government expenditures, including the wage bil, are expected to be in line with program targets and increased resources have again been devoted to basic health and education services. Most of the cross debts between public enterprises and the government will be settled by the end of 1996. Exceptional budgetary assistance from the World Bank and the European Union is likely to be somewhat higher than previously expected.  

10. As in the other member countries of the West African Economic and Monetary Union, monetary policy in Senegal is determined at the regional level by the BCEAO. Broad money growth fell sharply in 1995, reflecting the adjustment of cash balances to declining inflation (Appendix I, Tables 5-6). Net credit to the government was reduced, as the government's fiscal position strengthened. Credit to the private sector expanded moderately, reflecting the improved liquidity of enterprises and their correspondingly limited recourse to bank financing. Accordingly, the net domestic assets of the Central Bank remained below the applicable ceiling. In the context of these developments, the contribution of Senegal to the net foreign asset position of the BCEAO continued to increase during 1995.  

11. The monetary aggregates have been stable in l996: broad money increased by a mere 2 percent during the first half of the year and is expected to remain virtually unchanged during the second half. Net domestic assets of the banking system grew moderately during the first half of the year, as a nearly 10 percent expansion of credit to the private sector, mainly to finance construction activities and food imports, was partially offset by a larger-than-expected decrease in net bank credit to the government stemming from the improved financial position of the government. During the second half of the year, credit to the private sector is expected to increase only marginally and credit to the government should drop slightly, leaving the net domestic assets of the banking system broadly unchanged from the June 1996 level. Net foreign assets of the banking system are also expected to increase only moderately in 1996.  

12. With the decline in inflation, the discount rate of the BCEAO, which stood at 14.5 percent in January 1994, was graduately lowered to 7.5 percent at the end of 1995 and to 6.5 percent at the end of October 1996. Commercial interest rates have also declined, with the prime rate charged by banks faling to about 10 percent in October. Average spreads between lending and deposit rates widened in 1995 and have remained high, reflecting the need for banks to make provision for bad loans, the high cost of recovering claims, and, more generally, the relatively underdeveloped financial sector with few market-determined financial instruments.3  

13. The banking system in Senegal remains relatively sound and most banks have been complying with the prudential ratios set by the regional banking commission. The banking commission is currently carrying out audits of two banks that had been experiencing difficulties, as well as audits of two financial institutions; the authorities intend to ensure that its forthcoming recommendations are put in place. The Societe Nationale de Recouvrement, the loan recovery institution established following the banking sector restructuring in 1989-91, had recovered CFAF 35.4 bilion in outstanding loans by the end of September 1996, against a target of CFAF 54.2 billion set in 1991. All deposits of individuals and small and mediumsized enterprises held in the banks that were liquidated have now been reimbursed.  

14. Important and wide-ranging structural reforms have been implemented in the past three years (Box 1). In 1994-95, the government (i) undertook a far-reaching liberalization of prices and of domestic and foreign trade, including the elimination of administrative barriers to imports and exports and the renegotiation of special agreements protecting some enterprises from competition; (ii) liberalized international maritime transport; (ii) lowered customs duties and simplified the tariff structure; (iv) amended the labor code to introduce greater flexibility in the labor market; and (v) eased the regulatory framework. In the agricultural sector, a number of measures were put in place to reduce the role of the state. The government pressed ahead with structural reforms in 1996, notably in the areas of judicial and administrative reform and improving incentives and diversification in the agricultural sector.

15.The government has aiso begun to implement a broad program to restructure the public enterprise sector with the ultimate goal of withdrawing the state from commercial activities. This program, which is being carried out in several stages, includes the liquidation of nonviable public enterprises, the privatization of enterprises in which the state is the majority shareholder, the sale of the state's equity holdings in enterprises where it is the minority shareholder, and the strengthening of the performance of enterprises remaining in the state's portfolio through measures such as the privatization of the management of enterprises. The privatization program got off to a promising start, with the number of public enterprises reduced from about 90 in 1989 to 60 at the end of 1995, although most of these were small, and in many cases nonviable, enterprises: the government's holding in the capitalization of the public enterprise sector was only reduced by about 8 percent from 1989 to 1995. By the end of 1995, only one major enterprise, the water company (SONEES), had been privatized.  

16. Calls for bids were launched in 1995 and 1996 for the sale of the Hotel Meridien and a major portion of the capital of four important enterprises: Societé Nationale de Commercialisation des Oleagineux (SONACOS, the national oilseed marketing company) Societe Nationale des Telecommunications (SONATEL), Societe Senegalaise des Phosphates de Thies (SSPT), and Societé Nationale de Transport Aerien (SONATRA--Air Senegal). The privatization of these enterprises was delayed, however, partly because the authorities did not consider the technical and financial features of the bids to be satisfactory. This was particularly the case for SONACOS and the Hotel Meridien. The government renewed its efforts later in the year, and successfully concluded the sale of one-third of SONATEL in late November 1996. SONACOS, SSPT, and SONATRA are expected to be privatized in early 1997.  

17. There have also been delays in the reform of the energy sector, reflecting both difficulties at the technical level and strong resistance from trade unions and other interests. Reforms are needed to improve efficiency and lower production costs, especially electricity costs, which are among the highest in the region. Specific actions include the privatization of the electricity company (SENELEC) and the elimination of the special convention and other privileges to the Societe Africaine de Raffinage (SAR, the oil refining company). Energy sector reforms are also necessary to safeguard government revenues by adjusting domestic retail prices to reflect changes in world oil prices.  

# III. MEDIUM-TERM ADJUSTMENT STRATEGY AND PROGRAM FOR 1997  

# A. Strategy and Objectives for 1997-99  

18. Building on the successful adjustment efforts of the past three years, the government intends to continue to implement policies within the framework of the medium-term adjustnent strategy adopted in l994, with the objective of establishing the basis for strong, sustainable, and equitable economic growth led by a dynamic private sector. This is to be accomplished by consolidating macroeconomic and financial stability through sound demand management policies and by deepening and accelerating structural reforms in several areas, including the judiciary system, decentralization of the government, and regional economic integration (Box 2). A detailed list of the macroeconomic and structural adjustment policies designed to achieve these objectives, together with a timetable for their implementation, is set out in the matrix attached to the PFP; the key policy measures are summarized in Box 3.  

19. The medium-term macroeconomic objectives are to achieve average economic growth of at least $4\%$ percent a year, allowing for annual increases in real per capita incomes of at least $1\%$ percent; maintain low and stable inflation at 2-3 percent, compatible with the fixed exchange rate; and reduce the external current account deficit, excluding official transfers, to about $5\%$ percent of GDP by 1999.  

20. The key fiscal objectives are to eliminate the overall budget deficit (on a commitment basis and excluding grants) by 1998, with modest surpluses thereafter, while at the same time continuing to reorient government expenditures to emphasize public investment and basic health and education services. These goals will be accomplished by further measures to contain low-priority expenditures and to increase the productivity of tax administration in order to raise the ratio of government revenue to GDP by about $\%$ percentage point a year to a level of $16\%$ percent by 1999. The consistent and credible implementation of sound macroeconomic and structural policies, together with the improved competitiveness of the Senegalese economy, is expected to contribute to a marked rise in private investment and domestic saving, with the latter increasing to more than 14'/ percent of GDP by 1999.  

21. The government's social and environmental objectives are to reduce poverty, strengthen human resource development, and improve the management of natural resources (paragraphs 35-39 of the PFP). The authorities are implementing measures to raise enrollment rates in primary education and to reduce adult illiteracy, with particular emphasis on the female population in both cases. The government plans to expand the availability of health services, place higher priority on preventative health care, and promote greater use of generic drugs to reduce pharmaceutical costs. The authorities recognize that rapid increases in population constrain the improvement of living standards, and intend to outline a set of policies aimed at reducing the rate of population growth. The government has also outlined a number of major environmental initiatives in the areas of the conservation of soils, the sustainable use of forestry resources, the control of urban polution, and waste management.

22. A central element of the government's strategy for reducing poverty is higher economic growth resulting from improved competitiveness and strengthened incentives for investment. The special three-year social safety net put in place in 1994 to address the adverse effects of the devaluation on the poorest and most vulnerable groups has progressively been integrated into the government's social programs. In 1996, expenditures on the social safety net fell because price subsidies on basic commodities declined with liberalization. Although recent indicators of poverty or income distribution are not available, the devaluation of the CFA franc and the resulting shift in the internal terms of trade appear to have boosted incomes in rural areas. Between 1993 and 1996, for example, it is estimated that the value of agricultural output, adjusted for the rise in the GDP deflator, increased by about 40 percent (Appendix I, Table 10).4 Given that the agricultural sector in Senegal is dominated by small landholders, the large increase in the real value of agricultural output, which would account for most of the gross incomes of Senegalese farmers, suggests that living standards of Senegal's rural poor have improved during the past three years.  

23. The authorities believe that the macroeconomic adjustment policies and structural reforms that have been implemented during the past three years, together with those planned for 1997-99, will boost the medium-term growth potential of the economy above the 4/2 percent level. For this to occur, the government will have to move rapidly to make up for recent delays in implementing key reforms in order to reestablish momentum for the adjustment and reform program and foster a strengthening of private sector confidence and investment. It will also be necessary to implement planned reforms in the areas of education, health, and the judicial system as soon as possible, since it will take a number of years for these reforms to have an appreciable effect on economic growth.

Business laws are being harmonized at the regional level to encourage integration and trade. # C. Medium-Term Balance of Payments Outlook and Debt Sustainability  

30. The medium- and long-term balance of payments projections and debt sustainability analysis presented in EBS/96/92 have been updated to 2015 on the basis of the most recent available information and the latest World Economic Outlook projections (Appendix III). The World Bank staff agrees with the updated debt sustainability analysis. In the absence of major terms of trade or other exogenous shocks, and provided that the sound macroeconomic and structural policies described above continue to be implemented rigorously, the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) is expected to decrease steadily as the economy continues to benefit from improved competitiveness, allowing Senegal to strengthen its  

external position and reach external viability over the medium term (Appendix I, Tables 7-8). The terms of trade are expected to improve slightly in l997 and they are assumed to remain virtually unchanged from 1998 to 2001. Exports are projected to pick up strongly, with export volume growth reaching almost 6 percent in 1997 and then declining somewhat, to about 4/4 percent a year by 200l, reflecting the continued expansion of traditional exports as well as non-traditional exports such as horticulture, agro-industrial products, and textiles. The projected strength of traditional exports stems from groundnuts as well as fish products-- which are expected to benefit from the compliance to international standards--and chemicals, with an expected doubling of the production of Industries chimiques du Senegal (ICS) after its merger with the phosphate company (CSPT). Import volume growth is projected to average 33/4 percent a year over the 1997-200l period, with imports of intermediate and capital goods growing more rapidly than consumption goods.  

31. On the basis of the program objectives outlined above, Senegal will continue to rely heavily on concessional external assistance to finance investment projects included in the three-year public investment plan. After taking into account projected disbursements of project aid, private capital inflows, and Fund assistance under the ESAF arrangement, the remaining financing requirements for the three years from July 1996 to June 1999 are expected to be covered by multilateral and bilateral aid and debt relief. For 1997, the needed external assistance totals CFAF 52 billion. Most of this is expected to be covered by CFAF 2 billion in assistance from multilateral institutions (World Bank and European Union), CFAF 26 billion in official bilateral assistance,” and about CFAF 18 billion in debt relief already obtained from the Paris Club and other official bilateral creditors. The remaining financing gap of CFAF 6 billion for the last four months of 1997 is expected to be covered by additional debt relief to be obtained from the Paris Club in the context of a stock-of-debt operation, including the topping up of debt previously rescheduled on Toronto terms, envisaged before the end of 1997. In subsequent years, the financing gaps are expected to be covered by debt relief from such a stock-of-debt operation, as well as comparable debt relief by other bilateral creditors.

The operation will be financed by a grant from the IDA Debt Reduction Facility, contributions from bilateral donors, as well as a modest contribution from the government's own resources (less than 2 percent of quota). The amount eligible under the operation is US\$118 million (including US\$75 million in principal). The bonds will be transferable to banks and financial and nonfinancial institutions; they will also be convertible for investments in public enterprises, including privatization, and can be used to pay taxes and duties owed to the state in l997 and 1998.  

33. Three alternative scenarios were prepared to illustrate the sensitivity of the baseline projections for the external current account balance to alternative assumptions for the terms of trade. The first scenario shows that the external current account deficit would deteriorate by almost 1 percentage point of GDP by 2001 if world prices of groundnut products, which represented 11 percent of total exports in 1995, were 10 percent lower on average than assumed in the baseline. In the second scenario, the external current account deficit would widen by 1'/2 percentage points of GDP by 2001 if the price of fish exports (28 percent of exports in 1995) were 10 percent lower on average than in the baseline. Finally, the current account would widen by more than 1 percentage point of GDP by 200l if the average price of petroleum products (10 percent of imports in 1995) were 10 percent higher than in the baseline.  

34. Senegal's updated debt sustainability analysis suggests that with continued implementation of sound macroeconomic policies and prudent debt management, and with recourse to concessional financing, the debt service ratios are likely to fall below the critical thresholds used as eligibility criteria under the heavily indebted poor countries (HIPC) debt initiative. Total debt service would decline from about 19 percent of exports of goods and nonfactor services in 1996 to 14 percent in 200l, and to 8 percent in 2006 (Appendix III). The net present value of the debt stock would decline from 136 percent of exports of goods and nonfactor services in 1996 to 88 percent in 200l, and to 55 percent in 2006. The ratio of debt service to government revenue--at about 39 percent in 1996, 25 percent in 200l, and 14 percent in 2006--would remain relatively high, however. Repayments to the Fund are projected to peak at 4 percent of exports of goods and nonfactor services in 1997 and decline thereafter. This, together with the expected improvement in Senegal's balance of payments and fiscal positions, and given its good record of repayments to the Fund, should enable Senegal to continue to meet its future obligations to the Fund in a timely manner.

# IV. STAFF APPRAISAL  

36. Senegal has made important progress in the implementation of its medium-term adjustment and reform strategy during the past three years. The fruits of the strategy are apparent in the strengthening of economic growth, the increase in rural incomes, the successful containment of inflation, the improvement in government finances, the reduction in the external current account deficit, and the associated decline in the need for exceptional external financing. These improvements reflect the appropriately restrictive macroeconomic policies that have been pursued, as wellas the comprehensive structural reforms to liberalize the economy and promote the role of the private sector. The economic situation remains fragile, however, with the agricultural and fishing sectors, and the economy more generally, vulnerable to adverse weather conditions or other shocks.  

37. In general, the overallimplementation of the 1995-96 program supported by the second annual ESAF arrangement was satisfactory. There have, however, been delays in the reform of the energy sector, and the pace of implementation of the privatization program slowed during 1996, although it regained momentum later in the year. Slow progress in energy reform and privatization may retard the development of a dynamic private sector and the improvement in private sector confidence and investment, with important implications throughout the economy. Furthermore, the progress made toward financial viability is still fragile, given the slow improvement in government revenue mobilization, the vulnerability of revenues to developments in world energy prices, and the strong reliance on expenditure reduction.  

38. The coming year will be crucial for the government to establish full credibility for its privatization and structural reform programs, and to consolidate the financial situation. There are four key areas where the staff believes the authorities should focus their efforts:  

Improve government revenue performance. It is essential to raise the ratio of government revenue to GDP above the current level of about 15 percent in order to finance higher expenditures in priority areas such as health, education, and public investment, which are necessary to boost future growth prospects. The authorities should continue to give high priority to measures that will enhance tax compliance, reduce fiscal fraud, strengthen customs and tax administration, and widen the tax base. In this context, the staff urges the authorities to undertake a major reform of investment tax incentives by removing distortions in the current investment code. Measures to enhance domestic resource mobilization are particularly important to ofset possible short-term revenue shortfalls associated with the introduction of a common external tariff in January 1998 and other actions undertaken in the context of the WAEMU. The adoption of a mechanism to adjust consumer energy prices to reflect changes in world petroleum prices is also urgent in view of the revenue shortfalls experienced in 1996 and the risks of further shortfalls in coming years.  

Persevere with overall government spending restraint, while continuing to increase the share of government expenditures devoted to basic health and education. Although expenditures were successfully contained in 1996--which was particularly noteworthy in light of spending pressures associated with the campaign for the November 24 elections--continued efforts will be needed to restrain the government wage bill and reduce low-priority spending in order to increase investments in infrastructure and human resources. The staf welcomes the steps recently taken to replace the system of automatic wage increases by increases based on merit and to introduce an innovative system of compensation for contract teachers.  

Ensure the successful implementation of a market-driven, private sector based development strategy. It wil be particularly important to accelerate the privatization program and embark on a comprehensive energy sector reform program, including reform of the taxation of petroleum products and the mechanism for adjusting consumer energy prices. Concomitantly, greater emphasis will have to be placed on enhancing the functioning of the judiciary system.  

Pursue a prudent credit policy and deepen financial intermediation. The indirect monetary instruments of the BCEAO need to be improved to ensure that interest rates are market determined. To improve access to credit in Senegal, especialy in rural areas, the financial system needs to be modernized through better prudential and regulatory supervision, and broadened through the development of savings banks, credit unions, and leasing institutions. The creation of a regional financial market is also important.  

39. The authorities have reaffirmed at the highest level their commitment to continued implementation of the adjustment program, including an acceleration of reforms in politically sensitive areas. The program objectives are attainable, and the authorities have demonstrated their capacity to implement ambitious programs during the past three years. Nevertheless, there are important risks, notably developments in the terms of trade and internal political and social pressures. The government should therefore stand ready to take any additional measures that may be required by changing circumstances.  

40.If the policies and measures envisaged under the program continue to be implemented as planned, the illustrative scenarios prepared by the staff indicate that Senegal could reach a sustainable external and fiscal position in the near future provided that substantial, albeit  

declining, assistance on concessional terms can be mustered and that a comprehensive stock of debt operation is granted on Naples or similar terms before the end of 1997.  

41. In light of the progress made thus far and the strength of Senegal's proposed adjustment program, the staff recommends approval by the Executive Board of the authorities' request for the third annual arrangement under the ESAF.

# SENEGAL  

# Memorandum on Economic and Financial Policies for 1996-97  

November 7, 1996  

# I. Introduction  

1. Since January 1994, the government of Senegal has been implementing a comprehensive medium-term adjustment and reform strategy aimed at restoring domestic and external financial viability, reestablishing the conditions necessary for strong and sustainable economic growth, and improving the living standards of the population, with a particular emphasis on poverty reduction. This strategy, which is based on the realignment of the parity of the CFA franc, has focused on the strict management of demand in order to quickly gain control of inflation and fiscal deficits, as well as expediting structural reforms aimed at liberalizing the economy, reducing the size of the public sector, and fostering the development of a dynamic private sector. This strategy is consistent with the new opportunities for regional integration created by the signing of the treaty establishing the West African Economic and Monetary Union (WAEMU) on January 10, 1994. The strategy is being pursued within a framework characterized by the adherence to the rule of law, the deepening of democracy, increased decentralization, and strengthening of the consultations between the government, the private sector, and the labor unions. The implementation of Senegal's new strategy has been supported by the international community, particularly the International Monetary Fund, the World Bank, the European Union, and several bilateral partners which have also granted debt relief for the period extending through end-August 1997.  

2.As a result of the rigorous execution of the programs adopted in the context of the first two annual arrangements under the enhanced structural adjustment facility (ESAF), Senegal's economic and financial situation has improved significantly, and considerable progress has been made in implementing far-reaching structural reforms. Thus, real GDP increased by 2 percent in 1994 and by 4.8 percent in 1995, following virtual stagnation over.the three preceding years. The inflation rate, as measured by the change in the consumer price index for African households in Dakar, was brought down, on a year-to-year basis, to 5.5 percent at end-1995, after peaking at 37 percent in 1994. Despite the fact that nontax revenue fellshort of the initial objective, the overall fiscal deficit (on a commitment basis and excluding grants) was reduced to around 3 percent of GDP in 1995, and all verified domestic and external payments arrears were eliminated. These encouraging developments were accompanied by an appreciable pickup in investment and especially in domestic savings, which made it possible to reduce the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) from 10.3 percent of GDP in 1993 to 8 percent in 1995. Furthermore, the upturn in foreign assistance and the pursuit of a prudent  

monetary policy led to a substantial improvement in official foreign assets, which reached the equivalent of 68 percent of base money at end-1995, compared with barely l percent at end1993.  

3. Important structural reforms were implemented in 1994 and 1995 to take full advantage of the realignment of the parity of the CFA franc, by creating an environment conducive to expanded production and private sector development. Thus, the government undertook (a) a far-reaching liberalization of prices and domestic and foreign trade, including.the.elimination of administrative barriers to imports and exports, the dismantling of most monopolies, and the renegotiation of special agreements protecting certain enterprises from competition; (b) the liberalization of international maritime transportation; and (c) the streamlining of the regulatory framework governing economic activities. The government also lowered customs duties, simplified the tariff structure, and introduced major tax reforms leading to an in-depth restructuring of the tax system. Provisions of the Labor Code were revised to introduce greater flexibility in the labor market, particularly by eliminating the need to obtain prior authorization for layoffs on economic grounds. In the agricultural sector, where 60 percent of the Senegalese population is active, measures have been taken to ensure the state's gradual withdrawal from the sector and to transfer management of its various branches to farmers and other private transactors. The rice sector in particular was liberalized, inter alia by liquidating the Caisse de Péréquation et de Stabilisation des Prix (CPSP) in December 1995.  

4. In line with the structural policies described above, which are aimed primarily at enhancing the private sector's role in economic growth and refocusing the role of the state on the area of human development, the government has endeavored to accelerate the implementation of a wide-ranging program to restructure the parapublic sector, ultimately aimed at government withdrawal from the commercial sector of the economy. This program, which will be carried out in several stages after discussions with the social partners, includes the liquidation of public enterprises deemed to be nonviable, the privatization of enterprises in which the state is the majority shareholder, the sale of the state's shares in enterprises where the state is the minority shareholder, as wellas the rehabilitation of the financial situation of those enterprises remaining in the state's portfolio. During the period 1989-93, the number of public enterprises was reduced to 45; in addition, there were 18 enterprises in which the state held a minority stake. For the period 1994-96, the government's objective was to privatize or liquidate 18 enterprises, excluding the sale of various public assets. In 1994-95, the management of a major enterprise responsible for water production and distribution (SONEES) was opened to the private sector and two enterprises were liquidated (CPSP and SIAS).  

# II. Implementation of the Program in 1996  

5. The maintenance of rigorous macroeconomic policies and the pursuit of structural reforms should enable Senegal to achieve its main objectives for l996 and to surpass them in several areas, particularly with respect to economic growth. On the whole, structural measures have been implemented in accordance with the program, although delays were recorded in the privatization of public enterprises and in the liberalization of the energy sector. All of the. performance criteria and quantitative and structural benchmarks, as defined in the memorandum of October 12, 1995 and in the letter of May 15, 1996 had been observed at end-March and end-June 1996 (see attached Table 1). The indicators for end-September and end-December 1996 are also expected to be observed.  

6. Despite a significant deterioration in the terms of trade, the sustained economic growth recorded in 1995 continued and even accelerated slightly in 1996, with a real GDP growth of more than 5 percent (compared with the initial projection of 4.5 percent). Benefiting from favorable weather conditions, agricultural production increased by more than ll percent, which had a substantial positive efect on the industrial and trade sectors. Production in the phosphates and fertilizer sectors as well as in the maritime fishing sector slowed down, however, as a result of the temporary disruptions due to the merger of Industries Chimiques du Senegal (ICS) and the Compagnie Senégalaise des Phosphates de Taiba (CSPT), and the need to comply with international standards governing the fish canning industry. In contrast, activity in the private construction sector was extremely vigorous. The strong growth in this sector largely reflects the sustained recovery of private investment, although the latter's share of GDP (approximately 11.5 percent in 1996) remains modest. The increase in final consumption paralled the rise in nominal GDP, entailing the stabilization of the domestic savings rate. The slowdown of production in the extractive industries and technical delays in the shipment of groundnut products resulted in a smaller-than-expected increase in exports. As the expansion of domestic demand was also accompanied by a surge in imports, particularly of rice, and a major upswing in the prices of petroleum products, the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) remained unchanged at 8 percent of GDP.  

7.In 1996, inflation continued to decline, reaching an annual rate of 3.2 percent at endSeptember 1996, compared with 5.3 percent at end-September 1995. However, there are still some pressures on the price of rice, notwithstanding the accumulation of sizable rice stocks as a result of massive imports by private transactors following the liberalization of the rice sector. These pressures reflect the persistence of rigidities in the distribution channels, primarily at the retail stage.  

8.On the fiscal front, the main objectives of the program for 1996 are expected to be acheved. Accordingly, the overall budget deficit (on a commitment basis and excluding grants) would be brought down fom 3.2 percent of GDP in 1995 to around 2 percent in 1996. The increase in total government revenue should be broadly in line with program projections, notwithstanding sizable shortfalls in petroleum revenue as a result of the increase in the international prices of petroleum products since August and the delay in adjusting retail prices. These revenue shortfalls should be offset, however, by better-than-expected results in the area of income tax and the VAT (excluding petroleum), primarily on account of productivity gains attributable to measures aimed at strengthening the tax administration, implemented during the year in accordance with the provisions of the program. The collction of privatization receipts ir the amount of CFAF 10 billion by December 1996 will be essential to achieve the revenue objectives for the year. Expenditure, on the other hand, is expected to be kept within the envisaged limits, particularly as regards the wage bill and other current expenditure. Capital expenditure is estimated to be in line with projections, despite a delay in the mobilization of an exceptional project loan. Other exceptional budgetary assistance may exceed the initially programmed amount owing to possible additional disbursements from the World Bank and the European Union at year-end.  

9. The evolution of monetary aggregates in 1996 was characterized by remarkable stability. Afer increasing by 7.7 percent in 1995, broad money grew by a mere 2 percent in the first half of 1996 and is expected to remain virtually unchanged through the remainder of the year. The net domestic assets of the banking system grew modestly during the first half of the year. Credit to the private sector expanded by nearly 10 percent, particularly on account of the financing of construction activities and food imports, while net bank credit to the government recorded a larger-than-projected decrease as a resuit of the improvement in the government's financial position. For the second half of the year, net domestic assets are projected to remain broadly unchanged from their June level, reflecting a very limited increase in credit to the private sector and the government. At year-end, the level of bank credit to the government willnevertheless be higher than foreseen in May 1996, owing to the delay in mobilizing a substantial external loan. Reflecting these various developments, the net foreign assets of the banking system would increase moderately in 1996.  

10.In 1996, the government continued implementing the various structural reforms initiated in 1994-95 and embarked upon the preparation of further reforms to be implemented over the period 1997-99, with assistance from the World Bank. The principal reforms under way aim at: (a) strengthening the incentives for promoting and diversifying agricultural production; (b) consolidating the process of public enterprise privatization; (c) improving the legal and regulatory environment for enterprises; and (d) developing human resources through the expansion of basic education and primary health care. Overall, these programs are largely on track. In the area of streamlining the parapublic sector, account should be taken of the privatization of water production and distribution becoming effective in April 1996 and the calls for bids for the sale of a major share in the capital of four enterprises: the Societe Nationale de Commercialisation des Oléagineux (SONACOS), the Societé Nationale des Telecommunications (SONATEL), the Societe Senegalaise des Phosphates de Thies (SSPT), and the Societé Nationale de Transport Aerien (SONATRA--Air Senegal). Delays have occurred in the process of privatizing a number of entities, however, mainly on account of the difficulties encountered in obtaining bids that are satisfactory from both the technical and the financial standpoints. This is notably the case for SONACOS and the Hotel Meridien. The government was thus compelld to redraft the bidding specifications and to strengthen contacts with prospective buyers. It is now expected that the partial privatization of SONATEL will take place before year-end, whereas SONACOS, SSPT, and SONATRA will be privatized in early 1997. The restructuring and liberalization of the energy sector--electricity and petroleum products--have aiso experienced some delays on account of the technical complexity of the  

required reforms, as well as the need to ensure social consensus on these reforms before they get under way. After consulting with the parties concerned, the government now stands ready to commence negotiations regarding these reforms before year-end.  

# I1I. Medium-Term Objectives and Strategy  

11. In order to build upon the achievements of the last three years and to overcome the remaining structural obstacles to development, the government has updated and strengthened its medium-term adjustment strategy. The main objectives and policies associated with this strategy are described in the policy framework paper for the period 1996-99 and detailed in this memorandum with respect to 1997. This strategy takes account of the communal action plan decided upon by the heads of state and the governments of the WAEMU at the Ouagadougou summit in May l996, with the aim of harmonizing economic and financial policies and creating a regional common market beginning in January 1998. The implementation of this strategy should be facilitated by a definitive easing of the external debt service burden in the form of a reduction of the stock of eligible bilateral debt under the auspices of the Paris Club and a buyback of debt in arrears vis-a-vis external commercial banks. In addition, the government intends to continue cooperating closely with the donor community. In this context, a meeting of the Consultative Group for Senegal is scheduled for September 1997.  

12.The updated strategy for the period 1996-99 aims at laying a firm foundation for strong and durable growth through the implementation of additional reforms designed to significantly improve the competitiveness of the Senegalese economy and provide an environment more conducive to private investment and exports. This critical mass willbe achieved by pursuing the structural reforms undertaken in 1994-96-specifically in the areas of economic liberalization, the relaxation of the regulatory framework, and the privatization of public enterprises—-and by implementing complementary reforms designed to strengthen judicial security, modernize the state, and reduce the cost of public utilities (water, energy, transport and telecommunications). In this context, the government willcompensate for its withdrawal from productive activities by focusing its efforts more on the development of human resources (literacy programs, primary education, vocational training, and basic health care), the development of basic infrastructure, and the protection of natural resources.  

13.The implementation of these critical structural reforms, coupled with the pursuit of rigorous macroeconomic management, should make it possible to achieve economic growth of at least 4.5 percent on average during the period 1997-99, lower the inflation rate to less than 3 percent from 1997 onward, and bring the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) to less than 5.5 percent of GDP by 1999. The attainment of these objectives will require a steady increase in investment, particularly on the part of the private sector, as well as an increase in domestic saving. In the area of public finance, the objective will be to eliminate the overallbudget deficit (on a commitment basis and excluding grants) in 1998 and to achieve modest surpluses thereafter, to ensure a return to financial viability and the sustainability of the public debt. To enable the government to allocate additional resources to its essential tasks, the efforts made since 1993 to curb nonpriority expenditure and to enhance tax recovery will be maintained. As regards budgetary revenue, the government's objective is to increase the ratio of budgetary revenue to GDP (excluding privatization receipts) by approximately 1/2 of 1 percentage point of GDP annually over the next three years, to reach 16.5 percent in 1999.  

# IV. Elements of the 1997 Program  

14.In 1997, the economy of Senegal should continue to benefit from a relatively favorable environment, in view of the stability of raw materials prices and good weather conditions for cereal crops. However, the production of groundnuts may decline on account of the irregular rainfall observed at the start of the 1996/97 crop season. The economy should nonetheless continue to benefit from the improvement in competitiveness, the modernization of the productive apparatus, and the buoyancy of the construction sector. Taking account of these factors, the growth of real GDP is expected to be between 4.5 percent and 5 percent. Exports should increase by about 6 percent in volume terms and by more than 7 percent in value. With the slower growth of imports and the stable level of debt service obligations, the external current account deficit (exciuding official transfers) should fall by l percentage point of GDP, to less than 7 percent. It should be possible to lower the inflation rate to less than 3 percent on a year-on-year basis, that is, a rate slightly above that projected for Senegal's main European partners.

The decentralized activities will be financed through transfers of budget allocations to be included in special budget appropriations as of 1997.  

# 2. Money and credit policy  

23.Monetary policy in 1997 will continue to be conducted in accordance with the objectives established by the Council of Ministers of the West African Monetary Union (WAMU). Monetary policy willfocus in particular on strengthening the official external position of the Union while safeguarding the credibility of the parity of the CFA franc through the pursuit of a prudent monetary and credit policy. In the context of the regional monetary program for 1997, broad money in Senegal is expected to increase by around 5 percent, slightly below the rate of nominal GDP growth.Senegal's contribution to the net external assets of the Central Bank is expected to increase by 1.3 percent of the money stock estimated for end-December 1996. In order to generate sufficient margins to ensure private sector access to credit, the volume of net bank credit to the government will be reduced by an amount equivalent to l.9 percent of the money stock estimated for end-December l996. Bank credit to the major public enterprises-which is included in credit to the private sector-will be closely monitored. However, to prevent an excessive expansion of credit to the private sector, the net domestic assets of the Central Bank (excluding the stock of consolidated public securities issued in July 1994) will be kept within the specific limits indicated in the attached Table 2.  

24.In consultation with its WAMU partners, the government of Senegal will focus on improving the indirect instruments of monetary policy available to the BCEAO, with a view to facilitating the effective control of the lending capacity of banks and ensuring that interest rates are determined freely by market conditions. The system of BCEAO bill auctions, introduced in July 1996, should adequately serve the objectives of monetary policy in the current circumstances. In their interest rate policy, the monetary authorities will continue to maintain intervention rates at a level equal to, or higher than, the levels encountered on external reference markets in order to sustain the credibility of the fixed parity and keep capital within the Union. With respect to banking supervision, the government will support the efforts of the WAMU Banking Commission to ensure compliance with prudential ratios, thereby increasing the soundness of Senegal's banking sector. It will also encourage the implementation of recommendations emerging from the bank audits currently in progress (particularly those of the Caisse Nationale de Credit Agricole du Senegal (CNCAS), the Banque Senégalo-Tunisienne (BST), and two financial institutions), and the continuance of efforts by the Societé Nationale de Recouvrement (SNR) to collect on nonperforming bank claims.  

25.To support economic growth and private investment, the government, in cooperation with other WAEMU member countries, will work to improve business financing mechanisms and develop a reliable system of credit for small-scale enterprises, particularly in the agriculture, fisheries, transportation, and handicrafts sectors. The measures recently adopted to encourage the issuance of negotiable debt instruments and to set up a regional securities exchange by endJune 1997 should facilitate the obtaining of medium-term resources by banks and enterprises. Further diversification of financial services and instruments for the mobilization of saving, through existing commercial banks, leasing companies, and other institutions, would also make a contribution toward financing modern productive activities. To meet the needs and address the special problems of small enterprises, particularly in rural areas, approximately one hundred savings and credit institutions have been formed and licensed pursuant to the law on credit institutions adopted in 1995. The government intends to continue promoting such ventures, which supplement the lending mechanisms provided by major agricultural companies such as SONACOS or SODEFITEX. The authorities are aware that the development of credit to the private sector in its various forms will be contingent upon improving the legal and judicial security of banking transactions.  

26.The government will maintain an exchange and trade system that is free of restrictions on payments and transfers for current international transactions, in accordance with the provisions of Article VII of the Articles of Agreement of the IMF, which Senegal accepted on June l, 1996. The draft law on harmonized exchange regulations within the WAEMU was submitted to the National Assembly in October 1996; the implementing decrees and orders willbe signed once the law is adopted.  

# 3. Structural reforms  

27.In 1997, the government of Senegal will work hard to ensure the full implementation of the structural and sectoral reforms initiated in 1994, and to accelerate the introduction of reforms in the legal environment as well as in the areas of privatization, energy, and agriculture, with particular emphasis on making up for past delays. The acceleration and consolidation of these reforms, which willsupplement the remaining fiscal consolidation efforts, should enable Senegal to attain a higher and more sustainable growth path.  

28.The government of Senegal is convinced that the legal and judicial environment for economic activity must be improved as a prerequisite for promoting the private sector, fostering direct investment, and creating a climate of confidence conducive to economic growth. Since the adoption of major trade and price liberalization measures in 1994-95, the government has focused its efforts on: (a) the implementation of a package of measures designed to achieve substantial improvements in the functioning of the judicial system; and (b) the preparation of reforms aimed at modernizing positive law on the subject of enterprises and transactions. For purposes of coordination, a Legal Reform Committee chaired by the Minister of Justice was set up in February 1995 and a three-year work program was approved. With respect to the judicial system, the measures already adopted or in preparation include:  

Increasing the budget appropriations for personnel and equipment allocated for the justice administration;  

The organization of judicial professions associated with bankruptcy;   
The modernization of the Office of the Clerk of Court, Regional Court of Dakar;   
The introduction of arbitration procedures;   
The training of judges in commercial matters;   
The dissemination of legal information.  

With respect to financial law, Senegal is party to a set of harmonization initiatives taken at the regional level, particularly in connection with the regulations for insurance law (Code of the Interafrican Conference on Insurance Markets, CIMA Code, ratified in December 1994), the law relating to payment instruments (submitted to the National Assembly in August l996), and the general business law (the OHADA Treaty ratified in September 1995). The draft Uniform Instruments that have already been prepared are expected to be adopted in early 1997 and to enter into force by end-June 1997, in accordance with recent decisions adopted by the Council of Ministers of Justice and Finance of the acceding states on the establishment of the organizations envisaged in the Treaty. 1/  

29.On the basis of ongoing technical and financial studies, as wellas the experience acquired in 1995-96, the government willendeavor to complete the privatization of 18 public enterprises by end-1997 (see also the PFP, Section V, 2, and Table 1). For this purpose, a detailed action plan has been updated, specifying for each enterprise a timetable of actions to be taken to ensure the satisfactory sale or, if necessary, liquidation of the enterprise. In addition to the actual sale of a major share in the capital of SONATEL, SONATRA, and SONACOS, this program envisages the launching of cals for bids for most of the government's shares or assets in four other enterprises, 2/ including the Societé Senegalaise des Phosphates de Thies (SSPT) before the end of 1996 or in early 1997. Seven other enterprises are expected to be put up for sale by end-June 1997. 3/ The government will also undertake the privatization of SENELEC in 1997, in the context of a comprehensive reform of the energy sector (see paragraph 30). These various operations, once carried out, will reduce the government's share in the public enterprise sector by aimost 27 percent. In addition, before the end of 1997, the government is planning to privatize the management of three enterprises: the naval repairs company (Dakar Marine), Cap Vert urban transportation (SOTRAC), and the Dakar-Bamako line of the railroad company (SNCS) . The legal framework required to carry out these operations will be submitted to the National Assembly by end-June 1997. To ensure that this program is implemented in an orderly fashion, the government should strengthen the facilities and resources of the unit responsible for managing the public portfolio. Finally, the authorities will continue to report comprehensive data on the financial operations of key public enterprises on a quarterly basis.  

30. Since 1994, the government's sectoral policies have been geared toward restructuring and rehabilitating three key sectors, namely, agriculture, transportation, and energy. The principal elemenis of the government's policies in the area of agriculture and transportation are described in the November l996 PFP (Section V, 4 and 5) and are already incorporated in letters of development policy agreed upon with the World Bank and the main donors. The process of designing policies and institutional reforms in the energy sector (electricity, petroleum products, and household fuels) has run into some delays. The government intends to accelerate the reform process by deciding upon the main components of the reform by end-December 1996 and adopting a detailed reform program to restructure and liberalize the sector by end-March 1997, in cooperation with the World Bank. This reform program includes (a) the liberalization of the import, transportation, and distribution of petroleum products by end-June 1997; (b) the automatic quarterly adjustment of petroleum prices, based on movements in international prices, no later than end-June 1997; (c) the privatization of SENELEC in l997; and (d) the modification of the regulatory famework to encourage private sector investment in the production and distribution of electricity.  

31.The government takes the view that poverty reduction and sustainable improvements in the living standard of the population will result primarily from the efforts undertaken since 1994 to accelerate the growth of investment and to boost the productivity and competitiveness of the economy. The government has, however, been actively implementing policies to achieve a more direct impact in terms of improving the living conditions of the most disadvantaged members of society. Such policies encompass a package of targeted measures intended to (a) accelerate the development of basic education and primary health care; (b) promote the marketing of generic drugs; (c) expand the availability of water and energy; and (d) ensure a more prominent role for women in society and in the economy. The specifics of these policies are described in the PFP (Section VI).  

# V. External Financing  

32.On the basis of the program's budgetary objectives, as described above, the external financing requirements amount to CFAF 134 billion for 1997. These requirements will be covered by: (a) the commitments for project assistance already obtained (CFAF 75 billion); (b) the debt relief granted through end-August 1997 by Paris Club members and other bilateral creditors (CFAF 17.4 billion); and (c) exceptional financial assistance granted directly by multilateral institutions and bilateral partners (CFAF 41.5 billion). The bilateral aid includes a major loan, the terms of which are being negotiated and which wil be earmarked to finance priority investment projects included in the three-year program 1997-99. However, the period September-December 1997 willbe left with a financing gap corresponding to debt service obligations eligible for relief from bilateral creditors on the same terms as those granted in April 1995. The quarterly projections for financial aid disbursements (excluding the IMF) in 1997 are shown in Table 2 (attached).  

33.The financing requirements indicated above do not include a substantially discounted buyback operation involving Sengal's external debt with commercial banks. This operation, which is part of Senegal's debt reduction policy, wilbe financed by means of grants from the World Bank and three countries for an amount estimated at up to US\$13.4 million as well as by a contribution from Senegal in the amount of CFAF 1.6 billion. The terms and conditions of the buy-back operation are included in an agreement reached with participating banks on November 15, 1996. Eighty percent of the banks must give their support for this agreement to enter into force. The government is convinced that the conclusion of this agreement will heip to restore the international banking community's confidence in Senegal's future, while making foreign banks more willing to support private investment and other industrial and commercial activities in Senegal.  

34.As result of the cancellations and concessional restructuring of external debt obtained over the period 1994-96, coupled with the considerable fiscal consolidation efforts, it has been possible to substantially reduce Senegal's external debt burden, both in nominal and in net present value terms, relative to government revenue, exports, and GDP. To secure further improvements in the debt service profile and reduce the average cost of the interest bill the government of Senegal will continue to rely exclusively on grants or long-term external borrowing on highly concessional terms. Accordingly, the government will neither contract nor guarantee loans entailing a grant element of less than 35 percent, with the exception of: (a) normal commercial credits of less than one year for import financing purposes; and (b) loans associated with debt rescheduling or refinancing. All loans contracted or guaranteed by the government will be subject to prior authorization from the Ministry of Economy, Finance, and Planning. The government will ensure that its external obligations are settled in a timely fashion and will not accumulate any external payments arrears.  

35.The rigorous management of government finances and public debt, in combination with obtained and expected debt relief, should enable Senegal to maintain a level of indebtedness that is sustainable in the medium and long term. In this context, the government hopes to be able to obtain an appropriate reduction of its stock of bilateral debt with Paris Club members by end1997. An operation of this nature will help to achieve a lasting reduction in Senegal's debt service and consolidate the public finance situation.

Lastly, the progress in implementing the program willbe evaluated during a midterm review to be completed by end-May 1997. This review will address the following issues in particular: (a) the execution of the privatization program; (b) the earmarking of projected privatization receipts for cleaning-up of bank and nonbank liabilities and financing of public investment; (c) the progress in the implementation of the reform program of the energy sector; (d) the progress achieved in implementing the measures recommended in the civil service audit; and (e) the status of the legal and judicial reforms mentioned in paragraph 28.  

38.To facilitate the design and monitoring of economic policies, the government will take all required steps, if necessary with the aid of technical assistance, to strengthen its research and analysis capabilities in the area of statistics on prices, employment, industrial production, public and private investment, national accounts, and the balance of payments.

# Senegal - Debt Sustainability Analysis  

Senegal's total outstanding external debt, excluding payments arrears to commercial banks that were under debt reduction negotiations, is estimated to amount to CFAF 1,852 billion (US\$3.6 billion) at end-October 1996, or about 70 percent of GDP (Table 12). Multilateral creditors accounted for almost 60 percent of the total stock of outstanding debt at end-October 1996, with outstanding Fund credit representing about 9 percent, and debt owed to the World Bank about 34 percent.² The remainder was owed to official bilateral creditors (39 percent), including 23 percent to the Paris Club and 12 percent to Arab countries; shortterm debt, consisting of the negative balance on the operations account, amounted to about 1 percent of the total debt stock. The stock of debt (including interest arrears) to commercial banks would amount to less than 2 percent of GDP at end-1996.  

Scheduled debt service, before debt relief, is estimated at CFAF 155 billion in 1996, of which CFAF 63.8 billon consists of interest obligations (Table 13). The largest share of total debt-service payments (56 percent) is due to multilateral creditors. Debt-service obligations in 1996 would represent about 19 percent of exports of goods and nonfactor services, and 39 percent of total government revenue. After debt relief already secured, these ratios would decline to $15\%$ percent and 31 percent, respectively.  

Since l98l, Senegal has obtained several reschedulings from the Paris Club. The latest agreement, obtained in April 1995, provides for a flow rescheduling under Naples terms and involves a 67 percent reduction in the net present value. The rescheduling covers the period through August 31, 1997, in line with the three-year ESAF arrangement, and includes a goodwillclause providing for a stock-of-debt reduction in late 1997, on the condition that Senegal has an appropriate arrangement with the Fund and implemented the 1995 agreement in full. Assuming terms similar to those agreed with the Paris Club creditors in April 1995, such a stock-of-debt operation is estimated to provide annual debt relief of up to CFAF 5 billion; topping up of debt previously rescheduled on Toronto terms would increase this to a peak of CFAF 26 bilion in 2000, with the amount of debt relief declining steadily thereafter. Senegal also benefits from rescheduling obtained from other bilateral creditors.  

A commercial debt reduction operation was agreed upon with Senegal's London Club creditors on November 15, 1996. The amount of debt eligible under the operation is US\$75 million in principal and US\$43 million in interest arrears. Under the operation, the  

creditors have two options: a cash buy-back at 16 cents per dollar of debt, or an exchange of existing debt against long-term non-interest-bearing notes denominated in U.S. dollars. The operation will be financed by a grant from the IDA Debt Reduction Facility for an amount of up to US\$13.4 million, as well as by a modest contribution of the government of Senegal of up to CFAF 1.6 billion.  

A long-term balance of payments scenario for the period 1996-20l5.is presented in Tables 14 and 15. This scenario assumes that, as a result of the improved competitiveness of the economy, export volumes will grow at an average rate of $4\%$ percent a year over the period 1996-2001, largely because of the continued expansion of the volume of traditional exports, but also of a rise in nontraditional exports (agro-industrial products and textiles). After 2002, export volume growth is projected to decelerate to an annual rate of 4 percent from 2005 onward, in line with real GDP growth. Import volumes are projected to grow at an annual average rate of $3\%$ percent during 1996-2001, and then at $3\%$ percent in subsequent years. The terms of trade are assumed to remain virtually constant over the projection period. Thus, the external current account deficit, excluding official transfers, is projected to narrow steadily from 8 percent of GDP in 1996 to 3 percent by 2006. The debt-service projections were made on the basis of outstanding debt at end-October 1996, with the annual level of new borrowing declining moderately after 2002. More than three-fourths of new borrowing would be contracted from multilateral creditors. It is assumed that the future contracted debt bears an interest rate of 2 percent for multilateral creditors and $2\%$ percent for bilateral creditors, and an average maturity of 30 years. Given this baseline scenario, there willbe a financing gap of about 0.6 percent of GDP on average in 1997-200l, which should then drop to 0.4 percent in 2002-o5. These residual financing gaps are expected to be covered by a stock-of-debt operation from the Paris Club, including a topping up of debt previously rescheduled on Toronto terms, as well as comparable debt relief from other bilateral creditors.  

Under this scenario, the external debt position of Senegal is projected to improve considerably over the next 10 years. The stock of debt as a percentage of GDP would be reduced from 67 percent in 1996 to 46 percent in 200l, then to 30 percent in 2006. The debtservice ratio in terms of exports of goods and nonfactor services would decline from 19 percent in 1996, to 14 percent in 200l, and to about 8 percent in 2006. Debt service to multilateral creditors is projected to fall from 1l percent of exports of goods and nonfactor services in 1996 to 6 percent in 2001, then to $4\%$ percent in 2006. However, the ratio of debt-service obligations to government revenue would remain relatively high, although it would decline from 39 percent in 1996 to 25 percent in 200l, and to 14 percent in 2006. In net present value terms, total debt stock would amount to 42 percent, 27 percent and 16 percent of GDP in 1996, 2001, and 2006 respectively, and to $135\%$ percent, 88 percent, and $54\%$ percent of exports of goods and nonfactor services for the same respective years. Payments to the Fund are expected to reach a peak of over 4 percent of exports of goods and nonfactor services, in 1997, then to decline steadily until 2007, and disappear thereafter (Table 16). This baseline scenario therefore suggests that, with continued pursuit of appropriate policies and prudent debt management, Senegal's two external debt ratios required under the HIPC initiative are likely to fall below the critical thresholds used as  

eligibility criteria, and debt-service obligations would be sustainable over the next l0 years. However, the fiscal burden of debt-service obligations remains high, and would make it more difficult to achieve the increase in investment needed to establish conditions for high sustainable growth.  

An alternative scenario was prepared to assess the sensitivity of the results described above to lower export growth. With annual export growth 1 percentage point lower than in the baseline scenario, the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) would widen by almost 2 percentage points of GDP by 200l, and by almost 4 percentage points of GDP by 2006. The debt-service ratio in percent of exports of goods and nonfactor services would increase by $1\%$ percentage points by 2oo6 as compared with the baseline scenario, but would still be sustainable. Assuming a stock of debt operation on Naples terms, including topping up, Senegal's debt-service ratio to exports of goods and nonfactor services would decrease to $11\%$ percent in 2001 and further to $91\%$ percent in 2005; from 2006 onward, however, debt-service obligations would increase slightly.

# Commentary on lending operations  

1. Bank assistance strategy in Senegal seeks: (i) rapid, sustainable, and broadbased economic growth through policy and regulatory reforms aimed at improving productivity and eliminating constraints to private sector development and employment generation; (ii) increased human resources development and broader access to social services; (ii) targeted actions to address the needs of the most vulnerable groups; and (iv) measures to reduce costs and supply rigidities in key sectors. The instruments to support these objectives have been adjustment lending buttressed by appropriate technical assistance operations and sector-based rehabilitation/investment projects.  

2. As of October 31, 1996, the World Bank had approved 99 operations in Senegal for a total amount, less cancellations, of US\$1,484.1 million, consisting of 79 IDA credits (including 3 special fund credits and two SFA) for US\$1,354.8 millon and 20 Bank loans for US\$129.3 millon. Past projects have supported agricultural diversification, irrigation, human resources development, institutional development, and expansion of the country's infrastructure, particularly its transport system. In recent years, emphasis has shifted to better utilization and maintenance of existing facilities, and to helping the government resolve some of the key issues related to long-term development prospects. Ongoing operations include agricultural services (research, extension, help to farmer organizations), agricultural sector adjustment, municipal housing development, public works and employment, human resource development, community nutrition, higher education, pilot female literacy, industry sector operation, private sector capacity building, water, and transport sector adjustment.  

3. As of October 31, 1996, IFC had 7 ongoing operations in Senegal for a total investment of US\$6.3 million. Ongoing IFC projects inciude three financial services, two food/agribusiness, and two fertilizer/agriculture projects. The establishment of the Africa Enterprise Fund (AEF) has enhanced IFC's ability to reach smaller businesses.  

4. Proposed Bank operations in fiscal year 1997 include lending in health (endemic diseases), urban transport, and energy. The Bank and IFC will continue to coordinate their respective roles in developing policies in the areas of industry, private sector, and banking and finance. The Bank's private sector assessment project was a joint effort between the Government, IFC, and the Bank.
