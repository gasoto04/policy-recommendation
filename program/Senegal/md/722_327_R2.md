# EXECUTIVE SUMMARY  

# Background  

A three-year arrangement under the PRGF, in an amount equivalent to SDR 107.01 million (66 percent of quota) was approved on April 20, 1998. The second annual arrangement expired on July 11, 20o0. Senegal reached the decision point under the enhanced Heavily Indebted Poor Country Initiative (HIPC) on June 21, 2000.  

# Recent developments  

Abdoulaye Wade of the Senegalese Democratic Party was elected President in March 2000, ending 40 years of Socialist Party rule. Senegal's economy suffered from a sharp rise in prices for petroleum products, frequent power outages between July and October, and lower than expected economic growth in the region following political instability in the largest country of the zone. Nevertheless, real growth is expected to have been in line with program objectives, owing to favorable weather conditions in the agricultural sector and buoyant construction activity. Average annual inflation remained below 1 percent. Fiscal developments at end-June and end-September 2000 were better than targeted as a result of lower-than-projected cash payments for petroleum products and a decline in the deficit of treasury correspondent accounts. All quantitative benchmarks for end-June and end-September were met, except for the September ceiling on accrued petroleum product subsidies. ● The implementation of structural measures was mixed. The surtax on imported petroleum products protecting the domestic refinery was removed. The government also took a number of steps to improve the governance of public enterprises. However, the privatization program has been largely halted pending a review of the strategy with the World Bank. The planned replacement of the two rates of the valueadded tax (VAT) with a single rate of 18 percent was also delayed.  

# Program for 2001  

The government will strengthen domestic revenue and reduce distortions by implementing a single-rate VAT at the rate of 18 percent by May 1, 2001. Expenditures will be reallocated to increase the efficiency of public services and raise spending in priority areas. These efforts will be supported by (a) cutting subsidies through a reinstatement of the passthrough mechanism for petroleum prices; and (b) adopting a supplementary budget to allocate resources freed up by HIPC interim relief to rural infrastructure projects and health services.  

The management contract with the private investors in the power company was cancelled and their shares were repurchased in January 20ol. The government has launched a call for expression of interest in the acquisition of shares in the company by new investors. The social security system will undergo a major reform with World Bank assistance and be put on a sustainable basis. The government will also resume its privatization program after completing the review of the privatization strategy.  

# Issues highlighted in the staff appraisal  

The program targets are realistic and achievable. However, the government needs to work for a rapid resolution of bottlenecks in power supply to attain its growth objectives. Achieving revenue objectives depends on the timely implementation of the VAT at a single rate of 18 percent with limited exemptions. Subsidies for   
petroleum products should be strictly limited to the agreed ceiling pending   
reinstatement of the passthrough mechanism by May 1, 2001.   
The privatization program should be relaunched quickly to foster private sector growth and reduce government involvement in the economy. Recent efforts to improve governance in the public sector by conducting audits in a range of public enterprises and institutions as well as the decision to publish the results of these audits are very welcome and are strongly supported.   
The authorities' emphasis on enhanced transparency and the efforts they are making to improve the data base are encouraging. In this endeavor, the authorities should place the greatest emphasis on steps to improve the availability and timeliness of treasury account balances.   
The preparation of the poverty reduction strategy should be a priority for the   
government in 20ol, including a launching of the necessary surveys and studies, and ensure that members of civil society and the development partners are involved in the process.

# 1. INTRODUCTION  

1. A three-year arrangement under the Poverty Reduction and Growth Facility (PRGF), in an amount equivalent to SDR 107.01 million (66 percent of quota), was approved for Senegal on April 20, 1998 (EBS/98/68, 4/6/98), and to date four disbursements totaling SDR 64.21 million have been made. The Executive Board concluded the first review of the second annual arrangement under this facility, together with the 200O Article IV consultation on June 21, 2000 (EBS/00/95, 6/1/00). At that time, Senegal also reached the decision point under the enhanced Heavily Indebted Poor Countries (HIPC) Initiative (EBS/00/97, 6/2/00 and EBS/00/184, 8/30/00).  

2. Discussions on an economic and financial program that could be supported by a third annual arrangement under the PRGF were held in Dakar during October 31—November 15.' In the attached letter and memorandum on economic and financial policies (MEFP; see Appendix I, Annex I), dated January 31, 2001, the Minister of Economy and Finance reviews developments in 2000 and discusses the policy measures to be implemented in 20ol. Related quantitative and structural performance criteria and benchmarks are defined in a technical memorandum of understanding (Appendix I, Annex II). Because the commitment period of the current three-year arrangement would expire before the disbursement of the second and third loans under the third annual arrangement, the authorities have requested a one-year extension of the commitment period to April 19, 2002.2  

3． As of November 30, 2000, Senegal's outstanding use of Fund resources amounted to SDR 200.18 million (123.7 percent of quota). Assuming full disbursement of the remaining SDR 42.8 million under the third annual arrangement and taking into account scheduled  

repayments, Senegal's financial obligations to the Fund would amount to SDR 196.3 million (121.3 percent of quota) at end-December 2002 (Table 8). Senegal's relations with the Fund are summarized in Appendix II.  

4. The World Bank also actively supports the structural adjustment program in Senegal. As summarized in Appendix III, the World Bank's overall strategy aims at supporting policies that enhance growth and reduce poverty and is implemented through a combination of structural adjustment lending, sectoral investment programs and projects. The loan portfolio has performed in a satisfactory manner over the last 2 years. In September 2000, the World Bank approved a Trade Reform and Competitiveness Credit in the amount of \$100 million-—cofinanced by the African Development Bank (AfDB) with an additional SDR 20 million-and a first tranche of $\$50$ million under this credit was disbursed in November 2000. The second tranche could be disbursed in early 2002. In December 2000, a loan for a social development fund program in the amount of $\$30.5$ million was approved to assist capacity building of poor communities. A further extension of the 1998 Energy Sector Adjustment Credit is currently under consideration, with a split of the undisbursed second tranche of $\$75$ million. A first disbursement of $\$30$ million is expected to take place in the first quarter of 200l to support reform measures that were already implemented in the petroleum sector, with a second disbursement of $\$45$ million conditional on further reforms in the electricity sector. Other projects currently under consideration are a long-term water project and a community nutrition project. Work is also under way to prepare a new project for social security reform.  

5． Senegal's statistical database is comprehensive and sufficient for program monitoring purposes. However, weaknesses remain in the timeliness and quality of national accounts, fiscal accounts, and balance of payments data. Although these weaknesses do not prevent the staf from gauging overall economic and financial developments, they often hamper the assessment of current developments. The Fund has recently provided technical assistance in the areas of national accounting and in fiscal management to improve the accuracy and processing of available data, enhance data collection and fiscal projections, and reduce the time lags in data reporting. The authorities are committed to further improving their statistics and fiscal accounts (MEFP, paragraph 23).  

# I1. RECENT DEVELOPMENTS  

# A. Political and Economic Environment  

6. Presidential elections in February and March 2000 ended 40 years of Socialist Party rule. President Abdoulaye Wade of the Senegalese Democratic Party (PDS) took office in April after a smooth transition. The new government includes a range of former opposition parties across the political spectrum. A new constitution that strengthens the role of the prime minister but retains the presidential system was adopted by popular referendum on January 7, 20ol. Parliamentary elections are expected to take place before April 2001 and could constrain the government's ability to implement unpopular reform measures until then. The government has initiated a peace process in the southern Casamance region, where a  

protracted insurgency has adversely affected economic development of the region during the past 20 years.  

7. The sharp rise in world oil prices, the political and economic difficulties in the region, and shortages of power supply characterized Senegal's economic environment in 2000. Senegal's import bil for crude oil and petroleum products nearly doubled in that year. Because the world market price increase was only partially passed through to consumers, it had significant budgetary implications. At the regional level, the economic situation was clouded by the uncertainties surrounding political developments in Cote d'Ivoire and the economic difficulties in Mali. These problems are expected to have kept the growth of real gross domestic product (GDP) below 2.5 percent in the West African Economic and Monetary Union (WAEMU) in 2000, compared with the rate of 5 percent that was originally projected; the lower growth affected regional demand for Senegal's exports.3 Despite the implementation of some short-term measures to increase power supply, the national electricity company (SENELEC)-—-partially privatized in 1999 and under private management since then—-was unable to avoid the recurrence of power outages during the summer of 20o0. In the event, the government decided to end the management contract with the private investors at end-December 2000 and launched an urgent search for new investors (see Box 1).  

# B. Economic Developments During 2000  

8. In spite of the difficult political and economic environment, growth and price developments in 20oo are estimated to have been in line with the program objectives. An exceptionally large harvest and buoyant construction activity compensated for relatively weak growth of the dominant tertiary sector and contributed to an estimated overallreal GDP growth of 5.5 percent (Figure l and Table 2). Consumer prices rose by 0.7 percent in 2000 (Table 1).  

9. Fiscal outcomes at end-June and end-September 20o0 were better than targeted. On both dates, the respective quantitative benchmarks for the basic fiscal balance4 and net credit to the government were observed and so were all other quantitative benchmarks, with the exception of the September ceiling on petroleum product subsidies (Appendix I, Annex I, Table 1). At end-September, the central government recorded a basic budget surplus of  

2.5 percent of GDP, twice as high as projected, while the overall budget deficit (commitment basis and excluding grants) of 1.4 percent of GDP was significantly smaller than projected (Table 3). Tax revenue benefited from improvements in tax enforcement, particularly as regards large taxpayers, and the resulting recovery of tax arrears. This increase in collections enabled the authorities to meet program objectives in this area despite the delay in replacing the existing value-added tax with rates of 10 and 20 percent with a single-rate VAT of 18 percent.  

# Box 1. Senegal: SENELEC: Recent Developments  

The national electricity company (SENELEC) was partially privatized in 1999. A French-Canadian consortium acquired 34 percent of the shares for CFAF 39 billion (1.3 percent of GDP) and took over the management of the company.The consortium also obtained an option for acquiring an additional 15 percent of government shares that were held in escrow by alocalbank, but no agreementcould be reached on the sales price. The govermment furthermore intended to cede 10 percent of shares, also held in escrow, to SENELEC employees. In March 200, the consortium and the govermment agreed to an increase of CFAF 30 billion in the company's capital, of which the private partners paid CFAF 10 billion. The capital increase served almost entirely to cover previous losses.  

Measures to increase electricity supply taken by the new management were not adequate to meet the growth in demand and prevent the recurrence in 2000 of power outages that had already been experienced during the previous two years. The contract gave the investors in SENELEC the right to supply power up to the total installed capacity of 330 megawatts, of which only 186 megawats were available,owing to the poor state of the installed generators. Beyond the limit of 330 megawatts, power had to be supplied by new independent operators, some of whom began generating electricity in 1999. However, SENELEC retained the monopoly for distributing power. In the event, the management of SENELEC decided to repair and renovate its old power generators rather than replace them. To partially overcome the resulting supply shortages, diesel generators and gas turbines meant for peak loads were employed to supply base power.As a result, the cost of power generation surged because diesel generators and gas turbines are two to three times as expensive as large-scale fuel oil power generators.  

The lack of incentives to undertake large-scale investments probably reflected the limited profitability under the agreed regulatory environment, as wellas the minority position of the consortium. Owing to the regulatory formulathat links maximum revenues of SENELEC to past fuel oil prices and other indicators, the company was unable to raise electricity prices in 200o despite rising fuelcosts. As a minority shareholder facing prospects of low returns on investment and an apparent internal disagreement about strategy, the consortium opted for short-term solutions rather than adopting a more capital-intensive, long-term investment strategy.  

The government decided, therefore, to seek a separation from the investment consortium under mutually agreeable terms. A commisson negotiated a separation that was finalized on December 29, 2000. Under the agreed terms, the government will repurchase the shares held by the investors for CFAF 45.2 billion. In the meantime,the government has launched acallfor expression of interest to find a new investor willing to acquire substantial stakes in SENELEC and undertake large-scale investments. The World Bank remains actively involved in this process.  

10. The larger-than-expected basic surplus mostly resulted from much lower than expected payments for petroleum subsidies and a significant decline in the deficit of correspondent accounts. To limit the impact of rising oil prices, retail prices for petroleum products were frozen in February 2000 (see Box 2). The 15 percent across-the-board increase implemented in mid-September, when the program ceiling was reached, was insufficient to eliminate the subsidy. However, owing to technical lags in cash payments for subsidies, the full cost related to consumed quantities of petroleum products was not registered in fiscal accounts at end-September. The smaller overall deficit also reflected the much lower than programmed execution of foreign-financed capital expenditures, which was partially related to the time needed by the new administration to review the public investment program. Expenditure on health and education, excluding externally financed capital expenditure, is estimated at 4.7 percent of GDP in 2000, a moderate increase over 1999, but remained 0.1 percent of GDP below programmed levels on account of lower than expected expenditure on education.6  

11. Fiscal outcomes for the year 20o0 as a whole are also projected to have outperformed original program objectives. The basic fiscal surplus slightly exceeded programmed levels and the overall budget deficit (commitment basis and excluding grants) was l.4 percentage point smaller than originally expected. As outlined in paragraph 6 of the MEFP, these end-year projections build on (a) meeting programmed tax revenues in 2000 despite the delay in introducing the VAT; (b) cash payment of all petroleum subsidy bills, estimated to total about CFAF 24 billion (0.8 percent of GDP), excluding the month of December; and (c) an acceleration in implementing extermally financed capital expenditure in the last quarter, which is nonetheless expected to have fallen short of programmed levels by 1.3 percent of GDP in 2000.  

# Box 2. Senegal: Petroleum Sector Policies and Reforms  

Retail prices for petroleum products are set administratively in Senegal. The oficial price structure is based on ex-refinery prices of the Mediterranean region, augmented by costs for freight, insurance, and ancillary fees.To these, the govermment adds taxes and distribution margins to arrive at theofficial retail price. The official price structure is established every four weeks.However,in the face ofrising crude oil prices, retail prices were frozen in February 2000 by the outgoing government. The resulting subsidy-the differential between theofficial price structure linked to world market price developments and the frozen retail prices-had to be reimbursed by the budget to the Societe Africaine de Raffnage (SAR) refinery. Faced with further increases in crude oil prices and a depreciation of the euro relative to the U.S.dollar, the new government implemented an across-the-board increase of retail prices of 15 percent in mid-September, without however eliminating the subsidy.The subsidy as percent of the retail price for the four most important petroleum products as wellas crude oil prices and exchange rate developments are shown in Figure 3. In December 2000, the subsidy for diesel and kerosene was 15 and 20 percent, respectively,of the actual retail price, while the subsidy for premium gasoline had disappeared.Eliminating the subsidy therefore would mostly affect the urban popuiation, inciuding the poor residents of suburbs, who are dependent on privately operated transportation businesses (cars rapides) and the poor using kerosene for cooking and lighting.  

The SAR-90 percent of which is owned by four multinationals-benefits from special incentives that sustain its domination of the Senegalese market despite ineffcient refining facilities. These incentives increase the cost of petroleum products for the Senegalese economy.First, under a special 1999 agreement with the government, the SAR enjoyed an outright 15 percent protection against imports of refined products as long as new efficiency-enhancing investments to be undertaken within four years from the date of inception were not amortized.7 When the refinery failed to undertake these investments, the government decided in November 2000 to remove the protective surtax on petroleum products,a decision subsequently approved by parliament. Second,even though the SAR benefits from extrateritorialtaxstatus (usine exercee),it owes customs and taxes only on thecrudecontent of therefined products it sell in Senegal,rather than on thefull value of the refined products.Third, the official price structure is based on products of higher quality than those sold by the SAR and, by building on ex-refinery prices of the Mediterranean region, overstates the cost of imported products from Cote d'Ivoire or Nigeria.These special incentives,combined with the fact that the owners of the SAR also own the retail network and all storage facilities, have effectively prevented competition in this sector. According to preliminary staff estimates established in the context of a recent technical assistance mission, retail prices in Senegal are at least 20 percent higher than they would be under a system allowing competition from imports.  

After the removal of the surtax, the government intends to take further steps to liberalize the sector and lower thecosts of petroleum products.The future steps will focus on revising the offcial price structure so as to bring it closer to actual prices for imported products; reviewing the application of taxes and tariffs to the SAR; and constructing independent storage facilities to permit competition from other importers. The World Bank is actively supporting the reform process with an Energy Sector Adjustment Credit.  

12. Monetary developments until end-September 2000 were characterized by a strong growth in credit to the economy (23.1 percent). This growth largely reflected the difficulties of the groundnut company (SONACOS) in marketing its exceptional oil production in time to repay bank loans (see Box 3).  

# Box 3. Senegal: SONACOS : Challenges for the 2000/01 Campaign  

SONACOS is a publicly owned company that was established in 1974 to promote the development of groundnuts by providing fertilizers and seeds,as well as purchasing groundnuts directly from farmers. The company also produces groundnut oil and cake and markets these products. Itis facing challenges on three fronts:  

Problems in marketing the rapidly growing groundnut production. In the last two years, volumes of groundnuts collected by SONACOS have grown sharply, owing to favorable rainfalls, efforts to strengthen the distribution of seeds and fertilizers, and the problems encountered by groundnut companies in The Gambia and Guinea Bissau that have led farmers in those countries to market their crops in Senegal. For the 2000/0l campaign, SONACOS expects to collect 550,000 metric tons of groundnuts, which represents a more than twofold increase in the average volume collected annually during the period I991-98. SONACOS is required to buy all the production offered by farmers at a price that is administratively set at the beginning of the campaign. Because its traditional export markets were saturated, the company was unable to market about one-fourth of its oil production during the 1999/2000 campaign, which was stillheld in stock at end-October 2000. Marketing the even better harvest expected for the current campaign depends crucially on developing alternative external markets or raising domestic demand.  

Volatility of international prices and exchange rates. Dollar prices for groundnut oil have fluctuated widely in the 1990s, and prices fell from over \$900 per metric ton in 1998 to \$700 per metric ton in 20o0. However, the impact of this fluctuation on the price in CFA francs realized by SONACOS, which ranged between CFAF 490 and CFAF 510 during the period, has been Iess pronounced because of longer-term contractual arrangements and offsetting exchange rate movements, particularly in 1999 and 2000. Nonetheless, uncertain price and exchange rate developments pose challenges for SONACOS because production costs remain broadly constant, with a producer price that has already been fixed at CFAF 145 per kilogram.  

Outstanding debt of farmers. Farmers failed to repay loans in the form of seeds and fertilizers valued at about CFAF 1l billion during recent years.Moreover, at the request of the government, the company provided seeds to farmers during the 2000/0l campaign, valued at another CFAF 1l billion, which may not be fully recovered in the coming months. The government expects, therefore, to transfer up to CFAF 3 billion in 200l to SONACOS to cover the loans that farmers may fail to repay to the company.  

The company's difficulty in reducing its stocks in 2000 made it necessary to find temporary additional financing in order to finance the new campaign. Crop credits for the 1999/2000 campaign (CFAF 32.5 billion) were converted into ordinary bank loans.The government currently expects that the 2000/01 campaign will be financed with loans totaling CFAF 62 billion to be supplied by a consortium of foreign banks and local banks. These loans include short-term foreign bank loans of about CFAF 45 billion to be guaranteed with the remaining stock of groundnut oil. To finance the 2000/0l campaign, SONACOS will need to secure total new financing of up to CFAF 100 billion (3.2 percent of GDP), with the peak of outstanding new loans estimated at about CFAF 55-60 billion in March/April 2001.  

To assure the financing of the new campaign, SONACOS is expected to raise a CFAF 62 billion loan from a consortium of foreign and local banks, with the former contributing CFAF 45 billion.8 In addition, outstanding crop credits of CFAF 32.5 billion, which were not repaid, were converted into ordinary loans. The Central Bank of the West African States (BCEAO) took measures to tighten credit conditions in response to developments in the euro zone and the region (MEFP, paragraph 7). It also raised the reserve requirement for commercial banks from 3 percent to 9 percent in Senegal and Benin and from 3 percent to 5 percent in Niger.? In these circumstances, the expansion of credit to the economy was counterbalanced by a decline in credit to the government. The growth of broad money is estimated to have been less than 6 percent in 2000 (Table 4). Senegal's financial sector has generally regained its health due to the measures taken after the crisis in the late 1980s, including the introduction of banking supervision at the regional level. Most banks observe prudential requirements and appear profitable. The main risks arise from the concentration of loan portfolios on a few large enterprises, including for two banks with large exposure to SONACOS loans.10  

13.Senegal's external position suffered in 2000 from a deterioration in the terms of trade (6.3 percent) related to the sharp increase in international petroleum product prices. At the same time, the real effective exchange rate of the CFA franc depreciated by 7.7 percent during the 12 months period ending in October 2000, improving the competitiveness of Senegalese exports (Figure 2). In domestic currency terms, the rise in the import bill for petroleum products in 2000 was only partly compensated for by higher export earnings (from the exceptional groundnut harvest) as the effect of the depreciation of the euro was largely offset by the fall in world market prices for groundnuts. By partially cushioning the impact of the oil price shock, the government also contributed to a fall in national saving and the external current account deficit (excluding current official transfers) is consequently estimated to have deteriorated by 1.2 percentage points to 8.6 percent of GDP (Table 5).  

Senegal's net foreign reserves at the BCEAO declined by an estimated CFAF 53.6 billion (7.5 percent of beginning-of-period money stock). Senegal sought and received a rating from Standard & Poor's, the first West African country to have ever done so. The sovereign credit rating of $\mathbf{B}\mathbf{+}$ for long-term loans puts Senegal on a par with countries such as Brazil, Turkey, Lebanon, and Bulgaria, which bodes well for the country's ability to attract new investors; it will also help the authorities to successfully place treasury bills in domestic and regional markets, following the scheduled elimination of statutory advances at the central bank starting in 2002.  

# C. Structural Reforms  

14. The implementation of the structural reform program has been mixed, as summarized in Box 4 and paragraph 3 of the MEFP. On the positive side, parliament adopted a law to eliminate the surtax on imported petroleum products protecting the SAR refinery in December 2000. This represented a significant step to increase competition and reduce the high cost of petroleum products in Senegal (see Box 2). The new management of the postal service took a number of measures to improve accounting and avoid the recurrence of past governance problems. The new government also initiated audits of a wide range of public enterprises and institutions as part of its efforts to strengthen governance. The authorities intend'to publish the results of these audits and prosecute any criminal activity that might be uncovered; the corresponding audit report of the Fonds de Promotion Economique was submitted to Fund staff in December. As part of its efforts to liberalize the economy, the government recently signed an open-sky agreement with the United States, paving the way for direct commercial flights operated by airline companies of the two countries. By contrast, the privatization program has been largely halted, pending a thorough review of the remaining portfolio of public enterprises to be conducted jointly with World Bank staff.'  

15. The government has implemented the common external tariff (TEC) of the WAEMU. However, a number of tariff classifications and categories in the customs authority's electronic system still need to be adapted to the new tariff to achieve complete conformity. As regards the safeguards permitted under the TEC, the government applies the temporary protection tax (TDP) and compensatory import levy (TCl) to only two products (cigarettes and sugar, respectively) at this time. Surtaxes of 10 percent and 20 percent levied on a few food products will be converted into the TCI in the future to comply with TEC regulations. The use of administratively set customs values (as opposed to c.i.f. values) has been suspended, pending a decision by the World Trade Organization on Senegal's request for a temporary exemption from the application of c.i.f. values.

# II. PROGRAM FOR 2001  

17. The government's program for the period from October 1, 2000 to September 30, 2001, for which it seeks Fund support under the third annual arrangement, aims at consolidating Senegal's solid record of growth and prudent fiscal policies while strengthening the efforts to reduce poverty. The main factors constraining the private sector's contribution to growth are (a) a complicated tax system with distortionary tax and tariff exemptions; (b) low effciency and a lack of output orientation in public administration; (c) an outdated judicial environment; (d) the high cost of petroleum products owing to a lack of competition; (e) an insufficient power supply; (f) government involvement in and regulation of the agricultural and transport sectors; and (g) poor infrastructure. Poverty is both a rural and urban phenomenon in Senegal.'2 However, a majority of the overall population is located in rural areas, and poverty rates among the rural population tend to be much higher than in the cities. Rural income is highly dependent on a few cash crops, tends to fluctuate sharply with weather conditions, and is adversely affected by environmental degradation. Causes of poverty in Senegal include low educational attainment, lack of basic health services, and insufficient access to credit, owing to limited financial intermediation.  

18.The government's main objectives under the program to be supported by the PRGF arrangement are, therefore, to (a) simplify the tax system; (b) direct additional resources to priority social sectors, improve the efficiency of public administration, and strengthen budgeting procedures; (c) remove obstacles for competition and investment through deregulation and privatization; and (d) upgrade the rural and urban infrastructure.  

# A. Growth and Inflation  

19.Real GDP growth under the program is expected to pick up slightly from 5.5 percent in 2000 to 5.7 percent in 200l. Growth in the tertiary sector is projected to be stronger than in the previous two years, while both the primary and secondary sectors are likely to grow more slowly than in 20oo. In the primary sector, indications are that favorable weather conditions, combined with the increase in the distribution of seeds, will contribute to another bumper groundnut harvest, exceeding that of 2oo0. In the secondary sector, slower growth in construction will partially negate the increase in industrial activity resulting from the improving export opportunities to other WAEMU countries. Investment-—excluding the effect of the accumulation of stocks (0.5 percent of GDP) by SONACOS in 2000 and the sale of these stocks in 200l—would rise by about l percentage point of GDP in 2001, driven by higher public sector capital outlays.13 It would be supported by an increase in gross domestic saving of almost 2 percentage points (to 12.4 percent of GDP), stemming mostly from a rise in private sector saving resulting from the decline in crude oil prices and the income windfall from the exceptional harvest. The improving external environment is expected to raise the profits of private and public enterprises and household income, particularly in the rural sector.'4 This development would reverse the decline in private saving experienced in 2000 following the sharp deterioration in the terms of trade. Inflation in 200l is expected to remain subdued at about 2 percent.  

# B. Fiscal Policy and Reforms  

20. The government's policies aim at strengthening revenue from domestic sources and improving tax effciency. It will submit a revised VAT law to parliament and implement a single-rate VAT at 18 percent by May 1, 2001. Goods and services to be exempted from the VAT will be limited in order to achieve an O.3 percentage point increase in tax revenue to 17.4 percent of GDP in 20o1.'5 The VAT reform will reduce distortions; it will also be accompanied by simplifications of tax declarations in order to improve tax administration. To gauge the magnitude of revenue losses and the impact of remaining distortions stemming from a number of tax and customs exemptions, the government will prepare a detailed study of these exemptions by March 31, 2001. Nontax revenuel6 could decline substantially, given the uncertainty surrounding the renegotiation of a fishing agreement in 2001 with the European Union that would generate fishing royalties.17  

21. On the expenditure side, the 20o1 budget allocates resources to improve the quality of public services while maintaining a tight wage policy. This allocation is reflected in a 28 percent increase in spending on materials and supplies to provide more resources per government employee. By contrast, the increase in the wage bill will be kept to 3.5 percent in 2001, excluding the planned increase of CFAF 2.5 billion (1.3 percent) in government contributions to the National Retirement Fund (FNR).  

22. The programmed reallocation of expenditure will be made possible by a reduction in subsidies for petroleum products and a significant reduction in the deficit of special and correspondent accounts. The government will reinstate the pass-through mechanism for petroleum products by May 1, 2001, at the latest, and it will limit subsidies related to the price freeze to CFAF 12 billion (0.4 percent of GDP) in 2001 including the bill for December 20oo. Likewise, the subsidies for butane (liquefied petroleum gas) maintained for social reasons will decline from CFAF 12.8 billion (0.4 percent of GDP) in 2000 to CFAF 8.7 billion (0.3 percent of GDP), as agreed with the World Bank. However, the government expects to pay up to CFAF 3 billion to SONACOS to top up the partial reimbursements expected from farmers of the cost of seeds provided by the company during the 20o0/o1 campaign. The deficit of the special and correspondent accounts will be virtually eliminated as a result of the dissolution of the Urban Community of Dakar, improved management of the postal service, and the reform of the social security system.18 Capital outlays, excluding resources arising from HIPC Initiative interim relief yet to be allocated, willincrease modestly from 6.9 percent of GDP in 2000 to 7.1 percent.'9 HIPC Initiative interim relief already granted by the World Bank, the Fund, the AfDB, and the Paris Club-—estimated at CFAF 23.4 billion (0.7 percent of GDP) in 2001—will be allocated to projects in priority sectors in a supplementary budget (see below).  

23. The government intends to launch a comprehensive reform of its social security and pension systems with the assistance of the World Bank. The reform will cover the FNR, the Senegalese Retirement Institution (IPRES), and the Social Security Fund (CSS). The World Bank has recently completed actuarial studies of the FNR and IPRES and is providing assistance in developing a reform strategy. The government is seeking World Bank financial assistance of about CFAF 20 billion (0.6 percent of GDP) to finance outstanding contributions by public enterprises to the social security and pension funds and to recapitalize the funds. As part of the reforms, the government will submit a revised pension law to parliament by September 30, 2001 that will ensure the long-term balance of the FNR and IPRES.  

24.Excluding expenditures related to interim HIPC Initiative relief and the reform of the social security system, the government's fiscal position will strengthen, with an  

improvement in both the basic balance and the overall balance (on a commitment basis and excluding grants) in 20o1. Owing to the rise in tax revenue and the fall in current expenditure, the basic surplus (excluding spending financed with HIPC Initiative interim relief spending on and social security reform) would rise by 0.4 percentage point of GDP to 1.5 percent of GDP, while the overall deficit, measured on the same basis, would narrow by 0.4 percentage point to 2.5 percent of GDP.  

25. The program seeks to further strengthen tax administration and budget management. In this respect, all revenue-collecting agencies will begin using a single taxpayer identification number by March 31, 2001. In addition, as detailed in paragraph 23 of the MEFP, the government willimplement the five WAEMU directives harmonizing the framework of government finances. In this respect, the authorities will take a formal decision by June 30, 2001, to prepare the 2002 budget according to the WAEMU budget classification. Efforts are also under way to improve the timeliness of treasury account balances, which are critical for any meaningful assessment of the government's financial transactions.  

# C. Monetary Issues  

26.The regional central bank willcontinue to keep monetary aggregates consistent with maintenance of the fixed exchange rate with the euro. Broad money growth in 2001 (7 percent) is projected to be in line with GDP growth in Senegal. Owing to substantial external assistance in the context of energy sector and trade reforms, net credit to the government is projected to decline by about CFAF 17 bilion (2.2 percent of beginning-ofperiod broad money) despite the repurchase of SENELEC shares for CFAF 45.2 billion. Potential receipts of a resale of SENELEC, which have not been included in the program, would further help the government to reimburse its bank credit and reconstitute foreign reserves at the central bank. Credit to the economy would expand moderately in 2001, as large crop credits remained outstanding at end-2000. By contrast, crop credits of the 2000/01 campaign and those of the 1999/2000 campaign that were converted into ordinary loans are expected to be reimbursed by end-200l, allowing an expansion of credit to nonagricultural businesses of about 9 percent.  

# D. External Sector and Medium-Term Outlook  

27.The external current account balance (excluding current official transfers) is projected to improve by about 2 percentage points of GDP to 6.6 percent in 2001 as a result of strong export growth and a decrease in oil prices. Exports of groundnut products will rise following good harvests, while exports of phosphoric acids are projected to grow strongly after the coming onstream of a new production unit.20 The import bill particularly for petroleum  

products, is expected to decrease by about l percentage point to around 28 percent of GDP. The current account deficit would be fully financed by project grants and loans, by World Bank and AfDB program loans, and HIPC Initiative interim relief, permitting a net accumulation of reserves at the central bank of CFAF 52 billion, which will reverse almost entirely the reserve losses experienced in 2000.21  

28. The external position is expected to remain sustainable over the medium term. After a further improvement in exports in 2002, the external current account deficit (excluding current official transfers) is projected to stabilize at 6.2 percent during 2003-04. Export projections build on a continuing favorable environment for groundnut production in response to the government's policy to keep the distribution of seeds and fertilizers at the high level attained in 2000, a recovery of fishing activity, and a further rise in phosphate product exports. Import projections incorporate a stabilization of petroleum product prices at \$20-22 per barrel, in line with the latest World Economic Outlook projections. The external current account deficits over the medium term would be fuly financed by external grants and loans, as wellas by the debt relief granted under the HIPC Initiative. Based on the mediumterm outlook, Senegal's strong record in meeting debt-service obligations, and good prospects for future external assistance on concessional terms, the authorities should have no difficulties in repaying the Fund.  

# E. Structural Reforms  

29.As regards the privatization program, the government is reviewing its strategy with the International Finance Corporation (IFC) of the World Bank Group. By April 2001, a new overall privatization strategy will be developed that will include a list of enterprises to be privatized, as wellas the method, time frame, and the resources needed to conduct the privatization program. Furthermore, with assistance from the IFC, the government plans to review the regulatory environment, to create a new regulatory commission covering several utilities, and to develop a strategy of private sector involvement in infrastructure development. In cooperation with the World Bank's Foreign Investment Advisory Service (FIAS), the government is furthermore studying how to improve tax incentives for investors in general, through streamlining special tax regimes and eliminating distortions. These efforts will also be pursued on the regional level, where a investment code for all WAEMU member countries is near completion.  

30.The government will pursue reforms in the energy sector in collaboration with the World Bank. These actions will include finding new private investors for SENELEC and redefining the framework for electricity production and supply. In the petroleum sector, the government will maintain and deepen its liberalization policies. In particular, it will review the official price structure of petroleum products in light of the recommendations of a recent Fund technical assistance mission; in addition, in order to increase competition in the sector, the authorities plan to build independent storage facilities for petroleum products.  

# F. HIPC Initiative Relief and Poverty Reduction Strategy  

31. With the participation of the World Bank, the government is identifying several priority areas in which it intends to use savings derived from the HIPC Initiative interim relief. Appropriate allocations will be made in a supplementary budget to be submitted to parliament in 2001. Priority investment projects will aim at improving rural water and electricity supply (50 percent), expanding the health system, particularly as regards malaria prevention and treatment (25 percent), and improving the rural road network (25 percent). Some resources may also be allocated to infrastructure projects in the Casamance province, which has been beset by armed conflicts over the last two decades. During 2001, the government intends to design a mechanism to track the use of HIPC resources in future years to ensure that these funds willcontinue to be directed to priority areas after Senegal reaches the completion point.  

32. The preparation of the poverty reduction strategy paper (PRSP) is progressing (MEFP, paragraph 26) after responsibility for its development was clearly assigned to the Ministry of Finance and Economy. As outlined in the government's interim PRSP (EBD/00/44, 6/1/00), the practice of budgeting-by-objectives has been implemented in pilot ministries. The household survey to update poverty profiles and poverty indicators has been delayed for technical reasons. It will now commence in January 200l, with first results expected in March 200l. The authorities still plan to follow the timetable outlined in the interim PRSP by launching national consultations on the strategy in July 200l, and finalizing the document by end-2001.  

33. The measures the government intends to implement under the third annual arrangement will underpin its efforts to reduce poverty in several ways:  

· Reforming the VAT will help to broaden the tax base while protecting the poor through targeted exemptions, such as social tranches for utilities.   
● Eliminating tax loopholes will ensure that those with the capacity to pay contribute adequately to the financing of programs. It will also reduce governance problems.   
· Enhancing administrative efficiency and budgeting procedures will improve the targeting of expenditures to priority areas.   
● Pension reform willhelp retirees to maintain a reasonable standard of living.   
? Reducing the cost of petroleum products through increasing competition from imports will reduce both production costs for businesses and transportation costs for rural and urban dwellers. Strengthening the supply of electricity will encourage higher economic growth and reduce the incidence of power outages affecting the urban population, including the poor.   
? Reallocating resources toward social sectors by using additional resources from HIPC Initiative debt relief willhave a direct impact on the incidence of rural poverty, improve rural infrastructure and expand social services.

# V. STAFF APPRAISAL  

35. The new administration, which came to power early in 20o0, took over implementation of the macroeconomic and structural reform program in a determined manner and achieved encouraging results during the year in the areas of growth and inflation. Nearly all the quantitative and structural benchmarks set for end-September were met. The new program the government has prepared for 200o-0l aims at achieving further progress in these areas, provided that the government strengthens policies on a number of fronts:  

The growth, inflation, and balance of payments objectives specified in the authorities' program are realistic. However, the staff shares the authorities' view that a rapid resolution of the electric power supply bottlenecks will help the modern sector of the economy make the expected contribution to the achievement of these objectives. To this end, the staff encourages the authorities to complete negotiations at an early date with investors who will replace the current partners in the power company, and to ensure that needed investments will be made to modernize the power plants so as to enhance the efficiency and reliability of power supply at a competitive price.  

It is understandable that the new authorities would wish to review the public enterprise privatization strategy before new units are brought to the point of sale.  

However, the staff urges them to complete this review quickly and to relaunch the privatization program without undue delay. The staff believes that the implementation of the privatization program will enable the authorities to involve more the private sector in the economy, so as to increase efficiency in production and create jobs.  

The staff welcomes the sovereign rating received by Senegal, which puts the country on a par with a number of emerging economies. It is encouraged that the authorities' program already envisions effective measures in the areas of education, power generation, and transportation, and further reforms of public enterprises, allof which are listed as factors constraining the rating.  

With regard to financial policies, the staff considers that the fiscal objectives underlying the authorities' program are achievable. On the revenue front, introduction of the much-delayed VAT at a single rate of 18 percent, with very few exemptions, will be key in reaching the program revenue target. The coming onstream of the large-taxpayer unit, and the general use by all revenue collection agencies of a single taxpayer identification number are seen as critical complementary elements in this regard. On the expenditure side, the authorities are committed to reestablishing the passthrough mechanism at the latest by May 1, 2001. This measure would provide appropriate price signals to users on the true cost of energy to the economy, thus giving incentives for energy conservation measures and exploration of alternative energy sources, including solar energy. Pending this reinstatement, total spending on subsidies should be kept strictly within the program ceiling. The government also needs to reexamine its relationship with the groundnut company, SONACOS, so as to limit to the maximum extent possible any liabilities that could arise either from the inability of the company to meet its debt obligations or from difficulties in recovering crop credit extended to farmers. Ultimately, the staff is of the view that the authorities should seek assistance, including from the World Bank and IFC, in order to turn this company over to the private sector at the earliest possible date.  

On governance issues, the authorities have appropriately launched audits of a large number of public enterprises to ascertain whether they have been managed in a sound manner over the past four decades. The staff also supports the authorities' decision to publish the findings of these audits and to turn over to the courts for prosecution any documented misdeeds that may be uncovered. The staff recommends that such audits be extended to certain critical areas of public administration to verify that, over the years, no major diversion of public resources toward unintended uses has occurred. In this vein, the staff welcomes the authorities decision to conduct a study on tax and customs duty exemptions to ensure that no profit-making commercial and industrial activities are being shielded from taxation, and thereby imposing an undue tax burden on officially identified taxpayers in the modern sectors of the economy.  

With respect to data issues, the staff welcomes the authorities' emphasis on enhanced transparency and the efforts they are making to improve the data base. In particular, the staff encourages the authorities to diligently pursue current efforts to produce treasury account balances and to improve their timeliness in order to confirm the veracity of the government's financial transactions. Staf also encourages the authorities to enhance the government's capacity to track expenditures along the administrative expenditure process.  

With respect to regional integration, the staff would encourage the authorities to continue playing a leading role in bringing about an environment friendly to investors. Such an environment could be supported through the adoption of a regional investment code to replace the individual national codes; under the latter, goods and services originating from within the WAEMU area that are free of duty cannot be distinguished from those coming from outside the area, which should pay duty at the prevailing rate. The authorities should also rally support around an effort to achieve consensus at the regional level on the need to base customs duty assessment on cost, insurance, and freight value of imports, rather than on an administratively determined minimum value.  

The staff is heartened by the government's decision to assign to the Ministry of Economy and Finance the responsibility to lead the authorities' efforts to prepare the poverty reduction strategy by end-200l. The authorities will need to follow a clear and realistic timetable to conduct the required studies on the profile and scope of poverty in Senegal, seek donors' views and inputs on the strategy, and ensure full participation of the civil society in its elaboration. In this endeavor, the authorities should enlist the support of the World Bank and other donors in preparing the costing of various programs and in defining the most effective mechanisms to track outlays financed by assistance flowing from the HIPC Initiative relief.  

The major challenge to the program concerns pressures the authorities are likely to face in the run-up to the legislative election in late April for increasing civil service salaries, picking up a larger share than budgeted of the reimbursement of crop credit to the groundnut company or delaying further the privatization program to appease vested interests. The staff will press that the authorities effectively resist all these pressures and adhere strictly to program fiscal targets and to the structural reform agenda.  

36.In light of the determined implementation of the program thus far, and the policies and reforms the authorities intend to undertake during 200l, the staff recommends approval by the Board of the third annual arrangement under the PRGF for Senegal and the extension of the commitment period of the three-year arrangement under the PRGF to April 19, 2002.

# Memorandum on Economic and Financial Policies for 2000 01 Dakar, January 15 , 2001  

# 1. Introduction  

6. The government that came to power after the presidential elections in March 2000 embraced the broad macroeconomic and structural reform strategy adopted in consultation with the International Monetary Fund and World Bank for the period 1998-2001. The strategy aims at achieving strong and sustainable growth while contributing to poverty reduction. Since taking office, the government has continued implementation of the program supported by the second annual arrangement under the Poverty Reduction and Growth Facility (PRGF). The Executive Board of the International Monetary Fund conducted the first review of this program on June 21, 2000 and concluded that in 1999, economic growth, inflation, and the external position had all been satisfactory. As regards government finance, the basic budgetary surplus exceeded the program target, whereas the overall budget deficit was smaller than originally projected.  

7. The Executive Boards of the IMF and World Bank also concluded in June that Senegal had reached the decision point under the Heavily Indebted Poor Countries (HIPC) Initiative. They reviewed the interim poverty reduction strategy paper (PRSP), in which the government sets out (a) its objectives in the area of poverty reduction; (b) the studies it intends to carry out in order to better understand the extent of poverty and its profile in Senegal; and (c) the process it will follow to complete a comprehensive PRSP by December 3l, 200l. The strategy will be developed in consultation with Senegal's development partners and with the active participation of civil society-in particular, the poor and their representatives.  

# I1. Outcomes as of September 30, 20o0 and Estimates for the Year as a Whole  

8. Macroeconomic developments during the first 9 months of the program were broadly encouraging. All quantitative benchmarks at September 30, 2000 were observed, with the exception of the ceiling applicable to subsidies for petroleum products. Performance on structural benchmarks was mixed. The government has initiated the audit of the postal service (Societé Nationale La Poste) but only two enterprises out of four were privatized, and the law eliminating the surtax protecting the Societe Africaine de Raffinage (SAR) was adopted by the parliament with some delay.  

9. Owing to favorable weather conditions, the estimated real GDP growth rate for 2000 was about $5\%$ percent, despite the difficulties experienced during the third quarter from unanticipated power outages. Consumer prices rose on an annual basis by only 1.2 percent at end-September as a result of good harvests of food crops and the freeze in retail prices of petroleum products. The basic budgetary surplus at September 30, 200 was equivalent to 2.5 percent of GDP, or twice the program target. Fiscal revenue was in line with projections, although with a change in composition. Expenditure, net of externally financed capital outlays and on-lending, amounted to CFAF 369.1 billion, nearly 6 percent below the projected level. Spending on goods and services was in line with the program, while outlays on transfers and subsidies remained below projections, owing to a technical lag between the sale of petroleum products by the refinery and the date it submits the subsidy bill to the government for payment.  

10. The overall financial operations of the central government (including grants on a commitment basis) generated a surplus of about CFAF 10 billion in 2000, in contrast to a projected deficit of about CFAF 28 billion. This outturn reflects both the slower than expected execution of externally financed capital expenditure and a reduction by almost twothirds of the deficits in correspondent accounts at the treasury. The surplus, combined with the proceeds from repayment of Brady bonds, enabled the government to reimburse external debt equivalent to CFAF 6 billion, and to reduce its indebtedness to the banking system by CFAF 16 billion.  

11. Based on end-September outcomes and preliminary data for October, the government estimates an overall fiscal deficit (on a commitment basis, excluding grants) of CFAF 88.8 billion for the year as a whole, equivalent to 2.9 percent of GDP. Revenue and grants are projected at CFAF 616 billion (19.8 percent of GDP), with tax receipts reaching about CFAF 533 billion, an increase of 0.3 percentage point of GDP over their 1999 level (16.8 percent of GDP). Total expenditure and net lending amounted to CFAF 648.4 billion (20.8 percent of GDP). In terms of GDP, current expenditure increased by 1 percentage point to 13.l percent, while capital outlays declined somewhat (by -l.4 percentage point of GDP from 1999, when a supplementary budget law had authorized additional capital outlays). The rise in current expenditure largely reflects the cost of the subsidy needed to maintain the freeze in retail prices of petroleum products, which, for 20o0 as a whole, reached CFAF 24 billion, exceeding the program ceiling by CFAF 7 billion. The balance of correspondent accounts was in line with program forecasts, with the deficit of the national pension fund (FNR) amounting to CFAF 6 billion, and that of the postal service to CFAF 4.7 billion. The central government also covered the deficit (CFAF 6 billion) of the Urban Community of Dakar (CUD), which has been dissolved. External financing of the deficit amounted to CFAF 37.5 billion, including interim assistance granted under the HIPC Initiative. Part of drawings on external loans enabled the government to further reduce its indebtedness to the banking system by an estimated CFAF 5.9 billion in 2000.  

12. During the first nine months of the year, money supply grew at a rate above that of nominal GDP. Net domestic credit increased by 13.5 percent in terms of the beginning-ofperiod money stock, owing to the combined effect of a sharp increase in credit to the economy (23.1 percent), in particular crop credits, and a decline in net credit to the government (2.2 percent of the beginning-of-period money stock). The groundnut company (SONACOS) was unable to reimburse bank loans (CFAF 42 billion) because of difficulties encountered in marketing the 1999/2000 groundnut oil production. Nevertheless, at the request of the government, SONACOS, together with the Union Nationale Interprofessionelle des Semences (UNIS), lent seeds to farmers for CFAF 11l billion. The rapid expansion of domestic credit, in conjunction with the hike in European Central Bank interest rates, prompted the Central Bank of West African States (BCEAO) in June to tighten its monetary policy by increasing the discount rate and repurchase rate to 6.5 percent and 6 percent, respectively. In addition, it raised the reserve requirement for commercial banks from 3 percent to 9 percent in August.' The BCEAO's net foreign assets fell by CFAF 39.5 billion (6 percent in terms of initial stock of broad money) during the nine-month period to end-September 200O as a result of the deterioration in the external position.  

13. In 2000, the external current account deficit (excluding current official transfers) is estimated to have widened by 1.2 percentage points of GDP to 8.6 percent of GDP owing to a deterioration in the terms of trade. The deterioration is due to the sharp increase in petroleum prices and the drop in international prices of some of Senegal's exports. The import bill for petroleum products is now estimated at CFAF 206.3 billion in 2000, an increase of 83 percent in value terms from 1999. As a result of some delay in the disbursements of expected external assistance during the fourth quarter and despite the positive impact of interim assistance under the HIPC Initiative, the decline in net foreign assets of the central bank is estimated at CFAF 53.6 billion in 2000 (7.5 percent of the beginning-of-period money stock).  

14. Senegal actively participates in the initiatives under way to promote the economic integration of the WAEMU member countries. In this regard, the Convergence, Stability, Growth, and Solidarity pact adopted by the heads of state and governments of the union establishes quantitative convergence criteria for December 31, 2002, at the latest, and a multilateral surveillance procedure. According to available estimates, Senegal already observed four out of five of the first-level criteria and four of the second-level criteria in 2000.² As regards the supplementary protection measures accompanying the common external tariff (TEC), the government has adopted the temporary protection tax (TDP) and the compensatory import levy (TCI).  

# III. Measures to be Implemented in 2001  

15.The government's economic policies are being implemented in an international environment marked by high oil prices, the depreciation of the euro vis-a-vis the U.S. dollar, and weak world market prices for some of Senegal's exports. At the national level, the government must take into account the high social expectations in a political context marked by a constitutional referendum followed by legislative elections during the first half of 2001.  

The government is determined to pursue its program of macroeconomic and structural reforms, but it will reexamine with its principal partners, particularly the World Bank and the International Finance Corporation, some of the reforms, in particular the privatization program.  

16. The macroeconomic projections underlying the government's policies assume a real GDP growth rate of 5.7 percent, based on good harvests in the agricultural sector, sustained activity in the secondary sector, and a rebound in activities in the tertiary sector. It also incorporates an inflation rate (measured by the consumer price index) of 2 percent, and an external current account deficit (excluding current official transfers) of 6.6 percent of GDP. The projections depend on a quick resolution of the problems linked to the supply of electricity that have been experienced since 1998, and which have disrupted periodically activity in the secondary sector. The projected improvement in the external position is based on a 12 percent increase in the value of exports, mainly groundnut and phosphoric acid exports, and a reduction in the import billpredicated on an expected gradual decline in petroleum prices.  

17. The 2001 budget places special emphasis on public saving and investment, without neglecting social concerns. The basic surplus (excluding the cost of restructuring the social security system and additional outlays to be financed with savings deriving from the HIPC Initiative debt relief) will be equivalent to I.5 percent of GDP, compared with 1.1 percent in 2000. The targeted improvement stems from increased revenue collection efforts, a significant reduction in the subsidies associated with the freeze of petroleum product prices (see below), and the elimination of the deficit of correspondent accounts.  

18. The overall government deficit (on a commitment basis, excluding grants) will be limited to 3.8 percent of GDP. Budgetary revenue is projected to increase by almost 6 percent to CFAF 591.5 billion. This increase is attributable to tax receipts, which will grow by 0.3 percent of GDP to CFAF 579.1 billion. The increase in revenue results from the implementation of several important measures: the application of a VAT at the single rate of 18 percent, effective May 1, 200l; the setting up of a large-taxpayer unit in the tax department (Direction Generale des Impots et Domaines); the adoption of a single taxpayer identification number; the computerization of all revenue collection agencies; and the revision of the government property tax base. Projections of budgetary revenue do not include fishing royalties, notably from the European Union, which could reach CFAF 8 billion. Total expenditure and net lending (excluding additional HIPC Initiativerelated expenditure) will be limited to CFAF 695.4 billion. In terms of GDP, current expenditure will be kept at its 2000 level (13 percent). Wages and salaries will increase by less than 5 percent (4.8 percent). Expenditure on goods and services, which includes CFAF 9.3 billion to cover the cost of the forthcoming referendum and legislative elections, will increase by 28 percent, in order to provide adequate material and supply to government employees and thereby increase the efficiency of public administration. Transfer and subsidy outlays will be limited to CFAF 70.1 billion, including CFAF 12 billion, for the cost of maintaining the freeze on petroleum product prices until April 30, 2001, and CFAF 3 billion as reimbursement to SONACOS for the 2000/0l crop credit to be borne by the budget. In  

accordance with the stipulations of the energy sector adjustment credit of the World Bank, the subsidies for butane to protect low-income households (including the cost of freezing prices until May 2001) will be reduced to CFAF 8.7 billion in 2000. The deficit of the National Retirement Fund (FNR) for 2001 will be limited to CFAF 4.5 billion, following the reform envisaged by the government. The World Bank will provide technical and financial support for restructuring the entire social security system, including the FNR, the Senegalese Retirement Institution (IPRES), and the Social Security Fund (CSS). The restructuring will encompass a reimbursement of approximately CFAF 20 billion to these institutions, representing arrears owed by public enterprises.  

19.Total capital expenditure estimated at CFAF 235.6 billion will be financed in equal share by domestic and external resources. The principal actions envisaged in this area are the following: a) in the primary sector, improvement and refurbishing of fishing centers and offloading facilities, and the drilling of wells; b) in the secondary sector, rural and urban electrification, and the exploration of solar energy; c) in the tertiary sector, improvement of rural roads to enhance rural populations' access to improved social services; and d) in the social sector, increasing expenditures directed toward raising the primary school enrollment ratio and improving access to health care and safe drinking water (50 percent of the investment budget).  

20.The deficit will be financed with external loans of about CFAF 157 billion, which will also cover external debt-amortization payments and a reduction in the government's indebtedness to the banking system.  

21. The resources made available by the interim assistance under the HIPC Initiative will be used, in close cooperation with Senegal's development partners, to strengthen the policies adopted by the government in the areas of education (preschool and primary levels), health (the fight against malaria and AIDS), access to safe drinking water, and urban and rural development. A supplementary budget to be submitted to parliament during the first half of 2001 will provide specific allocations of HIPC Initiative interim relief to priority social sectors.  

22.The program's monetary objectives are consistent with the fiscal targets and a moderate rate of inflation. Under the 2001 program, money supply is projected to expand by 7 percent, the same rate as that of nominal GDP. Net credit to the government will decline despite the repurchase of SENELEC shares following the mutual agreement to end the contract with the private investors. Credit to the economy is to grow by about 3 percent.  

23． In cooperation with the other WAMU member states, Senegal will support the monetary authorities' efforts to improve indirect instruments of money supply. It will also back the regional banking commission's action plan to ensure banks' compliance with prudential ratios. The authorities will closely monitor the development and solvency of microcredit institutions.  

# IV. Structural Reforms  

24. The government has decided to initiate a comprehensive review of the privatization program with World Bank assistance and it intends to implement the new strategy with assistance from the International Finance Corporation. The strategy, as wellas the list and timetable of enterprises to be privatized, will be provided to the staff of the International Monetary Fund by March 31, 2001. Following a joint decision to terminate the partnership between Senegal and the investment consortium Hydro-Quebec/Elyo in the national power company (SENELEC), the parties have set up a joint committee to determine the terms of settlement of the partnership. The committee reached an agreement on the price for the repurchase of shares and payment modalities. A protocol of agreement was signed on December 29, 2000, accompanied by an immediate withdrawal of the private investors from the company. In the meantime, the government issued call for expression of interest in early November 2o0o, in an active search for new partners to ensure the reliable production and distribution of electricity. The government willinform the staff of the International Monetary Fund on a regular basis about the progress made in finding new investors. A new urban transport company, Dakar DEM DIKK, has been established with an initial capital of about CFAF 1.5 billion, and is operating since January 2, 2001.  

25.As regards SONACOS, the crop credits of the 1999/2000 campaign could not be reimbursed owing to the difficulties encountered in marketing the groundnut oil production. These crop credits were converted into ordinary bank loans in January 2001. The 2000/01 campaign will be financed by a consortium of local and foreign banks. The macroeconomic framework underlying the program is based on the following assumptions regarding the financing of the agricultural campaign: the total amount of new loans will be CFAF 62 billion, including a foreign loan of CFAF 45 billion, contracted at an annual interest rate of 7.5 percent, and the loans will have an average period of repayment of 7 months. The government expects that, except for the payment of CFAF 3 billion to top up the repayment of producer loans for seeds, it will not incur any direct budgetary cost. The macroeconomic framework underlying the program will be modified in case the financing conditions of the agricultural campaign change. The government expects that during 2001 all loans obtained to finance the 1999/2000 and 2000/01 campaign will be reimbursed. The staff of the IMF will receive on a monthly basis detailed information on the financial situation of SONACOS, the 20o0/0l producer loans it has recovered from farmers, and all possible costs borne or to be borne by the budget. The government has also initiated a comprehensive study of prospects for the groundnut sector and will take any necessary measures to assure the sustainability of the sector.  

26. The government has established a commission to certify the environmental impact of proposed investments and accelerate the timetable for issuing such certificates to a maximum of 15 days; it has also eliminated the requirement for businessmen to secure a permit (card) to engage in import/export activities.  

27. As part of its program to improve governance, including the fight against corruption, the government has carried out audits of several public enterprises and institutions. The findings of these audits will be reviewed by a specially appointed Committee, which will submit recommendations for remedial actions to the government. In this area, the government has provided the IMF staff with the second audit report of the Fonds de Promotion Economique (FPE) and willprovide the audit report and a restructuring plan for SN La Poste by March 31, 2001. The authorities are also concerned about the scope of tax and customs exemptions, which limit the tax base and make the reduction of duties and tax rates difficult. Consequently, they have decided to conduct a detailed study of these exemptions, including (a) their legal basis; (b) their beneficiaries; and (c) the revenue losses they entail. The conclusions of this study will be provided to Fund staff by March 31, 2001.  

28. In the energy sector, the government will reexamine the entire tax and pricing system for petroleum products, with a view to bringing it in line with the harmonized petroleum tax regime to be adopted by the WAEMU. From May l, 2001 onward, the government will resume the monthly pass-though mechanism for retail prices of petroleum products. The independence of the regulatory agency that is expected to replace the current Electricity Commission (established by the 1998 law to regulate the electricity sector) will be preserved, and adjustments in electricity tariffs willbe based on the price setting formula established by the Agency. Subsidies on fuel supplies to SENELEC and on butane will be reduced in line with the energy sector reform program agreed with the World Bank.  

29. The government continues to address statistical weaknesses in the areas of national accounts, foreign trade, and industrial production, with support from Afristat, among others. An effort is also being made to improve the statistics on social indicators. In this regard, the government has received technical assistance from the International Monetary Fund to review preparation of national accounts and integrate special accounts and correspondent accounts at the treasury into the budget. The government will adopt the five WAEMU directives harmonizing the legal, accounting, and statistical framework of government finance. In particular, it will implement the WAEMU chart of accounts and the budget classification system by 2002. The authorities are actively seeking technical support, including from the IMF, for setting up an independent statistical institute that will produce reliable data consistent with international standards.  

# V. External Debt and Poverty Reduction Strategy  

30. In June 20oO, the Executive Boards of the World Bank and International Monetary Fund confirmed Senegal's eligibility for the HIPC Initiative and determined that it had reached the decision point. Between the decision point and the completion point, set for end2001, Senegal will receive interim assistance estimated at US\$46 million (CFAF 33 billion), including the assistance recently granted by the African Development Bank. The government will develop projects and programs in the social sectors, with assistance from the World Bank, financed by the interim assistance. A preliminary plan for the use of debt-relief resources in 2001 has been provided to IMF staff.  

31.Senegal will continue to manage its debt prudently and will seek only grants and long-term external loans on concessional terms. The government will neither contract nor guarantee any nonconcessional loans or leasing agreements, with the exception of commercial credits of less than one year intended to finance imports. The Minister of Economy and Finance must first approve all loans contracted or guaranteed by the government. The government will continue to settle all external debt-service obligations in an orderly manner, and will not accumulate any external or domestic payments arrears.  

32. The government has given the Ministry of Economy and Finance the responsibility to lead efforts to develop a comprehensive poverty reduction strategy. A first meeting has already been held with Senegal's development partners to exchange information on the timetable for preparing such a strategy. A household survey will be carried out to explore the causes, regional distribution, and main manifestations of poverty. The strategy will also encompass measures to significantly improve the overall business climate with a view to promoting private sector initiatives. It will seek to improve the efficiency of local administration, in particular through the practice of budgeting by objectives and decentralization.

# Technical Memorandum of Understanding of the Third Annual Arrangement Under the Poverty Reduction and Growth Facility  

1. This technical memorandum of understanding defines the quantitative and structural performance criteria and benchmarks to monitor the program supported by the Poverty Reduction and Growth Facility. It also establishes the terms and time limits for transmitting the data that will enable Fund staff to monitor program implementation and provides numerical illustrations of these criteria and benchmarks based on past outcomes.  

# 1. Definition  

2. Unless otherwise specified below, the government is defined as the central administration of the Republic of Senegal and does not include any local administration, the central bank, or any government-owned entity with separate legal personality.  

# I. Quantitative Performance Criteria  

3. In the context of this program, quantitative performance criteria will be set for March 31, 200l and September 30, 2001 for the basic fiscal balance, net bank credit to the government, the subsidy to maintain the freeze of retail prices for petroleum products (excluding butane). Benchmarks for the same set of variables have been specified for December 31, 2000 and June 30, 2001. The criteria pertaining to the ceiling on contracting or guaranteeing of new nonconcessional external debt by the government, domestic and external arrears will be monitored on a continuous basis.  

# A. Basic Fiscal Balance  

# Definition  

4. The basic fiscal balance is the difference between the government's budgetary revenue and total expenditure and net lending, excluding capital expenditure financed from abroad and gross lending. It includes the deficit of special and correspondent accounts at the treasury.  

# Performance criteria and benchmarks  

5. The performance criteria for the basic fiscal balance are floors of CFAF --10.3 billion on March 31, 2001 and CFAF 29.3 billion on September 30, 2001, respectively. Floors of  

CFAF 34.1 billion on December 31, 2000 and CFAF 19.4 billion on June 30, 2001, constitute benchmarks.  

# Adjusters  

6. The floor will be raised (lowered) by the shortfall (excess) in expenditure financed with debt relief under the Heavily Indebted Poor Countries (HIPC) Initiative compared with the program. The same adjustment will be made in the event of lower-than-programmed spending (higher-than-programmed spending) on the externally financed restructuring of the social security system. Finally, the floor will be raised by higher-than-programmed revenues from fishing royalties.

# B. Net Bank Credit to the Government  

# Definition  

8. The definition of government for the purpose of calculating net bank credit to the government is the one applied by the BCEAO and is consistent with common IMF practice. It is broader than the definition of government in paragraph 2. Net bank credit to the government reflects the net credit position of the government-including postal checking accounts (CCP)—vis-a-vis the central bank and commercial banks. Net bank credit to the government is the difference between the government's gross borrowing from the banking system and its claims against the banks. Government claims include treasury cash holdings, deposits (including earmarked privatization receipts and other funds) at the central bank, deposits in commercial banks and $\mathbf{CCPs}$ , and secured liabilities (obligations cautionnees). The government's debt to the banking system includes central bank credit (mainly statutory advances, IMF assistance, refinancing of secured liabilities, and the deposit by Kuwait), government securities held by the central bank, commercial bank credit (including government securities held by resident commercial banks), ex-ONCAD securities and deposits at the CCP. Government securities held outside the Senegalese banking system are not included in net bank credit to the government. The net bank credit to the government as calculated by the BCEAO serves as the basis for program monitoring.

C. Subsidy Associated with the Freeze in Petroleum Product Prices  

# Definition  

12. The subsidy for maintaining the freeze of petroleum product prices is defined as the revenue shortfall incurred by the oil refinery, Societé Africaine de Raffinage (SAR), as a result of keeping domestic prices below international prices. It includes the subsidy for retail petroleum products and heavy fuels but excludes the subsidy for butane. The subsidy is currently paid by the SAR and billed to the government on a monthly basis. The amount of the subsidy on the petroleum product price freeze will be established on the basis of the official price structure, quantities sold, and international prices, as indicated in the attached Table l. The amount of the subsidy is calculated in the SAR invoices that have been validated by the authorities.  

# Performance criterion and benchmarks

Expenditure for the months of December 2000-April 2001, before the reintroduction of the pass-through mechanism, is capped at CFAF 12 billion and constitutes a benchmark. A ceiling of CFAF 24.4 billion on December 31, 2000, which includes all invoices up to November 30, 2000, also constitutes a benchmark.  

# Reporting requirements  

14.The authorities report to Fund staff on a monthly basis the amount of petroleum product subsidies for which the SAR has already submitted its bills.  

# D. Ceiling on the Contracting or Guaranteeing of New Nonconcessional External Debt by the Government  

# Definition  

15.This performance criterion applies not only to debt as defined in point No. 9 of the Guidelines on Performance Criteria with Respect to Foreign Debt (Executive Board Decision No. 6230-(79/140), last amended by Executive Board Decision No. 12274-(00/85) (08/24/2000) but also to commitments contracted or guaranteed for which value has not been received.  

16. The definition of debt as specified in point 9 of the Guidelines on Performance Criteria with Respect to Foreign Debt reads as follows: debt will be understood to mean a current, i.e., not contingent, liability, created under a contractual arrangement through the provision of value in the form of assets (including currency) or services, and which requires the obligor to make one or more payments in the form of assets (including currency) or services, at some future point(s) in time; these payments willdischarge the principal and/or interest liabilities incurred under the contract. Debts can take a number of forms, the primary ones being as follows: (a) loans, i.e., advances of money to obligor by the lender made on the basis of an undertaking that the obligor will repay the funds in the future (including deposits, bonds, debentures, commercial loans and buyers' credits) and temporary exchanges of assets that are equivalent to fully collateralized loans under which the obligor is required to repay the funds, and usually pay interest, by repurchasing the colateral from the buyer in the future (such as repurchase agreements and official swap arrangements); (b) suppliers' credits, i.e., contracts where the supplier permits the obligor to defer payments until some time after the date on which the goods are delivered or services are provided; and (c) leases, i.e., arrangements under which property is provided which the lessee has the right to use for one or more specified period(s) of time that are usually shorter than the total expected service life of the property, while the lessor retains the title to the property. For the purpose of the guideline, the debt is the present value (at the inception of the lease) of all lease payments expected to be made during the period of the agreement, excluding those payments that cover the operation, repair, or maintenance of the property. Under the definition of debt set out above, arrears, penalties, and judicially awarded damages arising from the failure to make payment under a contractual obligation that constitutes debt are debt. Failure to make payment on an obligation that is not considered debt under this definition (e.g., payment on delivery) will not give rise to debt.  

17.Any debt with a grant element of less than 35 percent is considered nonconcessional. For debt with a maturity of more than l5 years, the ten-year reference market interest rate, published by the OECD, is used to calculate the grant element. The six-month reference market rate is used for debt with shorter maturities.  

18. For purposes of this performance criterion, government is understood to include the government as defined in paragraph 2, as well as public administrative institutions (EPA), public institutions of scientific and technical nature, public institutions of a professional nature, and local administrations.'  

# Performance criterion  

19. Within the context of the program, the government will not contract, guarantee, nor otherwise back nonconcessional external debt.² This criterion is monitored on a continuous basis and does not apply to debt rescheduling and restructuring operations, and short-term, import-related credits.  

# Reporting requirements  

20.The government will report to Fund staff any new foreign borrowing and its terms to Fund staff as soon as the agreement has been signed.  

# E. External Payments Arrears  

# Definition  

21.External payments arrears are defined as the sum of external payments due but unpaid on outstanding foreign debt that is owned or guaranteed by the government. Debt is understood as defined in paragraph 16.  

# Performance criterion  

22. Under the program, the government will not accumulate any external arrears except for arrears arising from debt in the process of being renegotiated. This performance criterion will be monitored on a continuous basis.  

# Reporting requirements  

23. The government will report to Fund staff any accumulation of external arrears as soon as the due date has been missed.  

# F. Domestic Payments Arrears  

# Definition  

24. Domestic payments arrears are duly certified domestic expenditure commitments cleared for payment (depenses ordonnancées) not paid during a period of 90 days after the date the payment order (ordonnancement) was cleared. They include duly certified commitments cleared for payment not paid to public enterprises 90 days following the clearance date.  

# Performance criterion  

25. Under the program, the government will not accumulate any domestic payments arrears. This criterion will be monitored on a continuous basis.  

# Reporting requirements  

26. The authorities will report to Fund staff any accumulation of domestic payments arrears as soon as the 90 days period mentioned in paragraph 24 has elapsed. The government will also keep track of expenditure commitments duly certified not yet cleared for payment (depenses engagées (certifiees et liquidees) non encore ordonnancées) in order to minimize delays in payments.

40. The passthrough mechanism for petroleum products that links retail prices for petroleum products to international prices will be reinstated no later than May 1, 2001.

41. All revenue collecting agencies (regies financieres) will use a single taxpayer identification number no later than March 31, 2001.

43. The government will transmit to Fund staff by March 31, 200l a study detailing tax and tariff exemptions. This study will describe the legal basis of the exemptions as well as the beneficiaries of these exemptions, and the resulting revenue losses.

# VII. Exchange Rate Arrangement  

Senegal is a member of the West African Monetary Union (WAMU). The exchange system, common to all members of the union, is free of restrictions on the making of payments and transfers for current international transactions.' The union's common currency, the CFA franc, had been pegged to the French franc at the rate of CFAF $\mathbf{1}=\mathbf{F}0.02$ .Effective January l2, 1994, the CFA franc was devalued and the new parity set at CFAF $\boldsymbol{\mathrm{\mathbf{1}}}=\boldsymbol{\mathbf{F}}\boldsymbol{0.01}$ Effective December 31, 1998, the parity was switched to the euro at a rate of CFAF 655.96 $\mathbf{\sigma}=\mathbf{\sigma}$ EUR 1. On October 31, 2000, the rate of the CFA franc in terms of the SDR was SDR $1=$ CFAF 997.02.

# Commentary on lending operations  

1. The main objective of the Bank's assistance strategy for Senegal is to reduce the incidence of poverty and create gainful employment. The approved lending program for the period fiscal year (FY) 98-2000 under the Country Assstance Strategy (CAS) was implemented satisfactorily. To achieve the objective of the CAS, Bank support was a two-pronged strategy: (a) supporting policies and programs for more rapid and sustained growth; and (b) ensuring social sustainability of programs. The strategy was implemented through a combination of structural adjustment lending (energy sector reform and trade liberalization), sector investment programs (SIP), specific investment lending, and appropriate nonlending operations. Analytical work on the transitional costs of trade reform led to the preparation and approval of the Trade Reform Adjustment Credit. The public expenditure review (PER) is helping the govermment improve the size and composition of public expenditures for the period 1998-2000. To follow up on the achievement of the FY 98-2000 Country Assistance Strategy (CAS), preparation of a new CAS will commence in early calendar-year 2001.  

2. As of December 31, 2000, the World Bank had approved over 100 projects for Senegal for a total amount of about US\$2.2 billion. Past projects had supported agricultural diversification, irrigation, human resources development, institutional development, and expansion of the country's infrastructure, particularly its transport system. In recent years, emphasis had shifted to better utilizing and maintaining existing facilities and to helping the government resolve some of the key issues related to long-term development prospects. The current portfolio represents a commitment value of about US\$848.l millon equivalent, with an undisbursed balance of about US\$589.4 million. The portfolio is composed of 2l projects in various sectors (agriculture, human resourcespopulation/health/nutrition/education, infrastructure/urban development, energy/water, industry, and private sector development). Two projects have been approved this fiscal year (FY 2001): an adjustment operation (Trade Reform and Competitiveness) was approved on September l4, 2000 to assist in liberalizing trade, facilitating trade and tax procedures, and supporting regulatory programs that promote competitive pricing of public utility inputs to the productive sector; and a social development fund program was approved on December 20, 2000 to assist in building the capacity of the poor communities to effectively manage their own development resources and economic and social services with equal participation. Three additional projects are in the pipeline for FY 2001— long-term water, community nutrition, and energy sector adjustment.  

3. As of December 31, 2000, the International Finance Corporation (IFC) had six active investments totaling about US\$39.9 million. They comprise two financial services; one fishery project; one leather manufacturing operation; one cement factory, and one power project (the first independent power project in Senegal). Two additional investments, a supplement to the power project, approved in FY 1998, and a Small Enterprise Fund (SEF Royal Saly), approved this fiscal year (FY 2001), have not yet become effective.  

4. The Bank and IFC willcontinue to coordinate their respective roles in providing an enabling environment for private sector development for Senegal. The Bank will concentrate on factors that improve the business environment, and the IFC, with its office in Dakar, willincrease its direct support to the private sector, especially in the power, mining, small and medium-size businesses, privatization, and capital market areas.
