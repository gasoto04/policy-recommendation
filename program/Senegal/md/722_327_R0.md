# EXECUTIVE SUMMARY  

# Performance under the 1998 program and recent developments  

During the first annual program supported by a new three-year ESAF arrangement, Senegal has maintained the sound macroeconomic policy stance adopted during the previous three-year program and implemented the medium-term reform strategy in a broadly satisfactory manner.   
Economic performance in 1998 has been good, with strong real GDP growth, lower inflation and a reduction in the external current account deficit relative to GDP. Fiscal policies were in line with the program and progress was made in some areas of structural reforms, although delays were encountered on some other areas. The first step towards the regional Common External Tariff and the liberalization of   
petroleum product import and pricing were successfully implemented in April 1998. Five of the thirteen enterprises scheduled for privatization in l998 were sold; and after some delays, SENELEC, the electricity company, will now be privatized in 1999.  

# The 1999/2000 program  

Helped by a recovery in agricultural output, real GDP growth is projected to reach 6.4 percent in 1999 compared to 5.7 percent in 1998, inflation is expected to be limited to 2 percent, and the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers and after debt relief) is projected to be little changed from the 1998 deficit of 6.8 percent of GDP.

The original budget for 1999 has been modified through a supplementary budget (introduced in June 1999) to use privatization receipts for investment and domestic debt reduction during 1999 and 2000. This is broadly in line with previous understandings and consistent with the program's original path of fiscal consolidation.  

The Common External Tariff (CET) of the West African Economic and Monetary Union (WAEMU) will be fully implemented on January 1, 20o0. The top tariff of 25 percent will be reduced to 20 percent and the 5 percent customs stamp duty will be replaced by a 1 percent statistical tax. To offset the revenue losses, Senegal will replace the current two rates of VAT with a single rate of 18 percent, effective Juiy 1, 2000. In addition, an end-use tariff discounting mechanism on industrial inputs (précisions tarifaires), a distorting instrument for effective protection, will be eliminated.  

The program of privatization and restructuring of publicly-owned enterprises will be completed by end-20oo. Thirteen enterprises are scheduled to be sold and/or restructured in 1999 and a further five in 2000. Improvements in the environment for the private sector willinclude the implementation of the key recommendations of a recent Foreign Investment Advisory Service (FIAS) report; the adoption of the regional business laws under the OHADA treaty; and improvements in the administration of the rule of law. The latter is also part of the program for improving governance in Senegal. Other elements will include completion of a transparent audit of an off-budget fund, the Fonds de Promotion Economique (FPE) and an action plan to combat customs fraud.  

# Issues emphasized in the staff appraisal  

The continued pursuit of fiscal consolidation and the maintenance of macroeconomic stability compatible with the peg of the CFA franc to the euro are the foundations of the adjustment program in Senegal. To secure further trade liberalization, the CET should be implemented in full and on time, together with the elimination of the precisions tarifaires and the adoption of WTO-consistent WAEMU decisions on reference values and surcharges. Rice import taxation should be reformed. The CET-induced reduction in tarif revenue should be fully recovered through the single rate of VAT, with a minimal number of products being exempt.   
The acceleration of structural reforms, in particular with respect to the privatization program and the strengthening of the judicial system, and the forceful promotion of governance and transparency issues, should be sustained in order to bolster private sector confidence and investment.   
Decisive implementation of sectoral policies, in particular the liberalization of the energy and transport sectors, and in the areas of development of human resources, especially in education, health and the provision of rural infrastructure, will also be essential for promoting growth and reducing poverty.  

# I. INTRODUCTION  

1. Senegal has successfully implemented the macroeconomic and structural reform program supported by the first annual arrangement under the current three-year arrangement under the Enhanced Structural Adjustment Facility (ESAF). The arrangement approved on April 20, 1998 is in an amount equivalent to SDR 107.01 million (66.1 percent of quota). In the attached letter and memorandum dated June 4, 1999 (Appendix I, Annex), the Minister of Economy, Finance, and Planning requests the second annual arrangement under the ESAF, in an amount equivalent to SDR 35.67 milion (22 percent of quota).1 This request is in support of the policies and measures outlined in Senegal's Memorandum of Economic and Financial Policies for 1999--2000 (Appendix I, Annex), based on the policy framework paper (PFP) for 1999-2001 (EBD/99/78, 7/i/99). The PFP was prepared by the authorities in close collaboration with the staffs of the Fund and the World Bank.  

2. The policies aim at strengthening financial viability while reducing the structural rigidities that have hampered growth in the past. The new program falls under the new ESAF architecture approved by the Executive Board in September 1998; it provides for two reviews and a revised phasing of disbursements. If the planned disbursements under the arrangement and all repurchases and loan repayments are made as scheduled, Senegal's use of Fund resources would increase from SDR 200.9 million (124.6 percent of quota) on March 31, 1999 to SDR 217.1 million (l34.2 percent of quota) by December 31, 2001 (Tables l and 2). Senegal's relations with the Fund are summarized in Appendix II.  

3.The World Bank has been supporting Senegal's development efforts through structural and sectoral adjustment lending. The current portfolio consists of 18 operations, including an agricultural sector project and a transport loan approved in FY 1999. A public expenditure review focusing on the education, health, and judicial sectors was completed in 1998 and its recommendations are being implemented. The International Finance Corporation is involved in six investment projects in Senegal, in the areas of manufacturing and financial sector development. A Consultative Group meeting held in April 1998 secured donor support for the period 1998--2000 in the amount of US\$2 billion. Senegal's relations with the World Bank Group are summarized in Appendix III.  

4. Senegal's economic database is fairly comprehensive, although weaknesses remain with regard to coverage and internal consistency, in particular with regard to the national accounts, and in data on industrial production, international trade, and the balance of payments. The authorities have requested technical assistance from the Fund to improve the national accounts and to assess current practices in the area of budget forecasting. Although weaknesses in data collection and compilation have not prevented a meaningful assessment of economic policies, and the core minimum data have regularly been provided to the Fund, the analysis of current developments is sometimes hampered by the poor quality and timeliness of some data. The state of preparedness on the Y2K issue is progressing rather slowly and much of the information that is required has not yet been processed. The scheduled dates for testing most computer systems will leave little time for contingency plans, if needed.  

5.Following parliamentary elections in l998, indirect elections for the newly created Senate were held in January 1999. The incumbent Parti Socialiste won allof the 48 elected seats; l2 seats were filled by Presidential nominees, two of whom are representatives of the opposition. Presidential elections are scheduled for early 20oO. Insecurity continues to prevail in the southern province of Casamance. Senegalese troops had been dispatched to Guinea-Bissau earlier in the year in an effort to foil a coup d'etat, but have since been withdrawn. The social climate is calm overall, but a general strike, calling for a general wage increase, tax cuts, lowering of the retirement age, and the establishment of a nationwide health insurance fund, brough most economic activities to a halt for two days in late June 1999.  

# II. PERFORMANCE UNDER THE FIRST ANNUAL ARRANGEMENT UNDER ESAF AND RECENT DEVELOPMENTS  

6. Senegal's economy continues to perform well. Real GDP is estimated to have grown by slightly less than 6 percent in 1998 despite a decline in agriculture. Growth was boosted by a sharp increase in peanut oil milling and a generaly strong expansion in the tertiary sector. Consumer price inflation was below l percent at end-1998, and near zero so far into 1999 (Figure l and Table 3). The real effective exchange value of the CFA franc at end-May 1999 was 30 percent below its level at end-1993, indicating that most of the competitiveness gain from the 1994 CFA franc devaluation has been preserved (Figure 2). The external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) was lower than program projections, with interest payments on rescheduled debt lower than expected (Table 8). Both exports and imports were below projected levels. Capital inflows, including foreign direct investment, were somewhat above expectations, leading to a modest accumulation of net foreign assets with the BCEAO.  

7.In the fiscal area, the basic fiscal surplus² was little changed from 1997 (2.7 percent of GDP) and the overall deficit (on a commitment basis and excluding grants), on the basis of a new and more comprehensive definition of public investment outlays,4 widened to 3.3 percent of GDP in 1998 (Tables 5 and 6). Total government revenues relative to GDP was little changed from 1997. On the expenditure side, total outlays increased by one percentage point to 19.9 percent of GDP in 1998, due to increases in investment outlays and net lending. Budgetary allocations for the health and education sectors increased from about one-third of total current spending in 1997 to 36 percent in 1998, and the share in capital spending increased by 3 percentage points to close to 15 percent in 1998. The government used part of the proceeds from the sale of the government's share in SONATEL to eliminate outstanding extra-budgetary arrears, to increase investment spending in the social sectors, and to further reduce its indebtedness to the banking system in l998. II. MEDIUM-TERM ADJUSTMENT STRATEGY AND THE PROGRAM FOR 1999-2000  

# A. Strategy and Objectives for 1999-2001  

11. The key objective of the Government's medium-term strategy is to achieve higher growth by raising investment rates and productivity. This will require macroeconomic stability, external viability, and an acceleration of structural reforms. The main areas in the structural reform program are: (a) completion of the public enterprise privatization and  

restructuring program by end-20oO; (b) continued reform of the agricultural, water, transport, and energy sectors; (c) improvement of the environment for the private sector and promotion of investment; and (d) implementation of a program on good governance. These policies will be supplemented with a comprehensive social program centered on directly improving education and health services, including wel-targeted poverty reduction programs. These reforms are spelled out in the updated Policy Framework Paper (PFP) for 1999-2001.  

12. The program for 1999-2001 seeks to raise real GDP growth from 5.7 percent in 1998 to $6\%$ percent in 1999 and 6 percent in 2000 and 2001 (Table 4), with investment rising from 18 percent of GDP in 1998 to an average of about 20 percent in 1999-2001. At the same time, domestic savings are programmed to increase from 12 percent of GDP in 1998 to about 14 percent of GDP in 1999, and 15 percent in 2001. Annual inflation is to be held at around 2 percent and the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) is expected to narrow to less than 6 percent of GDP in 2001. # B. The Program for 1999-2000  

# Fiscal policy  

13.Senegal's original budget for 1999 projected a basic fiscal surplus of 2.6 percent of GDP and an overall budget deficit of 3.3 percent of GDP, unchanged from 1998. On June ll, 1999, Parliament adopted a supplementary budget, which authorized the government to spend the remaining resources (CFAF 60 billion) received in 1997-98 from the privatization of SONATEL. Including this supplementary finance law, the basic budgetary balance will continue to show a surplus in 1999, albeit a smaler one (1.7 percent of GDP) and the overall fiscal deficit will be limited to 4.4 percent of GDP.  

14. Two-thirds of the supplementary appropriations (CFAF 40 billion) will be on investment projects, the balance being spent on debt reduction (CFAF 9.2 billion) and on current expenditure (CFAF 10.8 billion). The bulk of investment expenditures are devoted to rural infrastructure projects and the construction of two regional hospitals that will ultimately benefit the poorer segments of the population. The planned current expenditures, which are of an exceptional nature, include the cost of severance pay for public enterprises restructured in 1998. No additional allocations were made for the health and education sectors, because of significant delays encountered in previous years in spending the regular budgetary allocations in these areas. The government willprepare, with the assistance of the World Bank, a report that should explain these bottlenecks and propose remedial actions before end-September 1999; it will also initiate well-targeted programs to alleviate poverty in major urban centers by the same date.  

15. Total revenue is projected to increase marginally in 1999 to 16.8 percent of GDP. This would reflect higher proceeds from petroleum products taxation more than offsetting the decline in customs revenue collection stemming from the 1998 trade liberalization measures, as well as the personal income tax reform. These projections incorporate repayment of tax arrears due by SENELEC/SAR, which were estimated at CFAF 7.2 billion at end-March 1999, and measures aimed at strengthening the customs and tax administrations including: (a) reinforcing the unit monitoring large taxpayers (b) generalizing the use of the single tax identification number NINEA; (c) computerizing the VAT collection unit; and (d) improving controls of custom duty-exempt regimes. The government willalso implement the decisions adopted in March l999 at the regional level on the use of temporary declining surtaxes (TDP) and establish minimum values for protection of certain products, and ensure that they are consistent with WTO rules. Following a study on the productivity of the rice sector to be finalized by end-July 1999, these supplementary protection mechanisms will be used to streamline the present rice import taxation by September 30, 1999. As the WAEMU Council of Ministers is expected to leave rice import taxation to the decision of each member country until the year 2002, Senegal intends to apply an ad-valorem tax to all rice imports--whole or broken-—while eliminating existing levies and surtaxes.  

16. The final stage of the adoption of the CET will take place on January 1, 2000. The customs duty on imports subject to the highest tariff will drop from 25 to 20 percent, the 5 percent stamp duty will be replaced by a l percent statistical tax, and duties will be abolished on imports of industrial goods processed in WAEMU countries. The cost of the trade liberalization, in terms of revenues loss, is projected at 1 percent of GDP. In part, to minimize the revenue loss, but also for efficiency reasons, the authorities will dismantle the tariff discounting mechanism by end-December 1999 as described in the Memorandum on Economic and Financial Policies (paragraph 16). The overall revenue impact of eliminating the précisions tarifaires is projected at CFAF 3 billion (0.1 percent of GDP) in 2000. The government will also implement, without delay, the WAEMU's Commission's decision concerning the 150 tariff lines for which it has lodged reservations.10  

17. Consistent with the agreed program of regional harmonization of indirect taxes and to compensate for the customs revenue losses, the government will reform the VAT effective July 1, 2000, rather than on January 1, 2000. The delay willallow the authorities to complete preparation of the reform and to initiate a public campaign to rally political support for the change which is expected to have a moderate impact on the price of some key consumption items. Under this reform, the current two-rate VAT (10 and 20 percent) willbe replaced with a VAT at the single rate of 18 percent. The VAT revenue yield after the reform is estimated at 6.9 percent of GDP on an annual basis, about one percentage point above its recent average. This willrequire, inter alia, that the VAT applies to as wide a base as possible, with a strictly limited list of exempt goods and services. Because of the 6 month delay in adopting the VAT reform relative to the date of impiementation of the CET, total VAT revenues are projected to be equivalent to 6.5 percent of GDP in 2000, implying a potential revenue loss of CFAF 11.6 billion (0.4 percent of GDP) in that year. Discussions during the midterm review on the budget for 20oO will cover, inter alia, identification of measures needed to close this gap.

21. Monetary policy will continue to be conducted at the regional level by the BCEAO through the implementation of an indirect, market-based monetary policy. Broad money is projected to grow by about 9 percent in l999, in line with growth in nominal GDP. # IV. MEDIUM-TERM BALANCE OF PAYMENTS OUTLOOK AND DEBT SUSTAINABILITY  

25. The external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) in 1999 is expected to remain at about its l998 level. A sharp increase in imports, mainly of equipment and intermediate products associated with a higher level of foreign direct investment, willead to a deterioration in the trade balance. This will be partially offset by an improvement in the service account. Relatively large private capital inflows, related to investments in the chemical industry, the privatization of SENELEC, and a World Bank loan to the energy sector, wil more than cover the current account deficit, ensuring that the program is fully financed, including the accumulation of some CFAF 71 billion in net foreign assets with the regional central bank.  

26. Beyond 1999, the external position is expected to improve to levels consistent with medium-term viability. With the share of exports in GDP growing (on the assumption that reforms are taking hold) and imports increasing broadly in line with output, the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) is expected to shrink steadily to about 6 percent of GDP in 200l and to remain at this level in the medium-term. This path of external adjustment should be sustainable, as deficits are expected to be financed by foreign capital  

inflows in the form of foreign direct investment, donors assistance, and borrowing on concessional terms. Further trade liberalization will be implemented under the last phase of the CET, as discussed above. The aggregate trade restrictiveness, as measured by the Fund's index, was estimated at 6 in l998 (on a scale from 1 to 10), but is expected to improve with the timely implementation of the final phase of tariff reduction in the year 2000.14  

27. The conclusions'' of the debt sustainability analysis conducted in April 1998 remain broadly valid, based on current guidelines. The staff plans to reassess Senegal's position under the enhanced HIPC Initiative proposed by the G-7 Cologne Summit, once an enhanced Initiative has been approved by the Fund and Bank Executive Boards. Nevertheless, the sustainability of the external position over the medium-term will need to be supported by a prudent external debt policy. After the signing of most rescheduling agreements with Paris Club creditors following the stock-of-debt reduction operation of June l998, the authorities are pursuing discussions with non-Paris Club bilateral creditors to secure debt relief on terms at least comparable to Naples terms. The Senegalese authorities have also expressed their intention to limit external borrowing to loans that are highly concessional, and to continue to seek support from donors mainly in the form of grants. In addition, Senegal is looking into the possibility of requesting debt conversions that would benefit the social sectors and the environment, after considering carefully the short-term implications of such conversions.  

28.In view of Senegal's medium-term balance of payments outlook, good prospects for continued international assistance on concessional terms, and given the authorities' good record in meeting debt service payments, Senegal should have no difficulties in servicing its obligations to the Fund.

# VI. STAFF APPRAISAL  

32. Over the last few years, Senegal has implemented strong macroeconomic and structural policies that contributed to improving the economic and financial situation. Real GDP growth has increased, inflation has abated, and the external current account position has improved. The authorities have continued these policies under the 1998/99 program supported by the first annual ESAF arrangement. The economy has responded wellto these policies, despite adverse weather conditions that reduced the contribution of the agriculture sector to growth. However, the economic and financial situation remains fragile and requires further action to achieve a growth rate high enough to enable the government to effectively address the problem of poverty. The key areas needing stronger action by the authorities include:  

Fiscal consolidation: In this area, the authorities' policies should aim at maintaining the basic balance in surplus so as to increase public sector saving. After the impact of the 1999 supplementary finance law, the overall expenditure ratio should revert to around 18 percent of GDP, a level that can be financed from current receipts. The  

authorities should also be fully prepared for the move to the final phase of the common external tariff on January 1, 2000, as intended. In this context, measures should be taken at an early date to remove all impediments, such as the precisions tarifaires, that are inconsistent with the CET. Appropriate measures must also be taken, including the introduction of a single rate VAT with strictly limited exemptions, to offset the revenue loss stemming from the lowering of the import duties. These steps should be supplemented with a reform of rice taxation in favor of a simple ad valorem customs duty applicable to all rice imports, and the elimination of all existing surcharges.  

Monetary and financial sector policy: The authorities should continue to support the prudent monetary policy of the BCEAO while seeking to develop further instruments of indirect monetary management. The government should continue to make every effort to ensure that all the prudential ratios of the WAMU Banking Commission are respected by all banks.  

Structural reforms: the authorities should expedite privatization or restructuring of all enterprises selected to be offered for sale or restructured in 1999. The reform of the National Retirement Fund should be adopted before year-end and implemented in 2000. Equally important is the implementation of the reform of the promotion system of the civil service. Lessons learned from the current seniority cum merit-based promotion system, particularly its impact on the wage bill, should be useful to the authorities in preparing further reforms to establish a promotion system solely based on merit. Pressing ahead decisevely with sectoral policies, especially concerning the liberalization of the energy and transport sectors, combined with strong policies to develop human resources, would result in significant efficiency gains and strengthened growth.  

Private sector development and investment promotion: the authorities should redouble efforts to strengthen the judicial system to ensure that private property rights are fully protected and that the OHADA Uniform Act on security is translated into appropriate national legislation on recovery and enforcement. More immediately however, the government should prepare and implement an action plan, based on the recommendations of the FIAS study, to address factors that inhibit private investors' activities in Senegal.  

Governance and transparency issues: the authorities are encouraged to step up the fight against fiscal fraud by tightening procedures at the customs office, while defining and enforcing transparent rules for granting fishing licences. The audit of the Fonds de Promotion Economique should be carried out without delay, and in a fully transparent manner.  

Finally, the authorities should continue to improve the statistical database to guide formulation of economic and financial policies in a timely manner. In this regard, the publication of a revised series on foreign-financed investment outlays is a welcome step. Also, they should ensure that the computer systems in Senegal are ready for the year 200o by accelerating the timetable of preparedness, devoting enough time to test the critical systems, and putting in place remedial actions before end-December 1999.  

33. The authorities are embarking on the 1999/2000 program a few months ahead of the start of the presidential elections scheduled for early 2OOO. There are some risks that a number of critical reforms may be postponed because of the political campaign. The authorities should be encouraged to resist such temptations which can only set back Senegal's reform program and durably affect prospects for growth and for reducing poverty. However, in light of the strong policies implemented over the last few years, the encouraging results obtained thus far, and the strength of the policies adopted by the government for 1999-2000, the staff recommends approval of Senegal's program for the second year under the ESAF arrangement.

# I. INTRODUCTION  

1. In 1998 the Government of Senegal embarked on a new medium-term economic and financial program supported by a three-year arrangement under the Enhanced Structural Adjustment Facility (ESAF), covering the period 1998-2000. The program is designed to build on the positive results achieved during the previous period (1994-97), to promote strong and sustained growth and reduce poverty, while reinforcing domestic and external financial viability. To achieve these objectives, the government has focused its actions on the consolidation of the fiscal position, liberalization of the trade regime, and on the deepening of structural reforms, including an ambitious privatization program; decisive reforms in the energy, transportation, and agriculture sectors; the modermization of public administration; the creation of a environment propitious for private sector development; and an intensification of efforts to develop human resources. To support these reforms, Senegal has continued to benefit from substantial assistance from the international community, in particular from the International Monetary Fund, the World Bank, the African Development Bank, the European Union, and several bilateral partners, which also granted a stock of debt operation in June 1998 under the aegis of the Paris Club.  

2. As indicated in Section Il of this memorandum, implementation of the first annual program has been satisfactory, though there were delays in completing several structural reforms. The government of Senegal is determined to make further progress in its reform program under a second annual program to be supported by the ESAF arrangement, as outlined in Sections Il and IV.  

# II. RECENT ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENTS  

3． Under the first year's ESAF-supported program, economic developments in Senegal were positive, with increased growth, lower inflation and a reduced external current account deficit compared to 1997. Real GDP growth is estimated to have been 5.7 percent in 1998, with a strong performance in the secondary and especially tertiary sectors offsetting a further decline in agricultural production. Inflation was reduced to an end-period rate of 0.9 percent compared with an increase of 1.9 percent at end-December 1997, helped in part by a decline in the retail prices of petroleum products. The external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) improved slightly compared with the previous year, partly because of the impact of debt service reduction, following the stock of debt operation under the aegis of the Paris Club.  

4. Budget execution in 1998 was better than programmed,' with overall revenue exceeding the target, and total expenditure held below program limits. The basic fiscal surplus,? at 2.3 percent of GDP, was nearly double the amount programmed, while the overal budget deficit (on a commitment basis and excluding grants), at l.2 percent of GDP, was well below the 2 percent of GDP programmed. Government revenue exceeded the program target by 1 percentage point of GDP to reach CFAF 460 billion (16.7 percent of GDP), largely because of strong receipts from taxes on foreign trade. These were higher than forecast, despite the tariff reduction implemented in April 1998 in the context of WAEMU's Common External Tariff (CET). The higher-than-expected customs duty collection was the result of continued efforts at improving customs administration, widening of the tax base, and also a lesser recourse by importing industries to the tariff discounting mechanism, the precisions tarifaires. Public expenditure, at 17.8 percent of GDP, was in line with the program target, with the overruns on the special and correspondent accounts being offset by lower-thantargeted outlays on current expenditures. The latter were below forecast because of a lower wage bill due to delays in introducing the merit-based promotion system in the civil service, reduced interest payments on account of the 1998 Paris Club stock of debt operation, and a lower level of subsidies on butane gas and fuel oil sold to SENELEC in line with depressed international oil prices. However, advances granted by the Treasury to cover the operational losses of the National Retirement Fund (FNR) and the Urban Community of Dakar, and delays in reimbursements by the National Post Office of advances granted by the Treasury contributed to a deterioration in the deficit on the special accounts and correspondent accounts.  

5. The money supply rose in line with nominal GDP growth. Senegal's net foreign assets increased by CFAF 34.5 billion (5.9 percent of beginning-of-period money stock), making a significant contribution to the increase of the net foreign assets of the Central Bank of the West African Monetary Union (WAMU). The strong fiscal performance enabled the authorities to reduce net bank claims on the government below program projections. Credit to the private sector increased by 1l.2 percent. The banking system remains broadly healthy, and compliance with the prudential ratios of the regional Commission bancaire was largely satisfactory, although the level of insider lending in one bank remains above the regional norm; on the other hand, the restructuring and recapitalization of the Banque SenegaloTunisienne were completed.

6. Significant progress was made towards adoption of WAEMU's CET. Firstly, the firstphase in tariff reductions was implemented in April 1998, which lowered the unweighted average assessed duty rate on non-WAEMU dutiable imports from 19 percent to 16.3 percent; secondly, the government adopted in January $1999^{3}$ a classification of imported goods into four tariff categories consistent with the WAEMU guidelines. Pending the adoption of community-wide arrangements, existing surtaxes were maintained. The number of précisions tarifaires had been halved in April l998, with the result that the c.i.f. value of imports benefiting from the application of precisions tarifaires fellby 45 percent compared to 1997. Nonetheless, because of a delay in the uniform application of appropriate tariff classifications by all WAEMU member countries, the précisions tarifaires were not fully eliminated by end-1998 as had been planned. Furthermore, petroleum product marketing and pricing were fully liberalized. The authorities began adjusting domestic retail prices automatically each month to reflect changes in world market price of petroleum products and of the value of the US dollar; as a result, it was possible to reduce below projections the subsidies granted on butane gas, and on the fuel used by SENELEC to generate electricity. Nevertheless, SENELEC was unable to pay the distributors for fuel deliveries and accumulated large arrears to them. As a result, distributors accumulated arrears to the refinery SAR, which, in turn failed to pay an equivalent amount in taxes to the Treasury. Also, the amendment terminating the convention between the state and the refinery SAR is still pending.  

7. Five enterprises were privatized in 1998,\* out of 13 originally slated for sale or liquidation. The urban transport company (SOTRAC) was liquidated as planned; however, the strategic partner selected to take over the concession and the assets of the former company withdrew in early 1999. The strategic partner for SENELEC was selected in February 1999 and has assumed full management of the company. The buyer of Air Senegal was chosen in March 1999 and will start operating in June 1999. The califor bids for the groundnut company SONACOS was launched in February 1999, and the selection of the strategic partner is scheduled for July l999. A report on the economic and financial performance during 1997 of the remaining public enterprises was provided to Fund staff as scheduled. Some delays were encountered in the planned improvements in investment programming and implementation of the public expenditure reviews. In the area of good governance, the first phase of the user survey on the quality of public services was completed. The judicial reform is progressing, including efforts to step up the training of magistrates and to modernize the courts.  

8. The quantitative performance criteria and benchmarks were observed, with the exception of technical deviations in three criteria at end-June, 1998. The structural  

performance criteria and benchmarks were also observed, except for (a) a delay until October 13, 1998, in the end-August benchmark pertaining to the call for bids for the privatization of the electricity company (SENELEC), and (b) the benchmark for completion of a plan for the reform of the National Retirement Fund (FNR) because of protracted negotiations with labor unions on measures that had been proposed for restructuring the fund. Waivers were granted for the nonobservance of the criteria after implementation of corrective measures.  

# III. MEDIUM-TERM STRATEGY AND OJECTIVES  

9. Taking into account the generally satisfactory results achieved in 1998, the government has updated its medium-term strategy and objectives as set out in the Policy Framework Paper for 1999-200l. The government maintains its basic medium-term objectives to preserve macroeconomic stability and external viability, and to accelerate and deepen structural reforms in order to achieve strong and sustainable growth and to significantly reduce poverty. The program projects real GDP growth of about 6 percent a year; seeks to keep inflation below 3 percent; to maintain the basic fiscal balance in surplus, the overallbudget deficit (on a commitment basis and excluding grants) below 4.4 percent of GDP;' and to lower the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) to around 6 percent of GDP in 200l, consistent with expected flows of project assistance and private foreign investment.  

10. Five key areas have been identified for action to accelerate and deepen structural reforms. First, the government will complete the privatization program and restructuring of the enterprises remaining in its portfolio by end-2ooo.% Second, with the assistance of the World Bank, it will embark upon an ambitious institutional reform of the transportation sector so as to lower costs of services and improve the competitiveness of the economy. Third, also with World Bank and other donors' assistance, the authorities will accelerate the reforms in the energy, water, and agricultural sectors. Fourth, they will deepen action for private sector development and investment promotion, by reducing the structural rigidities that impede private initiative, and by streamlining regulatory procedures affecting economic activity and investment. Fifh, the government will implement a coherent program of good governance with emphasis on: fostering the rule of law, vigorously tackling corruption and promoting transparency, reform and modernization of public administration and public services, improvement of the judicial system and its operations, and implementation of appropriate decentralization policies.

# IV. THE 1999 PR0GRAM  

11. Consistent with the revised medium-term strategy, under the 1999 program (April l, 1999-March 31, 20o0), the government projects a GDP growth of about 6 percent, led by a recovery in agricultural output, and seeks to limit inflation at 2 percent and contain the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) at 7 percent of GDP. The program also aims at increasing domestic savings and investment by about one percentage point of GDP each, to 14 and 20 percent of GDP, respectively.

# B. Monetary and Financial Sector Reform  

22. The Central Bank of West African States (BCEAO) will continue to conduct a prudent monetary policy consistent with the growth, inflation, and balance of payments objectives of the WAMU. Money demand in 1999 is expected to grow broadly in line with nominal GDP. Net foreign assets are projected to increase by the equivalent of 11.2 percent of beginning of period money stock, which should again enable Senegal to make a significant net contribution to the net foreign assets of the BCEAO. The net domestic assets of the banking system are expected to decline by the equivalent of about 2 percent of beginning-of-period money stock, principally because the projected expansion of credit to the economy of about 9 percent wil be more than offset by a decline in net credit to the government.  

23. The health of the banking system has continued to improve. Following the restructuring of the Banque Senegalo-Tunisienne (BST), all banks now meet minimum capital requirements and most meet the other prudential regulations of the regional Commission bancaire. Efforts are being made to ensure that a large commercial bank observes the insider lending ratio, including, possibly, through the transfer of some of the loans. Nonetheless, the amount of non-performing loans remains a potential source of fragility, and a bank specializing in agricultural credit may continue to require close monitoring. The adequacy of the legal framework and of procedures essential for the recovery of non-performing loans will be significantly improved by the adoption of the OHADA recovery and enforcement procedures. The government will continue to support the efforts of the WAMU Commission bancaire to ensure that allprudential regulations are observed and to strengthen the prudential arrangements in the zone, and will vigorously support any recommendations resulting from on-site inspections. It willalso pay particular attention to monitoring the development and solvency of mutual savings and loan institutions.  

24.The government will continue to support improved access of Senegalese business to capital markets, including through the WAEMU regional stock exchange, which began operations in late l998, so as to enhance the efficiency of intermediation between private savings and investments. It willalso contribute to the expansion of the network of mutual savings and loan institutions, and other forms of micro-lending, which is of particular importance for smaller enterprises.  

# C. Structural Reforms  

25.The policy actions in the structural area are among the government's highest priorities for the acceleration of economic growth and the achievement of a lasting reduction in poverty. They include the privatization program, support for private sector development, the national program of good governance, key reforms in the energy, agriculture, and transport sectors, the development of human resources, improvements in education and health, poverty reduction, and initiatives under way to promote trade and economic integration among the WAEMU countries.  

26.To complete the reform of the public enterprise sector, the government has selected eighteen enterprises to be privatized in 1999-2000. Thirteen of these willbe put up for sale in $1999^{9}$ , including six large enterprises: additional government shares in the electricity company (SENELEC); the groundnut processing company (SONACOS); the urban transport company (SOTRAC); the Meridien President Hotel; the textile development company (SODEFITEX); and the Dakar-Bamako line of the national railway (SNCS). Concerning SOTRAC, steps are being taken to select a new strategic partner and a new, mostly privately owned operating company will start its activities in November 1999. The hotel Meridien-President will be sold through international tender in August 1999. Concerning SODEFITEX, the government has submitted the draft law concerning its privatization to Parliament and intends to finalize this transaction by November 30, 1999; when finalized, the government's share of the capital will be lowered from 77.5 to 30 percent through a sale of shares to cotton producers, company employees, and small-holders. A company (SETl) has already been set up for the operation of the Dakar-Bamako line of the National Railways Company (SNCS); it will start operating by end-December 1999. The other companies scheduled to be privatized in 1999 are a tapestry weaving company (MSAD); an industrial promotion and research company (SONEPl); a real estate company (SICAP); and the company to manage the Dakar industrial estate (SODIDA). The status of the equipment research center (CEREEQ) will be reviewed, and the assets of the national drilling company (SONAFOR) will be sold.  

27. In 2000, three other companies will be privatized, the film distribution company (SIDEC); the company for development of the Petite Cote (SAPCO); and the reinsurance company (SENRE). The assessment of the national lottery (LONASE) and the international foreign trade center (CICES) willstart in 20O0 and their privatization could take place only in 2001. After completing these sales, only a very small number of public service companies will remain in the government's portfolio and the Government's share in the combined share capital of public enterprises will have been reduced from 87 percent in l994 to 43 percent in 2001. The remaining enterprises will continue to be restructured and strengthened.  

Concerning the National Postal Services, the government will establish a strategy to guarantee its long-term viability. The government will provide Fund staff with the report on the economic and financial performance of public enterprises in l998, as soon as possible, and in any case before end-December, 1999.  

28. Private sector development and investment promotion is a top priority on the government's agenda. This includes, in particular, the adoption of a comprehensive private sector strategy to promote private initiative and develop a favorable environment for private sector activity, including through a reinforcement of the operation of business associations. To streamline administrative procedures for private operators and investors, the recommendations retained by the Government from the World Bank FIAS's investors roadmap report will be implemented before end-1999. These recommendations could include a better functioning of the one stop window for investors.(guichet unique), a reduction in multiple sectoral approvals and registrations, and improved access to public land. Also, the Promotion Agency for investors and exporters will be set up before year-end.  

29. The government is firmly committed to implement its national program of good governance which has four major components. First, the strengthening of the rule of law, together with improving public awareness and the deepening of the democratic system, willbe foremost in the fight against corruption, including the reduction of all forms of fiscal fraud, for example customs fraud and irregularities in the granting of fishing licenses. Second, the reform of the central government willbe deepened and willinclude a further review of existing civil service regulations and improved human resources management, including preparation of staffing plans for ministries. The new pay and promotion system, which had been delayed, will be effectively implemented before December 31, 1999. This system, which combines both automatic and merit components, constitutes an intermediate step towards a fuly merit-based promotion system. The second phase of the user survey on the quality of public services (civil servants survey) will be undertaken before end-June, l999 and will be followed immediately thereafter by a national focus-group discussion that will include representatives of all sectors of society. Third, the ongoing reform of the legal and judicial system wil be accelerated, inter alia, by increasing budgetary allocations for the administration of justice, improving the training of magistrates and judicial personnel in commercial law, and implementing the new recovery and enforcement procedures consistent with the OHADA uniform acts on collateral. Fourth, the planned decentralization policies will focus on capacity building of local entities and their improved financing through the municipal development agency (ADM) and the regional development agency (ARD) which will be set up before end-1999.  

30. Concerning sectoral policies, the government wil move decisively to implement the measures outlined in the PFP in the priority areas of agriculture and fisheries, water management, energy, transport, and the environment. It will, in particular, adopt a concrete policy on the rational use and conservation of fish resources and launch a comprehensive reform of the transport sector with a wider participation of the private sector. Continued  

reforms in the energy sector include, in particular, the launch before end-December 1999 of the capacity enhancing investments at the refinery (SAR).  

31. The development of human resources and poverty alleviation are also high on the government agenda, which intends to implement fully the actions outlined in the PFP relating to the areas of health, education, population policy, the role of women, and the fight against poverty. In particular, the 1O-year education and training program (PDEF) wil be adopted before end-June, 1999. Also, as already noted, a study identifying the reasons for delays in spending regular budgetary allocations in the areas of health and education and an action plan to remedy this situation willbe communicated to the Fund staff before end-September 1999.  

32. Senegal actively participates in the initiatives under way to promote economic integration among WAEMU countries. These are guided by the action plan adopted in January 1999 by the WAEMU Heads of State and Government, which includes: reinforcing the convergence of macroeconomic policies and performance, continuation of structural reforms, and acceleration of regional integration, including through timely adoption of appropriate sectoral policies. The initiatives at the regional level pertain to moving to the last phase of the common external tarif, enacting the supplementary protection mechanisms, the common investment code, the harmonization of indirect and direct taxation, the common budgetary nomenclature, accounting and procedures framework, and ensuring the free movement of people, multilateral surveillance, sectoral policies, and structural funds.  

# V. EXTERNAL SECTOR AND DEBT SUSTAINABILITY  

33. Senegal's medium term balance of payments outlook remains favorable. The worsening of the trade deficit projected in 1999, due to a faster increase in imports compared to exports, willresult in a slight deterioration in the current account deficit as a ratio to GDP. Nonetheless, sizeable capital inflows linked to investments made by the chemical company (ICS), the privatization of SENELEC, and disbursements from the World Bank, are expected to lead to a surplus in the overall balance of payments position, and a build-up of net foreign assets with the BCEAO projected to reach some CFAF 70 billion. After taking into account financing expected from the World Bank and the Fund, the second year program supported by the ESAF arrangement will be fully financed.  

34.Following the stock of debt reduction operation granted by Paris Club creditors in June l998, the government has entered into bilateral agreements with most Paris Club creditors before the March 31, I999 deadline. A second follow-up report was forwarded to the Paris Club Secretariat in May 1999. The government is taking steps to conclude bilateral agreements with other non-Paris Club bilateral creditors, on terms that are at least as favorable as those granted by Paris Club creditors.  

35. To maintain a sustainable external debt position, the government will continue to seek foreign financing in the form of grants and highly concessional borrowing. It will not contract or guarantee any new nonconcessional loans,1° nor will it allow any accumulation of external arrears during the program period. In addition, the government will continue efforts to improve collection of data on grants and foreign assistance, with a view to reconcile these data with those recorded by donors, and those included in the budget.  

# VI. STATISTICAL ISSUES  

36. The government is aware of the need to step up actions required to improve the quality and coverage of economic and social statistics. In addition to the measures already taken and those now being implemented to improve monetary, balance of payments, and production statistics, the government will implement an action plan to reduce remaining statistical weaknesses in the national accounts, foreign trade, industrial production, and social indicators. To this end, it will increase the resources allocated to its statistics departments and will seek technical assistance from traditional development partners and the IMF. Also, to facilitate preparation and monitoring of economic policy, the government will ensure regular publication of economic and social development statistics by the Directorate of Forecasting and Statistics of the Ministry of Economy, Finance, and Planning. Finally, the government will persevere with the implementation of the accelerated program to tackle the potentially devastating impact of the Y2K bug in the administration's computer systems, in particular the customs GAINDE system. The Y2K Pilot Committee has been provided with the appropriate financial resources in order to complete the task of making the computer systems immune to the year 200o bug, by November 30, 1999.

In addition, the program will include continuous performance criteria on nonaccumulation of new domestic payments arrears and nonaccumulation of new external payments arrears. Also, during the program period, the authorities will not impose exchange restrictions, introduce multiple currency practices, introduce bilateral payments arrangements that are inconsistent with Article VIlI, and impose import restrictions for balance of payments reasons.

# VII. Exchange Rate Arrangement  

Senegal is a member of the West African Monetary Union (WAMU). The exchange system, common to all members of the union, is free of restrictions on the making of payments and transfers for current international transactions. # Commentary on lending operations  

1. The overarching objective of the Bank's assistance strategy for Senegal is to reduce the incidence of poverty and create gainful employment, especially for youth. The approved lending program for the period fiscal year (FY) 1998-2000 under the Country Assistance Strategy (CAS) is almost double the actual FY 1995-97 level; it aims at deepening the structural reforms under way. Achievement of the poverty-reduction objective willbe supported by a two-pronged strategy: (a) supporting policies and programs for more rapid and sustained growth; and (b) ensuring the social sustainability of programs. This strategy will be implemented through a combination of structural adjustment lending (energy sector reform and trade liberalization), the sector investment program (SIP), specific investment lending, and appropriate onlending operations. Analytical work on the transitional costs of trade reform, currentiy under way, is expected to lead to the preparation of the trade reform adjustment credit planned for fiscal year FY 2000. The ongoing public expenditure review is helping the government improve the composition and efficiency of public expenditures for the period 1998-2000.  

2. As of December 31, 1998, the World Bank had approved ll4 operations in Senegal for a total amount, less cancellations, of US\$1,872.l million, consisting of 9i IDA credits (including three Special Fund credits and two Special Facility credits for Africa) for US\$1,742.8 million, and 23 IBRD loans for US\$129.3 millon. Past projects have supported agricultural diversification, irrigation, human resources development, institutional development, and expansion of the country's infrastructure, particularly its transport system. In recent years, emphasis has shifted to utilizing and maintaining existing facilities better, and to helping the government resolve some of the key issues related to long-term development prospects. The current portfolio represents a commitment value of about US\$682.2 million, with undisbursed balance to about US\$454.1 million. The portfolio is composed of 18 operations, involving the agriculture sector, human resources (population/health/nutrition/education), infrastructure/urban development, energy, and private sector development. Two projects were approved in FY l999: an agriculture sector project (agricultural services and producers organizations) and a transport project (second transport sector adjustment).  

3. As of May 31, 1999, the International Financial Corporation (IFC) had six investments totaling about US\$30.3 million. The IFC's current portfolio includes two financial services (Banque de I'habitat du Senegal and Societé generale de crédits automobiles); one fertilizer operation (Industrie chimiques du Senegal); one leather-manufacturing operation (Senegal tanerie); and one power project (the first independent power project in Senegal). Two further investments in power and mining, totaling US\$7.23 million, were approved in FY 1998.  

4. The Bank and the IFC will continue to work closely and coordinate their respective roles in providing an enabling environment for private sector development in Senegal. The Bank will concentrate on factors that improve the business environment, and the IFC, helped by the opening of its office in Dakar in February 1997, will increase its direct support to the private sector, especially in power, mining, privatization and the development of a capital market.
