# Executive Summary  

# Recent developments  

Senegal has generally maintained a sound macroeconomic policy, and the implementation of the medium-term reform strategy has been broadly satisfactory. In the run-up to presidential elections, from late 1999 through the first quarter of 2000, some slippages emerged in the implementation of the fiscal program and structural reforms. The new government has recently taken corrective actions following extensive discussions with the staff.  

The quantitative and structural performance criteria and benchmarks for end-August and endSeptember 1999 were observed, except for the structural performance criteria governing the simplification of rice taxation, due to delays in approving the regional compensatory import levy, and the submission of the draft 200o budget to Fund staff which was delayed for technical reasons. Waivers are being requested. The end-December quantitative benchmark on net credit to central government, six out of seven structural benchmarks at end-December 1999, and some of the quantitative benchmarks at end-March 20O0 were also not observed. The measures adopted by the new administration will help address the underlying problems; the government has set new dates for meeting structural benchmarks that have been missed (one was already met in April 2000, and the five others will be implemented during the period June 2000 through January 1, 2001).  

Economic growth in 1999 was about 5 percent, somewhat below expectation, despite a rebound in agricultural output because of favorable weather conditions, as frequent power outages slowed down activities in the manufacturing and service sectors. Inflation was below 1 percent. The external current account deficit (excluding offcial transfers) is estimated to have been close to program target (7 percent of GDP), notwithstanding a strong rise of petroleum products imports bills due to price increases. The outlook for 2000 is for a GDP growth rate of around 5 percent, subdued inflation, and an external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) of about $6\%$ percent.  

Overall fiscal performance in 1999 was somewhat better than programmed, despite slippages in the electoral context, because of slightly higher than projected revenue collection and continued expenditure restraint. For 2oo0, the overall budget deficit (excluding grants) is projected to be 4.3 percent of GDP, exceeding initial projections. The widening of the deficit, despite larger than expected revenue, is due to increased appropriations (equivalent to l.3 percent of GDP) to accommodate a transfer to the refinery for the freeze in the retail prices of petroleum products since February 20oo, to cover subsidies on butane gas and fuel deliveries to the power company (to mitigate the impact of higher oil prices on consumers), as well as an unexpected deficit of the postal services, and a general salary increase for civil servants.  

Progress was made in the implementation of structural reforms in a number of areas. However, there was some weakening in the scope and pace of reforms. Trade liberalization was not as deep as envisaged; only 3 out of 13 enterprises slated for privatization in 1999 were sold; and the audit of the Fonds de Promotion Economique was somewhat delayed. As a result of the electoral process, the privatization program slowed further in early 2000 and it is only recently that the new government has begun taking steps to provide impetus.

An updated debt sustainability analysis indicates that the external debt burden of Senegal at end1998 is unsustainable by the criteria of the enhanced HIPC Initiative. A HIPC decision paper has been prepared and is being issued separately. Concurrently, the authorities have prepared an interim poverty reduction strategy paper, which lays out plans for developing a comprehensive poverty reduction strategy in a participatory manner.  

# Issues emphasized in the staff appraisal  

The importance of preserving a sound fiscal position. With the fullimplementation of the West African Economic and Monetary Union's (WAEMU) common external tariff on January 1, 2000, measures directed at improving government revenue will be required, including the introduction on July 1, 2000 of VAT at a single rate of 18 percent with few exceptions. Continuing prudent expenditure management also will be needed, including in particular containing the wage bill the subsidies needed to maintain the retail price freeze on oil products at current levels, and the deficit of the postal service.  

The urgency of steadfast implementation of the broad based structural and sectoral reform program, including trade liberalization, privatization, the judiciary, and energy and transportation sectors. These are needed to increase efficiency and productivity gains, and enhance competitiveness of the economy.  

The importance of working diligently to prepare a comprehensive poverty reduction strategy in a truly participatory process involving civil society and development partners.  

# 1. INTRODUCTION  

1. Discussions for the first review of the program supported by the second annual arrangement' under the current three-year Poverty Reduction and Growth Facility (PRGF) arrangement and for the 1999 Article IV consultation were carried out in Dakar during November 30--December 13, 1999.² The discussions were concluded in Washington in April 2000, after the new cabinet formed following the presidential elections of early 2000 had had an opportunity to reassess the policy commitments undertaken by the outgoing government covering the PRGF review, the Article IV consultation, the decision point document under the HIPC, as well as the Interim PRSP.3 The debt sustainability analysis (DSA) was updated in light of the new eligibility criteria under the Enhanced HIPC Initiative, and it is summarized in the decision point document on the HIPC Initiative (issued separately). In the attached letter and memorandum on economic and financial policies (MEFP) dated May 5, 2000 (Appendix I), the Minister of Economy and Finance, reviews progress made in 1999 and sets out the measures to be implemented in 2000 to achieve the objectives of the program. Also, drawing on Senegal's experience with poverty reduction policies and programs dating back to 1997, the authorities have prepared an interim poverty reduction strategy paper (I-PRSP), which lays out the process that should lead to preparation of Senegal's PRSP. Senegal's relations with the Fund and the World Bank are summarized in Appendices II and III, respectively.  

2. In concluding the last Article IV consultation on November 25, 1998, Directors urged the authorities to persevere with fiscal consolidation and to pursue more decisively their program of structural reform. Of particular importance were the completion of the privatization program as scheduled, and the elimination of obstacles faced by private  

investors so as to strengthen growth in line with Senegal's long-term economic potential, while diversifying the economy and tackling pervasive poverty.  

3． Senegal's database is generally satisfactory for the purpose of surveillance (Appendix IV), and the authorities are providing core data to the Fund on a regular basis.  

4.The new President, Mr. Abdoulaye Wade, of the Senegalese Democratic Party (PDS), was elected in the second round of presidential elections on March 19, 200 after 40 years of administration by the ruling Socialist Party. The new government was formed in early April 20o0. Following the election, there was some labor union action in the private sector to demand higher pay that was quickly settled.  

II. CURRENT SETTING  

# A. Background  

5. Senegal's medium-term strategy seeks to improve on the growth rates achieved since the 1994 devaluation of the CFA franc, by increasing investment and enhancing productivity through an acceleration of structural reforms. The strategy has a social policy component directly centered on improving education and health services in the context of a well-targeted poverty reduction program.  

6. Implementation of the strategy under the 1999 program has been mixed. The quantitative and structural performance criteria and benchmarks for end-August and endSeptember 1999 were observed, except for the structural performance criteria on the simplification of rice taxation and the submission of the draft 200o budget to Fund staff, for which corresponding waivers have been requested. The current system of rice taxation will be replaced by a much simpler regional import levy (TCI), which willautomatically come into force once the West African Economic and Monetary Union (WAEMU) Commission has determined the trigger price underlying this tax.4 The delay in forwarding the 20O0 draft budget to the staff was due to technical factors, as discussed in the government memorandum.  

7. In the run-up to the elections in late-1999 through the first quarter of 2000, macroeconomic management weakened and the schedule of implementation of structural reforms began to slip. Thus, several performance criteria and benchmarks were missed although the adjustment is now broadly back on track. The December 31, 1999 quantitative benchmark on net credit to central government was not observed, because some state entities drew down their deposits with the banking system at end-year. As of March 31, 2000, the quantitative benchmarks on the basic budgetary balance, net credit to central government, and the wage bill were not observed, due, notably to delays in implementing the reform of the National Retirement Fund (FNR), a freeze of the retail prices of petroleum products, and a large deficit of the postal service. Six structural benchmarks for November 30 and December 31, 1999 have not been observed. The external audit of the Fonds de Promotion Economique (FPE) was provided to the Fund with some delay; the implementation of the recommendations prepared by the World Bank (FIAS) on steps needed to facilitate private investment has been delayed until end-June 20o0; the call for bids for the productivity enhancing investments at the refinery (SAR) are now scheduled to be launched in May 2000 and the result published in July; the privatization of the cotton company (SODEFITEX) is scheduled for end-September 20o0; the launching of the new urban transport Company in Dakar is now scheduled for October 2000; and implementation of the reform of the FNR has been pushed back to January 1, 2001. However, the end-December 1999 structural performance criterion on the elimination of the remaining precisions tarifaires under Senegal's jurisdiction has been met, and the same holds for the structural benchmark on the reduction by CFAF 9 bilion in the stock of statutory advances from the central bank.  

# B. Economic Performance in 1999  

8. Economic developments in 1999 were encouraging. Indications are that real GDP growth in 1999 was about 5 percent, somewhat less than the authorities' initial projection of about 6 percent (Figure 1 and Table 1). While output in the agriculture sector rebounded because of good weather conditions, manufacturing and service sectors activities were adversely affected by frequent power outages during the first nine months of the year, due to the old age of the installations. Inflation was low, with end-of-period prices rising by only $\%$ a percentage point over the previous year. As a result, the real effective exchange rate, based on the consumer price index (CPI), depreciated by some 30 percent relative to the level reached before the 1994 devaluation of the CFA franc (Figure 2). This suggests that Senegal's competitiveness has not been eroded. The external current account deficit (excluding official transfers equivalent to 3.8 percent of GDP) is estimated to have been broadly in line with the program target (7 percent of GDP), notwithstanding the strong rise in import prices of petroleum products. Following the April 1998 Paris Club stock-of-debt operation, the authorities concluded all bilateral rescheduling agreements with Paris Club creditors. A non-Paris Club bilateral creditor also granted concessional debt relief, and the government is seeking similar relief with other bilateral creditors on terms comparable to those granted by Paris Club creditors.  

9. Fiscal performance. In 1999, the central government recorded a basic fiscal surpluss of 1.7 percent of GDP, and the overall budgetary deficit (excluding grants, totaling the equivalent of 2.1 percent of GDP) was better than targeted (4.4 percent of GDP). This is because revenue performance was slightly stronger than projected while spending and net lending remained below program projections. Externally financed investment and on-lending were also lower-than-expected, and the deficit in the special and correspondent accounts was sharply reduced by year's end through implementation of decisive steps to arrest slippages at the postal service.However, the end-December ceiling on net bank credit to the government was not observed because public entities, including the social security and the pension funds, drew down their deposits with the banking system toward year-end, and disbursement of the second tranche of the World Bank energy sector credit was delayed, pending full implementation of the agreed reforms in this area. The latter is in the process of being resolved.  

10. Senegal's monetary policy is conducted at the regional level by the Central Bank of West African States (BCEAO). In 1999, broad money growth in Senegal was 13 percent, well above nominal GDP growth. Instead of a programmed decline, net credit to the government6 rose by 1.1 percent of beginning of period money stock, reflecting the shortfall in external disbursement. Credit to the economy is estimated to have grown by about 10 percent, in line with program projections. The banking system remained healthy overall, and most banks were profitable in 1998. A bank that had experienced problems in meeting the solvency ratio was recapitalized and privatized, and the ratio of net nonperforming assets to total credit of the banking system declined marginally.7 Nonetheless, not all banks are in compliance with the prudential ratios.\* Also, in September 1999, the regional banking commission put one troubled savings and loan institution under temporary administration.  

11. Progress was made in the implementation of structural reforms in a number of key areas as indicated in paragraph 10 of the MEFP and summarized in Box 1. However there remain several areas where the results need to be strengthened significantly.  

Trade liberalization was not as deep as envisaged. Moreover, Senegal moved to include—in the initial list of products subject to WAEMU's compensatory import levy (TCI)—-six agricultural products that were already subject to surtaxes in Senegal, which ran counter to the strategy of regional trade liberalization.  

With respect to the privatization program, only 3 out of 13 state enterprises were sold. The authorities indicated, however, that preparatory work for the other enterprises was well advanced. They also argued that some of the delays reflected the advice.of investment banks helping with the privatization program, cautioning that sales prospects would be brighter after the February/March 20o0 presidential elections.   
In the area of sectoral reforms, some delays were encountered regarding the energy and transportation sectors, and urban waterworks.   
With respect to the promotion of good governance, the audit of the FPE was completed in March 2000, rather than at end-1999.   
Faced with rapidly increasing oil prices, the outgoing government suspended in   
February 2000 the automatic adjustment of retail prices of petroleum products in line with international price developments.   
The reform of the National Retirement Fund (FNR) was postponed to January 1, 2001, a delay of one year.

# III. POLICY DISCUSSIONS  

12. The policy discussions focused on the implementation of the PRGF-supported program in 1999, the macroeconomic and structural policies required for 2000, and the process that should lead to the preparation of Senegal's PRSP. The discussions highlighted the need to adhere to program objectives and to make up for the delays experienced in the structural and sectoral areas, in particular with respect to privatization. The new authorities reaffirmed their commitment to strengthen implementation of the reform program.  

# A. Macroeconomic Objectives  

13. The staff projected that real GDP growth would average 5 percent in 2000-2001, as the contribution of the secondary and tertiary sectors to GDP growth will be lower than projected (Table 2). To achieve this growth rate, domestic investment will need to pick up and be increasingly financed by domestic savings, with private sector savings playing a major role. The authorities broadly concurred with the staff assessment that a successful implementation of the government program of structural reforms will have a favorable impact on domestic savings and efficiency. Inflation is projected to remain subdued during the period, not exceeding 2 percent per annum. The outlook for inflation is helped by low  

food staple prices (which have a large weight in the CPI), reflecting a good crop year; this would offset the expected rise in the world market price of petroleum products.  

# B. Fiscal Policy and Reforms  

14.The staff welcomed the somewhat better than projected fiscal outcome in 1999, and noted that expenditure had been kept under control, that social spending was in line with targets, and that efforts had been made late in the year to reduce the deficit of the postal service. Looking ahead, the staf stressed the need to sustain the fiscal consolidation, in particular, given the need to fund the subsidy linked to the freeze in retail prices of petroleum products and a higher than expected deficit of the postal service. The 200O budget projects a basic surplus of 1 percent of GDP compared with a surplus of $1\%$ percent of GDP that was initially programmed. The overall budget deficit (on a commitment basis and excluding grants)willexceedtheinitialtarget(Table3)becauseofhigher outlaysas described below. To achieve this target, tax revenue will increase by 1.3 percent of GDP above the program level of 15.3 percent of GDP (Table 4). This is a realistic target because the negative impact of the external tariff reform on revenue (estimated at about 1 percent of GDP) is expected to be largely offset by a continued better revenue outcome than initially projected, due in part to actions taken by the new government to reorganize the tax and customs departments, to a significantly higher yield of petroleum taxation,l' and the effect of other revenue measures detailed in paragraph 16 of the MEFP. Important among these measures are the introduction of a single-rate VAT at 18 percent by July 1, 200 with a limited number of exemptions, and the adoption of tax administration measures proposed by a recent FAD technical assistance mission (see the MEFP for details).  

15. Expenditure will be higher than foreseen in the original medium-term macroeconomic framework, largely because it was not possible for the new government to adopt measures to fully offset the policy slippages that had occurred under the previous administration. The main contributing factors are (i) a general salary increase (the first since 1994) ranging from 5 percent to 12 percent of base salaries (depending on salary grades)12 implemented in January 2000, similar to the increases granted by the private sector in 1999; (ii) subsidies on butane gas and fuel deliveries to the power company (SENELEC) larger than originally projected because of the higher world market prices; (ii) the subsidy linked to the freeze in retail prices of petroleum products, which will be capped at CFAF 17 billion (0.5 percent of GDP); (iv) the one-year delay in the reforms of the FNR which will result in an operating transfer of about CFAF 5 bilion to the FNR; and (v) the higher-than-projected deficit of the postal service. A number of measures were identified to ensure that overall expenditure will be contained within the program limits (as detailed in paragraph 17 of the MEFP).  

# C. Monetary Policy  

16.The monetary and credit policies pursued by the regional central bank (BCEAO) continue to be prudent, and consistent with the maintenance of a pegged exchange rate with the Euro and the preservation of low inflation. Also, Senegal maintains an exchange system that is free of restrictions on the making of payments and transfers for current international transactions. The expectation is that broad money in Senegal would increase by about 7.3 percent in 20o0, in line with nominal GDP growth (Table 5). Net credit to the government by the banking system is expected to decline, consistent with the planned reduction in central bank statutory advances of CFAF 20 billion in 2000, ahead of the WAEMU recommended elimination of any outstanding balance by end-2001. Credit to the economy is forecast to increase by about 10 percent, sufficient to meet the needs of the private sector.Senegal is also expected to continue to contribute to the net foreign assets of the BCEAO by some CFAF 44 billion.  

17.The staff welcomed the regional monetary authorities' continued efforts to improve the instruments of indirect management of monetary policy, as wellas the planned issuance of treasury bills to replace the statutory advances.  

18. The staff stressed that further efforts were needed to strengthen financial intermediation and increase competition in the sector, including through the opening of new bank branches under the union-wide bank licensing. The staff recommended that banks comply rapidly with the revised prudential arrangements that had been adopted recently by the regional monetary authorities. The courts aiso need to enforce procedures for the recovery of nonperforming loans as stipulated by the regional legal framework (OHADA) adopted at the national level. Furthermore, the staff emphasized the importance of monitoring the development and solvency of microfinance and mutual saving and loan institutions.  

# D. Structural and Sectoral Reforms  

19. To make up for the delays and the policy slippages that had occurred in late 1999 and early 200O, the staff urged the authorities to accelerate implementation of the structural and sectoral reform program, emphasizing especially the privatization program, improvement in the environment for the private sector, good governance, and reform in the energy, transportation, urban waterworks, and agricultural sectors. The measures and timetable retained by the authorities are described in paragraph 20 of the MEFP. Among the key measures: are the privatization of 1l enterprises; the simplification of regulatory procedures pertaining to private investment; and the fight against corruption, including in particular a full audit of all public enterprises, which has already been launched by the new government.  

# E. Development of Human Resources and Debt Relief  

20. The staff warmly welcomed the authorities' continued emphasis on placing poverty reduction and human resource development at the center of their development program, the emphasis placed on the preparation of the interim PRSP, and commitment to develop a full-fledged PRSP by end-2001, in full consultation with the civil society and the donor community. Concurrently, the government continues to aim at the medium-term targets for social sector expenditures indicated in the Policy Framework Paper (PFP), including increasing the share of current government expenditure on health care by 0.5 percentage point per year to reach the World Health Organization target of 9 percent by 2002, reorienting spending on Education toward primary education, and implementing a program for combating illiteracy. Also, in observance of a structural performance criterion for endSeptember 1999, the authorities adopted an action plan to accelerate and not delay, as in the past, the spending of regular budgetary allocations in the areas of education and health; this plan includes setting up within the Ministries of Health and Education specific units to facilitate execution of projects and programs and to monitor spending trends. Regarding Senegal's eligibility under the enhanced HIPC Initiative framework, a debt sustainability analysis was undertaken based on end-1998 data. On the basis of this joint analysis by Fund and World Bank staff, Senegal was found to be eligible for assistance in order to reach the sustainability threshold under the Initiative.14  

# F. Statistical Issues  

21. Senegal's economic database is comprehensive, despite weaknesses in national accounts,'5 industrial production, international trade, balance of payments data, and social indicators. These weaknesses do not prevent a meaningful assessment of economic policies, but the analysis of current developments is hampered by the poor quality and timeliness of some data. The implementation of the action plan to reduce these weaknesses, including the allocation of increased resources to the statistics department, should contribute to improving the situation. The authorities have also initiated an action plan to improve the quality of social development statistics, in order to facilitate preparation and implementation of the poverty reduction strategy.  

# IV. MEDIUM-TERM OUTLOOK FOR THE BALANCE OF PAYMENTS AND DEBT SUSTAINABILITY  

22. The medium-term projections for the balance of payments involve a favorable outlook for agricultural output in 20O0 and assume no significant downturn in world market prices for Senegal's exports. It is assumed that prices for primary commodities have bottomed out in 1999 and will slightly rise thereafter, in line with the latest World Economic Outlook (WEO) projections. Oil prices are expected to increase further in 20o0, but not as fast as in the previous year. With export growth expected to exceed import growth (in value terms), the external current account deficit (excluding official transfers) is projected to narrow to about $6\%$ percent of GDP in 2000 and to drop further to below 5 percent of GDP in 2004 (Table 6). Imports will reflect slower real GDP growth than initially projected, while exports are benefiting from an improved outlook for the world economy as a whole and Europe in particular. Imports of capital and intermediate goods over the medium term are expected to grow at least as fast as in 1999, owing to the coming on stream of foreign private investment projects. However, public sector capital inflows in the form of grants and loans are projected to remain the dominant sources of external financing.  

23. Senegal is expected to continue to pursue a prudent debt-management policy and to implement sound economic, financial, and structural policies. Given Senegal's fine record in meeting its debt-service obligations to the Fund, its ability to repay future obligations to the Fund is expected to remain very strong. Debt service payments to the Fund in relation to exports of goods and nonfactor services are projected to average 1.6 percent during 2000 2008 following a peak of 2.3 percent in 1999. In relation to total debt service obligations, payments to the Fund are expected to average 16 percent during 2000-2008, peaking at 25 percent in 2004, before declining rapidly thereafter.  

# V. STAFF APPRAISAL  

24. The new authorities of Senegal have confirmed their intention to implement a program of macroeconomic and structural reforms during 20o0, building on the results achieved in 1999 under the program supported by the IMF. However, it has not been possible for them to devise measures that will fully make up for weaknesses in macroeconomic management and slippages in the implementation of structural reforms in late 1999 and the first quarter of 20oo. While the new program envisages some budgetary easing compared with the original framework, it still conforms to the medium-term macroeconomic objectives. Real GDP growth was strong and inflation low in 1999, the fiscal outcome was better than projected, and real exchange rate developments suggest that Senegal's external competitiveness has been preserved. It is important, therefore, that the authorities continue the reform program in order to consolidate the gains achieved in many areas, and particularly on the fiscal front. In the period ahead, the authorities should pay particular attention to the following areas:  

With the full implementation of the CET on January 1, 2000 and the removal of all the distortionary tariff discounting mechanisms (known as the précisions tarifaires), the authorities need to redouble efforts to protect the tax base by reducing tax exemptions, while fighting tax evasion. The decision to overhaul the tax and customs departments, introduce a large-taxpayer unit, generalize the taxpayer identification number, and deepen the reform of the tax administration department represent important steps. These measures should help stem the revenue loss expected from the CET. The single-rate VAT to be introduced on July 1, 2000 at a rate of 18 percent is an cssential element which should be very restrictive with regard to exemptions.  

The authorities should maintain efforts to contain overall spending and keep the wage bill strictly within the limits set in the program. The general wage increase granted in 2000 should be combined with the effective implementation of the merit-based promotion system, and new net recruitment should be avoided so as not to enlarge unduly the size of the civil service. These steps are necessary to contain the wage bill and to maintain incentives. Given the slippages that have emerged in the run-up to the recent presidential elections, it is essential that the authorities refrain from approving a further wage increase, and resist demands for the indefinite suspension of the reform of the pension fund; the additional studies that the authorities are considering should not lead to an indefinite postponement of implementation of the current proposals for reform.  

In order to contain overall outlays within the limits agreed under the program, it will be essential that the suspension of the passthrough system for the retail prices of petroleum products is reviewed on a continuous basis, taking into account oil markets developments, as well as the cost of the subsidy borne by the budget as a result of the current retail price freeze. The authorities appropriately intend to reactivate the passthrough mechanism as soon as market prices permit or the cumulative amount spent on subsidy reaches the threshold set, whichever occurs earlier. Fundamental reforms are required to avoid any further deterioration of the financial situation of the postal service, and to keep its projected deficit within the limits set in the program for 2000.  

The budgetary allocations for the health and education sectors should be monitored carefully, as should the new mechanisms put in place to remove obstacles to the efficient use of these allocations. Additional measures may be introduced, as needed.  

The authorities should, in particular, adhere to the new calendar that has been adopted fo1 meeting the structural benchmarks that were missed at end-December, 1999.  

On trade policy, the authorities should refrain from using the safeguard measures under the CET as a mean of granting additional protection to inefficient domestic production. Products to be protected by the TCI and administratively set customs valuations should be strictly limited, so as not to undermine the trade liberalization process under way in the WAEMU area. Also, the authorities should rapidly eliminate all remaining nontariff barriers.16  

As part of the private sector-led growth strategy, the authorities should press ahead with the privatization program, which has encountered major delays in the past. A large number of significant production units should be put up for sale before year's end and measures should be taken, at an early date, to implement the reforms agreed with the World Bank regarding improvement of the regulatory and judiciary systems in the energy, transportation, and water sectors. These measures provide scope to increase efficiency of production, lower unit costs and, therefore, enhance competitiveness.  

Finally, the staff welcomes the authorities' decision to prepare, in the coming months, a comprehensive poverty reduction strategy in consultation with the civil society and development partners. The strategy should focus on the medium- and long-term policies and programs required for improving the living conditions of the population, as well as to increase access to education and health services and to gainful employment.  

25.In light of the authorities' satisfactory implementation of their program during 1999, and given the policies they intend to pursue in order to consolidate the gains achieved thus far, the staff recommends that the Board grant the waivers requested by the government on structural performance criteria (related to rice taxation and communication of the 2000 budget to Fund staff) in order to complete the first review under the second annual PRGF program.  

26. Given the delay in completing the first review under this second annual arrangement and the expiration of this arrangement in early July, the disbursement that was anticipated as the third loan under this arrangement willbe added to the first disbursement under the third annual arrangement, which is expected to be requested in November 2000. Quantitative targets for end-June and structural measures scheduled for end-June, end-July and endSeptember will serve as prior actions for approval of the third annual arrangement. As the three-year commitment period expires in April 2001, an extension of the commitment period will be necessary at the time that the third annual arrangement is approved.  

27.It is recommended that the next Article IV consultation with Senegal takes place on the standard 12-month cycle.

1. The macroeconomic and structural program adopted by the government for the period $1999-2001^{17}$ aimed at achieving strong and sustainable growth by strengthening the viability of the government's financial position and liberalizing the economy. This entails scaling back the government's role in production and commercial activities, improving the legal and regulatory framework, taking effective action against corruption, promoting good governance, and developing human resources with a view to achieving a sustainable reduction in poverty. The specific macroeconomic and structural objectives are detailed in the documents referred to in footnote 1.  

2. Performance under the program through the third quarter of 1999 has been generally satisfactory and the government met allthe quantitative performance criteria established for September 30. The quantitative benchmarks were also observed, with budget revenue surpassing the target and expenditure wages on outlays slightly below program projections (see Appendix I, Table 1). The structural performance criteria and benchmarks set for August 31 and September 30, 1999 were also met, except for the simplification of rice import taxation-which awaits the approval by the Commission of the West African Economic and Monetary Union (WAEMU) of the modalities for introducing a compensatory import levy (TCl)-and the submission of the draft budget for 2000 to Fund staff, which was somewhat behind schedule (see Appendix I, Table 2).1 The government requests waivers for nonobservance of these structural performance criteria.  

3. However, performance after September was influenced by the electoral context, from late 1999 through the first quarter of 2000, which gave rise to slippages with respect to public finances and implementation of structural reforms. The December 31, 1999 quantitative performance criterion on net credit to the central government was not observed because some state entities drew down their deposits with the banks and disbursement of external financing was delayed. As of March 31, 2000, the quantitative benchmarks on the floor for the basic budgetary balance, the ceiling on net credit to central government, and the ceiling on the wage bill were not observed, due notably to a delay in implementing the reform of the National Retirement Fund (FNR), a freeze of the retail prices of petroleum products and a large deficit of the postal service.  

4. The structural performance criterion on the elimination of the remaining precisions tarifaires under Senegal's jurisdiction was met. The same holds for the structural benchmarks on the reduction by CFA 9 billion in the stock of central bank statutory advances to the government. However, the six other structural benchmarks at end-November and endDecember 1999 were not observed. The external audit of the Fonds de Promotion Economique (FPE) was finalized and provided to the Fund with some delay; the implementation of the recommendations of the World Bank report (FIAS) on obstacles faced by private investors has been delayed until end-June 20oO; the callfor bids for the productivity enhancing investments at the refinery (SAR) is now to be launched in May 2000, and the bids will be opened by end-July, 200O; the privatization of the cotton company (SODEFITEX) is scheduled for end-September, 200O; the launching of the new urban transport company in Dakar is now scheduled for October 20o0; and the implementation of the reform of the national retirement fund has been pushed back to January 1, 2001.  

# VI. RESULTS ACHIEVED DURING 1999  

5. Real GDP growth in 1999 is estimated at about 5 percent. This was slightly below the initial forecast despite the rebound in agricultural sector output facilitated by good rainfall, because of disruptions in the supply of electric power that adversely affected growth in the secondary and tertiary sectors. Average inflation, as measured by the harmonized consumer price index (Dakar) was 0.8 percent compared with the program target of 2 percent. Based on these price developments, the real effective exchange rate of the CFA franc remained stable during the year and depreciated by about 30 percent relative to its level prior to the l994 devaluation. Export volume is estimated to have increased by approximately 4 percent despite a slowdown in fertilizer and phosphoric acid exports, because of transportation problems which disrupted production. Although import volume grew moderately, the increase in the oil bill subsequent to the rise in world oil prices is estimated to have led to an external current account deficit, equivalent to 7.3 percent of GDP (excluding official transfers of 3.8 percent of GDP), slightly above program projections.  

6. With respect to government finance, budgetary revenue for 1999 as a whole reached 17.1 percent of GDP, slightly above the programmed level. Budget outlays were slightly lower than projected, offsetting a significant deterioration in the deficit of the special and correspondent accounts caused, inter alia, by the inability of the postal service to reimburse the advances received from the Treasury. The basic budget surplus'9 (1.7 percent of GDP) was in line with projections, and the overall budget deficit (on a commitments basis and  

excluding grants) was nearly l percentage point of GDP lower than program target (4.4 percent of GDP). Nonetheless, recourse to domestic bank financing was larger-thanexpected because some state entities, including the social security and pension funds, drew down their deposits with the banking system, and a delay in the disbursement of the second tranche of the World Bank energy sector credit due to failure to implement agreed reforms in this area. As a result, the government missed the quantitative benchmark set for December 31, 1999 on net borrowing from the banking system.  

7. Current expenditure (including the wage bill) was kept slightly below the program level through a strict management of public outlays. The level of investment expenditure was somewhat below forecast, due to a lower than projected inflow of foreign assistance. Expenditures authorized under the supplementary 1999 budget law were executed on schedule. As mentioned above, the deficit in the special and correspondent accounts worsened noticeably, foremost because the postal service was unable to repay advances made on its behalf by the Treasury, which reached CFAF 15.7 bilion by end-September, 1999. The government took decisive steps to correct this slippage, and the outstanding advances were reduced to CFAF 2.9 billion by December 31, 1999.  

8. The money supply grew by 13.3 percent in 1999, much faster than nominal GDP growth. Domestic credit rose by 8.3 percent of beginning of period money stock, reflecting the combined effect of an expansion of credit to the economy by 10.4 percent and a small increase in net credit to government, despite the reduction by CFAF 9 billon in the stock of Central Bank statutory advances to the government. The reduction in government deposits and delays in World Bank disbursement mentioned above were a factor behind the growth in net credit to the government. Net foreign assets rose further, allowing Senegal to maintain its contribution to the foreign asset position of the Central Bank of West African States (BCEAO).  

9. The BCEAO's reference interest rates were lowered by $\%$ of 1 percentage point early in 1999 and remained stable during the remainder of the year. Money market rates and the banks' average base lending rate likewise held steady at 4.95 percent and 8.7 percent, respectively, throughout the year. Senegal's banking system is generally sound: most banks were profitable in 1998; as a ratio to total credit, nonperforming loans (net of provisions) declined to 7.6 percent as at June 30, 1999 from 7.9 percent at end-1998; one bank that did not meet a number of prudential ratios specified by the West African Monetary Union (WAMU) Banking Commission was recapitalized and privatized during the year. However, some prudential ratios are still not being complied with at a major local bank, particularly the ratio regarding insider lending.  

10. Progress has been achieved in implementing structural reforms, but there were also delays in a number of areas:  

Tariff reform. 481 tariff lines in the tariff discounting mechanism (the precisions tarifaires) were eliminated on September 30, (more than the 473 precisions envisaged in the program), and another 391 précisions were removed at end-December 1999, as  

programmed. In addition, the government has already adopted a number of accompanying measures in the context of WAEMU's common external tariff (CET), including replacing the cigarette surtax by a degressive protection tax (TDP) as of August l999. However, the surtaxes on rice imports were not eliminated on September 30, as programmed, because of a delay in calculating the domestic production cost needed for implementing the compensatory import levy. The draft law on the reform of the civil service retirement system was submitted to parliament on schedule. Consistent with WAEMU requirements, the audited budget laws for 1986-96 have been submitted to parliament, and the draft audited budget law for 1997 is nearing completion.  

Public enterprise reform. Three out of the thirteen units selected for privatization have been effectively privatized. An initial tranche of 34 percent of the capital of the national electricity company (SENELEC) was sold to a private consortium in March; in order to lower the government's stake to the planned level of 41 percent, the government shares to be sold were transferred to a trust account with a commercial bank. Air Senégal was sold in March 1999. The hotel Meridien President was put up for sale on August 31, as scheduled.  

Private sector development and investment promotion. The drafting of legislation for carrying out many of the recommendations contained in the Foreign Investment Advisory Service (FIAS) report started in December 1999. In addition, draft legislation establishing the investment and export promotion agency (APIX) was submitted to the government in January 20o0. Finally, efforts are under way for the implementation by a unit named “Trade Point" of an electronic processing of documents, which will facilitate foreign trade.  

National program for good governance. In the context of the government's efforts to promote the rule of law and fight corruption, an action plan was adopted by the customs administration to combat fraud. The government also launched inquiries that led to the arrests of a number of civil servants involved in the embezzlement of public funds, particularly at the Treasury administration. Moreover, the courts have sentenced civil servants found guilty of embezzlement of in-kind assistance donated to flood victims. The audit of the FPE was completed on March 13, 2000, slightly behind schedule.  

Reforms pertaining to the legal and judicial framework for economic activities are progressing, including implementation of the Organization for the Harmonization of Business Law in Africa (OHADA) Uniform Acts for setting forth simplified recovery and enforcement procedures, and establishing collective procedures for the settlement of liabilities and arbitration law. In addition, the draft law pertaining to the profession of paralegals will be submitted to parliament before December 31, 2000.  

The reform of the central government is being pursued. In this area, the surveys of the quality of public services have been completed and the national dialogue on the quality of public services and good governance is in progress. The staffing plans for ministries have been prepared, and the mixed system of merit-based promotion in the civil service became operational in January 2o0o, following training seminars in the new system for evaluating performance of civil servants. Finally, decentralization measures are continuing, with the strengthening of the capacity of the Municipal Development Agency (ADM) and the setting up of the regional development agencies (ARD) in the ten administrative regions of Senegal.  

11. Progress has also been achieved with respect to sectoral reforms in the energy, transportation, and agriculture sectors, although there have also been some delays. In the energy sector, the convention with the Societé Africaine de Raffinage (SAR) has been cancelled; the distribution margins have been increased slightly, pending completion of additional studies pertaining to these margins; a supplementary study on the technical specifications for petroleum products has been completed, but the recommendations of the study will have to be harmonized with regional WAEMU guidelines; the regulatory commission for the power sector has been established in cooperation with the World Bank; and the pilot program to supply households with kerosene has already been carried out.  

12. However, faced with the sharp increase in petroleum prices, in February 2000 the government discontinued the monthly automatic adjustment of retail prices on the basis of world prices. Confronted with this situation, the incoming administration has decided to limit the resulting budgetary subsidy to CFAF 17 billion in 2000, and to restart the automatic monthly adjustment as soon as this ceiling has been reached. The pricing policy of petroleum products will be reviewed from time to time with Fund staff and, in particular, at the time of the negotiations for the third year PRGF program. The call for bids for SAR's investments for which a transitional surtax had been introduced in 1998 will be launched with some delay in May 2000 and the outcome of the bids will be announced before July 31, 2000. The establishment of the Senegalese Rural Electrification Agency (ASER) is also somewhat behind schedule. With respect to transportation, the secretariat of the Consultative Committee on Highway Transportation (CCTR) was set up by end-1999. However, the start of operations of the Autonomous Roadworks Agency (ATR), created by decree in December 1999, is running slightly behind schedule. The government is implementing the program of priority investments for the agriculture sector and has adopted a letter of policy for the fisheries sector, which clearly delineates the respective roles of the government and the private sector in the fishing industry.  

13. Human resource development and poverty reduction remain central to the government's concerns. Following a study that identified the obstacles impeding utilization of budget appropriations in the health and education sectors, (which was submitted to Fund staff on schedule), an action plan was implemented to overcome these obstacles. In particular, the government is working to improve the payments process to ensure that financing can swiftly reach local authorities.  

14. Senegal is very actively involved in initiatives to promote the economic integration of the WAEMU countries. In this context, a convergence, stability, growth, and solidarity pact was adopted by the Heads of States and Governments of the WAEMU in December 1999 that establishes quantitative convergence criteria and a multilateral  

surveillance procedure. Furthermore, all the complementary safeguard mechanisms in the context of the CET are being put in place. Efforts to harmonize indirect taxation and formulate sectoral policies continue, and the structural funds intended to assist the most disadvantaged regions within the union have been established.  

# VII. MEASURES TO BE IMPLEMENTED DURING 2OOO AND THE PERIOD AHEAD  

15.Building on the progress already achieved, the government is determined to press ahead with the programmed reforms, especially since the forecast for real GDP growth currently appears to be somewhat less favorable than originally expected, with the secondary and tertiary sectors projected to make a more modest contribution to overall value added. The latest projections suggest an average GDP growth rate of about 5 percent instead of 6 percent for 2000 and 2001, respectively. Inflation will be kept below the target of 3 percent. The external current account deficit (excluding official transfers equivalent to 3.8 percent of GDP) may reach 6.6 percent of GDP in 2000, exceeding somewhat the programmed level of 6.3 percent of GDP, largely because of the significant increase in world oil prices. Export volumes are projected to increase by about 12 percent, reflecting an upsurge in groundnut exports after the good harvest in 1999, while imports are expected to rise moderately in volume terms, given the sizable available stocks of sugar and rice.  

16. The 20oo budget was reviewed extensively with the Fund staff. The basic surplus was revised downwards to 1 percent of GDP from 1.5 percent of GDP, because of the cost of freezing retail prices of petroleum products and the higher-than-expected arrears of the postal system. The overall budget deficit (on a commitments basis and excluding grants) is projected to exceed the program projection of 3.4 percent of GDP by 0.9 percentage point.20 Including grants, the overall deficit is projected at 1.6 percent of GDP, compared with the programmed level of 1.2 percent of GDP. Budgetary revenue is targeted at CFAF 550 billion, somewhat higher than initially projected. As a ratio to GDP, tax revenue is expected to exceed the initial projections of 15.3 percent by 1.3 percentage points.21 With no change expected in the estimated revenue loss associated with the tariff reduction under the CET (approximately 1 percent of GDP), the stronger revenue projections for 2000 are largely predicated upon (i) stronger collection effort at the tax and customs departments where a number of senior managers have been replaced, and (ii) a significant increase in oil tax revenue, given the upturn in world prices. The projected revenue performance will also be comforted by three major sets of measures:  

The WAEMU's CET was introduced on January 1, 20o0, with the following key features: (i) elimination of the 5 percent customs stamp duty on most imports and introduction of the community-wide 1 percent statistical fee; (ii) implementation of the decision of the WAEMU Commission on reclassification of the 150 tarif lines for which Senegal had expressed reservations, as well as on the précisions tarifaires; (iii) submission of the necessary documentation to the WAEMU Commission to replace existing surtaxes by compensatory import levies (TCI);22 and (iv) submission of the necessary documentation to the WAEMU Commission to replace the variable import surtax on broken rice and the specific surtax on whole rice with a TCI, consistent with the program commitment to simplify rice import taxation system. Moreover, throughout the program period, the authorities will not introduce any new protective measures that will undermine the tariff reform.  

By July 1, 2000, a VAT will be introduced at the single rate of 18 percent, and the number of exemptions will be minimized, as envisaged in the program. All required steps will be taken, and Parliament will be convened to this end. The additional revenue expected from the single rate VAT is projected at 6.9 percent of GDP on a full-year basis as projected in the program, offsetting to some extent the revenue loss associated with the tariff reduction in 2000.  

Four important tax administration measures proposed by a technical assistance mission from the IMF's Fiscal Affairs Department, will be adopted, namely: (i) setting up a largetaxpayer unit by June 30, 2000; (ii) generalizing the use of a single tax identification number in allrevenue collection agencies by December 31, 2000 (upon completion of a study currently in progress); (ii) restructuring the tax roll assessment division by June 30, 200; and (iv) restructuring the tax department (DGID) along functional lines by June 30, 20o0. The government plans to increase budgetary allocations by CFAF 350 million for the computerization of the tax department, thereby strengthening the role of the DGID in the task of mobilizing government revenue, following the entry into force of the CET on January 1, 2000.  

At an any rate, in case a shortfall in revenue collection materializes, the government stands ready to cutback on expenditures commensurately, in order to achieve the programmed overall budgetary deficit target.

# IX. DEBT SUSTAINABILITY  

22. Following the debt-stock-reduction operation granted by Paris Club creditors in June 1998, the government signed bilateral agreements with all its creditors. The government has also concluded an agreement (on terms comparable to Paris Club terms) with a non-Paris Club bilateral creditor and is pursuing efforts with other creditors along the same lines.  

23. In light of the enhancements to the HIPC Initiative, the updated debt sustainability analysis (DSA) for Senegal at end-1998 shows the following results: a net present value (NPV) of debt-to-exports ratio of 162 percent, as compared to the target of 150 percent; an NPV debt-to-revenue ratio of 305 percent as compared to the threshold of 250 percent; a revenue-to-GDP ratio of 17 percent, as compared to the threshold of 15 percent; and an export to-GDP ratio of 34 percent, which exceeds the threshold of 30 percent. These preliminary results were verified and confirmed through a reconciliation of the data with bilateral and multilateral creditors. Hence, the government requests debt relief within the framework of the enhanced Initiative.  

24.Senegal will continue to implement a prudent debt-management policy and will have recourse only to grants and long-term external loans on concessional terms. Accordingly, the government will neither contract nor guarantee any nonconcessional loans, with the  

exception of import credits.3² All loans contracted or guaranteed by the government must first be approved by the Minister of Economy and Finance. The government willsettle all external debt-service obligations in a timely fashion and will not accumulate any external payments arrears.  

# X. POVERTY REDUCTION STRATEGY  

25. Poverty alleviation and human resource development are the centerpiece of the government's reform program. The government has prepared an Interim Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper and it will take appropriate steps to prepare a comprehensive poverty reduction strategy in coordination with its development partners and following extensive consultation with civil society. This strategy will be based, inter alia, on achieving significant improvements in the overall business climate by building an environment supportive of private sector initiatives, and it will aim at enhancing the efficiency of services provided to the population at the local level, particularly through budgeting by objectives and through decentralization.

# Commentary on lending operations  

1． The over-arching objective of the Bank's assistance strategy for Senegal is to reduce the incidence of poverty and create gainful employment, especially for youth. The approved lending program for the period FY98-2000 under the Country Assistance Strategy (CAS) is being implemented accordingly. The CAS objective is supported by a two-pronged strategy: (i) supporting policies and programs for more rapid and sustained growth; and (ii) ensuring social sustainability of programs. The strategy is being implemented through a combination of structural adjustment lending (energy sector reform and trade liberalization), sector investment program (SIP), specific investment lending, and appropriate non-lending operations. Analytical work on the transitional costs of trade reform has led to the preparation of the Trade Reform Adjustment Credit planned for FY200l. The Public Expenditure Review (PER) is helping the government improve the size and composition of public expenditures for the period 1998-2000.  

2. As of March 31, 2000, the World Bank had approved 87 projects for Senegal for a total amount of about US\$2046.6 million, consisting of 68 projects for IDA credits of US\$1883.6 million, and 19 projects for IBRD loans of US\$163.0 million. Past projects have supported agricultural diversification, irrigation, human resources development, institutional development, and expansion of the country's infrastructure, particularly its transport system. In recent years, emphasis had shifted to better utilization and maintenance of existing facilities and to helping the government resolve some of the key issues related to long-term development prospects. The current portfolio represents a commitment value of about US\$646.1 million equivalent with undisbursed balance of about US\$480.1 million. The portfolio is composed of 17 projects in various sectors (agriculture, human resourcespopulation/health/nutrition/education, infrastructure/urban development, energy/water, industry, and private sector development). Three projects were approved this fiscal year 2000, i.e., a national infrastructure program, a quality education for all, and a Y2K national action plan support. Two more projects (an urban mobility project and a distance learning LIL) wil be presented to the Board for approval this fiscal year (FY2000). About six project: are in the pipeline for FY2001; they trade reform, social development, long-term water, community nutrition, energy sector adjustment, and private sector development).  

3. As of March 31, 2000, the International Finance Corporation (IFC) had five active investments totaling about $\mathrm{US}\$26.2$ million. They include two financial services; one fishery project; one leather manufacturing operation; and one power project (the first independent power project in Senegal). Two additional investments (power project and mining operation) were approved in 1998 and one investment (cement industry) was approved in 1999; all these three new investments are expected to become effective this year.  

4. The Bank and IFC will continue to coordinate their respective roles in providing enabling environment for private sector development for Senegal. The Bank will concentrate on factors that improve the business environment, and IFC, with its office in Dakar, will increase its direct support to the private sector, especially in the power, mining, small- and medium-size businesses, privatization, and capital market areas.

# Senegal: Statistical Issues  

Overall, Senegal's economic database is comprehensive, but there are weaknesses in the data on national accounts, production, international trade, the balance of payments, and social indicators. The authorities are continuing their efforts to improve the quality and availability of data. In addition to ensuring that the legal provisions requiring economic agents to report statistical information on a timely basis are enforced, the government has started to implement an action plan to reduce remaining statistical weaknesses in the national accounts, foreign trade, industrial production, and social indicators data. To this end, the resources allocated to its statistics department have been increased and technical assistance from the IMF and from other partners is being sought.  

# Real sector  

The national accounts data suffer from serious deficiencies in their compilation and projections. Finalized historical data are based on returns from more than l,ooo firms, a process that typically takes up to three years. Preliminary data are often subject to substantial revisions, partly because they are increasingly based on the use of an inputoutput simulation model developed with French and Canadian technical assistance, and less on the analysis of a sample of 300 enterprises. In addition, there are some methodological difficulties in the treatment of indicators of the trend growth in value added in some sectors. The industrial production index is based on a highly outdated sample, and developments in the tertiary sector, which represents about 60 percent of total value added, are difficult to monitor. Trade data are incomplete, owing to the time taken by the customs directorate-up to three years--to report trade flows.  

Within the West African Economic and Monetary Union (WAEMU), Senegal embarked on a regional harmonization process of statistical methodologies. A new harmonized consumer price index was introduced in January 1998. Also, regional efforts on the measurement of informal activities are being undertaken, and a harmonized industrial production index is expected to be developed soon.  

# Public finances  

Government finance statistics (GFS) data are compiled by the Ministry of Finance from the following three sources: the customs, tax, and treasury directorates. GFS data have not been reported by the authorities to STA for many years. The latest data reported for Senegal were published in the 1990 Government Finance Statistics Yearbook and covered consolidated central government through 1984. However, comprchensive fiscal data are provided regularly without delays to AFR on a quarterly basis.  

# Monetary data  

Monetary data for Senegal are prepared by the national agency of the Central Bank of West African States (BCEAO). There has been improvement in the timeliness and quality of data on interest rates, monetary authorities, and deposit money banks. The authorities are now reporting monetary data to STA on a regular basis with a significant reduction in lag from about 6 months to 3 months. Most of the monetary statistical issues have been resolved. Nevertheless, memorandum items on Treasury and Post Office accounts in the report form of deposit money banks (2oR) are not provided on a regular basis.  

# Balance of payments data  

Balance of payments data for Senegal are compiled by the national agency of the BCEAO. A long-term technical assistant from STA was posted at the BCEAO headquarters to provide technical assistance to all BCEAO member countries (posting ended in July 1999). With his support, several actions were undertaken in recent years to tackle balance of payments deficiencies: (a) the methodology was adapted to integrate the new norms defined by the fifth edition of the Balance of Payments manual; (b) surveys sent to companies and banks were modified and simplified; (c) the computerization of procedures was improved; and (d) the training of staff was significantly strengthened. Quarterly balance of payments data are expected to be available for the first time in 2000. Despite these improvements, some further efforts will continue to be necessary in order to enhance the quality and the coverage of data.
